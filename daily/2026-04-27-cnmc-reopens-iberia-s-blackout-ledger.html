CNMC Reopens Iberia’s Blackout Ledger

On the eve of the first anniversary of the Spain-Portugal blackout, a European post-mortem and Spain’s new enforcement files have turned an invisible grid service into a public question: who is responsible for voltage when the power system changes faster than its rulebook?

By The Press • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

Voltage is the quiet table manners of electricity. Too low, and machines sag. Too high, and equipment starts excusing itself from the party before something worse happens. Most people never notice, because the grid is supposed to perform this etiquette routine millions of times a second without requesting applause.

On April 28, 2025, that manners problem became a public event. At 12:33 p.m. in Spain, 11:33 a.m. in mainland Portugal, the power systems of continental Spain and Portugal collapsed. Trains stopped. Metro entrances became small civic puzzles. Card readers failed. Mobile service thinned. Traffic lights went dark. Elevators, hospitals, airports, fuel pumps, ATMs and kitchen freezers all discovered, together, that the modern world is very good at pretending electricity is background.

On April 27, 2026, the eve of the blackout’s first anniversary, the story is no longer a single dark afternoon. It is paperwork, testimony, enforcement and a surprisingly lively argument about a dull-sounding service: voltage control. On March 20, 2026, the European Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity, or ENTSO-E, published the final report from a 49-member expert panel on the incident. In mid-April, Spain’s competition and markets regulator, the Comisión Nacional de los Mercados y la Competencia, or CNMC, opened sanctioning proceedings after finding possible signs of noncompliance by Red Eléctrica, the Spanish system operator, and a group of generating companies or installations.

The useful lesson is not that one villain flipped one bad switch. The useful lesson is stranger and more important: a grid can fail because many actors each do something that may look understandable in isolation, while the system as a whole runs out of invisible cushion.

That cushion has a technical name here: voltage and reactive power control. It sounds like something hidden in a substation cabinet because it is. It also helps decide whether a hospital generator, a commuter train, a grocery checkout and a phone mast get to remain boring.

The blackout did not wait for a satisfying story

Big outages attract simple explanations because simple explanations travel faster than electricity. In the hours after the Iberian blackout, cyberattack theories rushed into the gap. So did claims that solar power had broken the grid. So did early accounts pointing toward atmospheric conditions. Some explanations were plausible enough to ask about. Some were politically useful. None, on the record now available, can carry the story alone.

ENTSO-E’s final report says the blackout resulted from many interacting factors: oscillations, gaps in voltage and reactive power control, differences in voltage regulation practices, rapid output reductions and generator disconnections in Spain, and uneven stabilising capabilities. Spain’s government report, presented in June 2025 by Ecological Transition Minister Sara Aagesen, also described a multifactorial overvoltage event and said a cyberattack had been ruled out by the investigation it commissioned. Red Eléctrica, for its part, has emphasized that the European panel did not identify a cause attributable to the system operator alone.

That is the political knot. If the cause is distributed, responsibility may still exist, but it will not look like a detective story. It will look like operating procedures, protection settings, compensation rules, data-sharing habits, plant instructions, regulator decisions and control-room judgment under pressure.

Spain’s CNMC proceedings matter for that reason. They are not final findings of guilt. They are formal files opened after the regulator said it had detected possible breaches that should be investigated with due process. Spanish reporting described an initial batch of 20 proceedings: one involving Red Eléctrica and others involving installations or companies linked to Endesa, Iberdrola, Naturgy, Repsol, Bahía de Bizkaia Electricidad and Ascó-Vandellòs nuclear interests. Subsequent April reporting described more proceedings, some framed more broadly around alleged sectoral noncompliance rather than a clean causal chain for the blackout day itself.

That distinction matters. Enforcement headlines can make a messy technical record sound cleaner than it is. The regulator’s own reported caution is central: the opening of files does not, by itself, attribute the origin of the blackout to the companies affected. It says there may have been obligations worth formally testing.

Still, the files move the blackout out of the realm of national memory and into the realm of institutional consequence. The question is no longer only why the lights went out. It is who had a duty to see the voltage trouble coming, who had a duty to respond, what tools they actually possessed, and whether Spain’s rules paid for the right kind of help.

Reactive power is not a side quest

Electric grids need a balance of real power and something called reactive power. Real power is the part most of us think we are buying: it runs the blender, spins the factory motor, cools the vaccine fridge and charges the phone. Reactive power does not get consumed in the same obvious way, but it helps maintain voltage on alternating-current networks. Think of it as pressure support in electrical plumbing.

That analogy is imperfect, as all grid analogies are. Electricity is not water. But the pressure idea helps. If voltage climbs too high, equipment may disconnect to protect itself. If enough equipment disconnects at the wrong moment, the system can get even harder to control. The April 2025 event was unusual because the cascade involved rising voltage and generation disconnections, not the more familiar low-voltage collapse many planners historically worried about.

ENTSO-E described the blackout as the most severe European power-system incident in more than 20 years and a first-of-its-kind event for the Continental Europe synchronous area. In plainer language: the Iberian grid did not merely lose a few plants and muddle through. It separated, surged through a fast chain of disconnections and blacked out across continental Spain and Portugal. A small area near the Spanish border in southwest France saw brief disruption, but the wider European system avoided significant disturbance.

That separation is one reason the blackout is such a clean local-to-global systems story. It began in the physical behaviour of a particular grid, with important events in Spain, but it has become a warning to every region adding solar, wind, batteries, heat pumps, electric vehicles and digital demand onto infrastructure designed for a different century of machines.

The older grid was built around large spinning generators: coal, gas, hydro and nuclear units with heavy rotating equipment. Those machines naturally contribute inertia, a stabilising resistance to sudden frequency changes. They can also help with voltage control. The newer grid still uses many of those machines, but more electricity now arrives through power electronics. Solar panels, many wind turbines and batteries connect through devices called inverters, which convert electricity into the form the grid needs.

Inverters are not bad citizens. Modern inverters can be fast, precise and useful. They can provide services the old grid needs, including absorbing or supplying reactive power. Grid-forming inverters, a newer class of controls, can even help establish voltage and frequency rather than merely following a grid already shaped by big spinning machines. But those abilities depend on rules, settings, testing, markets and operator visibility. A device that can help is not the same as a device that is required, paid, configured and trusted to help at the exact second the system needs it.

The renewables blame game misses the harder engineering

Spain is one of Europe’s renewable-energy leaders. That fact made the blackout irresistible ammunition in arguments about wind and solar. It also made many arguments sloppy. The European and Spanish records do not support the lazy version: sunshine broke the grid, therefore clean power is the problem.

A better version is more uncomfortable for all sides. A system with high renewable output and changing demand patterns needs sharper voltage control, better models, more real-time monitoring, stronger rules for generator and inverter behaviour, and enough resources able to absorb or supply reactive power when called. That can include conventional generators, synchronous condensers, batteries, advanced inverters, grid devices and better operating procedures. It is not a referendum with only two boxes: fossil past or renewable future.

SolarPower Europe and allied renewable-energy groups seized on the ENTSO-E report to argue for faster regulatory updates, especially rules allowing renewables to provide dynamic voltage control, batteries and grid-forming inverter services. That is a self-interested argument, but not necessarily a wrong one. The U.S. Department of Energy describes modern inverters as capable of grid services, including reactive power support, when designed and operated for those functions. In the United States, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission approved new reliability standards in 2025 aimed at inverter-based generators, including ride-through capability, meaning the ability to stay connected during voltage or frequency disturbances rather than tripping offline too quickly.

On the other hand, conventional power companies and critics of rapid renewable buildout have a real point when they say physics cannot be legislated away. If a grid retires or idles sources that once provided voltage support, someone must replace that support with equal seriousness. If markets reward cheap energy but underpay stability, then stability becomes a charity project. Charities are a poor way to run a continent.

The Iberian blackout therefore complicates both slogans. It does not prove that renewables are inherently unreliable. It does not prove that renewables can be added indefinitely while control rules catch up later. It suggests that the energy transition is less like swapping one fuel in a boiler and more like changing the musicians, sheet music, conductor, acoustics and fire code while the concert is in progress.

A few seconds, then a very long day

The collapse itself moved with brutal speed. The human consequences did not. A grid can fall in seconds and leave people improvising for hours.

Payment systems are a good example because they are wonderfully ordinary. The Banco de España later examined the blackout’s effect on Spain’s payment infrastructure. The point was not that money vanished. It was that the everyday choreography of retail life depends on electricity, networks, terminals, bank systems and settlement patterns all staying upright together. When power and communications falter at the same time, a person with money in the bank can still be unable to buy lunch, fuel or medicine unless cash, trust or backup systems fill the gap.

Transport made the dependency visible. Rail systems stopped. Metros closed. Passengers waited, walked, crowded buses or slept in awkward places. Airports continued some operations but faced disruption. Streets inherited traffic from signals that had gone blank. Fuel logistics became harder because pumps, payment systems and communications all sit on the same electrical floor.

Hospitals and emergency services did what they are supposed to do: switch to backup plans. But backup power is not a magic cloak. It depends on generators, batteries, fuel, maintenance, communications, staff, prioritisation and time. The human aftermath is also difficult to count cleanly. Some deaths were reported as connected to the outage, including cases involving home medical equipment, fire and generator use. Other health effects are harder to attribute, because blackout harm can appear indirectly: delayed care, heat or cold exposure, lost communications, stress, missed medicines and frail people stranded by lifts or transport.

Portugal responded with its own repair list. In July 2025, the Portuguese government announced measures to strengthen the National Electricity System’s security, including investments in the grid, more black-start capacity and resilience measures for critical infrastructure. Black start is the ability of parts of the power system to restart without relying on electricity from the grid, like having a camp stove when the kitchen is down. Portugal’s geography makes this especially sensitive because its main electrical connection to the rest of Europe runs through Spain. A problem that starts elsewhere can still arrive at Portuguese sockets.

The grid’s paperwork is part of the machine

It is tempting to imagine the grid as a landscape of steel towers, humming transformers and control-room screens. That is only half of it. The other half is paperwork with consequences.

Operating procedures decide who must provide voltage control and when. Grid codes decide how generators and inverters should behave during disturbances. Market rules decide whether a plant is paid to be available for a service that may be desperately needed once every few years. Data rules decide whether the system operator can see enough of the distribution grid, where more small generators and flexible loads now live. Regulator timelines decide whether old procedures are updated before or after they become museum pieces.

That is why the CNMC proceedings are more than a blame exercise. If they are handled well, they can force a public accounting of which obligations were clear, which were obsolete, which were ignored and which were impossible to meet in real time. If handled poorly, they can turn a system failure into a legal trench war that teaches everyone to protect themselves first and the grid second.

Red Eléctrica’s position is important here. The operator has argued that the European expert panel confirmed the causes it had identified and did not find a cause attributable to Red Eléctrica. That is not the same as saying every action by every actor was ideal. It is a reminder that system operators work inside rules and asset fleets they do not fully own. They can instruct, monitor, redispatch and restore, but they cannot conjure physical voltage support from a plant that is not online, a device that is not configured, or data that is not available.

Power companies have their own defense. They can say they followed the rules as they understood them, that their plants were not solely responsible, and that system operation belongs to the operator. Some may argue that if voltage-control services were needed, the market or regulation should have procured them clearly. Those arguments deserve scrutiny, not caricature. A grid that depends on private, public and mixed actors must be explicit about who owes what to whom before the lights go out.

Spain’s post-blackout costs add another practical pressure. After the incident, Red Eléctrica used a more reinforced operating mode, including more gas-fired combined-cycle plants scheduled for technical restrictions. Spanish reporting has connected that operating posture to higher system costs. The lesson is not simply that caution is expensive. It is that missing stability can be expensive twice: first when the system fails, and again when operators buy extra insurance after public trust has been damaged.

Europe is not the only audience

The Iberian blackout is now a reference case far beyond Spain and Portugal. The International Energy Agency’s 2026 electricity work treats reliability as a strategic issue as electricity takes a larger role in transport, buildings, industry and digital life. The same electrification that can cut emissions also raises the stakes of failure. If cars, heaters, payment systems, warehouses, water pumps and medical devices lean harder on the grid, the grid’s hidden services stop being hidden.

North American regulators and reliability bodies have been wrestling with similar issues around inverter-based resources. FERC’s 2025 action on inverter-based generator standards did not happen because of Iberia alone, but the rhyme is clear: when large amounts of power electronics join a bulk power system, rules for disturbance ride-through, modeling and voltage support cannot remain optional footnotes.

There is also a communications lesson. Early blackout narratives are often wrong, incomplete or both. Governments want to reassure. Companies want to avoid liability. Citizens want practical instructions. Social media wants a culprit before dinner. The Iberian case shows the value of staged reporting: immediate restoration information, factual reconstruction, technical analysis, then enforcement where warranted. It also shows the frustration of that sequence. People whose businesses lost a day, whose train trips dissolved, or whose medical routines were disrupted do not experience an expert panel as comfort.

NASA’s Black Marble satellite imagery of the blackout offered one of the clearest metaphors: darkness seen from above. But the harder picture is at street level. A shopkeeper with a dead payment terminal. A commuter reading a handwritten sign. A hospital engineer checking generator fuel. A grid operator watching voltage rise in a place where the models, rules and available machines no longer leave much room for surprise.

What repair looks like when the lights are already back

Most infrastructure repair happens after public attention has wandered away. The lights return. The fridge hums. The train apps work. Then come the expensive, unphotogenic fixes: new monitoring systems, rewritten operating procedures, drills, compliance testing, software updates, contracts for ancillary services, battery rules, inverter settings, black-start plans, substation equipment, data-sharing agreements and regulator deadlines.

Ancillary services are the grid’s support jobs. They are not the headline electricity sold by the kilowatt-hour. They are the services that keep the system stable: frequency control, reserves, voltage support, reactive power and restoration capability. A blackout like Iberia’s says those services need to be visible, valued and verified. You cannot discover during a voltage emergency that the thing you thought you bought is not the thing the physics requires.

The repair list will not be identical everywhere. Spain’s voltage ranges, interconnection limits, generation mix and regulatory history are not Texas, California, India, Japan or South Africa. Portugal’s dependence on the Spanish connection is not the same as an island grid’s isolation. But the pattern travels: more electrification, more power electronics, more weather-dependent generation, more digital dependence and less tolerance for long outages.

The best version of the lesson is not fear. It is competence. A cleaner grid can be a reliable grid, but not by assumption. A digital economy can be resilient, but not if every backup plan requires the same network to be working. A regulator can assign penalties, but only if it also fixes incentives. A power company can defend itself, but it still lives in a system where everyone else’s settings matter.

One year on, the Iberian blackout is no longer simply a dark day in Spain and Portugal. It is a ledger. On one side are the seconds of collapse: voltage, oscillations, disconnections, separation. On the other are the months of responsibility: reports, proceedings, investments, rule changes and arguments over who should have done what sooner.

The ledger will not balance neatly. That may be its most honest feature. Modern infrastructure rarely fails as a morality play. It fails as a system. Then, if the public is lucky and the institutions are serious, it is repaired as one.
