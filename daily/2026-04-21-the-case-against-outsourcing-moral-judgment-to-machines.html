The Case Against Outsourcing Moral Judgment to Machines

As governments, employers, courts, and hospitals rush to fold AI into public life, the real philosophical question is no longer whether machines can think. It is whether democratic societies are willing to let systems that cannot bear responsibility make decisions humans must still be able to justify.

By The Press • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m.

There is a tempting story about artificial intelligence in 2026: the systems are getting more capable, more widely available, and more deeply embedded in everyday institutions, so the sensible response is to let them do more of the work. That story now reaches well beyond chatbots and image generators. It includes public administrations adopting AI to triage service requests and assist staff, companies deploying it in hiring and productivity tools, hospitals testing it for diagnosis and scheduling, and policymakers debating how to govern models that move across borders faster than laws do. But beneath the productivity pitch lies a philosophical error with practical consequences: a system can be useful without being entitled to make moral judgments on our behalf.

That is the core issue now. Public debate often treats AI governance as a technical matter of risk management, standards, model evaluation, or compliance. Those are real issues, and the institutions involved increasingly say so themselves. The OECD’s 2025 report on AI in government, for example, frames the central challenge as how to use AI to improve efficiency, effectiveness, and trust while building the enablers, guardrails, and engagement mechanisms needed for responsible deployment. NIST likewise describes its AI Risk Management Framework as a voluntary way to incorporate trustworthiness considerations into the design, development, use, and evaluation of AI systems. But the deeper question is ethical and political: when a machine ranks people, filters information, predicts behavior, allocates attention, or recommends a course of action, it is participating in a human practice that embeds values such as fairness, dignity, accountability, due process, and the right to contest an outcome. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

My thesis is simple: democratic societies should treat AI as an instrument of assistance, not an authority for moral outsourcing. That does not mean banning AI from public life. It means drawing a bright line between mechanical prediction and accountable judgment. Where rights, livelihoods, liberty, health, or reputation are at stake, AI should be constrained to advisory, auditable, and contestable roles. Humans may use AI to inform decisions, but institutions should not let the technology launder responsibility or convert a contested moral choice into a supposedly neutral output. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework?utm_source=openai))

Why this suddenly matters

AI governance is now a live political struggle, not an abstract seminar topic. In Europe, the AI Act has begun to apply in phases. According to the European Commission, prohibitions and AI literacy obligations have applied since 2 February 2025, governance and general-purpose AI obligations since 2 August 2025, and the main body of the law is scheduled to apply from 2 August 2026, with some high-risk product-related rules later. The Commission also says the rules on transparency will begin in August 2026, and it has proposed a Digital Omnibus adjustment that could affect the schedule for some high-risk obligations. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/navigating-ai-act?utm_source=openai))

In the United States, the fight has gone in a more deregulatory direction, though not without resistance. In March 2026, the White House urged Congress to preempt state AI laws, arguing for a light-touch framework centered on innovation, child protection, energy costs, intellectual property, censorship, and AI literacy. That federal push sits atop a year of clashes over state and local authority. In May 2025, House Republicans proposed a 10-year ban on state AI regulation; in July 2025, the Senate struck that provision by a 99-1 vote; and in December 2025, President Donald Trump signed an executive order aimed at blocking state AI regulations. On April 19, 2026, AP reported that Utah Republican Doug Fiefia was resisting the preemption push because of child-safety concerns. These are not merely turf disputes. They are arguments about political authority: who gets to decide what counts as acceptable AI risk, and who bears the consequences when automated systems fail? ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

One reason the issue feels urgent is that AI adoption is advancing faster than social consensus. The Stanford AI Index 2025 reported rising reported AI incidents in 2024 and wider deployment of systems across the economy, while also tracking continued progress in model capability. Stanford’s Foundation Model Transparency Index has moved in the other direction: the 2025 edition found declining transparency among major developers compared with 2024. The point is not that the technology has stalled. It is that institutional honesty, disclosure, and oversight have lagged behind the speed of deployment. ([hai.stanford.edu](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report/responsible-ai?utm_source=openai))

The policy world has noticed. The OECD’s 2025 report on governing with AI says governments are already experimenting in dozens of areas, from service delivery to forecasting and citizen-facing chat functions, but many efforts remain pilot-scale because of data quality, skills shortages, outdated IT, and concern about trustworthiness. The report’s central lesson is that efficiency and trust have to be designed together; one does not automatically produce the other. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

The moral lure of automation

Why do institutions keep reaching for AI even when they know the risks? Because AI offers a seductive promise: it appears to reduce human inconsistency. A hiring model promises to rank candidates fairly because it is “objective.” A risk score promises to allocate welfare checks, fraud investigations, or medical appointments efficiently because it is “data-driven.” A chatbot promises to answer public questions at scale because it is always on. The appeal is understandable. Humans are biased, tired, expensive, and often slow. The OECD explicitly notes that many AI use cases in government are incremental improvements or productivity gains, and NBER work on workplace AI found that generative AI can raise productivity, though unevenly across workers and tasks. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report/enablers-guardrails-and-engagement-for-unlocking-trustworthy-ai_2f817983.html?utm_source=openai))

But the word “objective” hides a philosophical mistake. Objectivity in this setting is not a property of the machine; it is a property of the institution that decides how to use the machine. Every AI system is trained on human-selected data, optimized for human-defined objectives, and deployed inside human legal and organizational structures. The fact that the output comes from a model does not make it morally neutral. It only makes its value judgments harder to see. NIST’s own AI-related publications stress trustworthiness, accountability, transparency, explainability, and contextual risk management, which is another way of saying that the system is not self-justifying simply because it is computational. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/trustworthy-and-responsible-ai?utm_source=openai))

That opacity matters because contestability is a core democratic value. When a person is denied a loan, a job, a benefit, or access to a service, they have reason to ask why. A human decision-maker can explain, even imperfectly, what factors mattered and whether discretion was exercised. An AI system may produce a probability, a score, or a recommendation, but if the institution cannot tell the affected person how that output was generated, the result is a procedural deficit. The decision may be efficient and still be unjust. NIST’s framework is built around that idea of managing risks while preserving trustworthiness, not around treating the model’s output as final authority. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework?utm_source=openai))

Research and governance work keep converging on this point from different angles. NIST’s AI RMF, while voluntary, is meant to help organizations manage risk across governance, mapping, measurement, and management functions. WHO’s ethics guidance for AI in health says that benefits in diagnosis, treatment, research, and public health are real, but ethics and human rights must remain at the heart of design, deployment, and use. In both domains, the message is not that AI must be banned; it is that AI must not be treated as an authority detached from accountable human institutions. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework/nist-ai-rmf-playbook?utm_source=openai))

The strongest case for letting AI decide

To argue for restraint is not to pretend the pro-AI case is foolish. It is often serious. The best defense of machine-assisted decision-making starts with scale. Governments and large institutions handle too many cases for careful human review of every file, every query, every anomaly. When properly designed, AI can help route requests, surface patterns, and reduce some forms of arbitrariness. The OECD report on governing with AI finds uses in public services, civic engagement, justice, forecasting, and citizen-facing chat functions. The point of such systems is not to replace the state but to make the state more responsive. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

There is also a fairness argument in favor of automation. Human decision-makers are not innocent benchmarks. They bring fatigue, stereotypes, mood, institutional incentives, and inconsistency. In some contexts, a standardized machine process can reduce these weaknesses. An AI system may apply the same criteria to every applicant, every case, or every image at a speed and consistency no human office can match. In hiring or admissions, that can look like progress; in medicine, it can mean faster triage; in public services, it can mean shorter waits. The promise is not merely efficiency but equal treatment. ([nber.org](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161?utm_source=openai))

Finally, there is the competitive argument. If AI tools genuinely improve productivity, then banning or over-restricting them could harm workers, firms, and public institutions. The ILO’s 2025 update says generative AI exposure is uneven across occupations and that the overall automation scores are slightly lower than in its earlier estimate, while also warning that nearly one in four workers may see their jobs transformed. IMF work in 2025 likewise suggests AI adoption can widen some inequalities even as it improves output, depending on how it is adopted and by whom. The lesson is not that AI is harmless; it is that the adjustment challenge is real and politically consequential. ([ilo.org](https://www.ilo.org/publications/generative-ai-and-jobs-2025-update?utm_source=openai))

These arguments deserve respect. If an organization can use AI to speed up benefits delivery, identify medical patterns, translate languages, or flag harmful content, refusing to use it can itself be unethical. Delay has costs. So does manual bureaucracy. So does human error. But the pro-AI case usually jumps too quickly from “useful” to “should decide.” That leap is the error. Utility alone cannot confer legitimacy. A system can be valuable and still be the wrong kind of thing to authorize. That is especially true when a decision affects someone’s standing, rights, or life chances. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report/enablers-guardrails-and-engagement-for-unlocking-trustworthy-ai_2f817983.html?utm_source=openai))

Why efficiency is not legitimacy

Suppose a model predicts who is likely to miss a court date, default on a loan, lose a job, or benefit from a medical intervention. If the model is accurate enough, it can be operationally useful. But the prediction is not the decision. The prediction identifies a statistical pattern; the decision imposes a moral and legal consequence on a person. A person is not the same thing as their risk score.

This distinction matters because institutions increasingly reward what can be counted and automated over what must be interpreted. The result is a quiet migration from judgment to optimization. Yet optimization cannot answer questions of justice by itself. Should a school use a model that predicts student failure if the model reproduces historical inequalities? Should a health system prioritize patients based on expected benefit if that disadvantages those with less access to past care? Should police use predictive tools to increase efficiency if the underlying data reflect over-policing in some neighborhoods? Those are not technical questions alone. They are moral questions about what kind of society we want. WHO’s governance framework for health and its 2025 collaboration on AI governance both underline that the benefits of AI in medicine must be balanced against ethics, rights, and evidence-based safeguards. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200?utm_source=openai))

The EU’s AI Act implicitly recognizes this by placing some uses in the “unacceptable risk” category and by imposing tighter obligations on higher-risk systems. The prohibitions include certain manipulative or exploitative uses, while separate rules govern general-purpose AI and high-risk applications. The Commission’s own explanation of the AI Act makes clear that the law is risk-based, with some uses restricted because they are incompatible with fundamental rights and democratic norms. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai?utm_source=openai))

That is the right philosophical instinct. But the deeper challenge is not only banning the worst uses. It is resisting a softer but more pervasive norm: that humans can delegate judgment whenever a system produces a plausible-looking output. The danger is not just automated abuse. It is moral abdication by institutions that still want the benefits of action without the burden of justification. NIST’s framework and playbook are useful here because they keep returning to trustworthiness, oversight, and risk management rather than surrendering authority to the model. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework/nist-ai-rmf-playbook?utm_source=openai))

Public trust is not a cosmetic issue

Supporters of broad AI deployment often talk about trust as if it were a branding problem: explain better, communicate better, reassure the public. But trust in institutions is not an accessory. It is one of the moral materials out of which democratic legitimacy is built. The OECD says trustworthy public services can enhance trust in government, and NIST makes trustworthiness part of the basic purpose of its AI framework. Those are not marketing claims. They are governance premises. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

Pew’s 2025 findings are important here because they show a public that is not merely uninformed but structurally uneasy. People worry that AI will amplify bad information, weaken human connection, and affect work in ways they cannot control. Experts are more optimistic than the public, but they share many of the same concerns, including data misuse, impersonation, misinformation, and bias. That gap should not be dismissed as technophobia. It may instead be a form of moral intuition: people sense that the systems being built around them are moving faster than the rules of accountability. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/04/03/views-of-risks-opportunities-and-regulation-of-ai/?utm_source=openai))

Transparency is part of the answer, but transparency alone is insufficient. Stanford’s 2025 Foundation Model Transparency Index found that average transparency scores fell to 40 out of 100 and that disclosure remained uneven across developers. That matters because opacity makes it difficult for outsiders to know what data shaped a system, how it was evaluated, or which failures were accepted as tolerable. Without such information, the public is asked to trust not just the outputs but the intentions and competence of the institutions behind them. ([news.stanford.edu](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/12/foundation-model-transparency-index-ai-companies-information?utm_source=openai))

And that is too much to ask in many settings. People are willing to delegate convenience to machines. They are far less willing to delegate power when the costs fall on them. In that sense, public resistance is not irrational; it is a demand for moral reciprocity. If a system can affect your life, you should be able to understand it, challenge it, and hold someone accountable for it. The WHO’s ethics guidance for health AI makes the same basic demand through another vocabulary: public benefit, human rights, accountability, and responsiveness to the people whose lives are affected. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200?utm_source=openai))

Work, inequality, and the hidden politics of AI

AI also raises a distributive question that philosophical debates sometimes understate. If the benefits of AI accrue mainly to firms, managers, and highly educated workers, while the risks land on clerical staff, contractors, and vulnerable communities, then AI becomes not a universal tool but a mechanism for concentrating advantage. IMF work in 2025 and Brookings analysis in 2026 both point in that direction: exposure to AI is uneven, and the capacity to adapt is not evenly distributed either. Brookings’ January 2026 study found that 26.5 million of 37.1 million highly AI-exposed U.S. workers also had above-median adaptive capacity, but it also identified 6.1 million workers with both high exposure and low adaptive capacity, concentrated in clerical and administrative roles. That is a reminder that aggregate resilience can coexist with concentrated vulnerability. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2025/04/04/ai-adoption-and-inequality-565729?utm_source=openai))

That inequality is not merely economic. It is ethical. A society that deploys AI to save money while leaving the costs of transition to workers is making a choice about whose time, dignity, and insecurity count. The philosophical language for this is recognition: do institutions recognize workers, patients, applicants, and citizens as persons with standing, or merely as inputs to be optimized? The difference shows up in whether people can appeal decisions, request human review, and participate in the design of the systems that govern them. Brookings’ work on AI and work emphasizes that displacement is not only about earnings; it also touches job insecurity, meaning, and identity. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/measuring-us-workers-capacity-to-adapt-to-ai-driven-job-displacement/?utm_source=openai))

Here the counterargument is powerful: past technologies also displaced workers, but societies adapted. New roles emerged. Productivity rose. The same may happen again. That is true. Yet it does not follow that the transition is morally trivial. Indeed, the history of automation suggests the opposite: benefits are often real, but they are not automatically shared. The political question is whether the gains from AI are distributed widely enough to justify the losses it imposes on specific groups. No model can answer that. Only institutions can. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2025/04/04/ai-adoption-and-inequality-565729?utm_source=openai))

Health care is the clearest test

Health care makes the ethical stakes especially vivid because the legitimacy of decision-making is already fragile. Patients expect expertise, yes, but they also expect care, explanation, and a human relationship of trust. WHO’s recent work on AI in health governance emphasizes exactly these values: ethical safeguards, evidence-based adoption, and collaboration. In March 2025, WHO designated the Digital Ethics Centre at Delft University of Technology as a WHO Collaborating Centre on AI for health governance, and WHO’s earlier 2021 guidance said AI in health should put ethics and human rights at the center of design, deployment, and use. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/10-03-2025-who-announces-new-collaborating-centre-on-ai-for-health-governance?utm_source=openai))

That matters because medicine is full of tempting uses for AI that are not obviously unacceptable. A model can help detect disease earlier, optimize staffing, predict treatment response, or assist with documentation. One may even wish that it would. Yet if the model begins to define what counts as a “good” patient, a “useful” treatment, or a “worthwhile” intervention, the institution risks replacing care with optimization. In medicine, that is not a small shift. It changes the moral grammar of the profession. Research published in 2025 on responsible AI in healthcare autonomous systems similarly emphasizes data integrity, interpretability, clinical relevance, fairness, regulatory compliance, human-AI collaboration, and user experience. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-25266-z?utm_source=openai))

A responsible framework therefore has to preserve a human veto in the places where value judgments are unavoidable. AI can rank possibilities. It cannot decide what counts as a life worth prioritizing when tradeoffs are inescapable. It cannot tell a patient whether risk tolerance should be traded for comfort, or how a family should interpret a diagnosis. Those are not deficits of the technology. They are reminders of the kinds of questions technology must not be allowed to own. WHO’s health guidance makes the same point by insisting that AI must support, not replace, the humane obligations of health systems. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200?utm_source=openai))

Education and children: the line is even clearer

If adult institutions sometimes blur the boundary between support and authority, child-facing institutions should not. Schools are already adopting AI for tutoring, grading support, and administrative tasks. Stanford’s AI Index notes that education systems are struggling with AI literacy and teacher preparedness, which suggests that many schools are moving faster than their educators can comfortably assess. That is a practical problem, but also a civic one: the next generation is growing up inside systems that will normalize machine-mediated judgment unless schools deliberately teach skepticism, transparency, and consent. ([hai.stanford.edu](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report/responsible-ai?utm_source=openai))

The ethical stakes are highest when AI systems interact with minors, because children cannot meaningfully consent to many of the data practices that feed these systems. They also cannot be expected to understand the long-term consequences of synthetic content, profiling, or recommendation systems. Recent reporting on California in 2025, for example, enacted a chatbot disclosure law for minors while vetoing a broader restriction bill, illustrating how unsettled the policy line remains even in one of the most tech-forward states. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/33be4d57d0e2d14553e02a94d9529976?utm_source=openai))

A society that treats children as a testing ground for ungoverned AI will discover too late that the burden of proof should always have been reversed. The question is not whether a tool can be used in classrooms. The question is whether the school can explain, audit, and justify the use in terms that respect students as developing persons, not data points. In Europe, the AI Act’s literacy obligations and phased implementation reflect the same insight: users and deployers need to understand what the systems can and cannot do before they are widely trusted. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/ai-literacy-questions-answers?utm_source=openai))

The governance compromise that is not a compromise

What would it mean, concretely, to refuse moral outsourcing while still benefiting from AI? It would mean adopting a hierarchy of uses. At the bottom are low-stakes, augmentative applications: translation, drafting assistance, summarization, accessibility, administrative routing. These can often be allowed with ordinary safeguards. In the middle are high-impact applications: hiring screens, credit scores, eligibility triage, medical decision support, and law-enforcement tools. These should face strict transparency, human review, and appeal rights. At the top are uses that should be presumptively prohibited because they are incompatible with democratic and human-rights norms: manipulative surveillance, social scoring, and certain forms of biometric or predictive control. The EU AI Act already points in this direction, and NIST and WHO frameworks reinforce it from different institutional angles. ([ai-watch.ec.europa.eu](https://ai-watch.ec.europa.eu/news/first-rules-artificial-intelligence-act-are-now-applicable-2025-02-02_en?utm_source=openai))

This is not a technophobic position. It is a moral architecture. It says that the more an AI system affects a person’s standing, the more the institution must prove that a human being remains answerable for the result. In practice, that means no unreviewable black-box denials, no hidden ranking systems that stand in for deliberation, and no policy environment in which the first question is whether the model performs well enough to trust, rather than whether the institution can still justify the decision to the person on the receiving end. The NIST glossary and playbook emphasize a common language for trustworthiness, while the Stanford transparency work shows why disclosure remains essential to making that language meaningful in practice. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/publications/language-trustworthy-ai-depth-glossary-terms?utm_source=openai))

There is also a democratic reason to insist on this architecture. If AI becomes the invisible middle layer between citizens and the state, it may erode the public’s ability to understand government. A welfare office that merely forwards model suggestions is not just using software; it is changing the citizen’s relationship to the state. A court that relies too heavily on automated risk tools may look efficient while becoming less legible. A hospital that hides its triage logic behind vendor confidentiality may appear modern while making fewer moral commitments to explanation. The institutions still exist, but the human face of responsibility thins out. The OECD’s government report and the UN’s 2024 AI-governance recommendations both warn, in different ways, that AI systems should strengthen institutions rather than hollow out accountability. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

The argument from progress, answered

The best objection to all of this is that it sounds slow. AI is advancing quickly. Competitors will not wait. If one country or institution restrains itself too much, another may reap the gains. That concern is not imaginary. It is the logic behind the current pressure on Europe’s AI Act, the arguments in Washington for national preemption, and the corporate push for voluntary codes over binding rules. OpenAI’s public policy materials and the broader industry landscape frame responsible deployment as compatible with growth and global leadership, not a substitute for it. Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy points in the same general direction, with company-specific thresholds and safety commitments. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/global-affairs/eu-code-of-practice/?utm_source=openai))

Still, speed is not the same as wisdom. History is full of technologies whose adoption outran governance. The result is usually not “innovation versus regulation” but “innovation plus regret.” The argument for restraint is strongest precisely because AI is already deeply embedded in public life. Once an institution normalizes machine recommendations, reversing course becomes harder, not easier. Early defaults matter. So do institutional habits. A society that lets AI become the default arbiter will eventually need to rediscover the reasons it valued human judgment in the first place. That is why the European Commission’s staged implementation timeline, rather than a single date of release, is philosophically significant: it treats governance as something that must be built in time, not proclaimed after the fact. ([digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/navigating-ai-act?utm_source=openai))

The pro-AI camp is right that rejectionism is not serious policy. We should not ask whether AI should exist, or even whether it should be widely used. It already is. The better question is whether we will insist on the dignity of explanation, the availability of appeal, and the preservation of human responsibility in the domains that define social membership. That is the line worth drawing. The OECD, NIST, WHO, the UN advisory process, and even critical transparency research all converge on the same practical implication: AI can assist institutions, but it should not be allowed to replace the moral agent that institutions are supposed to be. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287/full-report.html?utm_source=openai))

The deeper philosophy: what is a person for?

The most important reason to resist moral outsourcing is not that AI will always fail. It is that human institutions are not merely optimization engines. Their purpose is to deliberate about what should count, what should matter, and who should have standing. When a machine takes over that function, even partially, we are tempted to forget that decisions are not just outputs. They are acts of political and ethical interpretation.

This is why the language of “guardrails” can be misleading if it suggests that the only question is safety engineering. Guardrails matter, but they are not the whole road. A good society must also decide whether some judgments should remain stubbornly human not because humans are flawless, but because accountability itself is a human relation. We can apologize, explain, contest, forgive, and revise. A model can do none of those things on its own. Recent scholarship on accountability in human-AI relationships and on socioaffective alignment makes a related point from a different angle: people form moral expectations toward AI systems, but responsibility still has to be anchored in human institutions and social norms. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44387-025-00041-7?utm_source=openai))

That may sound old-fashioned. It is not. It is the oldest modern insight there is: institutions derive legitimacy not only from what they accomplish but from how they justify themselves to the people they govern. AI can help us do many things better. It cannot replace that obligation. In fact, the more powerful it becomes, the more urgent the obligation grows.

So the philosophical conclusion is not anti-technology. It is anti-abdication. Use AI to assist, compare, draft, detect, and illuminate. Do not use it to launder responsibility, hide value judgments, or turn human beings into entries in an optimization problem. When AI enters a domain of rights or dignity, the burden should remain on the institution to prove that a person, not a process, is still making the final moral call. WHO’s health guidance, NIST’s voluntary risk framework, the EU’s staged AI Act, and the UN’s governance work all point toward that same standard in different institutional languages. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200?utm_source=openai))

That is a demanding standard. It should be. In a moment when institutions are under pressure to move fast, cut costs, and automate authority, democratic societies need standards that are more demanding, not less. The point is not to slow down innovation for its own sake. The point is to keep human life answerable to human reasons.
