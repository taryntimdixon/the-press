The federal culture war has become a funding story

A string of court fights, grant decisions and institutional departures is turning America’s cultural life into a struggle over money, governance and public meaning.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 1:39 p.m.

For years, arguments over American culture were often narrated as arguments over taste: which works were canonical, which comedians were canceled, which museum label offended whom. In the spring of 2026, the more consequential fight is about something less glamorous and more structural. Federal power is being used, challenged, and sometimes blunted in court to determine who gets to fund museums, libraries, public broadcasting, and flagship cultural institutions — and on what terms. That shift matters because culture is no longer just content. It is payrolls, grant lines, visitor counts, student programs, archives, and the everyday institutions that decide whose stories are preserved and whose aren’t.

The latest flashpoint came on April 9, when plaintiffs challenging the Trump administration’s effort to shrink the Institute of Museum and Library Services reached a settlement with the Justice Department. The agreement preserved the agency’s ability to keep awarding grants and operating programs that support libraries and museums, according to the American Library Association and AFSCME, which said the settlement ensured IMLS would continue carrying out its congressionally mandated work. AP reported that the deal reversed some of the administration’s earlier steps, while the ALA said a separate appeal was withdrawn days earlier after a federal court ruling had already blocked the dismantling effort. In practical terms, the immediate crisis eased. In institutional terms, the fight showed how quickly a small federal agency can become a proxy battlefield over the public role of culture. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))

That would be enough to make this a policy story. But the dispute reaches further because it is happening at the same time as other federal pressure points on culture. On March 31, a federal judge permanently blocked the administration from ending federal funding for NPR and PBS, ruling that the directive violated the First Amendment by retaliating on the basis of viewpoint. In January, the Corporation for Public Broadcasting voted itself out of existence after Congress had already cut off the flow of federal money that had long sustained public broadcasting. And in March, AP reported that historic preservation groups sued to stop further physical changes at the Kennedy Center ahead of a planned two-year closure that President Trump says is needed for renovations. These are not isolated skirmishes. Together, they mark an attempt to reorder the relationship between the state and cultural institutions. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fbd9500e5f7400deab84ead188c35694?utm_source=openai))

The most important thing to understand is that the fight is not only ideological. It is managerial. Cultural institutions run on thin margins, layered funding streams, and a patchwork of public and private support. Once the money shifts, programming shifts; once programming shifts, audiences shift; once audiences shift, the institution’s political legitimacy can shift with them. That is why the stakes of a grant cancellation or an executive order are much higher than the dollar amount alone suggests. It is also why the administration’s actions have prompted lawsuits from libraries, arts advocates, and preservation groups. The question is not simply whether a government can criticize a museum or public broadcaster. The deeper question is whether it can pressure those institutions into becoming less independent while still claiming they serve the public. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))

A funding crisis disguised as a culture dispute

The headlines often focus on the culture-war surface: Smithsonian exhibits, Kennedy Center programming, public broadcasting ideology, the language of “divisive narratives” or “improper ideology.” But those phrases matter because they are attached to funding mechanisms. In March 2025, AP reported that the president signed an executive order targeting Smithsonian programs that advance what the White House called “divisive narratives” and “improper ideology,” and put the vice president in charge of overseeing efforts to remove that ideology from the institution’s museums, education centers and zoo. In January 2026, AP reported that the Smithsonian had responded to White House pressure by handing over more exhibit plans and that it was under pressure to provide detailed information about programs tied to America’s 250th anniversary. Those developments were paired with a private fundraising milestone: the Smithsonian said its campaign for our shared future had surpassed its $2.5 billion goal one year ahead of schedule. The two realities coexist. Public institutions are being asked to answer to politics even as they lean harder on private philanthropy. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/558ebfab722f603e94e02a1a4b06ed4d?utm_source=openai))

That private campaign victory is real, and it complicates any simple narrative of institutional collapse. The Smithsonian says the campaign goal was reached one year early, ahead of the nation’s 250th anniversary, and describes the effort as supporting four priority areas across the institution. Its visitor data also show enormous public reach: 2025 visits totaled 14.9 million across Smithsonian museums and the National Zoo, while the institution recorded 16.5 million in-person visits for fiscal year 2025 on its public engagement dashboard. The National Museum of African American History and Culture alone reported nearly 1.4 million visits in 2025, and the National Air and Space Museum drew 1.9 million at its Washington building, with the Udvar-Hazy Center adding another 1 million. So the institution is not disappearing. What is changing is the balance of power around it. Big attendance gives cultural institutions symbolic weight, but big attendance does not protect them from political bargaining over content or budgets. ([si.edu](https://www.si.edu/newsdesk/about/stats?utm_source=openai))

That distinction is important because public debate often treats “popular” institutions as if popularity were the same thing as security. It is not. The Smithsonian’s 2025 campaign triumph and its continuing visitor traffic coexist with ongoing threats to federal support and editorial independence. That tension is the story. America still likes its museums, still visits them, still donates to them. But that affection can be leveraged from above. If the government funds you, it can ask what you are saying. If it can threaten to stop funding you, it can ask it more forcefully. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/027cef04e347d5519b528e7dd632d65b?utm_source=openai))

Public media is the canary in the coal mine

The public broadcasting fight shows how quickly a cultural support system can unravel once politics turns punitive. AP reported on January 5 that the Corporation for Public Broadcasting voted itself out of existence after a year of funding pressure, and later reported that a federal judge permanently blocked the administration’s effort to end federal funding for NPR and PBS. Even the legal victory cannot restore what was already lost in the interim: planning certainty, staff stability, and institutional confidence. Public media does not simply switch back on when a judge rules. It has to rebuild after uncertainty, layoffs, and donor panic. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b68f441c227ec7e076c038821b4a5931?utm_source=openai))

The effect reaches beyond the broadcasters themselves. Public radio and television stations are often the cultural infrastructure for local communities, especially where commercial media has thinned. They host live performances, lecture series, local arts coverage, and educational programming. When public media loses money, the downstream effect is not only fewer broadcast hours; it is fewer microphones for local artists, fewer event partnerships for small venues, and fewer reliable civic stages for arts coverage that does not reduce culture to celebrity gossip. That is why a federal funding battle over NPR and PBS belongs on the culture desk rather than only in political pages. It changes the audience ecology of entire towns. The judge’s First Amendment ruling may slow the assault, but it does not erase the structural vulnerability exposed by the cuts. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fbd9500e5f7400deab84ead188c35694?utm_source=openai))

Why libraries and museums are especially vulnerable

Libraries and museums occupy a strange place in American public life. They are widely liked and often modestly funded. That makes them politically easy to praise and financially easy to squeeze. IMLS describes itself as the leading source of federal funding for American museums and libraries, and it was accepting FY26 applications this spring. AP’s April 9 report and the ALA’s account of the legal settlement show that the agency’s grantmaking role survived the administration’s attempt to shrink it. But the fact that the agency could be threatened at all underscores how dependent the cultural ecosystem is on a few relatively small public institutions. ([imls.gov](https://www.imls.gov/news/imls-opens-fy26-grant-funding-cycle-encourages-library-and-museum-applications?utm_source=openai))

That vulnerability is not abstract. Grants support digitization, accessibility, conservation, workforce training, rural outreach and preservation of local memory. When a museum loses a grant, it can mean a delayed exhibition, a postponed conservation project or a canceled school partnership. When a library loses a grant, it can mean less literacy work, fewer technology services or reduced support for community programming. These are not glamorous losses, but they accumulate into a thinner public sphere. In that sense, the battle over IMLS is also a battle over whether culture is understood as a public utility or a discretionary luxury. The administration’s effort to shrink the agency suggested the latter; the settlement and court ruling suggest the courts still recognize the former. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))

The broader cultural sector’s economic footprint makes the issue even less dismissible. NEA and Bureau of Economic Analysis data show that arts and cultural industries contributed $1.2 trillion to the U.S. economy in 2023, or 4.2% of GDP, and grew faster than the overall economy between 2022 and 2023. The NEA’s figures also show 5.4 million wage-and-salary jobs in the sector. Americans for the Arts’ AEP6 project says 86% of Americans see arts and culture as important to quality of life and livability, and 79% say they matter to business, the economy and local jobs. Those numbers do not resolve the policy debate, but they do make one thing harder to argue: that arts funding is a niche indulgence with little public consequence. ([arts.gov](https://www.arts.gov/news/press-releases/2025/arts-and-cultural-industries-grew-twice-rate-us-economy-adding-12-trillion?utm_source=openai))

The case for intervention, and the case against it

Supporters of the administration’s approach argue that cultural institutions have become too insulated, too elite and too ideologically self-protective. From that view, federal funding should come with stronger oversight because taxpayers should not subsidize museums or broadcasters that seem contemptuous of mainstream voters. The White House’s language about “improper ideology” is not merely rhetorical; it is a signal to cultural conservatives that institutions can be pushed to diversify their narratives, staffing and programming. On this reading, intervention is corrective, not destructive. It is a way to rebalance institutions that, critics say, have grown comfortable speaking to a narrow audience while claiming universal authority. AP’s coverage of the Smithsonian order captures the logic clearly: the administration sought to oversee the removal of ideology from institutions it believed had wandered politically. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/558ebfab722f603e94e02a1a4b06ed4d?utm_source=openai))

There is a serious version of that critique. Museums do sometimes flatten disagreement into institutional consensus. Public broadcasters can underrepresent some regions, social classes or perspectives. Large arts nonprofits can drift toward a donor class that does not resemble their broader public. A real culture policy should be able to examine those faults honestly. And there is a legitimate public-interest question about whether federal money should be used to support institutions that become echo chambers for any side of the culture spectrum. That is the strongest counterargument to the defenders of cultural autonomy. If a museum says it serves the whole nation, it should be prepared to demonstrate how. ([arts.gov](https://www.arts.gov/about/facts-and-figures/arts-economic-growth?utm_source=openai))

But the counterargument collapses when it moves from accountability to control. Once political leaders reserve the power to define “proper” culture, the issue is no longer pluralism but orthodoxy. Federal oversight that demands transparency, fiscal responsibility and broad public access is different from pressure that dictates historical framing or exhibition content. The Smithsonian’s scale and the Kennedy Center’s prestige make them tempting targets because they symbolize national culture. Yet symbolism is precisely why they need insulation. If every administration can rewrite the institution’s public meaning, then the institution ceases to be a shared civic forum and becomes a partisan trophy. That is the danger the lawsuits are trying to stop. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/558ebfab722f603e94e02a1a4b06ed4d?utm_source=openai))

The Kennedy Center is becoming a case study in institutional churn

The Kennedy Center is the most visible example of culture colliding with power in real time. Trump first signaled in February that he wanted to close the center for two years starting July 4, framing the move as a renovation that would turn the venue into a “World Class Bastion of Arts, Music, and Entertainment.” AP later reported that the closure proposal was part of a broader upheaval that had already produced cancellations and leadership changes. In March, historic preservation groups sued to stop further physical changes, arguing that the plan required public review and congressional authorization. Around the same time, AP reported that Jean Davidson, the executive director of the National Symphony Orchestra, was leaving for a position in Los Angeles, another sign of churn at the institution. AP also reported that Richard Grenell, Trump’s ally who had overseen major changes at the center, was stepping down as president. This is no longer a routine capital project. It is a governance crisis. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c6dba4a46e71b0d0e48d46501195366c?utm_source=openai))

The Kennedy Center’s problem is that it sits at the intersection of architecture, programming, labor and national symbolism. A renovation can be a normal capital project. But when a politically charged closure is paired with leadership upheaval, cancellations and staff warnings about “skeletal teams,” artists and audiences begin to ask whether the venue is still a neutral stage or has become a message. That question matters for bookings, sponsorships, union relationships and tourism. It also matters for Washington itself. The center is a civic landmark, and the city has long used the arts as part of its identity. If a major institution becomes a battleground, the city does not just lose events; it loses a piece of the narrative it sells to residents and visitors. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9eb9e9fa2368c3eb6fad1c57a90c3407?utm_source=openai))

What the numbers say about the public’s appetite for culture

One of the more striking parts of the current moment is that public appetite for culture remains high even as institutional confidence is shaky. Smithsonian visitor numbers remain enormous, despite post-pandemic fluctuations and the visible effects of political turbulence. The Smithsonian’s public dashboards show 14.9 million total visits in 2025, and its FY 2025 public engagement report shows 16.5 million in-person visits. Americans for the Arts’ AEP6 survey says most Americans believe arts and culture strengthen community life and cross-cultural understanding. The NEA says the arts and cultural sector grew rapidly and now accounts for a substantial share of the economy. These are signs of demand, not fatigue. The public still wants museums, concerts, readings, exhibitions, radio, dance and historical interpretation. The real contest is over who gets to curate them and with what resources. ([si.edu](https://www.si.edu/newsdesk/about/stats?utm_source=openai))

That demand can be deceptive. High visitation can hide staffing shortages. Strong branding can conceal brittle budgets. A museum can look crowded while deferring maintenance. A public radio station can sound robust while quietly depending on one or two grant cycles that may disappear. A nonprofit arts ecosystem can show economic impact at the macro level while individual workers face precarious pay, short contracts and relentless grant-writing. The sector’s public popularity is real, but popularity does not solve labor instability. It may even mask it by making the organizations seem healthier than they are. That is why culture reporting has to follow money as closely as it follows premieres and openings. ([arts.gov](https://www.arts.gov/news/press-releases/2025/arts-and-cultural-industries-grew-twice-rate-us-economy-adding-12-trillion?utm_source=openai))

Labor is the hidden story inside the cultural story

Culture wars often appear to be about curators and executives. They are also about staff. When an agency gets pressured, its employees do the work of uncertainty: answering emails, revising budgets, worrying about layoffs and managing public trust while private confidence erodes. The IMLS settlement matters partly because it preserves grants, but also because it reduces the risk that a federal agency central to museum and library life will be hollowed out by administrative attrition. Likewise, public broadcasting cuts do not only affect shows. They affect journalists, producers, engineers, development staff and local station employees who have to keep operations going amid shifting funding. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))

This labor dimension is easy to miss because cultural institutions rarely collapse all at once. They fray. A conservator position stays vacant. Education staff take on more schools. A local arts reporter gets laid off. A station cuts weekend shows. A museum postpones a family program. Then a year later the public says the institution feels smaller, less present, less useful. The damage was not one dramatic blow. It was a series of administrative decisions, often justified as efficiency, that made the institution less able to serve the public it claimed to serve. The current federal pressure campaign risks accelerating exactly that kind of attrition. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fbd9500e5f7400deab84ead188c35694?utm_source=openai))

The strongest counterevidence: culture is still adapting

It would be wrong to write the sector as if it were only a story of decline. Some of the best evidence points in the other direction. The Smithsonian is still fundraising successfully. NEA grantmaking is continuing, with more than $16 million awarded in the first round of FY2026 and additional America250-related grant programs and deadlines on the calendar. IMLS is still accepting applications, and the agency’s continuation after settlement means museums and libraries can still seek federal support. Private philanthropy, local donors and community partnerships have shown real resilience. In some cases, cultural institutions are discovering new audiences precisely because they are under pressure to justify themselves more concretely. ([si.edu](https://www.si.edu/newsdesk/releases/smithsonian-reaches-historic-25-billion-campaign-goal-one-year-ahead-schedule?utm_source=openai))

There is also a democratic argument for adaptation. Some institutions have historically relied too heavily on inherited prestige and not enough on local relationships. Pressure can force them to become more relevant, more transparent and more accountable. The problem is that not all pressure is constructive. A museum that broadens access because it wants to reach more people is different from one that narrows its mission because it fears punishment. A public broadcaster that diversifies storytelling because it wants to better reflect the country is different from one that trims independence to satisfy political overseers. Adaptation is healthy when it expands the public. It is corrosive when it narrows institutional courage. ([arts.gov](https://www.arts.gov/about/facts-and-figures/arts-economic-growth?utm_source=openai))

Why this matters beyond the headlines

The current fight over cultural institutions is a preview of what American public life looks like when symbolic institutions become policy targets. Museums and libraries are often treated as neutral spaces, but they are really civic memory banks. Public media is often treated as background noise, but it is a local information system. The Kennedy Center is often treated as a glamorous stage, but it is also a national claims process: a way for the country to say what kinds of expression count as central. When those institutions are pulled into a funding war, the result is not simply less art. It is a redefinition of public meaning itself. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))

The strongest lesson from April 2026 is that culture policy is no longer a backwater. It is becoming a front line in the broader contest over executive power, public spending and historical narrative. The court rulings, settlements, fundraisers, closures and departures all point to the same conclusion: in the United States, cultural institutions remain beloved, but their independence can no longer be taken for granted. If the sector wants to stay public in more than name, it will need not only money, but rules that keep political power from turning every stage, gallery, archive and studio into an instrument of the latest administration’s preferences. That fight has started already. The only question is whether the public notices in time to care. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a4dde0417f208054533aa726fceefc13?utm_source=openai))
