Hollywood’s movie theaters get a rare boost — and a warning — at CinemaCon 2026

A stronger-than-expected spring box office and Paramount Skydance’s vows to keep films in theaters longer are reviving old arguments over how Hollywood should sell movies. But the proposed Paramount-Warner Bros. tie-up is turning that optimism into a test of whether more scale means more screens — or fewer choices.

Written and Researched by AI • April 20, 2026 • 10:07 a.m.

The movie business arrived at CinemaCon 2026 with an unusual mix of confidence and alarm. The confidence came from the box office: after a sluggish stretch in the years since the pandemic, the spring theatrical market has produced some of the strongest domestic results in recent memory, led by event titles such as The Super Mario Galaxy Movie and Project Hail Mary. The alarm came from corporate consolidation: Paramount Skydance’s proposed acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery has become the defining business story in film, with theater owners, guilds, filmmakers, and regulators arguing over whether the deal would stabilize Hollywood or make it smaller, more concentrated, and less adventurous. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/weekend/2026W14/?sort=gross&utm_source=openai))

That tension is why this week mattered. CinemaCon, the annual gathering of studios and exhibitors in Las Vegas, is usually part trade show, part pep rally. In 2026, it has become a referendum on the theatrical future itself. Paramount Skydance chief executive David Ellison used the event to promise theater owners a minimum of 30 theatrical releases a year across Paramount and Warner Bros. if the merger is completed, along with a 45-day exclusive theatrical window beginning immediately for Paramount films. Warner Bros. then answered with a star-heavy presentation that looked, on its face, like a studio trying to reassure exhibitors that event movies still matter. Meanwhile, an open letter signed by more than 1,000 Hollywood professionals warned that the merger would reduce competition and cost jobs. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The result is a story that is bigger than one merger. It is about the basic shape of the film industry in 2026: how many studios there should be, how many movies deserve a theatrical release, how long movies should stay exclusively in theaters, how much power streamers should have over release patterns, and whether the box-office rebound is durable or merely a brief correction after years of disruption. The stakes are commercial, but they are also cultural. In film, distribution is never just logistics. It is taste made into policy.

A rebound is real, but it is not a full recovery

The near-term box office picture gave exhibitors something to celebrate. Comscore marked the 50th anniversary of its box-office measurement business on April 15 and emphasized the continuing importance of theatrical data, a reminder that the industry still depends on reliable box office reporting to set expectations, negotiate with partners, and judge whether a film is a hit or miss. Meanwhile, Box Office Mojo and The Numbers have both shown a spring slate dominated by tentpoles, with The Super Mario Galaxy Movie and Project Hail Mary driving the early 2026 market. Across the broader U.S. market, however, annual domestic grosses remain well below pre-pandemic norms, and theaters remain exposed to the volatility of release slates, consumer habits, and studio strategy. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

That split matters. A strong opening month or two can make an industry feel healthier than it really is. Theatrical business is lumpy by nature, and the rebound depends on whether studios continue to feed theaters with a steady supply of films that feel worth leaving home for. The box-office data available in April shows precisely why exhibitors are so sensitive to slate size and release discipline: a few giant titles can buoy the market, but a thin calendar can quickly expose how dependent theaters remain on studio pipeline decisions. The spring performance of Project Hail Mary and The Super Mario Galaxy Movie also underscores how concentrated demand has become around recognizable properties and large-scale releases. ([the-numbers.com](https://www.the-numbers.com/movie/Project-Hail-Mary-%282026%29?utm_source=openai))

That data supports an uncomfortable conclusion for anyone hoping that the recovery will be powered by a wide range of midbudget originals: the market still depends overwhelmingly on recognizable brands and event pictures. That, in turn, helps explain why the industry has spent so much time talking about windows, franchises, and consolidation. Studios are not merely deciding where to release films; they are deciding which kinds of films they can still afford to make and market in the first place. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/weekend/2026W14/?sort=gross&utm_source=openai))

Paramount’s promise to theaters is bold — and highly strategic

Ellison’s CinemaCon pitch was tailored to exhibitors’ fears. He pledged 30 theatrical releases a year between Paramount and Warner Bros., said he would maintain a 45-day exclusive theatrical window, and framed theatrical exhibition as the place where franchises become cultural events. That message was not just an olive branch to theater owners; it was also a signal to regulators, investors, and skeptics who worry that a merged Paramount-Warner would be tempted to prioritize streaming, subscription growth, or corporate synergies over the number of films shown on the big screen. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

On paper, the promise sounds pro-theatrical. In practice, the question is whether it would hold. A studio can pledge a release count, but release count is not the same as release quality. Exhibitors know that a slate filled with low-budget placeholders or titles that get short marketing support is not much help. The industry has seen versions of this before: corporate owners promise scale, then quietly compress the number of movies that receive meaningful distribution. Theater owners also remember that Disney’s acquisition of Fox reduced 20th Century Studios to a smaller theatrical supplier than it had been before the deal. Skeptics of the Paramount-Warner plan are already invoking that precedent. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-16/david-ellison-cinemacon-pledges-30-films-paramount-warner-bros?utm_source=openai))

Ellison’s window promise also lands in a market that is still negotiating the value of theatrical exclusivity. For years, studios experimented with shorter windows during and after the pandemic. Some films went day-and-date or near day-and-date, and even as the market normalized, the old 90-day theatrical window never fully returned. A 45-day window is longer than the most aggressive streaming-first approaches, but shorter than the classic pre-pandemic norm, and it reflects a compromise between exhibitors who want meaningful exclusivity and studios that want to move titles quickly into the home ecosystem. The fact that Ellison paired a theatrical promise with a later streaming path suggests a hybrid distribution model rather than a full return to old Hollywood. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/biz/global/paramount-skydance-q3-closing-warner-bros-discovery-ellison-33657/?utm_source=openai))

That compromise may be enough for some exhibitors. It will not satisfy everyone. A shorter window can help theaters if the film is strong and the marketing is concentrated, because it makes the theatrical run feel more urgent. But it can also train audiences to wait if they suspect that the home release is never far away. The box office is therefore not just a function of consumer appetite; it is also a function of policy, expectation, and behavior shaped by release strategy. That is why theater owners are treating the window issue as existential rather than technical. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/warner-bros-sale-paramount-netflix-wont-save-movie-theaters-33653/?utm_source=openai))

The merger fight is really about bargaining power

The proposed Paramount-Warner merger is being framed publicly as a contest over Hollywood’s future, but beneath that rhetoric is a simpler and more brutal question: who gets to set the terms? Studios negotiate with talent, theater chains, marketers, unions, vendors, and platforms. The more concentrated the studio side becomes, the more leverage it has in each of those relationships. That is what opponents mean when they warn of reduced competition. It is also why the box-office boom at the start of the year has not reduced merger anxiety; if anything, it has sharpened it, because a healthier exhibition market makes the fight over who controls the flow of films more consequential. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/30b8aa703141cec1fa7ea06a2c17dd50?utm_source=openai))

Opposition has come from multiple directions. The open letter signed by more than 1,000 industry figures argued that the merger would consolidate power and reduce competition. Cinema United, the theater owners’ trade group, has said it opposes the deal and wants safeguards if it cannot be stopped. Policymakers have also pushed for scrutiny, signaling that the transaction may face not only ordinary antitrust review but a broader political fight about media concentration and labor implications. These are not purely symbolic objections. In a business where distribution slots are finite, a smaller number of owners can mean fewer independent decision-makers, fewer greenlights, and less room for risk. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/30b8aa703141cec1fa7ea06a2c17dd50?utm_source=openai))

Still, the merger’s defenders have an argument of their own. They say the film business needs scale to compete with global streamers, that theaters need a healthier supply of event pictures, and that studios can no longer afford inefficient duplication in marketing and distribution. That view is not frivolous. The theatrical marketplace is fragmented and expensive. Prints and advertising costs remain high. Consumer attention is scarce. A larger studio footprint can, in theory, create more room for theatrical investment if the owners genuinely want to prioritize it. The problem is that the history of media mergers teaches a different lesson more often than not: consolidation usually produces savings first and promises later. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/warner-bros-sale-paramount-netflix-wont-save-movie-theaters-33653/?utm_source=openai))

Why the theater business still matters to Hollywood

For all the streaming rhetoric, theaters remain central to Hollywood’s economics and mythology. They are the place where stars are made, where franchises begin, where awards campaigns gain legitimacy, and where a film can create a public event instead of a private transaction. The Motion Picture Association used its CinemaCon speech to make exactly that case, arguing that cinema sits at the center of American culture and that the industry’s fight for production incentives, workers, and creative freedom is inseparable from the health of the theatrical ecosystem. The MPA also continues to present the film and television industry as a major economic engine supporting millions of jobs and more than 160,000 businesses. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

Exhibitors themselves are eager to reinforce that point, because their leverage depends on making theaters seem indispensable. The stronger the argument that a movie can be culturally consequential only if it plays on the big screen, the more power theater owners have in negotiating windows, premium formats, and release commitments. That is why CinemaCon speeches are so often drenched in civic language. They are not only selling tickets; they are selling the idea that the theater is where culture happens in public. The current moment, with a merger looming and a modest box-office rebound underway, is tailor-made for that argument. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-14/hollywoods-latest-power-grab-why-theater-owners-are-fighting-back?utm_source=openai))

But the theater business also has vulnerabilities that speeches cannot erase. AP-NORC found in September 2025 that about three-quarters of U.S. adults had watched a new movie on streaming instead of in a theater at least once in the previous year, and only about two-thirds had seen a newly released movie in a theater. Pew found that 83% of U.S. adults use streaming services, far more than subscribe to cable or satellite. Those findings do not mean theaters are dead. They do mean that theatrical moviegoing is now one entertainment option among many, not the default habit it once was. That makes the market more sensitive to event-level differentiation. In other words, theaters do best when they offer something streaming cannot replicate: scale, social energy, or urgency. ([ap.org](https://www.ap.org/news-highlights/spotlights/2025/streaming-is-overtaking-theaters-for-movie-watchers-an-ap-norc-poll-finds/?utm_source=openai))

Theatrical success is increasingly concentrated in event films

The early 2026 box office confirms a pattern that has been emerging for years: audiences remain eager to show up for films that feel like communal events, especially when they have recognizable IP, star power, or a promise of spectacle. Box Office Mojo’s April data show The Super Mario Galaxy Movie dominating the month-to-date chart, with Project Hail Mary also performing strongly, and the same charts show a market where weekend performance remains concentrated in a small number of titles. In that context, the theatrical debate is not about whether audiences still buy tickets. They do. The real question is what kind of movies can still justify wide theatrical distribution. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/weekend/2026W14/?sort=gross&utm_source=openai))

The answer increasingly appears to be: films that behave like events. Franchise sequels, animation, horror with strong word of mouth, and a small number of prestige titles still travel. The difficulty is that event-film dependence can hollow out the middle of the market. Midbudget adult dramas, modest comedies, and original concepts face a tougher path because they must compete not only with each other but with home viewing’s convenience and the marketing gravity of better-known brands. That is one reason the industry keeps returning to the question of slate size. If the big titles are fewer but larger, what happens to everything else?

One clue can be found in 2025’s domestic box-office rankings. The market’s top performers were heavily IP-driven, and while original films still broke through, they were exceptions rather than the rule. The success of a title like Sinners mattered precisely because it was unusual. It suggested that audiences will still embrace originality when marketing, critical response, and timing align. It also underscored how rare that alignment has become. A healthier theatrical market is not just one where franchise films do well; it is one where original films can become cultural events too. That remains the harder challenge. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/quarter/by-year/2026/?grossesOption=calendarGrosses&utm_source=openai))

Labor, production, and the fear of a smaller industry

One of the strongest objections to media consolidation is that it can shrink the number of jobs and reduce the amount of production work available across the ecosystem. The open letter opposing the merger made that point explicitly, and theater-industry leaders have echoed related concerns about fewer greenlights, less competition for creative talent, and a smaller pipeline of movies. Those worries land in a broader labor context that includes the lingering effects of the 2023 strikes and the continuing pressure on below-the-line work. Production is not evenly distributed, and when studio decisions change, the pain ripples through crews, vendors, locations, and local economies. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/30b8aa703141cec1fa7ea06a2c17dd50?utm_source=openai))

There is also a geographic dimension. The MPA has been pushing production incentives and federal tax support, arguing that film work should remain strong in the United States and that state-level incentives have become critical to attracting shoots. That campaign matters because the theatrical business does not exist in isolation. If more production migrates elsewhere, the domestic pipeline of films available for U.S. theaters can weaken, even if the number of global releases remains stable. Labor and distribution are therefore linked: where films are made affects what theaters can show, and who finances distribution affects where those films get made. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

Counterarguments remain. Supporters of consolidation say scale can make studios more efficient and provide more stable financing for large films, which in turn could support theatrical production. There is truth in the claim that theatrical distribution is capital-intensive and risky. But efficiency is not the same thing as vitality. A narrower industry can be more profitable for fewer companies while becoming less resilient, less pluralistic, and less hospitable to risky films that do not fit the dominant corporate logic. Film history is full of examples in which supposed efficiencies eventually led to creative sameness.

The window debate is about behavior, not just economics

Ellison’s 45-day theatrical promise is central to the current debate because windows are one of the few levers studios can still pull to shape audience behavior. A long exclusive window tells audiences a film is worth immediate attention; a shorter window says the opposite. But windows also serve as signals to theater owners, whose business is harmed if consumers assume the film will be available at home almost right away. That is why exhibitors continue to lobby for meaningful exclusivity even as studios insist on flexibility. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/biz/global/paramount-skydance-q3-closing-warner-bros-discovery-ellison-33657/?utm_source=openai))

The challenge is that audiences have changed. Pew’s moviegoing research and AP-NORC’s poll both show how streaming has become routine, while theater attendance is now more selective than habitual. In that environment, theatrical windows only work if the film itself is perceived as an event and the timing feels urgent. A strong window can help create fear of missing out; a weak window can accelerate delay. Thus, the debate is not just about how much money comes from ticket sales versus streaming revenue. It is about how the industry trains consumers to value a theater run at all. ([ap.org](https://www.ap.org/news-highlights/spotlights/2025/streaming-is-overtaking-theaters-for-movie-watchers-an-ap-norc-poll-finds/?utm_source=openai))

That is one reason the 45-day number has become such a headline. It sounds like a compromise, but it also signals a worldview. It says theatrical release is important enough to protect, yet not important enough to treat as the main event for three months. For exhibitors, that is better than a fast dump to streaming. For the studios, it preserves downstream flexibility. For audiences, it may simply confirm what many already suspect: if you want to see a movie the way a studio really wants you to see it, you should probably go now. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The cautious case for optimism

There is a plausible optimistic reading of the current moment. The box office is up. Studios are leaning back into theaters. Comscore is celebrating the durability of measurement at the very moment exhibitors need a proof point. Paramount is publicly embracing theatrical exclusivity, and Warner Bros. is still presenting its upcoming slate as a series of big-screen events. If the merger does not go through, the recent rebound could embolden studios to keep feeding theaters with a healthier mix of films. If it does go through, the merged company could, in theory, become a more disciplined and theater-friendly supplier than skeptics expect. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

But that reading requires several things to break right at once. It requires the spring box office to be more than a temporary upswing. It requires studios to resist the temptation to cut back on risk after a few expensive misses. It requires theater owners to translate current momentum into better terms without overestimating their leverage. And it requires regulators to evaluate the Paramount-Warner deal not only on market share but on the likely effect on film volume, release diversity, and bargaining power across the sector. That is a lot to ask of one season’s hot streak. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/30b8aa703141cec1fa7ea06a2c17dd50?utm_source=openai))

The bigger question: what kind of film industry does America want?

This is the broader significance of a story that may otherwise sound like trade-jargon about windows and merger synergies. CinemaCon 2026 has exposed a philosophical split inside the movie business. One side believes the best path forward is concentration, discipline, and a stronger emphasis on franchises and theatrical event movies. The other side argues that Hollywood’s health depends on competition, variety, and room for smaller films to survive alongside the tentpoles. The first camp sees scale as the answer to a fractured marketplace. The second sees scale as the problem. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-14/hollywoods-latest-power-grab-why-theater-owners-are-fighting-back?utm_source=openai))

The public, meanwhile, is sending mixed signals. Many Americans prefer the convenience of streaming. They still show up for the right movie. They still respond to spectacle. They still treat the theater as special when the film earns it. That is why the theatrical market is neither dying nor healed. It is being renegotiated one release at a time, one window at a time, and now one merger at a time. ([ap.org](https://www.ap.org/news-highlights/spotlights/2025/streaming-is-overtaking-theaters-for-movie-watchers-an-ap-norc-poll-finds/?utm_source=openai))

If there is a lesson in the events of this week, it is that movie theaters remain powerful enough to matter and fragile enough to worry about. Theatrical exhibition still shapes what becomes cultural memory. Yet the companies that feed the theaters are increasingly tempted to reorganize themselves around logic that begins somewhere else: subscription growth, corporate consolidation, and the economics of scale. Whether Hollywood can keep both the theater and the studio system healthy may be the industry’s defining question for the rest of the decade. For now, CinemaCon 2026 has made one thing clear: the future of movies is still being decided in the room where the studios and the exhibitors bargain over who gets to believe in the big screen first. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-14/hollywoods-latest-power-grab-why-theater-owners-are-fighting-back?utm_source=openai))
