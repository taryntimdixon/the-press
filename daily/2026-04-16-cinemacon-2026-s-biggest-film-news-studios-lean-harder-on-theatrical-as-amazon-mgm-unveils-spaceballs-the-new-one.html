CinemaCon 2026’s biggest Film news: studios lean harder on theatrical as Amazon MGM unveils ‘Spaceballs: The New One’

At CinemaCon in Las Vegas, Amazon MGM, Sony and other studios signaled a louder-than-usual bet on theaters, with new franchise titles, release plans and a fresh push to keep moviegoing central in a streaming-split market.

By The Press • April 16, 2026 • 2:47 p.m.

Hollywood’s annual courtship of theater owners has a new urgency this year, and CinemaCon 2026 is making that plain. In Las Vegas, studios are arriving with one core message: the big screen still matters. The most attention-grabbing reveal so far came from Amazon MGM, which used its presentation to unveil the title Spaceballs: The New One while also emphasizing a bigger theatrical strategy that includes 14 films slated for release in 2026 and a goal of 15 theatrical titles annually by 2027. ([aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/entertainment/amazon-mgm-studios-films?utm_source=openai))

The Las Vegas gathering has always functioned as both a sales pitch and a temperature check for the movie business. This year, it arrives at a moment when studios are trying to convince exhibitors that the theatrical market can still support a wide range of films, from tentpoles to originals. Amazon MGM’s message was particularly notable because it paired nostalgia-driven franchise play with a more overt commitment to theatrical exclusivity and volume, a combination that suggests the studio sees movie theaters not as a legacy channel but as a core part of its growth plan. ([aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/entertainment/amazon-mgm-studios-films?utm_source=openai))

Amazon MGM turns nostalgia into a theatrical sales pitch

The headline reveal was the return of Mel Brooks’ sci-fi spoof universe, now titled Spaceballs: The New One. Amazon MGM brought the project into the CinemaCon spotlight with a first-look presentation and a video message from Brooks himself, who joked that the new title is “just like the old one, but it’s newer.” The film brings back Bill Pullman and Rick Moranis, and is set up as a direct appeal to audiences who grew up on the 1987 original while also trying to recruit younger moviegoers who know the joke-popularity of the brand more than its box-office history. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))

That approach reflects a larger industry truth: studios are increasingly mining familiar properties not only because they are safer bets, but because recognizable titles still help theaters market a movie as an event. Amazon MGM’s slate presentation was packed with that logic. Alongside Spaceballs: The New One, the studio also previewed Masters of the Universe, The Sheep Detectives, How to Rob a Bank, and first looks at projects such as Highlander and Verity. The breadth of that lineup suggests a studio trying to build a theatrical identity that stretches beyond one brand and reaches across genres. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))

Why exhibitors are paying attention now

CinemaCon has become a referendum on the industry’s confidence in theatrical releases, and the language coming from the convention this week suggests studios are more willing to argue for theaters than they were even a year ago. Amazon MGM said it is working toward 15 big, global films in theaters each year by 2027 and is already ahead of schedule with 14 titles planned for 2026. That is not just a corporate brag; it is a direct answer to theater owners who have spent years pushing studios to commit more consistently to the big-screen model. ([aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/entertainment/amazon-movies-cinemacon/?utm_source=openai))

Theatrical windows remain part of that debate. Across the industry, exhibitors have argued for longer exclusive runs to help ticket sales stabilize, while studios have favored flexibility to optimize each title’s release path. Cinema United, the trade group representing theaters, said this week that the average theatrical window for the top 100 films in 2025 was 37 days, slightly longer than in 2024, underscoring that the old one-size-fits-all model has effectively disappeared. The renewed studio language around theatrical output, then, is less about nostalgia for the old system than about proving that theaters still deserve strategic priority. ([screendaily.com](https://www.screendaily.com/news/film-industry-heads-talk-consolidation-windows-at-cinemacon/5215613.article?utm_source=openai))

Amazon MGM’s presentation landed especially well because it came with proof of concept. The studio pointed to the continued box-office strength of Project Hail Mary, which has become Amazon MGM’s biggest theatrical release to date and is returning to IMAX screens for a one-week encore. For exhibitors, that matters: it is evidence that original or semi-original material can still generate event-level audience interest when marketed effectively and given premium formats. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))

Sony adds a darker bet with ‘Bloodborne’

Amazon MGM was not the only studio trying to read the room. Sony used CinemaCon to announce an animated adaptation of the video game Bloodborne, promising an R-rated feature that will stay close to the game’s gothic, violent tone. The project is being developed with PlayStation Productions and creator-producer JackSepticEye, signaling that Sony continues to lean into game adaptations as one of its most bankable pipelines. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/bloodborne-video-game-r-rated-animated-movie-adaptation-sony-cinemacon-35336/?utm_source=openai))

The announcement fits a broader pattern in current studio strategy: recognizable intellectual property is being used not just for sequels, but for cross-platform expansion into animation, prestige, and horror. Sony’s choice to frame Bloodborne as faithful to the source material, rather than softening it for mass appeal, suggests the studio believes niche intensity can still translate theatrically if the fan base is passionate enough. In the current marketplace, that is an increasingly important gamble. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/bloodborne-video-game-r-rated-animated-movie-adaptation-sony-cinemacon-35336/?utm_source=openai))

The contrast between Amazon MGM and Sony is instructive. One is leaning on comedy nostalgia and a wide slate of theatrical titles; the other is betting on a gory, fan-driven game adaptation. Both are making the same underlying argument to theater owners: audiences will show up if the movie feels like a true event. That proposition is shaping much of CinemaCon 2026’s tone, from franchise teases to slate announcements to the careful choreography of star appearances onstage. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))

A market that still wants events

The deeper story at CinemaCon is not just what got announced, but what kind of movie business these announcements imply. Studios are trying to reduce dependence on any single tentpole while still making each title feel large enough to justify a theatrical trip. That is why the presentation language keeps returning to words like “big,” “global,” and “cinematic,” and why so many of the projects highlighted this week are either familiar franchises or high-concept originals with built-in audience hooks. ([aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/entertainment/amazon-mgm-studios-films?utm_source=openai))

For theaters, the stakes are obvious. A stronger studio slate means more chances to program a mix of event films, crowd-pleasers and premium-format releases. For studios, the reward is equally clear: the theatrical run remains the most visible way to launch a movie into the culture. If CinemaCon 2026 is any indication, Hollywood is not retreating from theaters. It is making a fresh case for them, one title reveal at a time. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))

And in a year when the industry is still balancing consolidation, streaming habits and audience fragmentation, that case may be as important as any single movie on the slate. The returning jokes of Spaceballs, the brutality of Bloodborne and the promise of a packed 2026 calendar all point to the same conclusion: Hollywood still believes the best way to sell movies is to make them feel like worth-leaving-home events. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bcf2a4e6d3e4f226115ca0d1505b350f?utm_source=openai))
