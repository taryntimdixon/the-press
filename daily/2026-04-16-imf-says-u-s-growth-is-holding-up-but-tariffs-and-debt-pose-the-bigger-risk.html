IMF Says U.S. Growth Is Holding Up, But Tariffs and Debt Pose the Bigger Risk

The Fund’s April outlook sees the U.S. economy expanding again in 2026, yet warns that import taxes are still lifting prices and that rising federal debt is becoming a separate stability threat.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 16, 2026 • 4:57 p.m. • 1175 words

WASHINGTON — The International Monetary Fund says the U.S. economy remains sturdier than many feared, with growth projected to accelerate modestly in 2026 even after a year of whiplash from tariffs, a government shutdown and shifting policy signals. But the fund’s latest assessment makes clear that the bigger economic story is no longer just whether growth can endure. It is whether tariffs, higher prices and a swelling federal debt load can coexist without forcing a sharper policy reckoning later this year.

In its April 2026 Article IV consultation, the IMF said U.S. growth reached 2% in 2025 and is projected to rise to 2.4% in 2026, supported by strong productivity and a labor market that is cooling only gradually. The fund also said core PCE inflation should return to the Federal Reserve’s 2% target in the first half of 2027 as the tariff impulse fades and oil prices ease. In a separate write-up of the report, the IMF described the economy as “buoyant,” but warned that protectionist trade policy could end up being a larger drag on activity than many policymakers are currently assuming. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

Tariffs are still working their way through prices

The IMF’s central economic judgment is that tariffs have not vanished as an inflation problem simply because markets have adapted to them. The fund said inflation moved sideways during 2025 because tariff-driven goods-price increases offset softer services inflation, and it expects the inflationary impulse from tariffs to fade only gradually. That matters because the Federal Reserve has been trying to determine whether recent price pressures are a one-time level shift or something more persistent that could keep rates higher for longer. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

That tension is visible in the way the IMF frames the next two years. Growth is still expected to strengthen, but employment is projected to expand at less than half the pace seen in the five years before the pandemic, while unemployment stays near 4%. In other words, the economy may avoid a classic downturn while still feeling less dynamic than it did in the post-pandemic rebound. For households, the distinction matters: slower job creation and higher import costs can be a frustrating mix even when headline GDP is positive. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

Policy makers are already weighing how much of the tariff shock will ultimately be passed through to consumers. The IMF’s baseline assumes that effect diminishes over time, but it also notes that the near-term risks to activity and unemployment are broadly balanced, with energy prices creating upside risks to inflation. That combination makes the Fed’s job harder, not easier. If inflation re-accelerates, the central bank may have to stay restrictive longer; if growth softens unexpectedly, it may have to cut more aggressively. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

A resilient economy, but not an unbreakable one

The IMF’s optimism about near-term growth rests on a few pillars: productivity has been strong, household demand has held up, and the labor market, while slowing, is not flashing recession signals. The fund said the unemployment rate should remain close to 4% in 2026-27, helped by slower growth in the working-age population. That is a reminder that some of the labor market’s resilience reflects demographics as well as policy or cyclical strength. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

Still, the report also carries a warning about the broader policy environment. The IMF said the applied effective tariff rate on imports to the U.S. is expected to settle at 7% to 8.5% after recent changes to tariff authority take effect, and that higher tariffs will leave a lasting imprint on trade flows and the current account. That is not the profile of a short-lived bargaining tactic. It is a structural shift with implications for prices, sourcing decisions and business investment. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

The fund’s message is that the U.S. economy can absorb a fair amount of disruption, but not indefinitely and not at zero cost. Tariffs can protect some domestic producers while making imports more expensive, but they also raise costs for companies that rely on foreign inputs. Over time, that can show up in margins, investment plans and consumer prices. The IMF’s view is that the economy’s underlying strength is real, but that trade policy is now imposing a measurable tax on that strength. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

The deficit is becoming a second economic front

Beyond tariffs, the IMF singled out federal debt as a growing stability risk. The fund said general government debt stood at 123.9% of GDP in fiscal 2025 and could rise to more than 140% by 2031 if current trends continue. That is an uncomfortable backdrop for an economy that is still expected to grow, because it suggests the United States is borrowing heavily even in a period of relative strength rather than only during recessions. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

AP’s summary of the IMF assessment highlighted the same concern, noting that rising debt represents a “growing stability risk” even as growth and unemployment look manageable this year. The message from both reports is that the U.S. can enter 2026 with a decent headline growth outlook and still face a deteriorating fiscal outlook underneath. That is the sort of mismatch that often takes time to show up in markets, then appears suddenly in borrowing costs or investor sentiment. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a24bc87a8fc1447e84fa1e6366202fd8))

The IMF also said the fiscal deficit narrowed slightly in 2025, but not enough to alter the larger trajectory. The general government deficit is expected to remain in the 7% to 7.5% of GDP range, a level more associated with emergency-era policy than with a mature expansion. For economists, that is the most sobering part of the report: even if tariffs fade as an inflation shock, the debt path does not automatically improve with growth alone. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

What the report means for the Fed and the rest of 2026

The IMF’s conclusion is not that the U.S. economy is in trouble today. It is that the economy is now navigating several slow-moving pressures at once: trade barriers that are still feeding into prices, a labor market that is cooling without collapsing, and public finances that are drifting further out of alignment with long-run sustainability. That mix is manageable in the short run, but it narrows the margin for error. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))

For the Fed, the report supports a cautious approach. The IMF said the central bank could lower rates from 3.6% to around 3.4%, but should avoid deeper cuts unless the labor market weakens materially. That is a notable endorsement of patience at a moment when the inflation outlook remains clouded by trade policy and energy costs. The Fed may not need to choose immediately between fighting inflation and supporting growth, but the IMF’s forecast suggests that choice could become sharper if tariffs stay in place longer than expected. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a24bc87a8fc1447e84fa1e6366202fd8))

For now, the broader takeaway is that the U.S. economy is still outperforming many peers. But the IMF’s April message is that resilience is not the same as immunity. Tariffs are still lifting costs, growth is still vulnerable to policy shocks and debt is still rising. The economy may be expanding, in other words, while the list of things that can unsettle it is getting longer. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2026/english/1usaea2026001.pdf))
