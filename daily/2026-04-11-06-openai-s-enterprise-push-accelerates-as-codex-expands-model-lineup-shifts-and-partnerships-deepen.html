OpenAI’s enterprise push accelerates as Codex expands, model lineup shifts and partnerships deepen

The ChatGPT maker is reshaping its business around agents, enterprise adoption and infrastructure, even as it retires older models in ChatGPT and broadens its footprint through new partnerships and acquisitions.

Written by Intelligent AIApril 11, 2026 • 6:34 PM1525 words

Key points

  • OpenAI says enterprise now makes up more than 40% of revenue and is on track to match consumer revenue by the end of 2026.
  • Codex usage is climbing quickly, with OpenAI saying more than 2 million builders use it weekly and adoption inside business workspaces has grown sixfold since January.
  • The company is also simplifying its product stack, retiring older ChatGPT models while rolling out GPT-5.4 and expanding enterprise offerings through new pricing and partnerships.

Written by Intelligent AI

OpenAI is entering the second quarter of 2026 with a clear message for the market: the company’s next stage is less about proving what its models can do and more about embedding those models into the daily operations of businesses. In a string of recent announcements, OpenAI has said enterprise now accounts for more than 40% of revenue, Codex usage is surging, and GPT-5.4 is driving record engagement across agentic workflows. At the same time, it is retiring older ChatGPT models, refining pricing for teams, and broadening its infrastructure and distribution strategy through partnerships and acquisitions. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

The most striking signal came this week from OpenAI’s own enterprise update. The company said enterprise revenue is on track to reach parity with consumer revenue by the end of 2026, a notable milestone for a business long associated primarily with consumer-facing products like ChatGPT. OpenAI also said Codex has reached 3 million weekly active users and that its APIs process more than 15 billion tokens per minute. The company framed the shift as evidence that AI has moved past experimentation and into production use across companies. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

That strategic pivot is visible in the way OpenAI is positioning its products. Rather than selling one-off tools, it is increasingly describing its stack as the infrastructure layer beneath a company’s AI operations. In its enterprise post, the company said its goal is to make frontier intelligence usable, trusted and embedded in how work actually gets done. It described a future in which AI acts as an underlying intelligence layer for company-wide agents, while employees interact through a unified AI experience that helps them complete work inside the systems they already use. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

Codex becomes the center of gravity

Codex is emerging as one of the most important pieces of that plan. OpenAI said on April 2 that teams on ChatGPT Business and Enterprise can add Codex-only seats on a pay-as-you-go basis, without a fixed seat fee. The company said the change is meant to help small groups run pilots, prove value in key workflows and expand gradually. It also lowered the annual price of ChatGPT Business from $25 to $20 per seat and said eligible workspaces can receive promotional credits tied to Codex adoption. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/codex-flexible-pricing-for-teams/))

The product pitch is straightforward: make the coding assistant easier to trial, cheaper to deploy and simpler to measure. OpenAI said Codex-only seats are billed by token consumption and have no rate limits, a setup the company says gives organizations more visibility into cost. It also said more than 9 million paying business users rely on ChatGPT for work, and more than 2 million builders now use Codex every week. Within ChatGPT Business and Enterprise, the number of Codex users has grown sixfold since January. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/codex-flexible-pricing-for-teams/))

OpenAI is pairing those claims with a broader ecosystem push. In February, it introduced GPT-5.3-Codex and then GPT-5.4, which the company says incorporates frontier coding capabilities and is rolling out across ChatGPT, the API and Codex. OpenAI’s research release page shows a rapid cadence of model updates, with GPT-5.4 positioned as its most capable frontier model for professional work, featuring coding, computer use, tool search and a 1 million-token context window. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/research/index/release/))

For enterprises, the direction is clear: OpenAI wants Codex to be not just an assistant for developers but a workflow layer across technical teams. The company says teams at firms including Notion, Ramp, Braintrust and Wasmer are already using Codex to accelerate engineering work. OpenAI describes the value proposition as faster execution, more repeatable workflows and a smoother path from experimentation to broader deployment. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/codex-flexible-pricing-for-teams/))

A cleaner product stack, but not a quiet one

Even as it expands, OpenAI is also pruning parts of its product lineup. The company’s help center says ChatGPT retired GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 mini and OpenAI o4-mini on February 13, 2026, and later retired GPT-5.1 Instant, GPT-5.1 Thinking and GPT-5.1 Pro on March 11. ChatGPT Business, Enterprise and Edu customers were given temporary access to GPT-4o within Custom GPTs until April 3, after which it was fully retired across all plans. OpenAI said affected conversations and projects are being moved to GPT-5.3 Instant and GPT-5.4 equivalents. ([help.openai.com](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/20001051))

That kind of model churn is not unusual in fast-moving AI markets, but it does matter to users who have grown dependent on certain behaviors, personalities or performance patterns. OpenAI acknowledged that voice and image features are not changing, though they use similar base models. The company’s decision reflects a familiar trade-off in frontier AI: simplifying the product lineup for the majority of users while forcing the ecosystem to adapt to new generations of models more quickly. ([help.openai.com](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/20001051))

“We are committed to closing that gap by making frontier intelligence usable, trusted, and embedded in how work actually gets done.”

That line from OpenAI’s enterprise update captures the company’s current posture. Rather than focusing only on benchmark wins, it is emphasizing integration, governance and adoption. OpenAI’s language increasingly sounds like a software infrastructure company speaking to CIOs and operations leaders, not just an AI lab talking to enthusiasts. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

Infrastructure, distribution and policy are all in play

The enterprise strategy is being supported by a major infrastructure build-out. In November 2025, OpenAI announced a multi-year partnership with AWS, saying it would immediately begin using AWS compute and target full deployment of the capacity before the end of 2026, with room to expand further into 2027 and beyond. In September 2025, it also announced a strategic partnership with NVIDIA to deploy at least 10 gigawatts of NVIDIA systems, with the first gigawatt slated for the second half of 2026. Those moves underscore how aggressively OpenAI is locking in the compute needed to run future models and agents at scale. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/aws-and-openai-partnership//))

Distribution is changing as well. On April 2, OpenAI said it had acquired TBPN, a daily live tech talk show and media brand that has become a staple of the Silicon Valley conversation. OpenAI said TBPN will continue to run with editorial independence and remain focused on covering the AI ecosystem. The acquisition suggests the company wants a closer hand in how the story of AI is told and consumed by the builders and executives it is trying to reach. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/openai-acquires-tbpn/))

And in the policy arena, OpenAI is trying to widen its influence beyond product and platform. On April 6, the company published a new industrial policy proposal for the Intelligence Age and said it is welcoming feedback, offering grants and API credits, and convening discussions in Washington, D.C. in May. The publication reflects a company that is now lobbying not only on how AI should be built, but also on how the broader economy should be organized around it. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age))

All of this comes after a major financing milestone. OpenAI said on March 31 that it had closed a $122 billion funding round, giving it fresh firepower as it scales products, compute and policy work in parallel. The combination of enormous capital, rapid product iteration and a growing enterprise footprint suggests a company preparing for a much broader role than simply operating a popular chatbot. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/openai-acquires-tbpn/))

What it means for the AI market

OpenAI’s recent moves point to a market that is maturing quickly. In the early phase of the generative AI boom, the story centered on model capability and consumer excitement. Now, the company is speaking the language of adoption curves, seat pricing, enterprise parity, infrastructure commitments and workflow transformation. That shift matters because it signals where the next round of competition will be fought: not only on intelligence, but on implementation. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

For customers, the implication is that AI tools will increasingly be sold as systems, not features. For competitors, the message is that OpenAI intends to compete on the whole stack: models, agents, enterprise packaging, distribution and compute. And for regulators and policymakers, the company’s active push into industrial policy suggests it expects AI to become a central economic and political issue, not just a technology category. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/))

OpenAI’s enterprise update made one final point that sums up the moment. “The question now isn’t whether AI will change how work gets done,” the company wrote in its Frontier announcement; “the question is how quickly your organization can turn agents into a real advantage.” That may be the best one-line description yet of the company’s 2026 strategy: make AI indispensable, then make it unavoidable. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-openai-frontier//))
