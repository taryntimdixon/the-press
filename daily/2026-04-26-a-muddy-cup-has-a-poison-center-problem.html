A Muddy Cup Has a Poison-Center Problem

Kava bars sell a softer night out: no beer taps, no liquor bottles, and a promise of sociability without a hangover. New CDC poison-center data point to a messier 2026 question: what happens when an old Pacific drink becomes a U.S. wellness product, then shares the menu with kratom?

Written and Researched by AI • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most important object in a kava bar is not the neon sign, the couch, the open-mic flyer or the laptop glowing at midnight. It is the cup: cloudy, tan-brown, a little gritty, sometimes served in a coconut shell, sometimes poured into a mocktail with guava, ginger beer, matcha, kombucha or something that makes it sound less like roots worked through water.

That cup carries a promise. It says: here is a place to be social without alcohol. Here is a buzz without beer. Here is something plant-based, old, mellow, maybe even better than drinking.

The public-health story now is that the cup has picked up a second meaning. It has become a surveillance signal.

On April 2, 2026, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention published an analysis of kava-related reports to the National Poison Data System, the national database that gathers deidentified exposure information from U.S. poison centers. The numbers are not enormous compared with fentanyl, alcohol or acetaminophen. They are small enough to fit in one modest spreadsheet. But they are moving in a direction worth noticing: kava-related reports fell after a 2002 federal liver-injury advisory, then rose again after 2011, reaching 203 reported exposures in 2025. The CDC analysis counted 3,101 kava-related exposures from 2000 through 2025.

The sharper detail is what changed inside those calls. In 2025, 30 percent of kava-related poison-center reports also involved kratom, a different plant with compounds that interact with opioid receptors. Serious medical outcomes in the kava data rose from 12 percent in 2000 to 32 percent in 2025, after reaching 39 percent in 2024. In the subset involving both kava and kratom, poison centers recorded symptoms that included seizures, tremor and high blood pressure.

This does not mean every kava drink is dangerous. It does not mean every kava bar is reckless. It certainly does not erase the long cultural history of kava in Pacific Island communities, where it is traditionally prepared from the root of Piper methysticum and consumed in ceremonial and social settings. It does mean that the American version has become something different enough to deserve its own scrutiny: more concentrated products, more retail formats, more late-night menus, more smoke-shop adjacency, more customers looking for a substitute for alcohol and more confusion between old plant, new extract and mixed botanical shot.

Public health often sees a product this way before the rest of the country does. First it is a lifestyle. Then it is a shelf. Then it is a pattern of calls.

The phone number behind the trend

The National Poison Data System is not a census of all harm. It counts exposures reported to poison centers, which people and clinicians call voluntarily. Mild reactions may never be reported. Some serious events may be handled without a poison-center consult. A call can reflect anxiety, uncertainty, an accidental ingestion, deliberate misuse, a clinical emergency or a doctor trying to identify what was in a product.

That is why poison-center data should be read like smoke, not like a body count. Smoke can be overinterpreted. It can also tell you there is a fire starting in a wall before the flames are visible.

The CDC’s kava paper is careful about that. It defines an exposure as an actual or suspected ingestion for which a poison center was consulted, regardless of whether the person had clear toxicity. The authors list important limits: reporting is voluntary; data depend on accurate coding; the system cannot distinguish first-time users from repeat callers; and generic product codes do not reveal the exact product type, preparation method or dose.

Those limits matter because kava is not one thing in the real world. It can be a traditional water-based drink. It can be a capsule. It can be an extract. It can be a bottled shot. It can be a mocktail served in a lounge with board games. It can be combined with kratom. It can be sold beside nicotine products. It can be bought by someone who knows exactly what it is, or by someone who sees the word wellness and assumes the guardrails have already been built.

The CDC found that the early 2000s kava reports looked different from the recent ones. In 2000 and 2001, many reports involved women and a substantial share involved children 12 and younger. By 2025, reports were more likely to involve adults 20 and older, and men made up a larger share. Children were much less common in the recent data. That shift fits the retail world now surrounding kava: adult social venues, late-night nonalcoholic bars, smoke shops, gas stations, online products and college-town third spaces.

One reason the new CDC report matters is that it sees kava not only as a liver-risk question, the way regulators often saw it two decades ago, but as a product ecosystem. The risk is not just the plant. It is the form, dose, label, co-use, sales pitch and setting.

Roots, extracts and the word natural

Kava has a plain first explanation: it is made from the root or rhizome of a pepper-family plant native to the Pacific Islands. Its active compounds are called kavalactones. They can have sedating and anxiety-reducing effects. Some clinical research has supported short-term anxiolytic effects for certain standardized preparations, while other research has been mixed; federal health information says kava supplements may help with anxiety in some settings but that evidence is not enough to support broad claims for other uses.

The difficult part starts when the word kava travels from a traditional preparation into U.S. commerce. A water-based bowl of kava is not the same thing as a concentrated extract. A drink mixed by a careful kava bar that knows its supply chain is not the same thing as a tiny bottle with a supplement label bought at a gas station. A menu item that contains kava alone is not the same thing as a drink containing kava plus kratom, and possibly caffeine or other ingredients.

The FDA’s 2020 scientific memorandum on kava reviewed published safety literature and concluded that indiscriminate use of kava as a recreational or relaxation beverage is not safe for human consumption. The agency also said it did not see a basis for kava’s use as an ingredient in conventional foods to be considered generally recognized as safe, or GRAS, the legal term for ingredients widely recognized by qualified experts as safe under intended conditions of use. FDA said kava is an unapproved food additive when used in conventional foods.

That sounds simple until you reach the supplement aisle. Dietary supplements are regulated differently from drugs. FDA does not approve dietary supplements for safety and effectiveness before they are sold. Manufacturers are responsible for evaluating safety and labeling before marketing. FDA can act later if a product is adulterated, misbranded or unsafe, but the system is much more post-market than pre-market.

For a consumer, this distinction is almost invisible. A bottle can look polished. A website can say lab-tested. A label can use careful language about focus, calm or social energy. A store shelf can make a product feel ordinary. None of that is the same as a prescription drug approval, a standardized dose or proof that a drink made in one city resembles a drink with the same name made somewhere else.

That gap between appearance and oversight is where many modern botanical risks live.

The kratom complication

Kratom is the second plant in the cup, and it makes the public-health question harder.

Kratom comes from Mitragyna speciosa, a tree native to Southeast Asia. Its leaves contain alkaloids, including mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine, often shortened to 7-OH. Users report taking kratom for pain, mood, energy, anxiety, opioid withdrawal symptoms and recreation. Federal regulators have not approved kratom as a drug for any medical use. FDA has warned consumers against using kratom for medical treatment and says kratom is not lawfully marketed in the United States as a drug product, dietary supplement or food additive in conventional food.

But the kratom debate is not one flat argument between safety and danger. It has layers.

Some researchers, users and industry advocates distinguish between brewed leaf tea, powdered leaf, extracts and products enriched with isolated or concentrated 7-OH. That distinction became central in 2025, when FDA recommended federal control of certain 7-OH products while saying the action was not focused on natural kratom leaf products. The agency described 7-OH as a concentrated byproduct of the kratom plant with opioid-like properties and increasing availability in gummies, tablets, drink mixes and shots. FDA also issued warning letters to companies marketing 7-OH products and said 7-OH is not lawful in dietary supplements or conventional foods.

States have begun moving in their own ways. Florida’s attorney general declared 7-OH a Schedule I controlled substance in an emergency rule in August 2025, and the state agriculture department later issued requirements related to labeling 7-OH concentration in kratom products. California said in March 2026 that a statewide campaign had removed 3,308 kratom and 7-OH products from shelves at licensed businesses, while the state health department said foods, supplements and medical drugs containing kratom or 7-OH are illegal to sell or manufacture in California.

Advocates for kratom access argue that bans can backfire. Some people use kratom in an attempt to avoid illicit opioids, reduce alcohol consumption or manage chronic pain. A blunt prohibition, they warn, may push people toward more dangerous substances, illegal markets or products with even less transparency. Some kava-bar owners make the same argument in practical terms: a brewed botanical tea and an isolated high-potency compound should not be treated as the same product.

That counterargument deserves attention because public health has learned the hard way that supply can become more dangerous after a crackdown. But it does not erase the poison-center data. In a separate CDC analysis published in March 2026, kratom-related reports to U.S. poison centers rose from 258 in 2015 to 3,434 in 2025, an increase of about 1,200 percent. From 2015 through 2025, poison centers documented 14,449 kratom exposures among people 12 and older. Hospitalizations and serious outcomes rose for both single-substance and multiple-substance reports. Among 233 kratom-associated deaths reported during that period, 79 percent involved multiple substances.

The common public-health answer is not panic. It is product specificity. Which plant? Which extract? Which alkaloid? Which dose? Which other substances? Which claims? Which customers? Which setting? The CDC authors themselves noted that surveillance needs to distinguish product types to assess risk. That sounds boring. It is also the difference between regulating a cup of tea and regulating a pocket-size opioid-like concentrate as if they are the same thing.

The sober bar with a pharmacology menu

The kava bar has become one of the more interesting rooms in American health culture. It is a business, a hangout, a recovery-adjacent social space, a student study lounge, a nightlife substitute and sometimes a smoke-shop cousin. Local reporting has described kava bars as places where people work on laptops under soft lights, drink from coconut cups and talk about cutting back on alcohol. In Gainesville, a student newspaper reported this year on a new coffee shop and kava bar open late near the University of Florida, serving coffee, kombucha, kratom and kava as a sober late-night third space. In Philadelphia, a new kava company opened with kava- and kratom-infused mocktails, staff training and a pitch aimed at people looking for a different kind of social drink.

There is something genuinely appealing here. The United States has many bars and not nearly enough public-feeling places to sit with other people after dinner without buying alcohol. Younger adults have been drinking less than older generations did at the same age. Federal survey data show that past-month alcohol use among young adults ages 18 to 25 declined from 50.9 percent in 2021 to 47.5 percent in 2024. Gallup’s 2025 polling found a broader decline in adults who say they drink, with a particularly notable drop among adults under 35. Recovery communities have long needed social rooms that are not built around booze. A nonalcoholic night out can be a health gain.

The problem is that nonalcoholic does not automatically mean low-risk. A drink can contain no ethanol and still affect the central nervous system. It can be served in a room that feels wholesome and still interact with medications. It can be marketed as a sober alternative and still be habit-forming if it contains kratom. It can be plant-based and still produce vomiting, dizziness, drowsiness, high blood pressure, liver enzyme changes or withdrawal symptoms depending on the product and person.

Alcohol has a regulated percentage printed on the bottle. That does not make alcohol safe; it makes one part of its risk legible. A customer ordering beer can compare 4 percent lager with 9 percent double IPA. A customer ordering a kava-kratom mocktail may have much less useful information. How many milligrams of kavalactones? How much mitragynine? Any 7-OH beyond trace amounts? Is it brewed leaf, extract or concentrate? Was it tested for contaminants? How many servings are in the bottle? How does it interact with antidepressants, benzodiazepines, alcohol, acetaminophen or opioids?

The menu may say calm. The body reads chemistry.

The liver warning that never fully went away

Kava’s first major U.S. regulatory shadow was liver injury. In 2002, FDA warned that kava-containing dietary supplements might be associated with severe liver injury. The CDC report shows kava-related poison-center calls declined sharply after that warning, dropping to a low of 42 in 2010.

The liver story is complicated. NIH’s LiverTox resource notes that kava products have been linked to liver-injury reports, while also noting that several groups have disputed aspects of the evidence and suggested possible roles for adulterants, co-used drugs, other herbals, extraction methods or poor documentation. NIH’s complementary health center also describes rare but sometimes severe liver injury and advises against combining kava with alcohol or other sedating substances.

This uncertainty is not a reason to shrug. It is a reason to be precise. Kava liver injury appears uncommon, and the CDC’s recent poison-center analysis did not identify acute liver failure in its kava reports. Moderate elevations of liver enzymes were recorded in 1.7 percent of single-substance kava exposures and 6.3 percent of exposures involving only kava and kratom. That is not the same as saying kava routinely destroys livers. It is also not a clean bill of safety.

Rare harms are especially hard to manage in supplements because the denominator is blurry. If millions of servings are consumed and a handful of people are harmed, what product features or host factors explain those harms? Was it cultivar, dose, solvent extraction, contamination, medication interaction, genetics, underlying liver disease, alcohol, duration of use or something else? Without standardized manufacturing and better adverse-event reporting, the answer can stay muddy for years.

The mud in the cup is visible. The mud in the data is the harder problem.

When a wellness shelf behaves like a drug market

FDA’s April 2026 warning about Addall XR Shot and Addall XL capsules was not a kava story. But it belongs in the same newspaper because it shows the broader supplement-shelf problem. FDA said testing found unlawful or undeclared ingredients in those products: phenibut and undeclared 1,4-DMAA in the orange-flavored shot, and DMHA and undeclared 1,4-DMAA in the capsules. The agency warned of risks including elevated blood pressure, cardiovascular problems, neurocognitive effects, addiction and withdrawal.

That episode illustrates a rule consumers often learn too late: the front of a supplement package is not a chemistry report. A product can be sold online or in convenience stores, use the language of focus or wellness, and still contain ingredients the buyer did not knowingly choose.

Kava and kratom products are not all adulterated. Many sellers want clearer standards. Some bars train employees, avoid extracts, post warnings, check ages and try to separate traditional preparations from high-potency products. Still, the public-health system cannot rely on good actors alone. It needs labels that mean something, surveillance that can separate product forms and enforcement that can reach products before a trend matures into a patient population.

The Botanic Tonics Feel Free litigation shows why disclosure matters. A class-action settlement over kratom-containing Feel Free tonics reached $8.75 million, resolving allegations that consumers were not adequately warned about kratom-related risks. The company did not admit wrongdoing. The settlement and continuing debate around the product underline a consumer-protection problem: a product sold as a wellness or alcohol-alternative tonic may be understood very differently by a first-time buyer than by a toxicologist, a kratom regular or a plaintiff’s lawyer.

What prevention would look like

The easiest bad answer is to tell people that all plant drinks are safe because they are natural. The other easy bad answer is to treat every kava bowl, kratom tea, extract shot and 7-OH tablet as one identical threat. Prevention lives between those errors.

First, clinicians should ask about kava, kratom and other botanicals in ordinary language. Not everyone thinks of a drink from a kava bar as a drug, a supplement or a medication. A patient who says they do not use drugs may still drink three kratom teas a day. Someone in alcohol recovery may not volunteer that their sober bar drink contains an opioid-like botanical because they do not understand it that way, or because they are ashamed.

Second, poison-center surveillance needs better product detail. The CDC reports repeatedly point to the limits of generic coding. A future call record that can distinguish traditional kava, high-kavalactone extract, kratom leaf tea, kratom extract, 7-OH product and mixed beverage would be much more useful than a broad botanical bucket.

Third, labels should be legible to normal people. If a drink contains kratom, say so plainly. If it contains concentrated extract, say so. If a serving has a meaningful amount of kavalactones, mitragynine or 7-OH, make that visible. If people should avoid combining it with alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids, sleep medicines or liver-stressing drugs, do not hide that behind wellness fog.

Fourth, states and local health departments should avoid policy whiplash. One city may treat a brewed kava beverage as an unapproved food additive problem. Another may welcome kava bars as nonalcoholic nightlife. One state may restrict 7-OH; another may focus on age limits and labeling. Local experimentation can be useful, but a patchwork also confuses consumers and businesses. Public health should reward transparency, not just punish visibility.

Finally, prevention should respect why people are buying these drinks. A person choosing kava instead of five beers may be making a safer choice. A person choosing kratom instead of illicit fentanyl may be trying to survive. A college student looking for a late-night room without alcohol may be solving a real social problem. Public-health warnings that mock those motives will not land. Better warnings say: here is what is known, here is what is not known, here is when to call for help and here is how to reduce risk.

The cup is not the whole story

The cup on the bar is small. The system around it is large.

It includes federal supplement law, FDA post-market enforcement, state controlled-substance rules, local food codes, poison-center funding, emergency departments, campus health services, recovery communities, small businesses, online sellers, social media claims and customers who may be trying to drink less alcohol in a country that has made alcohol the default social lubricant.

That is why the CDC’s kava numbers are newsworthy even though they are not huge. They mark the moment when a cultural trend becomes visible to the public-health instruments. A product category that once seemed niche is now common enough, mixed enough and medically active enough to leave a trace in national surveillance.

The question is not whether kava bars are good or bad. The question is whether the United States can handle the gray zone between food, drug, supplement and nightlife without making consumers do toxicology homework at the counter.

A good kava cup should be allowed to be earthy, bitter and strange. It should not be mysterious in the wrong ways.

If the label says root, the customer should not have to guess what else is in the mud.
