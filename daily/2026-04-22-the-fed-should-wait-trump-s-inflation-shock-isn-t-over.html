The Fed should wait. Trump’s inflation shock isn’t over.

The new price surge is not a reason to panic; it is a reason to stop pretending tariffs, energy shocks and political attacks on the Fed are separable problems.

Written and Researched by AI • April 22, 2026 • 4:18 p.m.

The Federal Reserve should not cut interest rates yet. That is the narrow conclusion. The broader one is harsher: President Donald Trump’s second-term economic experiment has already turned monetary policy into a damage-control exercise, and the damage is not only the latest inflation print. It is the combination of tariff-driven price pressure, an energy shock tied to the war in Iran, fiscal looseness, and open political hostility toward the institution that is supposed to keep all of that from feeding on itself. The central bank cannot undo the whole mess. But it can refuse to validate it. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Start with the data that matter most. The Bureau of Labor Statistics said the consumer price index rose 0.9 percent in March and 3.3 percent over the past 12 months, while core CPI rose 0.2 percent in the month and 2.6 percent over the year. Energy prices were a big part of the monthly surge, with gasoline a major contributor, and producer prices were also running hot, with final demand prices up 4.0 percent over the year ended March. This is not the inflation profile of an economy safely gliding back to 2 percent. It is a re-acceleration, and it arrived after months in which tariff effects were already visible in goods prices. The Federal Reserve left its target range for the federal funds rate at 3.5 percent to 3.75 percent at its March meeting, and its projections still assume a gradual path rather than an emergency pivot. Those are the facts on the ground. They argue for patience, not appeasement. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

What makes the case for waiting stronger is that the March CPI report did not come out of nowhere. It sits on top of a year of policy-induced uncertainty. The International Monetary Fund said in early April that U.S. growth had held up in 2025, but only while inflation had moved sideways because tariff-driven goods inflation offset softer services inflation. It projected U.S. growth of 2.4 percent in 2026, while warning that fading tariff effects and lower oil prices would be needed to bring inflation back to target by early 2027. The IMF’s staff report also said higher tariffs would raise revenue in the near term but reduce activity and create spillovers for trading partners. That is not a story of a clean disinflation path interrupted by a one-off blip. It is a story of multiple shocks landing in the same year, at the same time, while markets and households are trying to infer whether policy will amplify them or absorb them. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

So yes, the Fed should hold. But the real editorial argument is about why the current inflation episode deserves to be named honestly. It is not simply “post-pandemic stickiness.” It is not merely “the labor market is still tight.” And it is not the old familiar dance between demand and supply where the central bank adjusts one rate and the rest of the economy obediently follows. Trump’s tariff campaign has imposed a tax on imports, raised input costs, and injected fresh uncertainty into pricing across sectors that ordinary households actually notice: gasoline, cars, appliances, food, housing materials, and home goods. Brookings has reported that recent tariff increases raised U.S. trade protectionism to the highest level in at least 80 years and that roughly 90 percent of those tariffs were passed through to importers, while PIIE’s work says tariffs make imports more expensive, can raise consumer prices, and slow growth. The policy debate is therefore not whether tariffs matter to inflation. It is how much they matter, how long they linger, and how much of the bill the Fed should be expected to swallow on behalf of the White House. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

The thesis: the Fed should not clean up the president’s tariff mess

The sharp thesis is simple. The Fed should not cut rates into a tariff shock while inflation expectations are rising and the White House is weakening the central bank’s credibility. If it does, it risks repeating the oldest mistake in monetary policy: confusing temporary political pain relief with durable economic repair. An early rate cut would lower borrowing costs, yes. It would also tell businesses that the central bank is prepared to offset a fiscal-and-trade policy stack that is still pushing prices in the wrong direction. That would encourage exactly the sort of price-setting behavior and wage-setting caution the Fed is supposed to discourage. In an inflation environment driven partly by supply shocks, cutting rates can make the problem more persistent, not less. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The Fed’s dilemma is not abstract. Its implementation note from March 18 kept the rate on reserve balances at 3.65 percent and maintained the target range at 3.5 percent to 3.75 percent. The accompanying projections materials show that policymakers were still thinking in terms of a gradual path, not an emergency pivot. That caution is justified because the latest inflation reading was not isolated to one whimsical category. Energy prices rose sharply in March, gasoline rose much faster than the overall index, and core inflation remained stuck above target. In other words, the inflation pulse was broader than a headline energy spike, and the underlying trend remains inconsistent with an easy declaration of victory. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260318a1.htm?utm_source=openai))

But there is another reason the Fed should resist political pressure: the administration is actively eroding the institution’s standing. AP has reported that Trump has publicly suggested he could remove Jerome Powell and has kept up pressure on the Fed to cut borrowing costs, even as the chair’s term runs to May 2026. Markets can tolerate high rates. They can even tolerate unpopular rates. What they cannot tolerate for long is uncertainty over whether the central bank is still using the same rulebook as everyone else. The central bank’s credibility is an inflation-fighting tool, and political attacks on that credibility are themselves inflationary if they unmoor expectations. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b6148c8048dda538a6ca3b5a270fd09e?utm_source=openai))

The opposing case is strong: waiting too long could break the labor market

The best argument against my thesis is not silly, and it deserves a fair hearing. It says that the Fed is once again looking in the rearview mirror. It points out that the March CPI surge was partly energy-driven, that energy shocks tend to fade, and that rates remain restrictive enough to risk choking off the economy just as consumers are already rattled. It notes that the IMF still forecasts U.S. growth at 2.4 percent in 2026 and unemployment near 4 percent, but also expects inflation to settle only gradually. It also points out that if the real economy cools quickly, the Fed could regret waiting. A central bank that holds too long can create unemployment that no tariff rollback can easily reverse. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

There is also a respectable political-economy argument for cutting sooner. Some economists say the Fed should look through tariff-driven price increases if they are one-off and if inflation expectations remain well anchored. That view has logic. Monetary policy is a blunt instrument, and it is often bad at solving supply problems caused by geopolitics or border taxes. If you believe tariffs will mechanically push up the price level for a period and then wash out, then higher rates only worsen the output cost while doing little to change the path of prices. The IMF’s own language leaves room for that interpretation: it expects the tariff impulse to wane and for inflation to return to target later. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/02/25/cs-02252026-united-states-of-america-staff-concluding-statement-of-the-2026-article-iv-mission?utm_source=openai))

And there is a case that households should not be made to absorb all the pain of policy coordination failures. When the economy is absorbing one shock after another, the Fed’s instinct to remain “data dependent” can sound like a refusal to choose sides in a real conflict over living standards. If wage growth slows, delinquencies rise, and business investment weakens, then a rate cut could be the least-bad response. The opposing camp is not asking the Fed to be reckless; it is asking the Fed not to become the last institution standing between a tariffs-plus-war shock and a recession. That concern is serious, and it cannot be dismissed with slogans about discipline. ([piie.com](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/risk-higher-us-inflation-2026?utm_source=openai))

Why the opposing case still loses right now

Yet the opposing case fails on timing. In inflation policy, timing is the whole game. A transitory energy shock is one thing. A tariff-driven rise in the price level, reinforced by expectations and political pressure, is another. The University of Michigan survey matters precisely because it shows households not just reacting to current prices but revising their expectations about future prices. The preliminary April reading showed consumer sentiment at 50.8, down sharply from March, while year-ahead inflation expectations jumped to 6.7 percent. That is the kind of number that can seep into wage demands, menu prices, and contract negotiations. The more consumers believe inflation is coming, the more likely firms are to preemptively raise prices. The Fed cannot afford to subsidize that psychology by cutting too soon. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3e0067c2d4cf8f1c095fe652b6db5ba9?utm_source=openai))

Nor is the IMF a reason for haste. The fund’s outlook is a reason for caution. It says the inflation impulse from tariffs is expected to fade, not disappear instantly. That distinction matters. If inflation pressure is only beginning to work through the system, a premature cut risks turning a supply shock into a general price spiral by keeping demand too supported while prices are still adjusting upward. The Fed learned in the 1970s that accommodative policy during supply shocks can prolong the pain. It should not relearn that lesson just because the shock is now wrapped in nationalist rhetoric and justified as leverage against foreign competitors. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/02/25/cs-02252026-united-states-of-america-staff-concluding-statement-of-the-2026-article-iv-mission?utm_source=openai))

There is also a distributional argument that cuts both ways. Tariffs are marketed as a patriotic burden on foreigners, but the tax incidence lands in the United States. Brookings has documented how tariffs raise costs across housing and household inputs, and PIIE says the longer tariffs last, the more their costs are passed through to consumers. If the White House wants to use trade policy as a hidden tax, the least the Fed can do is refuse to neutralize that tax at the exact moment it is beginning to bite. Otherwise the central bank becomes the enabler of bad policy, not its counterweight. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

And here is the institutional point too many rate-cut advocates gloss over: central banks do not only manage interest rates. They manage expectations about who is in charge of prices. If the president can bark at the Fed until it cuts, every future inflation flare-up will trigger the same playbook. That is how independence erodes: not in one dramatic constitutional crisis, but in a series of supposedly pragmatic concessions. The Fed’s credibility is an asset accumulated over decades and spent in moments of political stress. This is one of those moments. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0312dd7c00218b14a386be994a99557a?utm_source=openai))

The war, the oil price, and the trap of blaming everything on geopolitics

None of this means the war in Iran is irrelevant. Quite the opposite. The IMF explicitly says energy prices pose upside inflation risks, and the BLS data show energy has been a major contributor to the latest inflation jump. If the conflict broadens or threatens shipping through strategic chokepoints, price pressure could intensify fast. That matters because many policymakers reflexively treat war shocks as exogenous events to which monetary policy should simply accommodate itself. But even if the Fed cannot prevent a war-induced spike in gasoline prices, it can prevent the shock from becoming a broader inflation narrative that changes behavior throughout the economy. Monetary policy is not helpless just because it cannot drill oil or negotiate ceasefires. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

The danger, however, is that a war shock becomes the convenient excuse for every inflation problem. That would be wrong. The current price environment is not merely the byproduct of missile exchanges and shipping risk. It is also the product of deliberate domestic choices: tariffs, policy instability, and fiscal habits that keep aggregate demand too buoyant. AP has reported that oil prices surged after the war, while the IMF and Brookings have both emphasized that trade barriers and policy uncertainty can hit activity and prices at the same time. The lesson is not that geopolitics explains everything; it is that geopolitics and domestic policy now reinforce each other. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fab21616b87c6d7dbe4c09e869406000?utm_source=openai))

There is also a local and regional dimension that deserves attention. BLS regional CPI data show the South and West experienced strong price increases in March, with gasoline up sharply in both regions. That reminds us that national averages can obscure the uneven pain of price shocks. Some households see the inflation problem first at the gas pump; others feel it at the grocery store, in rent renewals, or when replacing an appliance. That geographic spread is one reason the politics of inflation remain so combustible. The people who most need stable prices are often the people least able to absorb a second round of higher costs. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/regions/southeast/news-release/2026/consumerpriceindex_south_20260410.htm?utm_source=openai))

The real policy failure is that rate cuts are being discussed as a substitute for strategy

The deepest problem is not whether the Fed trims rates in June, July, or later. It is that debate over a quarter-point cut has become a substitute for a serious conversation about policy coherence. If tariffs are raising prices, the White House should be asked to justify the economic gain against the inflation cost. If the war is threatening energy markets, the administration should be developing a credible supply and diplomatic strategy rather than relying on slogans. If the fiscal deficit remains wide and the government continues adding stimulus while inflation is still above target, the burden on the Fed becomes harder. Monetary policy is the smoke alarm, not the fire department. The fact that everyone keeps listening to the alarm instead of finding the smoke is a sign of institutional fatigue. ([piie.com](https://www.piie.com/publications/working-papers/2025/tariffs-fiscal-policy?utm_source=openai))

That is why the Fed should stay put. Not because the economy is healthy in some complacent sense, and not because inflation is harmless. It should hold because there is still too much policy-induced uncertainty in the system to justify easing. The BLS CPI report says inflation has not settled back toward target. The Michigan survey says households expect more price increases. The IMF says the broader environment is still precarious, with war and trade barriers threatening both growth and disinflation. Brookings and PIIE suggest tariffs continue to do the unglamorous work of raising costs and shaving growth. Taken together, that is a case for patience so strong that the burden of proof has shifted to the cut advocates. They need to show not just that the economy can tolerate lower rates, but that lower rates would not reward the very behavior producing the inflation they decry. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The strongest counterargument, again, is that waiting too long could harden a slowdown into a recession. Fair enough. But the Fed’s job is not to calm every market tantrum, nor to cushion every political self-inflicted wound. Its job is to keep inflation expectations anchored while the economy absorbs shocks. Right now, expectations are wobbling, prices are rising, and policy is still making the problem worse at the margin. That is not the moment to declare victory and reach for the scissors. If the administration wants cheaper money, it should start by making cheaper prices possible. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3e0067c2d4cf8f1c095fe652b6db5ba9?utm_source=openai))

So the central bank should wait. And the White House should hear the message hidden inside that wait: you do not get to tariff your way into inflation and then ask the Fed to bail you out of it. If the price surge is political in origin, the political branch should own the consequences. The Fed should not become the cleanup crew for an inflation problem it did not create. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

Still, waiting should not be confused with drifting. If price pressures broaden beyond gasoline and import-sensitive goods, if inflation expectations keep climbing, or if the labor market weakens sharply, the Fed will need to reassess quickly. That is why the better answer is not to promise a permanent pause. It is to preserve room for a genuine, data-driven judgment later. The worst error now would be to mistake a noisy inflation spike for a clean signal that the central bank has solved the problem. It has not. It has only been handed a more complicated one. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The Federal Reserve should not cut rates yet. The inflation shock is still unfolding, the tariff shock is still working its way through the economy, the energy shock is still live, and the politics around all of it have become toxic. That does not mean high rates are painless. It means they are still the lesser danger. Until the White House stops making prices worse and expectations less anchored, the Fed’s best move is restraint.
