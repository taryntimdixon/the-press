Inside pop culture’s new money map: why fandom now matters more than reach

Streaming, short-form video, superfans and AI are reshaping how music, TV and creator culture turn attention into revenue—and who gets left behind.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 22, 2026 • 4:18 p.m. • 2920 words

Pop culture is being reorganized around a single question: not just what is popular, but what makes people stay. The answer is increasingly not a hit in the old sense—a movie opening, a radio smash, a TV ratings bonanza, a one-week meme—but an ecosystem that can convert attention into repeat listening, repeat watching, paid subscriptions, merchandise, live-event demand and durable identity. That shift is why the biggest stories in entertainment right now are not just about stars or scandals. They are about the architecture of fandom itself: the platforms that feed it, the labels and studios that monetize it, the creators that can build direct communities, and the technologies that now threaten to flood the whole system with synthetic content. The latest industry data suggests a business still growing, but in ways that are more fragmented, more algorithmic and more dependent on superfans than ever before. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

This matters for a Pop Culture desk because the center of gravity has moved. The old mainstream still exists, but it is no longer the only route to scale. In music, IFPI says global recorded music revenue rose 6.4% in 2025 to $31.7 billion, the eleventh straight year of growth, with paid streaming up 8.8% and 837 million paid subscription accounts worldwide. Physical formats also returned to growth, with vinyl rising for a 19th consecutive year. The signal is not that people have abandoned digital; it is that fandom has become multi-format and identity-driven. Fans stream, collect, attend, post, remix and display taste across platforms and objects. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the discovery layer has become more unstable. TikTok says in its 2025 Music Impact Report with Luminate that 84% of songs entering the Billboard Global 200 in 2024 went viral on TikTok first. TikTok also says its Add to Music App feature has generated well over a billion track saves, and its newer sharing tools are designed to move users from discovery to saving and listening on streaming services. That is a powerful reminder of how discovery now works: a snippet, a sound, a dance, a meme, a joke or a fan edit can become the first market test of a song long before a full release has a chance to build context. The same platform that can launch careers can also compress songs into hook-sized content and make success feel increasingly contingent on an unpredictable feed. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

That tension is the headline. A lot of recent pop culture coverage still treats virality as a synonym for fandom, but they are not the same thing. MIDiA Research has argued the opposite, saying virality is not building fandom and warning that no single platform dominates music discovery. The distinction is important because it suggests a deeper business truth. TikTok may create the spark, but YouTube, streaming services, live shows and social communities often do the slower, more valuable work of turning a casual viewer into a repeat fan. In other words, the industry is not really selling discovery anymore; it is selling progression. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

The new pop culture unit of value is the fan, not the impression

The old media economy prized impressions, reach and gross audience size because those were the easiest things to measure at scale. The new one cares just as much about conversion: who listened all the way through, who followed the artist, who saved the song, who joined the Discord, who bought the deluxe variant, who saw the movie twice, who subscribed after the clip, who attended the tour date, who posted the reaction, who keeps returning. That is not just a semantic shift; it changes how entertainment is made. A project can be optimized for repeat engagement even if it does not command the broadest opening-week total. A TV show can become successful by sustaining conversation across multiple weeks rather than exploding in one. A music act can be treated as economically significant not because it owns the biggest chart peak, but because it has a reliable superfan base that buys, posts and persists. ([nielsen.com](https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2026/nielsen-announces-2025-artey-award-winners-following-record-breaking-year-of-streaming/?utm_source=openai))

Nielsen’s streaming data underscores how much of entertainment life now runs on this kind of repeat consumption. The company said audiences spent an estimated 16.7 trillion minutes streaming in 2025, up 19% from 2024, and that the year was the most heavily streamed on record. Within that universe, catalog and franchise content remain enormous. Nielsen highlighted titles like Bluey and Stranger Things, and even created a Streaming Icon of the Year category to capture creators whose bodies of work drive sustained viewing across platforms. This is useful because it reveals the economic logic of the current era: franchises, long tails and rewatchable comfort content are becoming as important as novelty. Pop culture is increasingly a library business with a hype problem. ([nielsen.com](https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2026/nielsen-announces-2025-artey-award-winners-following-record-breaking-year-of-streaming/?utm_source=openai))

That library logic also helps explain why studios, streamers, labels and brands increasingly talk about ecosystems. They are not just selling content; they are trying to manage a relationship graph. In practice, that means the most valuable IP is content that can travel across clips, playlists, spinoffs, live experiences, mobile games, merchandise and creator collaborations without collapsing when one channel changes its algorithm. Variety’s current events pages reflect that mindset, with summit programming focused on entertainment marketing, fandom and how companies create and sustain fan impact across TV, film, creator content, gaming and music. That kind of language may sound like consulting-speak, but it reflects a real market condition: attention has become portable, and the audience has become cross-platform by default. ([events.variety.com](https://events.variety.com/ces2026/home?utm_source=openai))

YouTube is still the quiet giant

In a lot of pop culture coverage, YouTube gets treated as the old platform that never stopped existing. That underrates it. YouTube’s 20th anniversary Culture & Trends report says the service now operates in more than 100 countries and 80 languages and that billions of monthly viewers collectively watch over a billion hours of video each day. The platform’s pitch is no longer simply that it hosts videos; it is that it hosts a borderless mainstream in which creators and audiences co-build entertainment together. That matters because YouTube is simultaneously an archive, a discovery engine, a learning platform, a music platform, a creator platform and, increasingly, a venue for live and quasi-live culture. ([blog.youtube](https://blog.youtube/culture-and-trends/trends-report-20th/?utm_source=openai))

YouTube’s recent trend work also suggests that fandom is becoming more participatory and more specialized. Its report on virtual creators showed that a sample of 300 virtual creators generated more than 15 billion views across videos, livestreams and Shorts in 2024, with about 1 billion of those views coming from the U.S. alone. Its Roblox community milestone, meanwhile, said videos related to Roblox surpassed 1 trillion views. Together, those reports imply something broader than niche internet weirdness: younger audiences are not merely watching culture; they are inhabiting systems. Virtual performers, game worlds and participatory spaces are becoming default settings for identity play. That is a very different relationship to entertainment than the old watch-and-wait model of TV and radio. ([blog.youtube](https://blog.youtube/culture-and-trends/youtube-culture-trends-report-virtual-creators/?utm_source=openai))

And unlike some social platforms, YouTube still has a powerful long-form advantage. MIDiA’s report says YouTube leads music discovery for many consumers, which fits with YouTube’s role as both a short-form and long-form repository. A fan may encounter a 20-second hook on Shorts and then move to a live performance, lyric video, behind-the-scenes documentary, interview or deep-catalog playlist. That progression is hard to replicate in a purely ephemeral feed. It is also why music companies keep treating YouTube as strategic even when the loudest hype lives elsewhere. The platform is where taste becomes habit. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

TikTok is still the spark, but the industry is learning to distrust sparks

TikTok remains central to the modern pop culture machine, especially in music. Its February 2025 report with Luminate framed the app as a key driver of discovery and chart behavior, and its newer product announcements have kept emphasizing features that turn social attention into more durable listening behavior. The company’s Songwriter Features, launched in beta in July 2025, are another telling example: if a platform knows the song itself is now a strategic asset, not just a soundtrack, then the platform is trying to make the people behind the song more legible and more monetizable. That is both a concession and a correction. It acknowledges that music culture is not only about clips and dances; it is also about authorship, credits and catalog control. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

But the relationship between TikTok and the rest of the music business remains fraught. A song can go viral there and still fail to become a durable fan relationship. In fact, that is part of the criticism. MIDiA’s September 2025 work argued that virality is not necessarily fandom, and that platforms built for quick consumption may not reliably deepen artist loyalty. The implication is uncomfortable for labels and managers who have spent the last several years chasing short-form momentum: a viral audio clip may function more like a market teaser than a market foundation. It can accelerate awareness, but awareness is not the same as belonging. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

There is also a regulatory shadow hanging over TikTok, but the cultural significance is bigger than one company’s legal problems. For pop culture, the key fact is simpler: the app has become so embedded in taste formation that uncertainty about its future affects creators’ strategies. That uncertainty pushes artists and labels to diversify across YouTube, Instagram, streaming services and direct-to-fan channels. The result is a more redundant but also more resilient content economy. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-gb/tiktok-and-luminate-release-the-latest-music-impact-report?utm_source=openai))

Superfans are now the real growth market

If there is one term that keeps resurfacing in entertainment strategy, it is superfan. That is not a coincidence. In a mature streaming and digital music market, the biggest upside often comes from deepening revenue per user, not merely adding more users. MIDiA’s report on the superfans of music superstars argues that fandom is becoming the industry’s golden goose, with labels focusing more explicitly on identifying and monetizing the small share of listeners who are willing to spend on music rather than merely stream it. That strategy is visible everywhere: premium fan tiers, presales, exclusive drops, community memberships, collectible variants, meet-and-greets and platform-native experiences built to reward loyalty. ([musicbusinessworldwide.com](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/bad-bunny-was-spotifys-most-streamed-artist-globally-in-2025-with-over-19-8bn-streams/?utm_source=openai))

IFPI’s 2026 report offers the macroeconomic case for why this matters. It says paid streaming remains the largest driver of growth, but physical formats also rose, suggesting that fans are not choosing between digital and tangible consumption so much as using both to express identity. Vinyl’s 19th consecutive year of growth is a striking marker of that dual behavior. It implies that collectability still matters in a digital age, especially when fans want something that feels durable, scarce and displayable. Pop culture is not only content; it is status signaling, memory storage and social belonging. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

There is a risk, of course, that the superfan frame can become too flattering to the industry. It suggests that all that matters is converting devotion into revenue, when in fact many fans are not trying to maximize spend at all. Some want access, community or recognition; others are price-sensitive, transient or casual by design. There is also a cultural cost to overfitting entertainment to the most intense users. When every franchise is built to keep the most obsessive customers engaged, creators can wind up serving a tiny fraction of the audience while still claiming mass-cultural relevance. That can produce bloated release strategies, endless variants and a sense that every fandom is being managed as a subscription funnel. The backlash to over-monetized fandom is becoming part of fandom itself. ([musicbusinessworldwide.com](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/half-of-all-paid-music-streams-globally-derive-from-just-4-countries-and-other-highlights-from-luminates-latest-report/?utm_source=openai))

AI is the spoiler, and not just for jobs

No current pop culture story is complete without AI, because AI now affects authorship, authenticity, economics and trust all at once. IFPI explicitly says AI innovation and the industry response to streaming fraud will shape music’s next era. That is a broad statement, but it captures a practical anxiety: synthetic music can scale fast, streaming fraud can distort dashboards, and engagement can become less meaningful if bots or low-cost model outputs flood the supply. The issue is not simply whether AI-generated songs sound good. It is whether the ecosystem can still reward human artists, preserve rights and distinguish between genuine audience affection and machine-amplified noise. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

The pop culture stakes are especially high because AI threatens the confidence economy that fandom runs on. Fans do not just buy music or watch shows; they invest in the idea that a creator is real, legible and narratable. When voices, likenesses, scripts, edits or entire channels become cheap to synthesize, the premium shifts toward verified identity and lived experience. That could deepen demand for live performance and direct community spaces. It could also make audiences more skeptical, more polarized and more attached to provenance. Platforms are already responding with credit and discovery tools, including TikTok’s songwriter features and artist analytics products. But the broader problem remains unresolved: the more entertainment can be generated, the more authenticity itself becomes a luxury product. ([newsroom.tiktok.com](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/en-us/tiktok-announces-the-beta-launch-of-songwriter-features//?utm_source=openai))

What the audience behavior data says about the culture shift

Pew Research Center’s 2025 social media survey helps explain why this is happening at all. YouTube still tops the list of platforms used by U.S. adults, while Instagram, TikTok, WhatsApp and Reddit also continue to grow. Pew also says YouTube is the most widely used online platform among U.S. teens. That matters because the entertainment industry is no longer reaching a single mass audience through a handful of gatekeepers. It is dealing with an audience that is multihomed: people move among platforms depending on whether they want music, comedy, news, snacks, live streams, discourse or visual identity play. The result is a culture that feels simultaneously more connected and more fragmented. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/11/20/americans-social-media-use-2025/?utm_source=openai))

Nielsen’s audio research adds another layer. Its listening reports show that radio still occupies a major share of ad-supported listening time, podcasts are large and stable, and streaming music holds a significant but not exclusive place in the daily audio diet. That means the much-discussed everything-is-streaming-now narrative is too simple. Old and new formats coexist. The question is less whether one medium has won than how each medium fits into a broader attention stack. People may discover a song on TikTok, extend it on YouTube, hear it in a streaming playlist and later encounter it on radio or in a live venue. Fandom is increasingly a relay race across channels. ([nielsen.com](https://www.nielsen.com/insights/2022/streaming-is-the-future-of-tv-but-abundance-of-platform-choice-is-overwhelming-for-viewers/?utm_source=openai))

This is why local reporting and market-specific context matter. Billboard Canada’s recent coverage of the Canadian music industry, for example, points to ongoing questions about streaming regulation, AI-manipulated vocals and the fragility of platform negotiations. Those are not separate stories. They are evidence that the same platform systems driving global fandom also create policy conflicts about rights, royalties and cultural sovereignty. Pop culture is now an infrastructure story. The music you hear and the shows you watch are shaped by licensing deals, ranking systems, content moderation, ad tech and distribution rules you rarely see. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/11/20/americans-social-media-use-2025/?utm_source=openai))

Why the current pop culture boom feels both richer and thinner

The paradox of the moment is that pop culture may be generating more revenue, more data, more content and more participation than ever, while also feeling thinner to many audiences. That feeling is not imaginary. When content is optimized for retention and conversion, it can become repetitive. When algorithms reward familiarity, the center can feel crowded with clones. When platforms reduce culture to snippets, the context that makes art memorable can disappear. And when studios and labels chase every possible revenue layer, consumers can begin to experience fandom less as joy and more as monetization pressure. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

Still, the counterargument is strong: this is not cultural decline so much as cultural redistribution. More creators can now reach audiences directly. More artists can build businesses without traditional gatekeepers. More niche communities can survive at scale. More fans can find each other. More formats can coexist. The data on YouTube’s reach, TikTok’s discovery power, global streaming growth and the persistence of physical formats all suggest a more plural media world, not a narrower one. The system may be messier and less predictable, but it is also more open to different kinds of success. A breakout today does not have to look like a 1990s MTV moment or a 2010s radio hit. It can be a slow-burn catalog act, a creator-led universe, a virtual performer, a regional scene or a fandom that grows sideways across platforms. ([blog.youtube](https://blog.youtube/culture-and-trends/trends-report-20th/?utm_source=openai))

That is the real news in pop culture right now: the audience is not just watching; it is organizing. It is organizing around identity, algorithm, community and collectability. The businesses chasing that audience are reorganizing too. Labels are investing in superfan strategies. Streamers are turning catalog into recurring revenue. Platforms are adding tools for credit, conversion and creator loyalty. And AI is forcing the whole sector to prove that human culture still has a premium. The mainstream has not vanished. It has multiplied into many smaller centers of gravity, each with its own economics, rituals and power brokers. The challenge for everyone in entertainment is no longer simply to make a hit. It is to build a relationship that survives after the hit is over. ([nielsen.com](https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2026/nielsen-announces-2025-artey-award-winners-following-record-breaking-year-of-streaming/?utm_source=openai))

For the Pop Culture desk, that is the story worth following through 2026: how fandom becomes infrastructure, how platforms become tastemakers, how AI reshapes authenticity and how the audience learns to value not just what is viral, but what is lasting. The numbers say the market is growing. The culture says the terms of growth are changing. And the gap between those two realities is where the most important entertainment stories now live. ([ifpi.org](https://www.ifpi.org/global-music-report-2026-global-recorded-music-revenues-grow-6-4-as-record-companies-drive-innovation/?utm_source=openai))
