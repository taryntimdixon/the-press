A Service Notice Reaches the Closed Crossing

Cambodia’s new military-service bill is being sold as a national-defense repair after last year’s border fighting. Its harder test lies in the same places the conflict has already shaken: migrant wages, household debt, school lists, temple stones, aid ledgers, trucks, observers and the patient regional work of making a ceasefire ordinary.

By The Press • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most important object in Cambodia this week may not look like a weapon. It may look like paper.

A registration form. A stamped notice. A name checked against an age line. A family asking whether a son who might have worked in Thailand, a garment factory, a cassava field, a construction crew, a delivery job or a training course is also about to become part of the country’s military plan.

On April 23, Cambodia’s Council of Ministers approved a new draft law on military service. The bill would require Cambodian men ages 18 to 25 to serve for two years, while women could serve voluntarily. It still has to pass the National Assembly and the Senate and then be signed by King Norodom Sihamoni. But the signal has already arrived: after two rounds of deadly border fighting with Thailand in 2025, Cambodia’s government is moving compulsory service from an old unused law into the practical machinery of the state.

The proposal would replace a 2006 military-service law that was never implemented. Government accounts describe the new bill as an urgent measure tied to Article 49 of Cambodia’s Constitution and to the need to strengthen national defense, build discipline and cultivate patriotism. Local reporting adds the key change that matters to households: the earlier framework had a higher maximum call-up age and an 18-month term; the new bill narrows compulsory service to men ages 18 to 25 and lengthens the term to 24 months.

Conscription is not rare. Thailand has its own draft system. South Korea, Singapore, Israel, Finland and other countries use different versions of compulsory service or reserve obligation. Governments usually defend these systems with sturdy words: readiness, fairness, reserve forces, national duty, discipline, deterrence.

In Cambodia’s case, those words now carry the sound of the border. The frontier with Thailand is not only a line between two states. It is a work route, a shopping route, a remittance route, a fuel route, a tourism route, a trucking route, a family route and, since last year, a military flashpoint. When the crossing closes or the shelling starts, the effect travels through rice fields, temples, schools, clinics, bank books, bus stations and factories far from the map line.

That is why the bill is not only a domestic legal story. It is a World desk story: a state-building measure linked to a disputed frontier, a fragile ceasefire, ASEAN diplomacy, Chinese security engagement, U.S. pressure, returned migrant workers, humanitarian displacement and supply chains that were built on the assumption that border gates would keep opening.

The bill does not prove that new fighting is coming. The most careful available reporting says there has been no new fighting since the late-December ceasefire, even though tensions remain high. But a conscription bill can still change the political weather. It tells families that the conflict has moved from the border post into the registry office. A ceasefire can silence guns faster than it can reopen trust.

The border was also a payroll system

For many readers outside Southeast Asia, the Cambodia-Thailand border may first suggest the Temple of Preah Vihear, colonial-era maps and armed patrols. Those matter. But the more ordinary border was a wage machine.

Before hostilities escalated in July 2025, the World Bank estimated that about 1.3 million Cambodians worked in Thailand, nearly half of them unregistered. From June to early October 2025, about 940,000 Cambodian migrant workers returned home, roughly 70 percent of all Cambodian migrants working in Thailand. These were not abstract labor-market units. They were daughters and sons sending money to parents, spouses paying debt, young adults trying to build a life in the wage gap between the two countries.

That wage gap was sharp. The World Bank estimated that Cambodian migrants in Thailand earned about $400 a month, compared with about $248 in Cambodia, and typically sent home about $150 a month. Total remittances reached about $2.6 billion in 2024, equal to 5.5 percent of Cambodia’s gross domestic product, with nearly 80 percent originating in Thailand. The return of workers from Thailand, the bank warned, could reduce annual remittance inflows by about $1.7 billion. In surveys cited by the bank, 85 percent of returnees reported that their incomes had fallen to zero.

A labor-rights assessment gave the same disruption a household shape. The Center for Alliance of Labor and Human Rights, known as CENTRAL, reported that more than 900,000 Cambodian migrant workers returned from Thailand in 2025 after renewed border tensions. In the summary published by the Business and Human Rights Resource Centre, 71 percent of returnees were reported to be in debt, only 30.5 percent received any assistance during or after return, and support was largely limited to short-term food aid and transport. CENTRAL also reported average household debt of about $5,500 among indebted respondents and monthly household income far below expenses after return.

These figures should not be treated as if they come from one perfect ledger. A World Bank macroeconomic report, a labor-rights survey and a government count use different methods and time frames. But they point in the same direction: the border crisis pushed a very large number of workers back into Cambodia faster than the domestic economy could comfortably absorb them.

That is the first complication for conscription. A draft can build a reserve force. It can also take young adults away from households that have already lost wages. It can offer some men a state paycheck and structure; it can also become one more claim on families whose debt, food bills and job searches have been unsettled by the very conflict used to justify the law.

A ceasefire can be signed faster than a school reopens

The humanitarian story also refuses to stay inside a military frame.

According to the United Nations in Cambodia, the country experienced two major conflict-induced displacement waves in 2025. The first escalation began on July 24 and displaced more than 172,000 people across Preah Vihear, Siem Reap and Oddar Meanchey. After a 13-point ceasefire on August 8, more than 85 percent of displaced households had returned by early September, though thousands remained away from home into October.

The second wave was larger. Renewed hostilities on December 7 produced a displacement crisis that peaked from December 25 to 27. Cambodia’s National Committee for Disaster Management reported 644,589 displaced people, including 336,302 women and 204,992 children. More than 347,000 were in more than 200 temporary sites, while nearly 297,000 were staying with host communities.

The numbers later fell, which matters. By January 15, the same national committee reported 141,850 people still in 102 displacement sites or staying with relatives. By early March, World Vision said about 600,000 people — 93 percent of the more than 640,000 originally displaced in that wave — had returned, while 47,714 remained displaced across sites and host communities.

But return is not the same as recovery. A family can leave a shelter and find a damaged house, a closed shop, a field that cannot yet be worked safely, a clinic with reduced service, a school operating out of temporary space or no immediate way to earn money. The Humanitarian Response Forum, a coordination platform involving Cambodian authorities and humanitarian partners, listed continuing needs in shelter, sanitation, food security, nutrition, health care, psychosocial support, protection, social protection and education.

One number translates the conflict into a child’s morning. On January 16, the forum reported that 69 schools were still closed, affecting 12,601 students. Temporary early-childhood classes were operating in camps, but emergency education is not the same as the ordinary noise of a school day. In a crisis, boredom can be a sign of safety.

Conscription enters this scene as a second demand on a society trying to restore ordinary life. The government’s argument is not frivolous: states have a right to defend their territory, and border communities have a right not to be shelled. But the same communities also need toilets, classrooms, demining, health services, cash, food, transport, markets and enough calm to plant, sell and travel. National defense and civilian recovery cannot be treated as separate columns if the same young men, the same provincial budgets and the same households are being asked to carry both.

Trucks learned the border before soldiers did

The Cambodia-Thailand border was an economic design as well as a security problem.

For years, manufacturers used a model often called Thailand Plus One. Companies based in Thailand moved some labor-intensive work into nearby countries, including Cambodia, while keeping more advanced operations in Thailand. That arrangement depended on predictable crossings. Parts could move, workers could move, managers could move, and trucks could keep schedules.

A recent analysis by the ISEAS-Yusof Ishak Institute’s Fulcrum showed why the border is expensive to pull apart. Thailand was Cambodia’s third-largest source of imports in 2024 and its eighth-largest export destination. Thailand supplied Cambodia with oil, food and beverages, vehicles and other essential goods. Cambodia exported agricultural raw materials such as cassava, as well as metallic waste, wiring sets and electronic parts.

Fulcrum estimated that overland trade averaged about $1.2 billion per quarter in the two years leading up to the first quarter of 2025. With border closures, that traffic vanished. Trade by other transport modes also fell, from $1.9 billion in the second quarter of 2025 to $0.9 billion in the fourth quarter.

Those figures make the border look less like a single gate than a wire harness: many small lines bundled together. Cut one and the damage may be local. Cut enough and the machine stops.

The damage is uneven. Large multinational companies may reroute some cargo by sea or air, though at higher cost. Smaller border traders, market sellers and farmers have fewer options. A cassava farmer cannot easily turn a perishable crop into a geopolitical hedge. A market stall that depended on Thai goods cannot simply become a logistics company.

The World Bank and AMRO both framed the border conflict as one shock among several. Cambodia was already dealing with a property-sector slowdown, trade-policy uncertainty and pressure on domestic demand. The border conflict added disruption to labor markets, remittances, tourism and supply chains.

That matters for the conscription debate because military manpower is never free. Uniforms, food, training, medical care, barracks, pay, transport, administration and officers all cost money. So does the time of the conscripts. If a two-year system is implemented, the question will not be only whether Cambodia can train young men. It will be whether it can do so without deepening stress on families, employers, schools and public finances that are already absorbing returned workers and displaced households.

The temple is old. The institutions around it are modern.

The border dispute did not begin in 2025. Its roots include colonial-era maps, national memory and the cliff-top Temple of Preah Vihear, an 11th-century Khmer temple that both sides have treated as more than stone.

The International Court of Justice ruled in 1962 that the temple was in territory under Cambodian sovereignty, and the court’s later interpretation in 2013 reaffirmed Cambodia’s sovereignty over the promontory. Those rulings did not settle every part of the border. Thailand has often favored bilateral demarcation mechanisms. Cambodia has leaned heavily on international legal rulings. Maps, temple stones, nationalism and armed positions have kept meeting one another ever since.

The cultural dimension sharpened again after renewed clashes. UNESCO expressed strong concern in December 2025 about tensions near Preah Vihear, a World Heritage site, and reminded parties of obligations to protect cultural property in armed conflict. Associated Press journalists who visited the site in March 2026 reported stone debris, artillery craters, burned vegetation, roped-off areas and warnings about unexploded ordnance. Cambodian officials said all five notable gateway pavilions had been damaged and that some structures might be at risk during the rainy season. Thailand has disputed Cambodia’s account and has argued that it fired at military targets, while Cambodia denies using the temple for military purposes.

That last point is important. Verification is difficult, and the most responsible account must distinguish between visible damage, official claims and independent proof. AP reported that no independent outside evaluations of the damage were available at the time. Even so, the temple’s condition has become part of the politics around the border. A damaged wall can become an argument. A rumor about a gate can become a rallying cry. A map can become an heirloom.

In such a climate, conscription is not only a staffing policy. It is a story a state tells its citizens about danger, identity and obligation. That story can unify people. It can also narrow the space for compromise. If leaders spend months telling the public that the frontier is sacred and the neighbor is treacherous, the boring work becomes harder: survey teams, buffer arrangements, mine cooperation, prisoner releases, customs schedules, school reopenings, market access and the quiet permissions that let civilians return to normal life.

ASEAN is in the room, but not always at the wheel

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations prefers consensus, quiet diplomacy and noninterference. That style can keep channels open. It can also look slow when artillery is moving faster than statements.

ASEAN foreign ministers said on July 27, 2025, that they were deeply concerned by casualties, property destruction and displacement along the Cambodia-Thailand border. They urged maximum restraint, an immediate ceasefire and peaceful settlement under the United Nations Charter, the ASEAN Charter and the Treaty of Amity and Cooperation in Southeast Asia.

Malaysia, ASEAN’s 2025 chair, played a visible mediating role. A special ASEAN foreign ministers’ meeting in Kuala Lumpur on December 22 heard from Malaysia and from the ASEAN Observer Team. The chair’s statement appreciated the roles of Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim, U.S. President Donald Trump, ASEAN member states and China in encouraging a peaceful resolution, and urged the two countries to revive humanitarian demining and de-escalation under observation by the ASEAN Observer Team.

The December 27 ceasefire was more than a photo opportunity. According to AP, it called for a halt to fighting, no further military movements, no military airspace violations and the repatriation by Thailand of 18 Cambodian soldiers after the ceasefire held for 72 hours. AP also reported how fragile the deal was, with Thai officials linking prisoner return to proof that the ceasefire had truly taken hold.

The United Nations secretary-general welcomed the ceasefire as a step toward easing civilian suffering and thanked Malaysia, China and the United States for supporting efforts toward a peaceful resolution. That crowded diplomacy is partly reassuring. Many actors want the ceasefire to hold. It is also a warning that the border has become a regional management problem.

ASEAN can convene, observe and encourage. It cannot by itself settle domestic politics in Bangkok or Phnom Penh. It cannot guarantee that local commanders, mines, rumors, social-media outrage or electoral incentives will not weaken restraint. Cambodia’s military-service bill is therefore also a test for ASEAN’s quieter machinery. If conscription becomes a disciplined domestic reserve system inside a durable ceasefire, it may be absorbed as a sovereign policy choice. If it becomes part of a cycle of mobilization and fear, ASEAN’s job becomes harder.

China is not watching from the balcony

This week added another layer. On April 22, Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Defense Minister Dong Jun met Cambodian counterparts in the first China-Cambodia “2+2” strategic dialogue, a format that brings foreign and defense ministers together. Wang then continued to Thailand and Myanmar.

The timing matters. China is Cambodia’s closest major-power partner and a major investor and aid donor. Thailand is also deeply tied to China economically. Beijing has an interest in preventing a prolonged conflict that disrupts trade, security and Chinese influence in mainland Southeast Asia.

Chinese official accounts said China supports Cambodia and Thailand in using existing bilateral mechanisms, strengthening dialogue, rebuilding mutual trust and improving relations. Beijing also said it would continue humanitarian support for Cambodian border residents’ resettlement and other needs. In Bangkok, AP reported that Wang met Thai Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul on April 24, with cooperation on transnational crime and cyberscams also on the agenda.

This does not mean China controls the dispute. It does not. Nor does it make ASEAN irrelevant. Regional diplomacy is not a single steering wheel. It is several hands trying to keep a cart from tipping: bilateral military talks, ASEAN process, Malaysian mediation, Chinese engagement, U.S. pressure, United Nations statements, aid coordination and domestic politics in both countries.

The counterargument deserves to be heard

A fair reading of Cambodia’s bill has to include the government’s case.

Cambodian leaders can point to real fighting, real deaths, real displacement and real damage or alleged damage to civilian and cultural sites. A state that cannot defend its frontier leaves people exposed. The Royal Cambodian Armed Forces may need younger personnel, trained reserves, clearer mobilization rules and a more predictable way to fill gaps. Prime Minister Hun Manet said last year that conscription would be used to fill shortages and upgrade military capabilities. Supporters can also argue that compulsory service spreads the burden more evenly than relying on poorer volunteers.

There is a regional comparison. Thailand’s own draft system shapes citizenship and military politics across the border. The Guardian reported this month that nearly 50,000 Thai men volunteered to enlist in 2026, a 22 percent increase from 2025, with analysts citing possible factors including nationalism after the Cambodia conflict, a weak economy and the desire to avoid uncertainty in the draft lottery. In other words, militarized citizenship is not only a Cambodian phenomenon. It is appearing on both sides of the frontier in different forms.

The strongest argument for Cambodia’s bill is capacity, not revenge. If the country believes it faces a long-term security risk, a reserve system may look prudent.

But that is not the end of the argument. Conscription systems can be unfair in practice even when they sound equal on paper. Wealthier families may find ways to delay, soften or avoid service. Poorer families may lose the most income. Corruption can turn national duty into a market for exemptions. Poor training can produce bored young men in uniform rather than a useful reserve. Harsh discipline can become abuse. A security curriculum can crowd out civic life.

Those risks are not unique to Cambodia. They follow conscription everywhere. They are sharper, however, in a country dealing with returned migrants, household debt, displacement, school disruption and a political environment in which civil-society groups have long warned about limited space for criticism.

What to watch next

The next steps are practical.

First, the legal text matters. Cambodians need to know who must register, who can be deferred, who is exempt for health, disability, study or family reasons, what pay and benefits are provided, whether service can be extended, what penalties apply, what appeal rights exist and how the government will prevent bribery or favoritism.

Second, implementation matters more than ceremony. A law on paper is one thing. Selection lists, medical examinations, transport, barracks, training officers, safety rules and complaint systems are another. If the state cannot administer the system fairly, resentment will do its own recruiting.

Third, the border economy matters. If crossings remain disrupted, remittances stay weak and returnees cannot find decent work, military service may become tangled with desperation. Some young men may see the army as a stable paycheck. Others may see it as another loss of control over their future.

Fourth, the ceasefire needs boring success. That means no new shelling, no surprise troop movements, no inflammatory stunts at temples, no careless claims about maps, continued observer access, mine cooperation, transparent prisoner-related steps and practical arrangements for civilians who need to farm, trade, study or go home.

Finally, ASEAN’s role should be judged by outcomes, not vocabulary. The region does not need another elegant statement if border families cannot reopen a shop, send a child to school or trust that a checkpoint will be calm tomorrow. Quiet diplomacy can still be valuable if it keeps commanders talking and gives leaders a way to step back without looking weak.

The conscription bill is a document about soldiers. But the story around it is about much more than soldiers.

It is about whether a country can defend a border without letting the border consume its economy. It is about whether young men become reserves for national security or shock absorbers for political failure. It is about whether regional institutions can turn a ceasefire into habits. It is about whether a line between two countries can become a working seam again, rather than a rip.

For now, the paper is moving through Cambodia’s state machinery. At the frontier, the harder paperwork is still unfinished: school lists, debt ledgers, customs forms, aid registrations, worker documents, crop sales, medical records, demining reports and the quiet accounting every family does when peace has not quite become normal.
