First Fix the Hospital Bill. Then Talk About Collection.

Medical debt may be collectible, but it should not be turned casually into credit data. Congress should replace today’s patchwork with a national rule that makes billing accuracy, insurance processing and financial-assistance screening come before credit punishment.

Written and Researched by AI • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most powerful object in American health care is sometimes not the stethoscope, the MRI machine or the syringe. It is the envelope that arrives later.

Inside may be a hospital bill, a separate physician bill, a laboratory bill the patient did not know was coming, and an explanation of benefits from an insurer that is not actually a bill but can look more frightening than one. The patient is then asked to do a job that would test a professional: identify the service, understand the negotiated adjustment, spot a coding error, wait for insurance, challenge a denial, ask whether charity care applies, decide whether a payment plan is safe, and tell a real collection letter from a premature or mistaken one.

Then comes the extra threat. If the balance is not paid, it may not merely remain a bill. It may become a mark in a credit file. That mark can follow a family into a car loan, a rental application, a mortgage inquiry or a job screening. It can turn an illness into a financial character reference.

That is the wrong sequence. Medical debt should be treated first as a billing, insurance and health-system problem. Only after the system has done its own work should the law even consider whether a patient’s unpaid balance is useful credit information. Congress should write a clear national rule that keeps most medical debt out of consumer credit reports, bars routine underwriting use of medical debt, and requires hospitals, collectors and furnishers to prove that a bill is accurate, final, legally collectible and screened for available assistance before any serious collection pressure begins.

This is not an argument that every medical bill should vanish. Nor is it an argument that lenders have no interest in a borrower’s real obligations. It is an argument about proportionality. A credit report is supposed to help predict whether a person will repay voluntary credit. A medical bill often records something else: an ambulance ride, a cancer scan, a childbirth complication, a deductible built into an insurance contract, an out-of-network bill, a delayed insurance payment or a charity-care application the patient was never helped to complete.

Those are not the same facts as walking away from a signed car loan.

The rule that was supposed to settle this did not survive

The country is now living in a legal zigzag. In January 2025, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau finalized a rule that would have removed medical bills from the credit reports used by lenders and barred lenders from using medical debt information in credit decisions. The bureau said the rule would remove about $49 billion in medical debt from the credit reports of roughly 15 million people.

That is not the law today. On July 11, 2025, the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Texas vacated the rule after the CFPB, under new leadership, joined industry plaintiffs in asking the court to set it aside. The court concluded that the bureau had exceeded its authority under the Fair Credit Reporting Act because federal law permits certain coded medical-debt information to be furnished and considered if it does not identify the provider or reveal the nature of the medical service. The CFPB’s own rule page now treats the materials as reference-only.

That reversal did not make the underlying problem go away. It shifted the fight. States including Colorado, New York, California, Washington and Oregon have adopted laws that try, in different forms, to keep medical debt from appearing on consumer reports or to stop providers and collectors from furnishing it. In October 2025, the CFPB issued an interpretive rule taking a broad view of federal preemption, arguing that the Fair Credit Reporting Act generally leaves states little room to regulate what categories of information may appear in consumer reports. The bureau also acknowledged that the interpretive rule itself does not have binding legal effect outside the federal government. Consumer advocates reject the bureau’s new reading. Industry groups have begun testing state restrictions in court, including a challenge to Colorado’s law.

The result is confusion where the law should be plain. A patient in California or New York may be told that medical debt cannot appear on a credit report. A collector or credit-reporting trade group may answer that federal law preempts the state rule. A consumer may never know which position will matter until a credit file is pulled.

Congress has been circling the broader credit-reporting question. On April 16, 2026, a House Financial Services subcommittee held a hearing on credit access, Fair Credit Reporting Act changes, alternative data and the CFPB complaint system. That is a useful room for the debate. But the question should not be whether every unpaid obligation is interesting to a lender. Of course it is. The question is whether medical debt is accurate, final, fair and predictive enough to serve as a gatekeeper to ordinary life.

A credit report is not a moral diary

A credit report is a constructed file. It is assembled from information supplied by lenders, collectors and other furnishers. It is used by creditors and, in many settings, by landlords, insurers and employers. The practical promise is accuracy and relevance: the file should help a decision-maker understand risk without smuggling in noise, error or punishment for events that say little about future repayment.

Medical debt strains that promise in three ways.

First, the patient often did not choose the transaction in any ordinary market sense. No one comparison-shops an emergency room from the back of an ambulance. Even scheduled care is wrapped in networks, facility fees, prior authorization, separate professional groups, opaque prices and insurer rules that a careful patient may not be able to see clearly before treatment.

Second, the amount owed may be unstable for months. An insurer can reprocess a claim. A provider can resubmit with a different code. A hospital may discover that the patient qualified for financial assistance. A surprise-billing protection may apply. A balance can move from terrifying to manageable, or from patient-responsible to not patient-responsible, only after calls, appeals and paperwork.

Third, medical debt is unusually close to private life. The Fair Credit Reporting Act has long contained special rules for medical information, including coding rules meant to avoid revealing the provider or the nature of the service. But the existence of medical collections can still say more than a balance. A large or repeated medical collection account may point toward disability, chronic illness, a family crisis, a mental-health episode, a complicated pregnancy or long-term treatment. The file may not name the diagnosis. It can still whisper.

That is why credit reporting is such a powerful collection tool. It is not merely a way to record information. It is leverage. A patient who disputes a bill may pay the wrong balance anyway to protect a mortgage application. An older adult may pay a confusing collection notice to stop the calls and avoid a credit hit. A parent may put groceries on a credit card so a hospital account does not stain a rental application.

That is not financial responsibility. It is pressure applied through a system the patient cannot easily inspect or control.

The evidence for restraint is practical, not sentimental

The case for keeping most medical debt out of credit files does not depend on pretending that debt is painless or irrelevant. It rests on the evidence that medical debt is a poor and messy signal.

In 2014, the CFPB studied millions of anonymized credit records and found that medical collections were less predictive of future repayment problems than nonmedical collections. The bureau’s later work described medical debt as common in credit files and unusually prone to confusion and dispute. In 2022, the CFPB estimated that medical collections tradelines appeared on 43 million credit reports, with medical bills making up a large share of collections in credit records at that time.

The private credit market has already acknowledged part of the problem. Equifax, Experian and TransUnion announced in 2022 and 2023 that they would remove paid medical collections, extend the waiting period before unpaid medical collections would appear, and remove medical collection debt under $500 from U.S. consumer credit reports. The CFPB later estimated that the under-$500 change would remove at least one medical collection from the credit reports of 22.8 million people and all medical collections from about 15.6 million people.

Scoring companies have moved, too. VantageScore said its 3.0 and 4.0 models would no longer use medical collection data in calculating scores. FICO says paid medical collections and medical collections under $500 are no longer reported by the national credit reporting agencies and therefore are not considered in any FICO score; newer FICO models also give unpaid medical collections over $500 less weight than older versions did.

Those changes do not solve the problem. They expose it. If credit bureaus and scoring companies themselves have decided that wide categories of medical debt are too noisy or too weakly predictive to treat like ordinary collections, Congress should not describe the remaining medical debt in credit files as a sacred pillar of safe lending.

The credit-file snapshot also understates the burden. KFF’s health-care debt survey found that 41 percent of U.S. adults had some form of debt due to medical or dental bills, including bills on payment plans, credit cards, loans or money borrowed from relatives. A narrower count of medical collections misses the many people who hide the bill somewhere else before it reaches a collector.

Recent research also shows that medical debt does not remain neatly inside the billing office. A 2026 JAMA Network Open study using 2023 to 2025 panel data examined medical debt and later housing instability. A 2024 JAMA Network Open study found county-level associations between medical debt and worse health status, premature death and mortality. These studies do not prove that a credit-reporting ban alone would cure illness, poverty or housing insecurity. They do show that medical debt belongs in the category of household shocks whose consequences spread well beyond a single invoice.

The best opposing argument deserves an honest answer

The strongest case against excluding medical debt from credit reports is not cruelty. It is a systems argument.

Lenders need information. If a borrower owes $18,000 to a hospital and may soon face lawsuits, garnishment, bankruptcy pressure or a payment plan, a lender can reasonably say that obligation affects ability to repay. A credit file that hides real liabilities can make risk appear smaller than it is. If lenders cannot price risk accurately, they may raise rates, tighten access to credit or use rougher proxies that are less transparent and perhaps more discriminatory.

Hospitals, physicians, ambulance providers and nursing homes also need to be paid. Collection agencies argue that credit reporting is one of the few tools that encourages some patients who can pay to respond. If reporting is barred entirely, they warn, some patients will move medical bills to the bottom of the pile. Providers may then collect less, raise prices, require more money upfront, sell debt more quickly or pull back on payment flexibility.

There is also a uniformity argument. Credit reporting is national. A borrower may move from Denver to Dallas to Buffalo. A lender may operate in every state. A patchwork in which some states ban medical debt reporting, others delay it and others permit it can make compliance harder and consumer reports less consistent. This is the industry’s strongest operational point, and it is one reason Congress should act rather than leaving consumers to agency reversals, state statutes and federal preemption lawsuits.

Finally, there is the boundary question: why medical debt and not rent debt, utility debt, student debt, funeral debt or grocery debt that ends up on a credit card? Bad luck can produce any of those debts. Once the credit system suppresses one category, critics ask, where does the exception stop?

These are real objections. They are also reasons to write a careful law, not reasons to keep treating every hospital balance like a defaulted consumer loan.

The medical bill travels a different road

The opposing argument becomes weaker when it treats all debts as if they arise in the same way.

A car loan begins with a price, an application, a rate, a payment schedule and a signed agreement. A medical bill may begin with a frightened person signing registration forms before a child’s surgery. A credit card balance is visible as it grows. A hospital balance may appear weeks later, after partial insurance payment, after a separate physician group bills, after an out-of-network contractor sends its own invoice, or after a charity-care program that should have been considered was never explained.

The law already recognizes that medical billing is different. The No Surprises Act protects many insured patients from certain unexpected out-of-network bills. The CFPB has warned collectors and furnishers that collecting or reporting debts covered by those protections may violate federal consumer-protection laws. Nonprofit hospitals, under section 501(r) of the Internal Revenue Code, must make reasonable efforts to determine whether a patient is eligible for financial assistance before engaging in extraordinary collection actions. Those actions include reporting adverse information to credit agencies, selling debt, filing lawsuits, placing liens and garnishing wages.

That principle should be extended and simplified. Before a medical bill can become a financial weapon, the system should have to prove it is valid, final and actually owed by the patient.

Right now, the burden too often runs the other way. The patient must request an itemized bill. The patient must know the difference between an insurer’s explanation of benefits and a real invoice. The patient must know that hospital financial assistance may exist. The patient must catch the coding error. The patient must dispute the collection. The patient must figure out whether a federal rule was vacated, whether a state law applies, whether the CFPB thinks that state law is preempted, whether the credit bureau’s voluntary policy covers the amount, and whether a scoring model will actually count it.

That is not consumer responsibility. That is administrative dodgeball.

Congress should build a national floor

A sensible federal law would begin with a simple rule: a debt can be collectible without being reportable. Providers and collectors could still send bills, negotiate, offer payment plans and, in limited cases, sue. But credit reporting should not be the first club out of the bag. It should be reserved, if used at all, for debts that have cleared a high evidentiary threshold.

First, Congress should bar medical debt from credit reports used for ordinary consumer credit, housing and employment decisions unless the debt is large, final, recent, undisputed and truly patient-responsible. Even that may be too generous to reporting. But at minimum, the law should categorically exclude paid medical debt, low-balance medical debt, debt under active dispute, debt that may be covered by insurance, debt subject to a pending appeal, debt that may qualify for charity care, debt barred by surprise-billing protections and debt that was recently incurred and still moving through normal billing channels.

Second, Congress should require proof before escalation. A hospital, collector or furnisher should have to document that insurance processing is complete; that the patient received a plain-language bill; that any required good-faith estimate, surprise-billing protection or financial-assistance policy was considered; that the patient had a meaningful chance to dispute the balance; and that the amount reported is the final patient-responsible amount, not a pre-adjustment charge or a balance likely to change.

Third, Congress should regulate medical credit cards and medical installment loans more carefully. A hospital bill should not lose protections simply because a patient is steered into a third-party financing product at a reception desk. Deferred-interest medical credit cards and health-care installment loans can turn a medical obligation into ordinary consumer credit, changing the legal protections and credit-reporting consequences. A national rule must follow the origin of the debt, not just the costume it wears after financing.

Fourth, Congress should require transparent reporting by hospitals and large health systems. Hospitals that sue patients, sell debt, garnish wages, place liens, refer accounts to collectors or report adverse information should publish how often they do it, how much debt is involved, how many accounts were screened for financial assistance, how many patients qualified, and how many collection actions were reversed after assistance or error correction. Policymakers cannot govern a collection system they cannot see.

Fifth, Congress should make enforcement practical. Patients need a clear dispute right, fast deletion of invalid medical debt, penalties for repeated furnishing of inaccurate or ineligible debt, and accountability for hospitals that outsource collection while pretending the collector’s conduct is someone else’s problem.

This would not be anti-lender. It would be pro-accuracy. Lenders deserve reliable information. A half-cooked hospital bill is not reliable information. It is noise with consequences.

Debt relief is not the same thing as billing reform

Medical-debt forgiveness programs can be humane. Cities, states and charities have purchased and forgiven old portfolios of debt, often for pennies on the dollar. For some families, a letter saying an old balance has been erased brings real relief. But debt relief is not a substitute for fixing the pipeline that creates bad debt in the first place.

The more important work is upstream. Make financial assistance automatic where income data already show eligibility. Standardize plain-language bills. Require hospitals to pause collections while insurance appeals or charity-care applications are pending. Give patients one accountable billing contact instead of sending them through a maze of departments, physician groups, labs and contractors. Ban the reporting of any medical debt that has not been validated against insurance records and assistance rules. Penalize repeat players who furnish invalid balances.

Medical debt is often described as if it were created by irresponsible patients. Sometimes patients do avoid bills they can pay. But often the debt is created by fragmented administration. The American health-care invoice can pass through a hospital, an insurer, a claims processor, a physician group, a lab, a pharmacy benefit manager, a debt collector, a credit bureau, a scoring model and a lender. When that machine misfires, the patient should not be the only person expected to repair it.

The compromise should not be the middle of the mess

The worst compromise would be vagueness. The current landscape is already vague: a vacated federal rule, voluntary bureau practices, state bans, a CFPB preemption interpretation, industry lawsuits, scoring-model changes and consumers trying to guess what applies to them. A family should not need a credit lawyer to know whether a colonoscopy bill can affect a mortgage application.

The better compromise is clear and asymmetric. Put the burden on the party that wants to convert medical debt into credit data. If a hospital, collector or reporting agency wants to tell the financial world that an unpaid medical bill predicts future default, it should first prove the bill is accurate, final, legally collectible, screened for assistance, outside surprise-billing protections, not under active dispute and genuinely relevant to credit risk.

Most medical debts will not clear that bar. That is not a defect in the rule. That is the point.

Some debts may clear it. Congress could create narrow exceptions for very large adjudicated obligations, or for cases where a court has already determined liability after due process and the remaining obligation is not protected by charity-care, insurance or surprise-billing rules. But those exceptions should be rare enough that ordinary patients stop treating the mailbox as a trapdoor under their credit scores.

There is a line worth holding: illness should not be laundered into financial suspicion. A credit report may tell a lender something about habits, obligations and repayment history. A medical bill tells a more complicated story. It may be the story of bad luck, bad coding, bad insurance design, bad hospital policy or a body doing what bodies do.

Before that story is used to deny someone a home, a loan or a second chance, the country should ask whether the credit system is measuring risk or merely recording pain.

The envelope will still arrive. The bill may still have to be resolved. But the bill should not get to bring a hammer until the system has first done its own work.
