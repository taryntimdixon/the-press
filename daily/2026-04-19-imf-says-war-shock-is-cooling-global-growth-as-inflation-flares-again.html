IMF says war shock is cooling global growth as inflation flares again

The fund’s April outlook cuts into the idea of a soft landing, warning that conflict-driven energy costs, tighter financial conditions and lingering tariff effects could keep prices elevated into next year.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 2:26 p.m.

The International Monetary Fund has sharpened its warning on the global economy, saying the conflict in the Middle East is now the main force clouding growth prospects just as the world had begun to settle into a fragile disinflation trend. In its April 2026 World Economic Outlook, released this week, the fund said global growth is projected at 3.1 percent in 2026 and 3.2 percent in 2027, while headline inflation is expected to rise modestly this year before easing again. The IMF framed the outlook as a test of how much resilience the world economy still has after a year of higher trade barriers, elevated uncertainty and now a fresh geopolitical shock. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

For economics policymakers, the report lands at a delicate moment. Central banks in major economies have spent two years trying to thread the needle between slowing inflation and avoiding a hard downturn. The IMF’s latest assessment suggests that effort is becoming harder, not easier, because the shock is hitting both sides of the ledger at once: energy prices and expectations are pushing inflation higher, while the hit to sentiment and financial conditions threatens demand. The fund said rising commodity prices, firmer inflation expectations and tighter financial conditions are already testing the recent resilience of the global economy. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

Energy prices are once again driving the macro story

The clearest economic transmission channel is oil and fuel. The BLS said the U.S. Producer Price Index rose sharply in March, with processed goods for intermediate demand up 2.6 percent and a big portion of that increase tied to an 11.3 percent jump in processed energy goods. Diesel fuel alone rose 42.0 percent in the month, while the index for stage 1 intermediate demand advanced 1.2 percent, its fastest monthly gain since February. Those producer-price moves matter because they often pass through to shipping, manufacturing and ultimately consumer prices if they persist. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/ppi_04142026.htm))

That is why the IMF is treating the current episode less like a short-lived geopolitical headline and more like a potentially durable inflation shock. The fund said global headline inflation is projected to rise modestly in 2026 before resuming its decline in 2027, a reminder that the path back to price stability may not be smooth. In its briefing, IMF officials also pointed to tariff reductions after a U.S. Supreme Court ruling on IEEPA tariffs as one reason China’s outlook improved at the margin, underscoring how trade policy remains intertwined with the inflation outlook. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

Consumers are already feeling the strain

The warning signs are showing up in the mood of households, even before the full effect of the latest shocks can be measured in official price data. Ipsos said its April 2026 Primary Consumer Sentiment Index for the United States fell to 50.0, down 3.4 points from the prior month, in a survey fielded from March 20 to March 25. While that is not a hard economic indicator like GDP or CPI, it is an important read on household willingness to spend, borrow and make big purchases. ([ipsos.com](https://www.ipsos.com/en-us/april-2026-lsegipsos-primary-consumer-sentiment-index))

That weaker sentiment is economically consequential because consumption is still the backbone of the U.S. and global economy. When households become more cautious, businesses tend to see softer sales, more conservative hiring plans and lower appetite for investment. The IMF did not say a recession is imminent; instead, it described a world economy that is still expanding but facing renewed tests that could gradually erode momentum. In practical terms, that means the soft-landing narrative is still alive, but it is under growing pressure from the combination of geopolitics, tariffs and sticky energy costs. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

What the IMF is really warning policymakers about

The deeper message in the fund’s outlook is that inflation is no longer just about domestic demand. Supply shocks are back. The IMF said the war in the Middle East could disrupt growth and disinflation, and it warned that defense-spending increases, while supportive in the short run, can also intensify inflationary pressures and crowd out other public priorities. That creates a difficult policy mix for governments already wrestling with high debt, fragile public finances and political pressure to cushion consumers. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

For central banks, the implication is more nuanced than a simple call to cut or hold rates. The IMF’s March and April messages suggest that inflation may be re-accelerating for reasons monetary policy cannot fully control, yet weaker growth and softer confidence argue against keeping rates restrictive for too long. In the United States, IMF officials said unemployment is expected to decline and inflation to reach the Federal Reserve’s 2 percent target by 2027, while also suggesting the Fed could lower rates somewhat further but should avoid deeper cuts unless the labor market weakens materially. That advice captures the central dilemma of 2026 economics: a shock that is not severe enough to cause collapse, but serious enough to keep policymakers on edge. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a24bc87a8fc1447e84fa1e6366202fd8))

Markets, meanwhile, have learned to treat each new energy-price jump as potentially broader than it first appears. Fuel costs can hit transportation, food distribution and industrial inputs almost immediately, and the March PPI release showed pressure radiating through multiple stages of production rather than one narrow category. If those upstream increases persist, consumers could see more of them in coming inflation reports. If they fade, the current episode may prove to be a temporary shock. The IMF’s view, for now, is that the world economy has enough momentum to avoid a downturn — but not enough to absorb repeated hits without consequence. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/ppi_04142026.htm))

That is why this week’s message from Washington matters beyond the usual spring forecast cycle. The global economy is still growing, but it is doing so under the shadow of war, with inflation risks turning up again just when policymakers hoped they were finally turning down. The next few months will show whether this is a brief detour or the start of a more durable shift in the world’s economic baseline. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))
