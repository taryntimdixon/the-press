Mozambique’s Outdoor Classes Ask What Access Really Counts

Samaria Tovela’s reported 10,500 outdoor classes are a local repair list with a global lesson: school access is not a doorway alone. It is a roof, a teacher, a meal, a usable language, a safe route, and a count that notices when attendance starts to slip.

Written and Researched by AI • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

Some education statistics behave themselves on the page. They line up in columns, travel neatly through ministries and aid reports, and make it possible to say that a country has enrolled more children than before. Then the rain comes, and the statistic has to sit under a tree.

That is the uncomfortable power of the number Mozambique’s education and culture minister, Samaria dos Anjos Tovela, put back into public view in April. Local and regional reports quoted her saying that about 10,500 classes were still being held outdoors, with Cabo Delgado, Nampula, Zambézia and Maputo among the most strained provinces. A few days earlier, O País reported that roughly 40 days after the 2026 school year began, some schools still had not resumed classes because of flooding, including at least 16 schools in Gaza Province affecting about 7,000 students and 180 teachers. In late February, UNICEF said floods had damaged 299 schools and 844 classrooms and affected 233,557 students and 12,632 teachers; Save the Children put the education disruption at more than 230,000 children after severe flooding damaged schools, homes and roads.

Those numbers belong first to Mozambique. They belong to children trying to copy a lesson while rain, heat, hunger, noise, insecurity or a missing desk competes with the teacher. They belong to parents deciding whether a child should walk to school, fetch water, help in a field, care for a sibling or stay home because the classroom is not really a classroom that day. They belong to officials trying to stretch tents, repair funds, food stocks and teacher assignments across a country where conflict, climate shocks and poverty keep arriving in the same ledger.

But they also belong to a larger argument about education. UNESCO’s 2026 Global Education Monitoring Report, launched in Paris on March 25, says 273 million children, adolescents and youth were out of school in 2024. The same report says primary and secondary enrollment has expanded massively since 2000, especially in low-income countries and sub-Saharan Africa. Both facts can be true. That is the problem, and also the beginning of a better idea.

The idea is simple to say and difficult to govern by: access to school is not a single doorway. It is not only a law that permits enrollment, a name in a register, or a national enrollment rate that looks respectable in a spreadsheet. Real access is durable participation. It means a child can arrive, stay, learn, return tomorrow and keep returning when life becomes noisy. It is the roof and the road, the desk and the meal, the teacher who shows up, the lesson in a language the child can understand, the bathroom that helps keep an adolescent girl from disappearing, and the data system that notices absence before it becomes dropout.

That is a more demanding definition of access. It is also a more honest one.

The register is not the child

Mozambique is a sharp case because its data tell two different stories at once. UNESCO’s Mozambique case study for the 2026 report says administrative records from the Ministry of Education and Human Development show the primary-age out-of-school rate falling from about 45 percent in 2000 to nearly zero in 2022. Household surveys, however, put the primary-age out-of-school rate at about 25 percent in 2022. That is not a minor technical disagreement. It is the difference between a solved problem and a stubborn emergency.

There are understandable reasons for the gap. Administrative records often count children who enroll. They depend on age-by-grade reporting, and UNESCO notes that birth registration remains incomplete. They also depend on population projections, which can be thrown off by migration, displacement and census uncertainty. Most important, administrative records can include children who enrolled but later stopped attending. A household survey is not perfect either, but it can catch what a school register may miss: the child whose name remains in the system while the body is somewhere else.

This distinction matters because dropout is often not a cliff. It is a slope. UNESCO’s global report says children typically move through irregular attendance and partial disengagement before leaving school permanently. In human terms, that means a child is present, then present sometimes, then embarrassed about falling behind, then useful at home, then missing, then remembered by a teacher as someone who used to come.

A society that counts only the first moment and misses the slide has built a smoke alarm that rings after the house is gone.

That is why Tovela’s outdoor-classroom number matters. It does not let the public hide inside the comfort of enrollment. A class held outdoors is still a class. A child under a tree may still be officially enrolled. A teacher may be present, a lesson may happen, and everyone involved may be doing admirable work. But the scene also tells us that access is fragile. Rain can cancel it. Heat can shorten it. Noise can swallow it. A missing latrine, desk, textbook or meal can make the day feel optional, humiliating or impossible.

Open the gate, then build the school

The counterargument deserves respect. The great education victories of the past generation came from opening gates. Countries abolished fees, built schools, recruited teachers and told families that public education was not a privilege. In many places, this worked. UNESCO reports that since 2000, sub-Saharan Africa has more than doubled primary enrollment and more than tripled secondary enrollment; in low-income countries, secondary enrollment has almost quadrupled.

Those gains are not cosmetic. They represent children in classrooms who might otherwise have been working, married, displaced, idle or invisible. It would be glib to scold enrollment from countries where generations of public spending have made roofs, records and roads ordinary. A child cannot learn in a school she cannot enter.

But the point is not to downgrade enrollment. The point is to refuse to stop there.

Mozambique’s own reforms show why. UNESCO’s case study says a major push came in 2004, when the government abolished tuition and other school fees for primary education and began providing free textbooks through school grants known as Direct Support to Schools. The policy reduced direct costs and brought gains for poor and rural children. Yet construction did not keep pace with enrollment growth. UNESCO cites estimates that about 10,000 classes were being held outdoors under trees, and April reporting put the current figure at about 10,500.

This is the paradox of a successful access policy. More children come. That is the victory. Then classrooms swell, desks disappear, teachers are stretched and temporary arrangements become routine. If the next phase of policy does not build the conditions for staying and learning, the open gate leads to an overcrowded yard.

None of this means fee abolition failed. It means free schooling is a beginning, not a building.

The school day has a stomach

One of the least abstract ways to understand access is lunch.

In Mozambique, poverty and food insecurity are not background conditions. They are part of the school system whether the education ministry wants them or not. UNESCO’s case study says school meals have had a positive impact on preventing dropout, while noting that provision remains limited. The World Food Programme’s Mozambique page says it supported the government’s home-grown school feeding program with hot meals for more than 250,000 students in 340 schools in 2024. Jornal Notícias reported in 2025 that more than 657,000 students were receiving a hot snack through PRONAE, Mozambique’s national school feeding program, in 1,085 primary schools, about 8 percent of the national education network.

Food changes the household calculation. If a child is hungry, the blackboard competes with the belly. If a family is under pressure, a meal at school is not a soft benefit; it is an attendance policy. It tells a household that the child’s presence in class matters today, not only in some future labor market.

School meals are not magic. They can be underfunded, logistically difficult, nutritionally thin or vulnerable to donor cycles. World Vision reported that Mozambique’s education ministry was looking for longer partnerships to keep PRONAE moving after Russian funding ended in 2025, with the goal of expanding coverage from 8 percent to 15 percent by 2029 and 25 percent by 2034. AIM reported that Finland contributed 500,000 euros in 2025 to help WFP provide daily hot meals to more than 56,000 students for three months in primary schools along the Nacala Corridor in Nampula. Those are useful commitments, but they also show how much of the system still depends on stitching together partners and time-limited money.

Globally, the World Food Programme says at least 466 million children received school meals in 2024, nearly 80 million more than in 2020. That is hopeful. It is also clarifying. If food is what keeps a child in class, cutting the meal is not merely a nutrition decision. It is a school attendance decision written in reverse.

Language is infrastructure, too

Concrete is not the only material a school needs. Language is infrastructure.

Mozambique is multilingual, while Portuguese is the official language of schooling. UNESCO’s case study says fewer than 17 percent of people speak Portuguese as a mother tongue, and only about 5 percent in rural areas. The government began implementing bilingual education in 2003, officially recognized it in 2018 and set out a 2020-2029 expansion strategy. UNESCO says the share of primary students enrolled in bilingual education rose from about 1 percent in 2015 to 19 percent in 2024.

That matters because a child can be physically present and intellectually locked outside. A first grader who does not understand the language of instruction is not receiving the same education as a child who does. Teachers often improvise, translating, code-switching and coaxing meaning across the gap. Improvisation is a valuable human skill. It is not a national language policy.

Bilingual education is sometimes treated as cultural accommodation, a nice extra if budgets allow. It should be treated more like a bridge. If the early grades are where reading, number sense and confidence are built, then teaching children in a language they understand is not sentimental decoration. It is load-bearing.

The global version of the same problem is large. The World Bank, UNICEF and partners have used the term learning poverty to describe children who cannot read and understand a simple text by age 10. The phrase is harsh, but the idea is useful: a child may have been in school for years and still lack the basic reading skills that make the rest of schooling possible. In 2025, the World Bank highlighted evidence from low- and middle-income countries showing that many children are not acquiring foundational literacy skills despite being in school. Presence is necessary. It is not enough.

Crisis makes averages too neat

National education data can smooth the events that decide whether a child goes to school. Mozambique’s recent experience is a case in point: floods in the south and center, conflict and displacement in the north, damaged classrooms, families moving, schools serving as temporary accommodation, teachers trying to keep routines alive in places where routine keeps breaking.

Human Rights Watch’s 2026 Mozambique chapter describes a 2025 human rights situation marked by intensifying conflict in Cabo Delgado, food insecurity and displacement. UNICEF’s 2026 Mozambique appeal says nearly 1.8 million people, including 1 million children, require life-saving assistance in 2026, and that conflict, climate shocks and public health emergencies are compounding needs. UNICEF also says school closures due to attacks prevented 50,000 children from going to school, and that three consecutive cyclones between late 2024 and March 2025 damaged more than 4,800 classrooms.

Then came the 2026 floods. UNICEF’s February 25 flash update said more than 724,000 people had been affected across Gaza, Maputo, Sofala and other provinces, with children making up more than half of those affected. UNICEF’s February 10 update said the start of the school year had already been delayed by one month and could be further compromised because many classrooms remained occupied as accommodation centers or inaccessible because of flooding. O País’s April report suggests that the calendar did not simply snap back into place.

This is why the global headline number, 273 million out of school, is necessary but insufficient. It shows scale. It does not show churn. A child may be out for three weeks after a flood, back for a month, out again when a road is washed out, back when a temporary learning space opens, gone again when a family moves. In crisis settings, the line between in school and out of school can flicker.

UNESCO’s 2026 report says official projections may undercount out-of-school populations in conflict-affected countries by about 13 million. UNESCO’s education-in-emergencies work makes the same practical point: fragmented data can misidentify vulnerable groups, misdirect scarce funding and weaken decisions. Bad counts do not merely annoy statisticians. They steer money. They decide where classrooms are rebuilt, where teachers are posted, where school meals go, where temporary learning spaces are set up, and which children are treated as an unfortunate exception rather than the center of the problem.

The right to education is ordinary on purpose

There is a familiar temptation in stories like this: praise the heroic child learning despite everything. There is truth in that story. Children do learn under trees. Teachers do teach in heat, dust and noise. Communities do make schools out of churches, tents, borrowed rooms and shade.

But admiration can become a trap. If outsiders praise resilience too much, they may forget that resilience is often what children are forced to use when adults have not provided ordinary things.

The right to education is not meant to be a test of a child’s toughness. It is meant to make childhood less dependent on luck. That is why desks matter. That is why roofs matter. That is why safe routes, bathrooms, birth certificates, school meals, pregnancy policies, child-marriage enforcement and teacher attendance matter. They are not side issues. They are the boring machinery of dignity.

Mozambique’s girls show this sharply. UNESCO’s case study notes very high child-marriage rates and says a 2019 study found that 70 percent of pregnant girls, many still in primary school because of late entry, left school. The government criminalized child marriage in 2019 and revoked a 2003 ministerial order that had required pregnant girls to transfer to night school. Those legal changes matter. But law on paper is not the same as a girl feeling safe, welcome and able to continue. A school that admits her legally but pushes her out socially has split the right in two.

The same is true for children with disabilities, displaced children, rural children, children who work, children who repeat grades and become much older than classmates, and children who enter late because birth registration, distance or household poverty made the official schedule unrealistic. These children do not need systems designed for the imaginary average child. They need systems designed for actual children.

What should change in the count

The practical answer is not to throw away enrollment data. Governments need to know how many children enter school, how many classrooms exist, how many teachers are assigned and whether legal barriers have fallen. The first count still matters.

But it should be paired with a second count: the conditions of staying.

Every education ministry and donor should ask a small set of stubborn questions alongside enrollment. How many enrolled children attend regularly? How many classes are outdoors? How many schools have usable latrines, safe water and enough desks? How many children receive meals? How many teachers are absent or covering multiple shifts? Which grades have the highest repetition? Which children vanish between primary and lower secondary? Which language is used in the first years of instruction, and which language does the child speak at home? Which schools close during floods, conflict or heat, and how long does reopening take?

These are not luxury indicators. They are access indicators.

UNESCO’s new Equitable Financing Index, introduced in the 2026 report, is one attempt to ask whether public money is deliberately geared toward disadvantaged learners. The index does not prove that programs work, and UNESCO is clear that it measures policy intent and financial effort, not actual equity outcomes or efficiency. That limitation matters. A country can intend equity and still fail to deliver it. But intent is not nothing. It helps reveal whether budgets follow the children furthest from the classroom door.

The same spirit should guide crisis planning. If floods repeatedly damage schools, rebuilding the same fragile classroom in the same fragile way is not thrift. It is delayed waste. If conflict displaces children into host-community schools, that crowding is part of the emergency. If school meals keep attendance stable, then meal funding belongs in education planning, not only in humanitarian appeals. If a school register says a child is present while a household survey says otherwise, the next question should not be which spreadsheet wins. The next question should be where the child is.

The tree is a policy memo

Sustainable Development Goal 4, the global education promise, calls for inclusive and equitable quality education. The phrase is so large that it can float away. Mozambique pulls it back to earth.

Inclusive means the child under the tree is not treated as enrolled enough. Equitable means the child in Cabo Delgado, Nampula, Zambézia, Gaza, Maputo or a flooded district is not averaged into invisibility by better conditions elsewhere. Quality means that being present should lead to learning. Education means more than survival in a school-like place.

The world will probably miss the 2030 education targets. UNESCO’s 2026 report is blunt about the distance: at historical rates, universal upper-secondary completion remains far beyond the deadline. Aid is under pressure. Governments face debt, demographic growth, climate damage and competing emergencies. Anyone promising a quick fix is selling a poster, not a plan.

Still, fatalism is too easy. The record also shows progress. Countries have expanded enrollment at scales that would have looked implausible a few decades ago. School meal programs are growing. Bilingual education, targeted financing, fee abolition, social protection and emergency learning spaces all offer pieces of a better architecture. The World Bank’s INTEGRA project in Mozambique, approved in 2025, is notable because it treats schools as hubs for learning, meals, basic health care and social protection in vulnerable districts. That does not solve the problem. It at least points in the right direction: toward the whole child, not only the registered pupil.

The idea worth defending is modest and radical at the same time: count school the way children experience it.

A child does not experience a gross enrollment ratio. A child experiences a morning walk, a classroom, a teacher’s mood, an empty stomach or a full one, a language, a friend, a storm cloud, a desk, a notebook, a period, a pregnancy, a checkpoint, a flood, a harvest, a parent’s worry, a government’s budget and a minister’s repair list. The statistic should not pretend those things are separate from access. They are access.

So when Mozambique says thousands of classes are outdoors, the right response is not pity from afar or a shrug about poor-country conditions. It is to take the tree seriously as evidence. It is a classroom, yes. It is also a policy memo with leaves.

The children under it are already doing their part. The adults should count better, fund smarter and build as if the promise of school begins not when a child’s name enters a register, but when that child can sit, listen, learn and come back tomorrow.
