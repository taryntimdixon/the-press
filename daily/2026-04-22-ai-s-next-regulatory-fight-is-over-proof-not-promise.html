AI’s next regulatory fight is over proof, not promise

A wave of new rules, lawsuits, and standards is forcing companies to show how their models are trained, tested, secured, and governed — while the battle over whether Washington or the states set the terms intensifies.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 3:16 p.m.

Artificial intelligence has entered a new phase of governance in the United States. The argument is no longer just about whether companies can build faster, larger, or more capable models. It is increasingly about proof: what data went into a model, what safeguards were in place, who is accountable when a system fails, and whether federal or state authorities get the final say. In the spring of 2026, those questions are colliding in courtrooms, statehouses, agency dockets, and corporate policy teams at the same time. The result is a regulatory picture that is more fragmented than the industry wants, more active than critics of oversight expected, and more consequential than the usual AI-policy headlines suggest. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

The broadest signal came from Washington on March 20, 2026, when the White House released a national AI legislative framework urging Congress to create a uniform federal policy and warning that a patchwork of state laws could impede innovation and U.S. competitiveness. The framework explicitly discusses preventing censorship, protecting free speech, and building an AI-ready workforce. It also says Congress should consider preempting state AI laws that impose undue burdens. That is a clear statement of federal ambition, but it is not the same as a new law, and it does not erase the state rules already in force. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

That tension matters because AI is becoming embedded in more of the economy at precisely the moment its physical and legal footprint is expanding. On the infrastructure side, the race to build data centers and secure electricity has become a core AI story rather than a side effect. In March, the Department of Energy announced a public-private partnership to develop a major data center with its own power supply at the former Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant in southern Ohio, a project expected to include a 10-gigawatt data center and up to 10 gigawatts of new power generation. In January, Meta announced nuclear-power agreements supporting its Prometheus data center in Ohio. These projects are not just about computing power; they are about who pays for the grid, how quickly new generation comes online, and whether AI expansion can be squared with industrial, environmental, and community concerns. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the policy debate has moved from abstract guardrails to operational mechanics. NIST launched its AI Agent Standards Initiative in February 2026 and has continued work on AI evaluation, secure deployment, and identity-and-authorization concepts for agentic systems. Those documents show a significant shift in posture: rather than treating AI governance as a broad ethics discussion, the federal standards apparatus is now trying to define how agents identify themselves, how they get authorization, and how to measure whether systems stay safe after deployment. That is a much more technical, and potentially more durable, way of regulating AI than speeches about “responsible innovation.” ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/node/1906621?utm_source=openai))

California is moving in parallel and sometimes in opposition. Governor Gavin Newsom signed SB 53, the Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence Act, on September 29, 2025. The law requires large frontier developers to publish a framework describing how they incorporate national standards, international standards, and industry best practices. It also creates a reporting channel for catastrophic-risk concerns and authorizes civil penalties for noncompliance. California also has in force AB 2013, which requires developers of generative AI systems made available in California to publish high-level information about the data used to train those systems, including broad descriptions of data sources and whether personal or copyrighted material is involved. The practical effect is to force companies to put more of their model-development process on the record. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))

The legal fight around California’s training-data law is already underway. In March 2026, a federal court declined to block enforcement of AB 2013 in a challenge brought by xAI, signaling that disclosure mandates aimed at generative-AI developers may survive at least some constitutional attack. The argument is not trivial. AI companies say forced disclosure can expose trade secrets and impose burdens on speech and innovation. Supporters say the public has a legitimate interest in knowing whether a model was trained on medical records, personal data, copyrighted works, or other sensitive material. The court’s refusal to halt the law at the preliminary-injunction stage does not resolve the constitutional questions, but it does suggest that a transparency regime built around consumer information, rather than direct control over model outputs, has a better chance of surviving judicial review. ([law-ai.org](https://law-ai.org/focus-area/misspecification/?utm_source=openai))

That point is central to understanding the current moment. A lot of the public discussion around AI still treats “regulation” as a binary choice between freezing innovation and doing nothing. The actual pattern is more specific. Regulators are increasingly trying to regulate process, not creativity: disclosure, documentation, testing, logging, incident reporting, data governance, procurement standards, and liability rules. That approach is visible in California’s laws, in NIST’s standards work, in federal procurement and workforce guidance, and in FTC enforcement and research. It is also visible in the private sector, where companies are writing their own safety frameworks, publishing selective evaluations, and trying to convince customers and lawmakers that they can police themselves before the government forces a more explicit regime. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/ai/ai-inventory?utm_source=openai))

A federal push for preemption meets a state-by-state reality

When the White House urged Congress to take a national approach to AI in March, it was responding to a market and a political problem. The market problem is that companies dislike operating under a growing patchwork of state rules. The political problem is that state lawmakers are under pressure from constituents worried about fraud, deepfakes, schoolwork, employment discrimination, privacy, and the use of AI in sensitive decisions. The administration’s framework is explicit that a uniform national policy would better serve innovation and competitiveness. But even if Congress adopts some version of that agenda, it will still face the question of what to do with existing state rules already on the books. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

California is not alone. Colorado’s AI Act remains in the background as a broad, risk-based state model. Utah, Texas, and other states have also moved on AI-related measures. The significance is not just that states are acting; it is that the most economically influential states are treating AI as an ordinary consumer, labor, civil-rights, privacy, and product-safety issue rather than waiting for Congress to settle the field. That increases compliance costs, but it also gives regulators leverage. Companies building products for national deployment cannot easily create one model for California and another for the rest of the country, at least not when the product is the same platform available online everywhere. ([oag.ca.gov](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-bipartisan-coalition-urge-congress-stand-states%E2%80%99?utm_source=openai))

The federal government’s own behavior underscores the same trend. DOJ’s AI inventory shows that agencies are being required to catalog AI use cases and post releasable entries publicly. The Department of Labor published an AI literacy framework in February 2026, and the Department of Education issued AI guidance for schools in July 2025 and maintains a current guidance hub updated in February 2026. The EEOC’s budget materials describe an AI governance board, an AI compliance plan, and additional AI initiatives. In other words, AI is not being treated as a separate domain outside law; it is being folded into existing regulatory categories. Instead of asking whether AI needs a single new law, agencies are asking which existing legal duties already apply to AI systems and where additional documentation or technical controls are needed. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/ai/ai-inventory?utm_source=openai))

Courts are shaping the rules faster than Congress

The courts have become one of the most important AI-policy venues because so many of the most contested issues are already framed as disputes under copyright, privacy, consumer-protection, employment, contract, and trade-secret law. The most notable example remains the copyright fight over training data. In June 2025, a federal judge ruled that some training on copyrighted books could qualify as fair use, while still allowing claims to proceed over allegedly pirated copies used in the training pipeline. In September 2025, a judge approved a $1.5 billion settlement in the Anthropic authors’ case, though later reporting showed continued judicial scrutiny over the terms. Those developments gave both sides something to claim victory over and made clear that the legality of model training and the legality of acquiring training material are not the same question. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1e5cece51c2e4bd0bb21d94de2abb035?utm_source=openai))

Those copyright fights matter not only for media companies and model developers, but also for the future of licensing markets. If courts continue to say that training itself is often transformative fair use while punishing unlawful acquisition methods, the pressure may shift from existential copyright litigation toward a more transactional market in which publishers, record labels, news organizations, stock-photo libraries, and software firms negotiate explicit access rights. That could create a more stable revenue stream for rights-holders, but it could also lock smaller developers out of high-quality data if licensing costs become a barrier to entry. That tension — between orderly compensation and market concentration — is one of the most underappreciated parts of the AI policy debate. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1e5cece51c2e4bd0bb21d94de2abb035?utm_source=openai))

Another courtroom trend is that AI is now entering legal discovery, privilege, and professional-responsibility disputes. Reuters reported in April that lawyers are warning clients not to treat AI chatbots like confidential advisors because chats may be discoverable or otherwise create litigation risk. The broader lesson is obvious: AI assistants are not an invisible layer of thought. They create records, and records can be subpoenaed. That should affect how businesses, employees, and professionals use them. ([oag.ca.gov](https://oag.ca.gov/sb53?utm_source=openai))

This is one reason the policy debate is widening from model outputs to institutional records. Companies are learning that AI governance is not only about whether a chatbot says something harmful. It is about whether there is a retrievable log of training data summaries, safety frameworks, risk assessments, procurement decisions, access controls, and escalation paths. The more AI becomes integrated into core business processes, the more it becomes legible to regulators and litigants — and the less plausible it is to treat model behavior as some purely technical black box outside normal law. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))

Why frontier-model transparency is becoming the center of gravity

California’s SB 53 and AB 2013, NIST’s standards work, and the FTC’s research all point to a common governance instinct: require companies to reveal enough about how AI systems are made and managed that outsiders can assess risk. That may sound modest, but it is politically potent. The central innovation of these laws is that they do not rely entirely on regulators understanding every line of code or every parameter of a model. They instead seek standardized disclosures about data, risk-management practices, incident response, and post-deployment monitoring. This is closer to financial reporting than to command-and-control engineering regulation. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))

That approach has supporters across a surprisingly broad spectrum. Pro-transparency policymakers say the public cannot rely on marketing claims about AI safety if it has no independent way to inspect the basis for those claims. AI companies that favor transparency argue that predictable disclosure rules are easier to live with than ad hoc investigations or sweeping bans. Even some skeptics of heavy regulation prefer documentation and auditing to broad restrictions on model release. The attraction is that transparency promises accountability without freezing experimentation. The risk is that it can become a box-checking exercise if no one has the capacity or will to read the boxes. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a more uncomfortable possibility: transparency rules may end up advantaging the largest companies. Big firms can absorb the compliance cost of reporting, risk reviews, and dedicated legal teams far more easily than startups can. They can also use their size to shape the standards themselves. That concern is not theoretical. The FTC’s 6(b) report on cloud-provider and AI-developer partnerships described how major platform partnerships can affect access to compute, engineering talent, switching costs, and sensitive technical information. Those same dynamics mean that a regulatory regime built around reporting and standards can unintentionally reinforce the incumbents best able to navigate it. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/ftc-staff-report-ai-partnerships-investments-6b-study?utm_source=openai))

Still, a regime that is mainly about process and evidence may be the only politically sustainable path right now. Aggressive model restrictions risk being swept aside in federal court or preempted by Congress. A pure self-regulatory approach has already struggled to persuade critics who see AI firms moving fast and revising safety commitments whenever commercial pressure rises. Transparency, incident reporting, and third-party standards are less glamorous than grand AI bills, but they may be more durable because they are easier to attach to existing legal systems. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

The labor story is shifting from job loss to job redesign

AI policy is often narrated as a battle between automation and employment. The actual labor picture in 2026 is more complicated. The Department of Labor’s February initiative to expand AI literacy through a formal framework suggests a government view that AI will reshape jobs as much as replace them. The Department of Education has likewise emphasized AI in schools, grants, and professional development rather than treating it as a marginal add-on. And the EEOC’s budget materials show an agency trying to update its AI compliance plan while using AI internally for tasks such as casework support and customer service. ([dol.gov](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/eta/eta20260213?utm_source=openai))

That does not mean displacement is not real. It means the near-term policy challenge is less about a single wave of job destruction and more about uneven adoption. Some workers will use AI to become faster, more productive, and more capable. Others will have their tasks decomposed, monitored, or benchmarked more aggressively. The most visible labor risk may not be a mass layoff event, but a long squeeze on entry-level work, white-collar training, and the apprenticeship ladder itself. If AI systems draft legal memos, summarize reports, generate code, analyze resumes, or create lesson plans, institutions must decide which human tasks remain essential for skill-building and which can be automated without hollowing out the pipeline. The policy response has to account for that distinction. ([dol.gov](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/eta/eta20260213?utm_source=openai))

That is one reason the workforce angle is so important. If government is serious about AI diffusion, then labor policy cannot just be about retraining slogans. It needs curriculum standards, apprenticeships, wage progression, supervisor training, and procurement rules that reward safe use rather than raw adoption. The Department of Labor’s AI Literacy Framework is an early sign of that shift, but it is only a beginning. A workforce that can use AI responsibly is also a workforce that can notice when AI is unsafe, wrong, or biased. That makes labor policy part of safety policy whether regulators say so or not. ([dol.gov](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/eta/eta20260213?utm_source=openai))

Safety is getting narrower, but also more operational

One of the most notable changes in 2026 is that AI safety is becoming more concrete. The era of broad ethical principles is not over, but the policy action has moved toward specific threat models: cyber abuse, companion-chatbot harm, model theft, deceptive outputs, unauthorized agent behavior, and catastrophic-risk reporting. NIST’s AI Agent Standards Initiative and related concept paper are about identity, authorization, and monitoring. Anthropic’s responsible-scaling materials point to structured safety thresholds and red-team-style assessments. OpenAI’s transparency and evaluations pages show a similar emphasis on recurring reporting. These are not abstract promises; they are attempts to make safety measurable. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/node/1906621?utm_source=openai))

But the tighter focus on measurable safety also reveals what remains unresolved. There is still no universally accepted test for catastrophic AI risk, no agreed benchmark for model honesty in open-ended settings, and no single standard for evaluating post-deployment behavior as models are updated or connected to other tools. NIST’s own work acknowledges the challenge of monitoring deployed systems. That means a system can look robust at launch and still drift in the wild. The governance problem is not only what the model does in a lab, but what it does once users, plugins, workflows, and external systems start pushing it into new territory. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/caisi/ai-agent-standards-initiative?utm_source=openai))

That is especially important for AI agents, which federal standards teams and companies are increasingly treating as a separate category. Agents can take actions, not just generate text. They can manage inboxes, write code, shop, call APIs, and interact with systems that have real-world effects. That raises old security questions in a new setting: identity, authorization, auditing, least privilege, and revocation. NIST’s work on agent identity and authorization suggests the next governance fight may be less about what an AI can say and more about what a software agent can do once it is authenticated. That is a subtle but significant shift. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/node/1906621?utm_source=openai))

The business model is becoming the political model

The AI economy is no longer just a story about model quality. It is a story about distribution, compute, cloud partnerships, licensing, and procurement. The FTC’s 6(b) study on large AI partnerships and investments is one of the clearest official acknowledgments that the industry’s structure matters to competition and consumer welfare. If a few cloud providers, chip suppliers, and model developers control the key inputs, then policy questions about interoperability and access become as important as questions about chatbot performance. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/ftc-staff-report-ai-partnerships-investments-6b-study?utm_source=openai))

That is where the political economy of AI gets tricky. Regulators want transparency and safety. Companies want scale and speed. Cloud providers want long-term lock-in. Developers want access to compute and distribution. States want local control and consumer protections. Workers want training and wage security. Schools want useful tools without cheating, privacy breaches, or academic collapse. Each of these constituencies is using the language of AI innovation, but they are not asking for the same thing. The policy framework that emerges will likely be a compromise among all of them — and compromise is usually messy, incremental, and full of loopholes. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

That may be frustrating to anyone hoping for a single coherent national AI law in 2026. But it may also be the only realistic way to govern a technology that now touches copyright, competition, education, labor, privacy, civil rights, energy, and national security at once. The deeper story is not that one of these arenas will win. It is that AI is forcing all of them to operate together. A training-data disclosure rule can become a privacy rule. A data-center deal can become an energy policy. A chatbot privilege case can become a legal ethics issue. A standards document can become de facto federal policy. The boundaries are dissolving, and the institutions are scrambling to catch up. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

The next phase of the AI story will likely hinge on three tests. First, whether Congress takes up the White House’s call for a national framework and, if so, whether it preempts state laws or merely overlays them. Second, whether courts continue to uphold disclosure and transparency rules like California’s while narrowing more aggressive claims about model governance. Third, whether the infrastructure buildout — data centers, power plants, grid upgrades, and hardware supply — keeps pace with the industry’s ambitions without triggering a political backlash over costs, land use, emissions, or public subsidies. Each of those tests could reshape the industry’s trajectory. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

For now, the most important thing to understand is that the center of gravity has shifted. The question is no longer whether AI deserves oversight. The question is what kind of oversight can survive economics, politics, and the courts. The answer, at least in the spring of 2026, appears to be a regime built less on promises than on records, disclosures, evaluations, and enforceable duties. That may not settle the big philosophical disputes about machine intelligence. But it may decide who gets to build, sell, deploy, and contest the next generation of AI systems. And that is the fight underway now. ([gov.ca.gov](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2025/09/29/governor-newsom-signs-sb-53-advancing-californias-world-leading-artificial-intelligence-industry/?utm_source=openai))
