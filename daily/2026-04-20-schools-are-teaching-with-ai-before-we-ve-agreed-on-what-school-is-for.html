Philosophy

Schools Are Teaching with AI Before We’ve Agreed on What School Is For

Generative AI has moved from novelty to infrastructure in classrooms, but the deeper crisis is philosophical: if schools can no longer tell the difference between assistance and authorship, they must decide whether education is about performance, understanding, or becoming a certain kind of person.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 20, 2026 • 4:20 p.m. • 3430 words

By 2026, the question is no longer whether students use generative AI. They do. The question is what schools are trying to preserve when they allow it. That sounds like a practical policy dispute, but it is really a philosophical one: education has always embodied a theory of the human being. It assumes that reading, writing, problem-solving, and revision are not just output mechanisms, but exercises in becoming capable, patient, discerning, and independent. Generative AI threatens to flatten that moral architecture by making it easier than ever to separate the appearance of learning from the labor of learning.

This is why the current AI-in-school debate is so much bigger than cheating. Cheating is the surface symptom. The deeper issue is whether schools still believe that education should make students do difficult intellectual work because the work itself changes them. UNESCO’s guidance on generative AI in education explicitly frames the issue in human-centered terms, emphasizing ethics, safety, equity, and meaningful use rather than mere acceleration of productivity. UNESCO’s 2025 survey of higher-education institutions found that 19 percent already had a formal AI policy and another 42 percent were developing one, a sign that institutions are now trying to catch up with practices students and staff have already adopted. The OECD’s 2026 Digital Education Outlook likewise says GenAI can support learning when guided by clear teaching principles. Those two positions are not contradictory. They are an index of the real dilemma: AI can help students learn, but it can also help institutions stop noticing when learning has disappeared. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

The strongest case for embracing generative AI in education is straightforward. Good teachers have always used tools that extend human capacity: calculators, spellcheck, search engines, grammar aids, tutoring software, and adaptive learning platforms. Seen in that lineage, chatbots are not an alien intrusion but a more conversational form of support. UNESCO’s AI-for-skills programming in higher education treats AI as a training and capacity issue, not only a compliance issue, and the OECD argues that generative AI can support learning when teaching practices are explicit about goals and constraints. In some settings, evidence suggests students do benefit. The educational literature and institutional guidance now consistently describe AI as useful for feedback, brainstorming, translation, and early-stage drafting, particularly when learners already have enough background knowledge to judge the tool’s output. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/node/217106?utm_source=openai))

The philosophical temptation here is to say: if a tool improves performance, why resist it? But that argument quietly changes the definition of education. It assumes that the point of school is mainly to produce better products with less friction. On that view, a student’s paper matters as a deliverable, not as a record of thought. A math solution matters as an answer, not as a demonstration of reasoning. A discussion post matters as content, not as evidence of a mind at work. Once that shift happens, schools become credential factories for assisted cognition: the system rewards polished outputs while the student’s internal development becomes secondary, invisible, or optional.

That is not a fantasy. It is already visible in policy and practice. UNESCO’s 2025 survey of higher education institutions found broad movement toward formal AI guidance, with regional variation, and the organization’s 2023 global survey of schools and universities found that fewer than 10 percent had formal guidance at all. In the United States, state governments have started pushing schools toward concrete policies. Ohio law now requires the state education department to produce a model policy on AI in schools by December 31, 2025, and requires each district, community school, and STEM school to adopt its own policy by July 1, 2026. At the federal level, the U.S. Department of Education issued guidance in July 2025 on how federal education funds may support responsible AI use, while the White House in March 2026 urged Congress to take a relatively light-touch approach to AI regulation and to preempt some state laws. Education is therefore caught in a larger policy conflict between centralized permissiveness and local caution. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/unesco-survey-two-thirds-higher-education-institutions-have-or-are-developing-guidance-ai-use?hub=343&utm_source=openai))

The old promise of schooling was never efficiency

To understand why AI feels philosophically disruptive, it helps to remember that modern schooling has never been only about transferring information. The best traditions of education, from liberal learning to civic education to progressive pedagogy, treat effort as formative. Students are meant to wrestle with language, sources, and ideas because the struggle builds judgment. Reading difficult texts teaches more than the text’s content; it teaches attention, tolerance for ambiguity, and the discipline of reconstructing meaning. Writing teaches more than grammar; it teaches self-knowledge, sequencing, and the capacity to think under constraint. Assessment, at its best, is not merely a sorting mechanism but a public account of whether that transformation occurred.

Generative AI complicates this because it collapses the visible distance between needing help and doing the work oneself. If a student can ask a model to summarize a chapter, draft an essay outline, generate code, or propose a counterargument, the intermediate steps that once revealed partial understanding can vanish. The student may still be learning, of course. But the institution can no longer easily tell whether the student has internalized the process or merely outsourced it. That matters because education is a social practice, not a private feeling. Schools certify competence to others. They also model norms: honesty, patience, and the willingness to improve through effort. When those norms erode, the loss is not just administrative. It is ethical. UNESCO’s guidance on generative AI explicitly recommends human-centered, age-appropriate, and privacy-conscious approaches precisely because educational tools shape the relationship between learner, teacher, and institution, not just the speed of task completion. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

This is where some advocates of AI in education make a subtle philosophical error. They claim that since AI will be ubiquitous in work, schools should mirror real-world use and teach students to collaborate with it. That sounds pragmatic. It may even be partly true. But ubiquity is not the same thing as educational legitimacy. We do not permit every workplace norm inside every classroom. We do not let students bring all external aids into every exam simply because professionals have databases, assistants, and editors. School is precisely the place where society slows down the pressure to perform and asks whether the person can still think without constant scaffolding. If education abandons that function, it risks becoming a rehearsal for dependence.

The pro-AI case is stronger than its critics sometimes admit

Yet the anti-AI position can be equally simplistic when it imagines that authenticity alone is a sufficient educational ideal. It is not. The reality of school has always included forms of assistance that were once controversial. Dictionaries, calculators, graphing software, plagiarism detection, and accessibility tools all changed what counted as mastery. For many students, AI can level access: it can help non-native speakers translate, support students with disabilities, provide first-pass feedback, and reduce the intimidation of a blank page. UNESCO’s recent guidance and competency frameworks explicitly acknowledge the need for safe, ethical, inclusive, and responsible use. The institution is not rejecting AI in principle; it is trying to distinguish between use that supports learning and use that replaces it. UNESCO’s 2024 competency framework for teachers defines knowledge, skills, and values around a human-centered mindset, ethics, AI foundations, pedagogy, and professional learning. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

There is also a genuine equity argument. Wealthier students have always had more tutoring, editing, and family support. If AI can offer some approximation of that assistance at low cost, banning it everywhere may harden existing inequalities. A student who lacks access to human tutoring might use AI to clarify concepts after school. A first-generation college student might use it to decode assignment instructions or draft an email to a professor. In a world where institutions often underprovide human support, the moral appeal of a broadly available assistant is obvious. It would be perverse to insist that only students with private tutors get help while others must struggle alone. This is the strongest charitable reading of the pro-AI position: technology can democratize access to cognitive support that was previously unevenly distributed. UNESCO’s rights-based report on education and AI also warns, however, that access without safeguards can deepen existing divides because nearly one-third of the global population still lacks internet access. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-education-protecting-rights-learners?utm_source=openai))

But equity cuts both ways. The more schools normalize AI, the more the benefits may accrue to students who already know how to ask better questions, spot errors, and revise machine output. That is not a trivial skill. It is a form of cultural and educational capital. UNESCO’s survey of higher education institutions found that even where AI policies exist or are being developed, implementation differs widely across regions, and the organization has also stressed that many educators still lack confidence in AI’s foundations and pedagogical uses. In other words, the students most likely to benefit from AI may also be the ones already advantaged by strong prior preparation. If that is true, AI can amplify inequality even as it claims to reduce it. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/unesco-survey-two-thirds-higher-education-institutions-have-or-are-developing-guidance-ai-use?hub=343&utm_source=openai))

What the evidence says about learning, and what it does not

The most intellectually honest position is not that generative AI is either good or bad for learning, but that its effects depend heavily on design, task, and learner maturity. The OECD’s 2026 outlook says emerging research suggests AI can support learning when guided by clear teaching principles. The broader literature is similarly conditional: AI can assist with drafting, feedback, and personalization, but it can also encourage shallow processing, overreliance, and automation bias. A 2025 systematic review in Humanities and Social Sciences Communications concluded that ethical risks in education cluster around technology, education, and society, not just one isolated domain. UNESCO’s guidance also stresses human-agent and age-appropriate approaches, which is another way of saying that the educational value of AI depends on how it is framed and governed. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2026/01/oecd-digital-education-outlook-2026_940e0dd8.html?utm_source=openai))

That ambiguity matters because advocates often talk as if the tool’s existence itself resolves the question. It does not. A calculator can liberate students from repetitive arithmetic while still requiring understanding. A chatbot can do the opposite if it is used to generate final answers without any metacognitive struggle. The philosophical question is therefore not whether AI is in the classroom, but what kind of classroom it is. If the course objective is to teach writing, should a model produce the first draft? If the objective is to teach argument, should the model supply the argument structure? If the objective is to teach coding, should the model write boilerplate code while the student debugs? Each answer may be different. A mature policy must be task-specific rather than ideological. UNESCO’s 2023 guidance and the OECD’s 2026 outlook both point toward that kind of specificity. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

Still, the burden of proof should not fall only on skeptics. The educational value of AI should be demonstrated, not presumed. That is especially important because many studies in this area are early-stage, context-specific, and vulnerable to hype effects. Some are based on self-reported use, which can overstate learning benefits. Others measure short-term productivity rather than long-term retention or transfer. The question that matters philosophically is not whether students can finish assignments faster with AI. It is whether they become more capable, more truthful, and more autonomous over time. On that standard, the existing evidence is promising but not decisive. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05252-6?utm_source=openai))

Assessment is where the moral stakes become visible

No part of schooling is more exposed to AI than assessment. If a student can generate an essay in minutes, what exactly is the essay measuring? If a professor grades a polished response that the student partly or wholly outsourced, what was rewarded: thinking, editing, prompt engineering, or access to a powerful tool? Schools have responded by moving toward oral exams, handwritten in-class work, staged drafts, process logs, and assessment designs that emphasize reflection over one-shot output. This is not mere bureaucratic innovation. It is a recognition that assessment is a moral contract. It tells students what counts.

That contract has become unstable because AI makes it possible to separate labor from appearance with unusual ease. This creates a fairness problem for conscientious students, but also a deeper epistemic one. Teachers rely on assignments as evidence of understanding. If the evidence becomes noisy, they will either over-police students or under-trust all work. Both outcomes are corrosive. Over-policing breeds suspicion and makes school feel punitive. Under-trusting devalues honest effort. The result is an institution that no longer knows how to witness learning. OpenAI’s own educator guidance acknowledges this problem, noting both the limits of AI detectors and the need for educators to interpret evidence carefully rather than rely on automation alone. ([help.openai.com](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/8313351-how-can-educators-respond-to-students-presenting-ai-generated-content-as-their-own%3F.pict?utm_source=openai))

There is also a consequentialist argument for caution: if AI becomes a shortcut in writing and problem-solving courses, students may miss the very struggle that makes later mastery possible. That is especially troubling in foundational education. A freshman who asks AI to solve every problem may get better-looking homework but weaker conceptual grounding. A middle-school student who relies on AI to summarize every reading may lose the habit of sustained concentration. A young writer who uses AI to polish every sentence may never fully develop a personal voice. These are not nostalgic worries about purity. They are developmental concerns about how skills are built. The OECD’s 2026 report frames GenAI as potentially useful when teaching is explicit, which implies that unchecked substitution can undermine the very learning the technology is meant to support. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2026/01/oecd-digital-education-outlook-2026_940e0dd8.html?utm_source=openai))

The counterargument: maybe school has always overvalued unaided performance

The strongest philosophical challenge to this essay is that it romanticizes struggle. Critics can argue that education has never been purely about unaided thought. Students have always leaned on libraries, tutors, formula sheets, and each other. There is no pure baseline of original cognition to protect. What schools call “independent work” has always been supported work. If AI helps students think better, then insisting on drudgery may be less a defense of learning than a defense of tradition.

This objection should be taken seriously. Some assignments do fetishize labor in ways that are pedagogically empty. Students asked to generate endless handwritten summaries, for example, may not be learning anything meaningful. In such cases, AI may expose the poverty of the assignment rather than the student’s failure. The right response is not simply to ban AI, but to ask whether the task actually tests judgment, creativity, or understanding. UNESCO’s guidance and OECD’s outlook both point toward this more nuanced approach: AI should be integrated with clear pedagogical purpose, not added for novelty alone. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

There is a second, more radical version of the counterargument. Perhaps education should not be framed as the cultivation of unaided internal ability at all. Perhaps the true goal is to prepare students to operate within a technologically mediated world, where the skill lies in orchestrating tools, not resisting them. On this view, the best student is not the one who can reproduce a skill from memory, but the one who can deploy a network of supports intelligently and ethically. That is a real philosophical possibility, and one worth respecting. Human intelligence has always been distributed across artifacts and institutions. A culture that ignores that may be defending an illusion of pure cognition that never existed. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-education-guidance-policy-makers?utm_source=openai))

But even if that is true, it does not follow that every task should become tool-mediated, especially not before students have internalized the basics. One learns chess by playing against others, but also by calculating moves unaided. One learns music by listening, but also by practicing scales. One learns writing by reading, drafting, and revising, not by handing the first version to a machine. Tools are valuable in proportion to the capacities they augment. The ethical mistake would be to let assistance become a substitute for formation.

The governance question is bigger than the classroom

AI in education is also a governance problem because it reveals who gets to decide what counts as acceptable knowledge work. UNESCO’s recent programming and the OECD’s outlook reflect a multilateral effort to build norms around ethical and human-centered use. In the United States, the policy picture is more fragmented. Ohio’s Department of Education and Workforce now says state law requires each district, community school, and STEM school to adopt an AI policy by July 1, 2026. That is a sign of a state system trying to catch up with the technology on the ground. But the patchwork itself has ethical consequences. Students’ rights and obligations may vary by ZIP code, and the very definition of academic honesty may depend on where they attend school. ([education.ohio.gov](https://education.ohio.gov/Topics/AI-in-Ohio-s-Education?utm_source=openai))

That fragmentation raises a philosophical question about legitimacy. If AI changes the meaning of learning, who should define that meaning? Teachers? Districts? States? International bodies? Parents? The market? The answer cannot be the market alone, because vendor incentives favor adoption. Nor can it be only technocratic rule-making, because education is bound up with public values: fairness, citizenship, pluralism, and dignity. A good AI policy must therefore be democratic in the deeper sense: it should be justified not just by efficiency metrics, but by an account of what kind of person and what kind of society schools ought to serve. UNESCO’s 2025 rights-based report makes that case directly, arguing that innovation should strengthen learning opportunities for all rather than widen the AI divide. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-and-education-protecting-rights-learners?utm_source=openai))

The ethical stakes are not only about students

It is tempting to focus exclusively on whether AI harms students. But teachers are affected too. If every assignment may have been AI-assisted, instructors are pushed into surveillance modes they never wanted. If institutions adopt AI broadly for grading, feedback, or administrative tasks, educators may become less certain about their own judgment and more dependent on system outputs. The emotional tone of teaching can change: suspicion replaces trust, and prompt management replaces mentorship. UNESCO has repeatedly stressed that AI in education should remain human-centered. That is not just a slogan. It is a warning against turning pedagogy into a compliance machine. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

There are also privacy concerns. Educational AI systems often require data about students’ work, habits, and weaknesses. The more personalized the system, the more intimate the profiling can become. That may help instruction. It may also create new forms of vulnerability, especially for minors. If schools adopt third-party tools too quickly, they may expose students to data practices they do not understand and cannot meaningfully consent to. UNESCO’s guidance explicitly says the absence of national regulations leaves privacy unprotected and educational institutions unprepared. Its rights-based 2025 report likewise warns that transparent governance, strong data protection, and accountability mechanisms are essential if AI is to serve the right to education rather than undermine it. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

A practical philosophy of restraint

The right response to all this is not technophobia. It is disciplined restraint. Schools should distinguish among at least four categories of AI use. First, allowed support for accessibility, translation, and basic tutoring. Second, bounded assistance for brainstorming, outlining, and feedback, where the student remains accountable for final judgments. Third, transparent AI use in courses designed to study AI itself, where prompting and critique become explicit learning objectives. Fourth, prohibited substitution in assessments intended to measure individual mastery. That framework is not perfect, but it respects both innovation and formation. It accepts that AI is already part of the educational environment while refusing the idea that every educational task is equally outsourceable. UNESCO’s guidance and teacher competency framework both point in this direction. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

More importantly, schools should re-center process. A final essay should not be the only evidence of learning. Teachers can ask for source annotations, reflection memos, oral defenses, revision histories, in-class writing, and short concept checks. If a student used AI, that use can be disclosed and assessed. The point is not to restore a fantasy of a machine-free classroom, but to make thinking visible again. Assessment should reward not just the product but the path.

That is the philosophical lesson of the AI moment. Technology has not merely given students a new tool. It has forced schools to confront a question they had often left implicit: are they trying to optimize outputs, or to cultivate minds? The first goal can be achieved with software. The second cannot. If schools forget that difference, they may become more efficient while becoming less educational.

So yes, generative AI should be in education. But only under a doctrine of suspicion toward convenience, humility about what tools can measure, and seriousness about what learning is for. The task is not to stop students from using AI. It is to stop education from becoming nothing more than the management of AI-assisted performance. That would not be modernization. It would be a philosophical surrender.
