The Cheese Was Aged. The Warning Still Had to Catch Up.

A small E. coli O157:H7 outbreak tied by federal investigators to raw dairy is testing a larger public-health problem: unpasteurized products can move through stores, homes and ideology faster than interviews, sequencing, sampling and voluntary recalls can remove them.

By The Press • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The latest federal updates on the raw-dairy outbreak were not dramatic in the way food scares often are. As of Saturday evening, April 25, the most recent CDC and FDA postings, dated April 16, listed nine confirmed E. coli O157:H7 infections in three states: California, Florida and Texas. Three people had been hospitalized. One had developed hemolytic uremic syndrome, a serious kidney complication. No deaths had been reported. Illness onsets stretched from September 1, 2025, to February 20, 2026.

The scale is small by the usual arithmetic of American foodborne illness. The sharper public-health signal is not the total. It is the clock. Federal investigators linked the outbreak to Raw Farm brand raw dairy products, including raw cheddar cheeses sold in blocks, shredded bags and bulk formats. The recalled cheese carried expiration dates reaching into May, August and September 2026, which means the product was not a meal that vanished when dinner ended. It was a refrigerator item, a freezer item, a lunch-box item, a snack item, and potentially a product sitting in a household long after a federal advisory had been posted.

That is why this outbreak is bigger than its case count. It exposes the distance between detecting a pattern and removing a product. Public-health laboratories can now connect cases across states with molecular precision. Investigators can compare patient interviews against population exposure surveys. FDA inspectors can collect product samples and run whole genome sequencing. But none of that automatically empties a home refrigerator, changes a parent’s mind, forces a contested recall to happen at once, or makes an old food rule mean more than it actually says.

The recalled products are not obscure in the legal sense. Federal rules generally bar interstate sale of unpasteurized fluid milk in final package form for direct human consumption, but many cheeses made from unpasteurized milk may move in commerce if they meet standards of identity. For cheddar, the federal standard allows unpasteurized dairy ingredients if the cheese is cured at not less than 35 degrees Fahrenheit for at least 60 days. On a label, that aging requirement can sound reassuring. In public health, it is more limited: a minimum process rule, not a promise that every pathogen in every batch has disappeared.

CDC’s investigation explains why federal officials treated the pattern as meaningful. Of eight sick people or caregivers interviewed, all reported consuming or being served unpasteurized milk or cheese. CDC compared that exposure rate with the FoodNet Population Survey, in which 6.4 percent of respondents reported eating any raw or unpasteurized milk or cheese. Of seven interviewed people who could identify a brand, all seven named Raw Farm. In 2026, five people reported consuming or being served Raw Farm raw cheddar cheese. In 2025, two sick people reported consuming Raw Farm raw milk; federal officials said the affected raw milk from 2025 should no longer be on shelves.

The laboratory picture is more complicated, and that complication matters. FDA said it collected and analyzed 19 samples of Raw Farm raw cheddar cheese during the investigation. One cheddar sample tested positive for E. coli O157:H7. Whole genome sequencing showed that the organism found in that cheese was not the same strain as the current outbreak strain. Instead, FDA said, it matched illnesses from a separate 2025 outbreak that is not ongoing. The agency also said the cheese sample that tested positive in 2026 was not shipped to stores and was not available for sale.

That is not the tidy courtroom version of proof many consumers expect. It is the messier public-health version: epidemiology, patient interviews, dates, product histories, brand information, genomic relatedness among patient isolates, inspection findings and product sampling layered together. A product test can miss the outbreak strain for reasons that do not make the epidemiology vanish. The contaminated piece may already have been eaten. The lot may have been discarded. The organism may have been unevenly distributed. The relevant package may be in a freezer, not a plant. A mismatched positive sample can still tell regulators something important about hazard in a product category or production setting, even if it does not identify the exact strain that sickened the current patients.

The recall sequence shows where the system slows. FDA first warned consumers and advised Raw Farm to remove affected raw cheddar products from the market in mid-March. The company disputed the federal conclusion and initially declined to recall the cheese. On April 2, Raw Farm issued a voluntary recall of certain raw cheddar products. On April 7, the company clarified its recall language, withdrew earlier phrasing that the recall was being performed under protest, and added that while it reserved its rights and disputed being the cause of the outbreak, it was issuing the recall in the interest of public health and safety.

That language is not just public-relations debris. It is part of the health story. FDA has mandatory recall authority for many foods when statutory conditions are met, including a reasonable probability that exposure will cause serious adverse health consequences or death, but the agency must first give the responsible party an opportunity to conduct a voluntary recall. In practice, the routine path still runs through request, negotiation, advisory, voluntary action and follow-up. When a company disputes epidemiologic evidence, the clock keeps moving while product remains in commerce or in homes.

The current Raw Farm recall covers original raw cheddar blocks, jalapeno raw cheddar blocks, shredded raw cheddar and bulk formats with specific batch and expiration ranges. CDC and FDA advised consumers, retailers, restaurants and foodservice customers not to eat, sell or serve the recalled cheeses. They also advised cleaning and sanitizing surfaces or containers the cheese touched. That last instruction is easy to overlook, but it is where an online notice becomes kitchen labor: a drawer wiped down, a cutting board washed, a wrapper checked, a bag thrown away, a receipt searched for, a caregiver trying to remember whether a toddler was served a few shreds in February.

The patient profile changes the ethics

The known patients are strikingly young. CDC’s investigation update listed an age range of 1 to 28 years and a median age of 2; more than half were under 5. That does not prove raw dairy is uniquely consumed by small children. It does show why the outbreak cannot be weighed only by its total number of confirmed cases. Shiga toxin-producing E. coli can cause severe stomach cramps, vomiting and diarrhea that is often bloody. Most people recover with supportive care, but some develop hemolytic uremic syndrome, which can cause kidney failure, require hospitalization, and in some cases leave lasting harm.

CDC clinician guidance makes the risk concrete. Stool samples should be cultured for E. coli O157 and assayed for Shiga toxin. Presumptive O157 isolates and Shiga toxin-positive specimens should be sent to public-health laboratories. Patients with profuse diarrhea or vomiting need rehydration, and early intravenous fluids may reduce the risk of renal failure in children with STEC infection. Clinicians are warned that antibiotics may increase the risk of hemolytic uremic syndrome in suspected STEC cases, and that antimotility drugs should be avoided for bloody diarrhea and STEC infection because they can increase the risk of complications.

That clinical reality is where raw dairy stops being only a consumer-preference story. Adults can decide to accept risks for taste, tradition, distrust of industrial food or belief in a less processed diet. Children cannot meaningfully consent to the same risk. Public-health agencies consistently identify children under 5, pregnant people, older adults and people with weakened immune systems as higher-risk groups for raw dairy-associated illness. The American Academy of Pediatrics has warned parents against giving children raw milk or products made with raw milk, and CDC says pasteurized milk offers the same nutritional benefits without the risks of raw milk consumption.

Raw-milk advocates reject the idea that the meaningful divide is simply raw versus pasteurized. Their stronger argument is that milk intended for direct raw consumption can be produced under different standards than ordinary milk intended for pasteurization. The Raw Milk Institute, for example, promotes farmer training, risk-management plans, rapid chilling and stringent bacterial testing, and argues that carefully produced raw milk is fundamentally different from pre-pasteurized milk moving through the conventional supply chain. That view matters because it explains why categorical warnings often fail. Many consumers believe they are buying from a cleaner, more transparent, more careful system than the mainstream dairy aisle.

The public-health answer is not that pasteurized dairy has never been implicated in illness, or that all food can be made perfectly safe. It is that pasteurization is a validated kill step. Raw dairy asks sanitation, animal health, equipment cleaning, refrigeration, testing, labeling and consumer behavior to do the work without that kill step. Testing is a snapshot. A clean result does not sterilize the next bottle, vat, block or shredder. A warning label does not prevent a parent from serving cheese to a child. A careful farm still operates in a biological environment where fecal contamination, mastitis, wildlife, worker handling, biofilms and refrigeration failures can change risk from day to day.

The 60-day rule is not a force field

Cheddar is an especially confusing vehicle for this debate because it sits inside the exception that many consumers only partly understand. Under federal regulation, cheddar made with unpasteurized dairy ingredients must be cured at a temperature of at least 35 degrees Fahrenheit for at least 60 days. That standard has become a kind of cultural shorthand: aged raw cheese is different from raw milk, and 60 days is supposed to make it safe enough for interstate commerce.

But 60 days is not a sterilization claim. Scientific reviews and studies have long raised questions about pathogen survival in raw-milk cheeses under particular conditions. FDA’s own 2014-2016 raw-milk cheese sampling assignment found low pathogen contamination rates, below 1 percent for each pathogen tested among 1,606 samples aged at least 60 days, but it also found generic E. coli in 5.4 percent of samples. That result supports two truths at once. Most sampled cheeses did not test positive for the targeted pathogens. And low prevalence is not zero prevalence, especially when the exposed person is a toddler, a pregnant person, an older adult or an immunocompromised patient.

Older and more recent research makes the same point from another angle. Studies have found that E. coli O157:H7 can survive through the 60-day aging period in cheddar under experimental conditions, with viable organisms detected beyond that window in some work. National Academies material on dairy hazards has also described the limits of assuming aging alone resolves pathogen risk. Cheese safety is not one variable. Moisture, pH, salt, starter cultures, milk quality, sanitation, handling and storage all matter.

The H5N1 dairy-cattle outbreak has made the aging rule feel even older. This raw-cheddar outbreak is about E. coli, not influenza, and there is no evidence that the illnesses in the current cluster involve bird flu. But emerging-pathogen research is relevant to the confidence people place in raw-milk cheese rules. FDA began a domestic sampling assignment to test aged raw cow’s milk cheese for highly pathogenic avian influenza H5N1. A 2025 Nature Medicine study found that H5N1 could remain infectious for extended periods in raw-milk cheeses under certain pH conditions, including through aging in some experimental and naturally contaminated cheeses, although ferrets fed contaminated cheese in that study did not become infected. The point is not that E. coli and H5N1 behave the same way. The point is that a rule built around old assumptions about bacterial hazards is now being asked to reassure consumers about a changing microbial landscape.

Surveillance can find the cluster, not the wrapper

The strongest part of the modern food-safety system is molecular surveillance. PulseNet, a national network of public-health and food-regulatory laboratories, connects foodborne illness cases by comparing the DNA fingerprints of bacteria isolated from patients. Whole genome sequencing gives investigators a much finer fingerprint than older methods, helping separate related outbreak cases from unrelated background infections. CDC’s SEDRIC platform then helps investigators combine epidemiologic, laboratory and traceback data in real time across jurisdictions.

That infrastructure can connect a sick child in California with a patient in Florida and a patient in Texas who never met, never shopped in the same store and may have eaten the product weeks apart. It can turn scattered illnesses into a signal. It can show that patient isolates are closely related genetically, suggesting a shared source. It can help state and federal investigators decide what questions to ask, which records to examine and where to inspect.

But surveillance is not prevention. Someone has to get sick. Someone has to seek care. A clinician or laboratory has to test in a way that detects E. coli O157 or Shiga toxin. The isolate or specimen has to move into the public-health system. Someone has to interview the patient or caregiver and get a useful food history. Then investigators have to compare those answers against other cases and background exposure data. CDC routinely cautions that the true number of illnesses in an outbreak is likely higher than the confirmed total because many people recover without medical care and are never tested. CDC also notes that it can take three to four weeks to determine whether a recent illness belongs to an outbreak.

Raw dairy makes that delay especially difficult. Milk is consumed quickly. Cheese may be cut, repackaged, shredded into a meal, served by a relative, sampled at a gathering or frozen without the original label. A caregiver may remember cheddar but not the brand. A product may be gone by the time investigators call. In this outbreak, the brand signal among interviewed patients was unusually pointed. Yet the positive product sample did not match the current outbreak strain. That gap creates space for doubt, and doubt can slow recall action.

Public-health agencies therefore need the public to understand a kind of evidence that is powerful but not cinematic. A recall may be justified by converging evidence before investigators find a matching organism in a package. A product sample may be negative or mismatched even when epidemiology is strong. A shared genomic cluster among patients can show a likely common source even if the contaminated bite has long since been eaten. Those ideas are routine in outbreak work, but they are not intuitive to many consumers, especially in a political environment where trust in federal health agencies is uneven.

Raw dairy has become a symbol, not just a food

Raw milk consumption remains uncommon, but the issue is no longer culturally invisible. The Annenberg Public Policy Center reported in March 2025 that 4 percent of U.S. adults said they had consumed raw or unpasteurized milk in the previous 12 months, while many more were unsure about safety claims. Only a majority knew raw milk is less safe than pasteurized milk; large shares were unsure whether children are more vulnerable to germs in raw milk. KFF polling on bird flu found that many adults were unsure which precautions public-health officials recommend, including avoiding unpasteurized or raw milk products.

The regulatory map adds to the confusion. FDA says it does not regulate intrastate raw-milk sales; states decide whether and how raw milk may be sold within their borders. FDA’s raw-milk page says 20 states explicitly prohibit intrastate raw milk sales in some form and 30 allow it. A 2026 University of Wisconsin Extension review described the result as a patchwork of retail, on-farm, herd-share and prohibition regimes. Consumers do not experience that patchwork as a legal diagram. They experience it as inconsistency: legal here, illegal there, sold in a specialty store in one state, available through a farm arrangement in another, barred across state lines as fluid milk but permitted as certain aged cheeses.

That inconsistency helps turn a food-safety question into an identity argument. Public-health officials talk about pathogen risk, vulnerable groups and pasteurization. Raw-milk supporters talk about local farms, autonomy, natural food, taste, distrust of industrial agriculture and the belief that official agencies dismiss their experience. Some political figures and wellness influencers have amplified raw milk as a marker of resistance to what they describe as overregulated or overprocessed food systems. In that setting, a warning about E. coli can be heard not as a safety advisory but as an attack on a way of life.

Public-health messaging has to be accurate without becoming culturally naive. Saying that raw dairy can contain E. coli, Salmonella, Campylobacter, Listeria, Brucella and other pathogens is true. Saying that pasteurized milk provides the nutritional benefits of milk without the same raw-product risks is also true. But the message that may matter most in a recall is narrower: testing cannot guarantee each serving; 60 days of aging is not a magic shield; children change the risk calculation; and a product can be removed because the epidemiology is strong even when the public has not seen a matching contaminated package.

The policy issue is speed

The immediate policy question raised by this outbreak is not whether the United States should settle the entire raw-milk debate in one law. It is whether outbreak response is fast enough when a producer disputes epidemiologic evidence and a refrigerated product with long-dated packages has been distributed nationally. Voluntary recall may work well when a company agrees quickly. It works less well when public warnings and company disagreement occupy the same weeks in which households are deciding what to feed children.

There are policy options short of a national raw-dairy ban. Regulators could require clearer point-of-sale warnings for raw-milk cheeses, especially about children, pregnancy and immune compromise. States that allow raw-milk sales could require faster electronic traceability and consumer notification systems for permitted producers. FDA and state agencies could make escalation criteria clearer when a firm refuses or delays recall during a high-risk outbreak. Retailers could treat federal outbreak advisories involving raw dairy as immediate stop-sale events even before recall language is finalized. Pediatricians and obstetric clinicians could ask about raw dairy more routinely, the same way food-safety counseling already addresses deli meats, unpasteurized juices, certain seafood and high-mercury fish.

The federal updates in this case were careful in ways that matter. CDC and FDA identified what was known, what was still under investigation, and what the product testing did and did not show. FDA disclosed the positive cheese sample and also disclosed that whole genome sequencing linked it to a different 2025 outbreak rather than the current cluster. That transparency is necessary. But technical transparency is not the same as public comprehension. If agencies want faster compliance from consumers, they have to explain why epidemiology can be decisive, why a product sample is not always the missing key, and why an online advisory should lead to action in a kitchen even before every scientific thread has been tied.

Uncertainty remains. The outbreak was still listed as open in the April 16 federal updates. Case counts can change. Additional testing may or may not find the current outbreak strain in product or environmental samples. The strain found in a cheese sample may lead investigators toward more information about a separate 2025 outbreak, or it may remain a troubling but limited finding. Raw Farm continues to dispute causation. Those caveats should not be treated as weakness; they are part of honest outbreak communication.

What is already clear is enough to guide prevention. Federal and state investigators have linked a raw-dairy outbreak to Raw Farm products through patient interviews, brand information and genomic evidence among sick people. The known patients are heavily concentrated among very young children. The recall happened only after federal warnings and company resistance. The 60-day aging rule for raw cheddar is a minimum process requirement, not a guarantee that a pathogen cannot survive. Molecular surveillance can connect cases across the country, but it cannot reach into every refrigerator drawer.

The recall notice is a small document. The system behind it is large: the cow, the milking line, the vat, the aging room, the shredder, the label, the retailer, the caregiver, the pediatric emergency department, the state epidemiologist, the public-health laboratory, the FDA inspector, the CDC analyst and the law that determines how quickly a disputed product leaves the market. In this outbreak, the system eventually moved. The harder question is whether it can move faster the next time, before the evidence has been eaten.
