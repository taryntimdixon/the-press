CinemaCon 2026 shows Hollywood still believes in theaters — but the business has become harder to read

At a moment when studios are again promising longer theatrical windows, bigger event films and more support for exhibitors, the spring trade-show circuit is exposing a split-screen reality: moviegoing can still generate blockbuster power, but audiences, labor and streaming economics now pull the industry in different directions.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 11:22 a.m.

The film business arrived at CinemaCon 2026 with a familiar contradiction: Hollywood still talks about theaters as the center of the movie experience, even as the market around those theaters has changed in ways that make old assumptions unreliable. The convention, held April 13-16 in Las Vegas and hosted by Cinema United, landed in the middle of a flurry of studio promises, exhibitor optimism and strategic anxiety. The most visible signals were bullish. Cinema United, the trade group for theater owners, has been arguing that moviegoing is entering what it calls “the next great era of cinema,” and this year’s gathering gave that message a very large stage. Studios leaned into the theatrical pitch with franchise footage, premium-format marketing and renewed language about exclusive windows. But the countercurrent was just as strong: streaming remains the default for a large share of U.S. movie watchers, consolidation is looming over the studio landscape, labor groups are still fighting over AI and job security, and even a strong box-office spring does not erase the fragility of the business model. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c3d7ed8782da1dc46d20476a2f9eca9b?utm_source=openai))

What makes this moment especially newsworthy is that it is not a slow, abstract debate about the future of the movies. It is happening in real time, around a concrete set of events and numbers. One of the clearest spring indicators was the success of The Super Mario Galaxy Movie, which AP reported opened to $130.9 million domestically and $372.5 million globally in its first five days, then followed that with a strong second weekend and a worldwide total of $629 million. That kind of performance reinforces the old truth that family and franchise pictures can still make theaters feel unavoidable. Yet the same week also brought reminders that box-office strength is not the same thing as structural stability. AP-NORC polling published in October 2025 found that about three-quarters of U.S. adults had watched a new movie on streaming instead of in a theater at least once in the previous year, including about three in 10 who did so monthly. That does not mean theaters are dying. It does mean the audience’s habits are bifurcated, and the industry is now trying to build a theatrical case inside a media environment that is no longer organized around theatrical first. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/58b52a8aa469d6e7fed889c6762dd3aa?utm_source=openai))

The week Hollywood tried to reset the narrative

CinemaCon is more than a trade show; it is where the industry performs confidence for itself. In 2026, that performance came with real stakes. The event was the annual convention hosted by Cinema United, which rebranded from the National Association of Theatre Owners in March 2025 and says it represents more than 32,000 screens in the United States and more than 30,000 screens in 88 countries. The group’s public mission is to promote moviegoing, and its messaging this spring emphasized that the theatrical experience is still central to exhibition. The schedule, press materials and related programming made clear that exhibitors were focused not just on titles but on audiences, particularly younger moviegoers, premium large formats, concessions and marketing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/517435592b448aa9db50c470f8a7a9b6?utm_source=openai))

The studio side followed suit. Disney’s presentation at CinemaCon, for example, included major franchise footage and a traditional attempt to remind theater owners why the big screen still matters. AP reported that the company showed the opening to the new Star Wars film The Mandalorian and Grogu and fresh footage from Avengers: Doomsday, along with appearances tied to Toy Story 5. That kind of presentation is not just promotion; it is a signal that studios still see theatrical eventization as central to monetizing their most expensive intellectual property. A film like that is not being sold as content. It is being sold as a communal occasion that needs the scale, sound and schedule of theaters to maximize cultural and financial impact. Disney’s own box-office record in 2025, as AP noted, also helped support the pitch that theatrical can still be a major revenue engine when the right movies arrive. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))

But the more interesting story is the gap between tone and structure. Studios can still create excitement in the room. They cannot fully recreate the distribution environment of the 2010s, when theatrical and home entertainment windows were more predictable and the theater run had a longer, more protected commercial life. Paramount’s recent public commitment to a 45-day theatrical window is one example of how exhibitors are trying to claw back some leverage. Yet the mere fact that such promises have to be reiterated at all shows how much the norm has shifted. Window strategy is now a competitive variable rather than a settled industry rule, and the debate has become inseparable from merger politics and platform strategy. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/paramount-warner-bros-movies-theatres-david-ellison-33768/?utm_source=openai))

Theatrical still works — but mostly when it behaves like an event

The strongest evidence that movie theaters still matter is also the evidence that the market has become narrower. Early 2026 box office has been driven heavily by event films, especially family titles and franchise pictures. AP’s spring coverage framed the year as one built on a pipeline of recognizable properties, including Avatar: Fire and Ash, the new Super Mario sequel, Pixar’s Hoppers and other large releases. This is not a market in collapse; it is a market in which a relatively small number of tentpoles carry an outsized share of theatrical vitality. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f0881c905b0c1c3c23ea79fd1966fe59?utm_source=openai))

That pattern has important implications for critics and exhibitors alike. If the theatrical model increasingly depends on family attendance, premium formats and recognizable brands, then the economics of theaters become more volatile. An uneven release calendar can produce strong weekends followed by soft stretches that are hard to make up with smaller films. That challenge was visible in the April box-office charts, where a handful of wide releases dominated the weekend while specialty titles fought for scraps. The same dynamic is why theater owners keep emphasizing loyalty programs, upgraded auditoriums and concessions. They are no longer selling only a ticket; they are trying to sell a whole outing. Cinema United’s 2025 update said 77% of Americans ages 12-74 saw at least one movie in the past year, the number of habitual moviegoers grew, and Gen Z went more often than older cohorts. Those are real positives, but they also imply that the industry’s best hope may be a more segmented audience model rather than a universal one. ([cinemaunited.org](https://cinemaunited.org/2025/12/17/cinema-united-releases-strength-of-theatrical-exhibition-2025-update-spotlighting-important-movie-theatre-industry-metrics/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a subtle but important distinction between attendance and dependence. A family blockbuster can rescue a weekend, but not necessarily rebuild the habit of theatrical moviegoing for all consumers. Cinema United’s own data suggests that many Americans are still moviegoers, yet AP-NORC’s polling indicates that a large share of them are also comfortable defaulting to streaming for new releases. The trend line is not “people stopped caring about theaters.” It is “the theater is no longer the only place where movie habits are formed.” That matters because the industry has long relied on the theater run to create cultural urgency, generate marketing buzz and establish prestige. Those functions still exist, but they now compete with immediate home access and with social-media discovery that often begins outside the cinema ecosystem. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a1b6b6d48f47389f422ab7299c4e3e74?utm_source=openai))

Streaming has not killed moviegoing, but it has changed its default setting

The AP-NORC poll is one of the clearest reminders that theaters are now competing with a habit, not just a platform. The survey found that about three-quarters of U.S. adults watched a new movie on streaming instead of in a theater at least once in the past year, and about three in 10 did so at least monthly. That does not mean theatrical release has lost all value. It means the consumer’s first instinct has changed. Convenience, cost and the sheer abundance of at-home options all pull viewers away from theaters unless the film offers an unusually compelling reason to leave the house. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a1b6b6d48f47389f422ab7299c4e3e74?utm_source=openai))

This is why the theater industry has increasingly leaned into experience inflation: premium large-format screens, immersive sound, luxury seating, recliner upgrades, better concessions and loyalty programs. Cinema United’s research material has emphasized loyalty-program growth, Gen Z frequency and the importance of upgraded experiences, while its March 2025 special report argued that the large-screen communal experience needs continued innovation to remain durable. The argument is straightforward: if the home is convenient, the theater must become differentiated. But that strategy has limits. A better chair and a larger soda can help, yet they do not solve the bigger issue that a large percentage of consumers are now trained to wait. The movie business has to persuade them that waiting means missing the point. ([cinemaunited.org](https://cinemaunited.org/2025/12/17/cinema-united-releases-strength-of-theatrical-exhibition-2025-update-spotlighting-important-movie-theatre-industry-metrics/?utm_source=openai))

There is evidence that this persuasion still works under the right conditions. The recent family-heavy box office run suggests audiences will still mobilize for films that feel culturally unavoidable, especially when they are pitched as social experiences for kids and parents. AP’s April 2 dispatch on family movies noted that exhibitors are actively tailoring their theaters for children with coloring stations, mascots and collectible popcorn containers. That is not accidental fluff; it is a strategy aimed at cultivating future moviegoers through ritual and memory. In other words, the theatrical business increasingly has to manufacture the same kind of emotional stickiness that streaming services enjoy by default, only it must do so in a single outing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8d9623e3d2229c4bfd4bc548f31f0ffe?utm_source=openai))

Consolidation is the industry’s quiet threat

One reason the current optimism remains fragile is that the business itself may soon become more concentrated. Reporting in recent months has centered on the proposed Paramount-Warner Bros. transaction and the broader possibility that Warner Bros. Discovery could end up under ownership that is publicly committed to theatrical, but also deeply focused on cost savings and corporate integration. Even before any deal closes, the strategic implications are obvious: fewer independent major studios, more centralized decision-making, and potentially more pressure on theatrical windows and slate composition. That is why exhibitor anxiety remains high. A studio that owns more content may have more leverage over how and when that content reaches theaters. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/biz/global/paramount-skydance-q3-closing-warner-bros-discovery-ellison-33657/?utm_source=openai))

For theaters, consolidation matters because their negotiating position depends on variety. The more the industry is dominated by a handful of corporate ecosystems, the more exhibitors have to rely on those same companies to supply must-see product. Paramount’s public statements about maintaining theatrical releases and offering at least 30 movies a year if its deal goes through are designed to calm that fear, but promises are not pipelines. The core question is whether a smaller set of corporate decision-makers will prioritize the theatrical window as a standalone revenue engine or treat it as one step in a broader platform strategy. Theatrical owners want the former; streamers have historically preferred the latter. The debate is especially charged because any deal involving Warner Bros. could reshape the release calendar for years, not months. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/more/global/david-ellison-cinemacon-paramount-windows-streaming-35518/?utm_source=openai))

The uncertainty is heightened by the fact that 2026 is not a normal production year. Studios are still managing the aftereffects of the 2023 labor upheaval and the business’s uneven recovery from the pandemic years. In that context, every acquisition rumor and every window promise has to be read alongside the underlying supply question: will there be enough commercially attractive films to keep theaters consistently busy? Cinema United’s own materials argue that investment and innovation can meet robust demand, but that is a thesis, not a guarantee. The same data that shows a healthy core audience also suggests an uneven casual audience. Theaters can win if they get enough event movies. They are still vulnerable if the slate thins out. ([cinemaunited.org](https://cinemaunited.org/2025/12/17/cinema-united-releases-strength-of-theatrical-exhibition-2025-update-spotlighting-important-movie-theatre-industry-metrics/?utm_source=openai))

Labor and AI are now a theatrical issue, too

At first glance, labor disputes and generative AI might seem peripheral to the exhibition story. They are not. They shape what films get made, how quickly they are made, what rights performers and crews retain, and how audiences may come to view the authenticity of what they are paying to see. SAG-AFTRA has continued to press for AI guardrails, and in its materials the union describes protections for informed consent, compensation and control over the use of performers’ voice, likeness and performance. The union’s AI-related messaging also distinguishes between conventional production tools and synthetic performers, underscoring how complex the policy questions have become. That matters to theater owners because moviegoing depends on trust in the value of the filmed human performance. If viewers come to believe that major films are increasingly synthetic, the emotional basis of event cinema could change. ([sagaftra.org](https://www.sagaftra.org/ethovox-agrees-sag-aftras-ai-guardrails?utm_source=openai))

Other unions have already moved to lock in protections. The American Federation of Musicians ratified new motion picture agreements in 2024 that included AI protections, compensation when performances are used with AI tools, and broader streaming-related changes. The union also staged an AI-protections rally in March 2026. Those developments show that labor is not simply resisting technology; it is trying to negotiate its terms. That distinction matters. The industry’s future is unlikely to be a binary choice between humans and machines. It is more likely to be a contested mix in which labor tries to preserve attribution, compensation and creative agency while studios pursue efficiencies. Theatrical exhibition sits downstream from that struggle because a movie’s economic life begins long before a ticket is sold. ([afm.org](https://www.afm.org/2024/04/afm-musicians-ratify-motion-picture-agreements/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a reputational dimension. CinemaCon remains a place where stars and filmmakers sell the experience of cinema as human and communal, not algorithmic and isolated. That is why the industry seized on appearances by filmmakers and on the idea of the theater as a cultural institution rather than merely a retail outlet. But if AI becomes more embedded in visual effects, localization, dubbing, de-aging, or even script development, the film business will have to defend not just the theater, but the artistic legitimacy of the work shown there. In that sense, the labor debate is not separate from exhibition; it is part of the same struggle over what a movie is worth. ([sagaftra.org](https://www.sagaftra.org/contracts-industry-resources/member-resources/artificial-intelligence?utm_source=openai))

Why the exhibitor pitch is more convincing than the doomsayers’ narrative

It is tempting to say the theater business is doomed because consumer behavior has changed. But that argument is too flat. The better reading of the evidence is that theatrical moviegoing has become more conditional, not obsolete. The conditions are stricter than they used to be: films must feel urgent, audiences must perceive value, and the theater experience must justify the trip. Yet when those conditions are met, the results can still be enormous. The early-April box office showed that. CinemaCon 2026 showed that. Even the industry’s self-critique showed that. No one in Las Vegas was pretending that every movie can perform like a franchise tentpole. The real argument was narrower: that theaters still provide a unique commercial and cultural service that studios cannot easily replicate elsewhere. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/58b52a8aa469d6e7fed889c6762dd3aa?utm_source=openai))

The strongest counterargument is that this may simply be a transitional defense, the kind industries make while gradually shrinking. After all, a business can remain profitable while losing its place at the center of consumer life. Some evidence points that way. The AP-NORC poll suggests the home has become the default venue for a large swath of new-movie viewing. Theatrical attendance still has a core audience, but the audience is not universal in the way it once was. If the business ends up serving mostly event films, families, loyalists and premium-format buyers, that may still be a viable market. It just will not be the mass-market theatrical culture that older executives remember. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a1b6b6d48f47389f422ab7299c4e3e74?utm_source=openai))

That is the unresolved tension at the heart of CinemaCon 2026. On one side is a real surge of confidence: blockbuster openings, studio pledges, upgraded theaters, youth engagement and a trade group that now defines its mission around moviegoing rather than just ownership. On the other side is the less glamorous reality of consumer substitution, platform consolidation, labor friction and the uncertain economics of a narrower theatrical universe. The industry is not choosing between success and failure. It is choosing between different kinds of success, and some of them are harder to sustain than others. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/517435592b448aa9db50c470f8a7a9b6?utm_source=openai))

What comes next

Over the next year, the key questions will be practical rather than philosophical. Will studios honor longer windows, and if so for how many titles? Will premium formats and loyalty programs deliver enough repeat attendance to offset the decline in casual traffic? Can theater owners translate Gen Z interest into habits that last into adulthood? Will consolidation among distributors make exhibitors more dependent on fewer corporate decision-makers? And how quickly will AI reshape both the production pipeline and audience expectations of film authenticity? Those questions are harder than the nostalgia-driven talking points that usually accompany a convention like CinemaCon, but they are also the questions that will determine whether this year’s optimism becomes a durable recovery or merely a well-produced sales pitch. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/more/global/david-ellison-cinemacon-paramount-windows-streaming-35518/?utm_source=openai))

For now, the evidence says this much: theaters are not done, but they are no longer the unchallenged center of film culture. They have become one part of a larger attention economy in which the theatrical run has to be actively justified, not assumed. That makes every blockbuster more important, every window negotiation more consequential, and every audience insight more valuable. CinemaCon 2026 did not solve that problem. It clarified it. And in the film business, clarity is usually the first step toward a fight. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c3d7ed8782da1dc46d20476a2f9eca9b?utm_source=openai))
