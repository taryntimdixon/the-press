The Dead Should Not Get New Lines

Voice clones, griefbots and posthumous avatars are moving from novelty to market. The ethical mistake is not only that they can deceive. It is that they turn memory into a product that can keep speaking after consent has ended.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 10:09 a.m.

There is now a live consumer market in making the dead seem newly available. Some services promise a cloned voice from a short audio sample. Others offer conversational systems built from old texts, recordings, photographs, or social-media traces. The pitch varies by company and by use case, but the underlying proposition is increasingly familiar: the archive is no longer just something to preserve. It is something to animate.

That change is not merely a product shift. It is a philosophical one. For a long time, digital remains were mostly a problem of storage, access, and custody. Who gets the password? Who can download the photos? Which accounts are deleted, frozen, or memorialized? But generative AI changes the moral category. It does not only keep traces available; it can manufacture fresh outputs in a dead person’s apparent voice, style, or point of view. What had been a record becomes a performer.

The law has not caught up cleanly. Congress has considered the NO FAKES Act of 2025, introduced in the Senate on April 9, 2025, as a federal framework against certain unauthorized digital replicas, but as of April 24, 2026, the bill remains at the introduced stage in the Senate Judiciary Committee. Major platforms, meanwhile, still treat death largely as an account-management problem. Google’s Inactive Account Manager allows users to designate trusted contacts for certain data after inactivity. Apple offers Legacy Contact features and a separate process to request access to or deletion of a deceased person’s account. Facebook memorializes accounts when notified of a death and restricts login to memorialized accounts. Those are rules for succession and custody, not a settled ethics of synthetic posthumous speech.

The public argument about synthetic media often starts elsewhere: election disinformation, celebrity deepfakes, scams, and nonconsensual sexual imagery. Those are real harms, and they matter. The Federal Trade Commission has repeatedly warned that AI-enabled voice cloning can be used in family-emergency and business-impersonation scams, and in 2023 it launched a Voice Cloning Challenge aimed at reducing those harms. In April 2026, Senator Maggie Hassan sent letters to major voice-cloning companies pressing them on safeguards after evidence that some services still relied too heavily on weak verification or self-attestation. Consumer Reports, in a 2025 assessment of six major voice-cloning products, similarly found that most lacked meaningful safeguards against misuse. But fraud is not the whole philosophical story. The quieter and more intimate problem is what happens when memory itself becomes a subscription-ready simulation layer.

My argument is simple. The dead should not get new lines. We may preserve what they actually said. We may quote them, curate them, interpret them, study them, stage them, and remember them. We have always done that. But generating fresh first-person speech in their voice, under their identity, for situations they never encountered, crosses a moral boundary that existing law only partially recognizes. It turns remembrance into authorized ventriloquism. It trains mourners to confuse fidelity with fabrication. And it invites companies to treat bereavement not as a condition to be respected but as a retention problem to be monetized.

Death used to stop the performance

Human beings have never been cleanly finished with the dead. We keep letters. We replay voicemails. We preserve family recipes and marginal notes. We quote the dead at weddings and funerals. We imagine what a parent would have advised. In grief studies, the idea of “continuing bonds” has long complicated the older expectation that mourning is healthy only when the bereaved decisively detach. That complication matters here, because the strongest criticism of grief technology cannot simply be that it prolongs attachment. Attachment often does continue, and not always pathologically.

Recent research reflects that complexity. A 2026 article in the Journal of Responsible Technology argues that debates over AI grief technologies often rely on shaky assumptions about what “normal” grief is supposed to look like, and warns against simplistic claims that such tools are obviously therapeutic or obviously harmful. A 2026 Frontiers in Psychology study similarly found that attitudes toward griefbots are shaped by a mix of ethical concern, emotional openness, and technological readiness rather than a single public consensus. That is useful caution. Mourning is culturally variable, religiously inflected, psychologically uneven, and deeply personal. Any serious ethical framework has to begin by admitting that the living do not all need the same forms of continuity.

Still, the fact that grief is plural does not mean all technologies of mourning are morally interchangeable. A voicemail and a griefbot are not the same kind of thing. A preserved message is evidence of what someone once said. A generative system can produce what they never said at all. A photo album records a life. A conversational avatar stages a fresh performance of that life. A memorial page testifies to absence. A chatbot can simulate availability.

That distinction is not pedantic. It goes to the structure of truth in mourning. Memory is selective, emotional, and imperfect, but it still has an evidentiary dimension. It ties the living, however incompletely, to something that actually happened: a letter really written, a phrase really spoken, a face really photographed, an opinion really held. Generative simulation loosens that bond. It offers plausibility in place of actuality. And once that shift occurs, the archive is no longer only a witness. It becomes raw material for new speech.

Scholars have begun to name that transformation with striking language: digital ghosts, deadbots, griefbots, interactive deadbots, AI afterlives. A 2025 paper, The Making of Digital Ghosts: Designing Ethical AI Afterlives, identifies the central tensions as grief and well-being, truthfulness and deception, consent and posthumous privacy, dignity and misrepresentation, and the commercialization of mourning. Another recent paper in Philosophy & Technology argues that interactive deadbots expose a governance problem broader than familiar privacy law: they blur intimacy, representation, and control in a domain where the subject cannot respond. These writers are circling the same core concern. The dead are being moved from the category of remembered persons to the category of re-performable identities.

An archive says: this person was here. A simulation hints: this person is still available. The first can be loving and truthful at once. The second is where the ethical trouble begins.

Platform policy is about custody, not imitation

One reason the issue can appear more settled than it is is that internet platforms already have death policies. But those policies mostly govern access, not animation. Google’s Inactive Account Manager lets users choose who may receive some account data after prolonged inactivity, and Google says it may also work with immediate family members or representatives in some circumstances regarding a deceased user’s account. Apple’s support materials distinguish between Legacy Contacts designated in advance and later requests for access to or deletion of a deceased family member’s Apple Account. Facebook’s policy is to memorialize an account when the company is informed that the user has died; memorialized accounts are marked as such, protected against login, and given restricted management tools for legacy contacts. In rare cases, Facebook says it may consider requests for additional content from a deceased person’s account, typically requiring proof of authority and a court order.

All of that matters. It shapes the practical reality of digital death. But none of it answers the harder question raised by generative systems: may anyone make the dead say new things?

The gap between custody and imitation is now especially obvious because companies outside the largest platforms can build products from a relatively small set of traces. Consumer tools can clone a voice from brief audio samples. Companies market AI “companions,” life-story systems, memory archives, and digital-legacies products with increasingly conversational interfaces. Bereavement professionals are now discussing griefbots as an operational issue in care. Hospice News reported on April 9, 2026, that hospice and bereavement workers are confronting “new complexities” around AI grief bots, with consent, privacy, and family impact emerging as central concerns. The fact that the care sector has begun taking the issue seriously is itself a sign that this is no longer a science-fiction thought experiment.

What is missing is not awareness that digital remains exist. What is missing is a clear norm separating preservation from synthetic continuation.

Consent after death is not ordinary consent

Defenders of posthumous AI often reach, understandably, for consent. If someone explicitly wanted their voice preserved, their stories searchable, or even their conversational style extended for descendants, why should the rest of us object? If a terminally ill parent wishes to leave interactive messages for a child, is that not an exercise of autonomy rather than a violation of dignity?

Those are serious questions. A flat prohibition on all posthumous computation would be too crude. There are humane cases at the margins: preserving a voice for someone losing speech to ALS, leaving recorded messages for future milestones, organizing family stories so children and grandchildren can access them. The FTC itself has noted that voice-cloning tools have beneficial uses, including for people who lose the ability to speak because of illness or injury. The existence of legitimate uses is one reason the ethical argument has to be more discriminating than a simple anti-technology reflex.

But consent in this context is thinner than it first appears. To consent meaningfully, a person must have some intelligible grasp of the use to which their data and identity will later be put. That is unusually hard here. A person may understand recording a library of messages. They may understand appointing a legacy contact. They may even understand that software can imitate their voice. But can they really foresee the future product forms, business models, emotional dependencies, and downstream training uses that later systems may enable?

This is not an abstract worry. Recent scholarship on AI afterlife and digital legacy repeatedly returns to the problem that consent given at one technological moment may not cleanly authorize what later tools make possible. The 2025 arXiv study “AI Afterlife” as Digital Legacy: Perceptions, Expectations, and Concerns found that users and prospective users frame these systems through a mix of aspiration and unease, especially around boundaries, identity, and future use. The concern is not only whether consent was obtained, but whether anyone can consent in a fully informed way to an open-ended engine of synthetic persona.

Even where prior consent is real, another problem remains. Human speech is accountable because a speaker can be questioned, corrected, contradicted, or asked to clarify what they meant. The dead cannot do that. They cannot retract, object, update, or refuse. Once a system is generating first-person outputs under their name or in their voice, the form of agency is being simulated while the substance of agency is gone. That matters ethically because speech is not just content. It is an act bound to responsibility.

This is where the phrase “the dead should not get new lines” does its work. The issue is not whether we may replay old ones. It is whether we may mint fresh ones when the speaker has permanently lost the power to own them.

The law is moving, but unevenly

American law is beginning to recognize synthetic identity as a live rights problem, though in fragments. At the federal level, the NO FAKES Act of 2025 would create a civil framework aimed at unauthorized digital replicas of voice and visual likeness. As of April 24, 2026, however, the Senate bill remains introduced and referred to committee rather than enacted. Civil-liberties critics, including the Electronic Frontier Foundation, have argued that versions of the proposal risk overbreadth and could chill lawful speech, parody, and creative expression. That critique deserves attention. The choice is not between no rules and perfect rules. It is between a patchwork that misses the deepest harms and new regulation that may itself create speech problems if drafted carelessly.

At the state level, recent changes are more concrete. Tennessee’s ELVIS Act, signed on March 21, 2024, updated the state’s protection of personal rights to include voice in response to AI misuse concerns, especially in the music industry. California already had postmortem publicity protections for certain deceased personalities under Civil Code section 3344.1, and in 2024 it expanded the terrain around digital replicas. AB 1836, signed by Governor Gavin Newsom on September 17, 2024, addresses unauthorized commercial use of digital replicas of deceased performers, while AB 2602 addresses contractual issues involving digital replicas for living performers. Those measures are significant, but they also reveal the current legal emphasis: performers, commercial use, estates, contracts, and entertainment markets.

That emphasis is understandable. Famous people, professional voices, and entertainment uses create obvious economic interests and obvious plaintiffs. But the moral problem is broader than celebrity. The ordinary dead are not less dead because they had no commercial estate. A grandmother’s voice note, a teacher’s old emails, a teenager’s texts, a mechanic’s home videos, a spouse’s recordings of private affection: these may have little market value and enormous dignity value. Existing publicity-rights law only partially captures that. It protects some people especially well because they were profitable identities. It does not fully answer what we owe people whose identities were intimate rather than monetizable.

That is why rights discourse alone is not enough. Ownership is too narrow; privacy is too unstable after death; fraud is too dependent on deception of a particular kind. One can imagine a fully disclosed, family-authorized, noncommercial deadbot that still feels wrong because it stages a person as an ongoing first-person speaker when that person can no longer govern the meaning of what is said. The wrong there is neither pure theft nor pure privacy invasion. It is closer to misattributed agency.

Grief is vulnerable, and the market knows it

The rhetoric around grief technology is predictably soft. Companies speak of remembrance, continuity, tribute, preservation, legacy. Some products are plainly less troubling than others. A searchable archive of recorded stories is not the same as a free-form chatbot. A bounded library of actual clips for future birthdays is not the same as an always-on simulated parent. The category is not morally uniform.

But industries are shaped by incentives, not just labels. A memorial object is finite. A conversational service is recursive. It invites return, prompts attachment, generates data, and can be sold as an ongoing relationship. That difference matters because the most profitable version of posthumous AI is not a static archive but a system that keeps producing fresh interactions: subscriptions, upgraded voices, premium memory features, family plans, enterprise storage, synthetic video, licensed personas, and companion interfaces. The business logic points toward more responsiveness, more realism, more agency, and more engagement.

This should make us cautious for reasons beyond ordinary privacy concerns. Bereavement is a vulnerable condition. It scrambles judgment, magnifies longing, and can make impossible offers feel irresistible. Markets have always circled grief: funeral contracts, memorial upsells, keepsake industries, and a long history of charging the bereaved for forms of meaning at moments when refusal is hardest. AI afterlife systems intensify that pattern because they do not merely sell objects; they sell responsiveness.

The consumer-protection side of the story also overlaps with the grief story more than it first seems. The FTC has repeatedly described how voice cloning can be used in family-emergency scams. Its consumer guidance warns that a cloned voice can sound like a loved one and be used to induce urgent payment or disclosure of personal information. Consumer Reports’ 2025 assessment found that a majority of tested voice-cloning products lacked meaningful safeguards against fraud or misuse. Senator Hassan’s April 16, 2026 letters echoed those concerns, asking companies to explain what they were doing to prevent scammers from exploiting their tools. Local reporting around the United States has continued to document police and Better Business Bureau warnings about AI voice-cloning scams targeting families in 2026.

Why does that matter to the philosophy of grief? Because a culture that normalizes synthetic family voices as commercially ordinary also softens the expectation that a familiar voice corresponds to a present, accountable speaker. The fraud office and the mourning room are not separate worlds. They train the same habits of belief. Once society learns to hear the impossible as emotionally normal, verification burdens spread outward: code words, extra checks, suspicion of recorded speech, uncertainty about provenance. Trust gets more procedural because authenticity has become cheaper to fake.

Counterargument: continuing bonds are real, and some uses are humane

The strongest objection to my argument is neither libertarian nor technological. It is pastoral. People have always spoken to the dead, spoken for the dead, and kept them present through objects, rituals, recordings, and imagination. Why draw a hard line now? If a widower uses a clearly labeled private chatbot built with prior consent, and finds comfort rather than confusion, why should an outsider condemn that? If a terminally ill person wants descendants to ask questions of a curated life-story system later, why deny that wish? If some communities understand memory as ongoing conversation rather than strict archival fidelity, does a ban on synthetic interaction simply impose a narrow, secularized austerity onto plural mourning practices?

This objection has force. There is a real danger that elite discourse treats grief as acceptable only when it follows a tidy modern script of managed closure. The current evidence does not justify declaring that every interactive memorial is inherently traumatic or exploitative. Even the emerging scholarship is more careful than that. Some users may experience comfort, continuity, or a sense of preserved family history. Some use cases, especially involving terminal illness and voluntary voice preservation, are morally different from opportunistic reanimation after death.

But none of this defeats the central distinction. The question is not whether the living may maintain bonds with the dead. Of course they may. The question is whether software should generate fresh first-person performances under a dead person’s identity. Reading an old letter is one thing. Asking a model to write a new one “in Mom’s voice” is another. Playing an actual voicemail is one thing. Prompting a system to answer, as your dead spouse, a question they never heard is another. Family storytelling extends remembrance. Synthetic first-person reply crosses into impersonation, even when everyone present knows it is synthetic.

That is why disclosure is not enough. A label can tell you that an output was It cannot restore the absent person’s agency over what is being said in their name. “This is synthetic” is useful honesty. It is not ethical absolution.

The civic cost arrives at home first

When synthetic-media debates turn public, the focus is usually institutional: elections, courts, finance, celebrity rights. But family life may be where the most consequential norms erode first. Democracies depend on many small truth habits before they depend on grand constitutional ones. We need ordinary expectations that a recorded voice was actually spoken by the person identified, that testimony can be attributed to a source, that archives preserve rather than invent, and that family messages correspond to actual acts of communication.

Those assumptions are already under strain. Once synthetic voices become hard to detect, the epistemic burden shifts onto relationships themselves. Recognition no longer works the way we thought. Familiarity becomes evidentially weaker. Households, not just institutions, inherit the cost.

And that cost is not merely practical. It changes the moral environment of memory. If voices can be readily generated, replayed, edited, and personalized, the temptation grows to treat all testimony as revisable. The dead become especially vulnerable because they leave abundant training material and no possibility of rebuttal. The person who cannot answer back becomes the easiest to script.

There is a reason older moral traditions surrounded the dead with ritual constraint. Constraint was not only about religion or superstition. It was a way of acknowledging that when someone can no longer defend their own meaning, the living acquire duties of restraint. A high-capacity generative market pushes in the opposite direction. It invites us to see every trace as underused creative inventory.

What we need is narrower than ownership and broader than privacy

Current legal categories each catch part of the problem and miss the rest. Privacy law can matter before death and in some postmortem contexts, but privacy interests often weaken once a person dies. Property law can help if there is a transferable economic interest, but many ordinary people do not have a commercially managed identity. Fraud law matters when someone is deceived into parting with money or something of value, but deception is not necessary for every ethical wrong here. Publicity law is strongest where name, voice, or likeness have market significance. None of these categories fully captures the injury of making a dead person appear to speak anew.

That is why scholars and legal commentators have increasingly entertained ideas such as a “right to remain dead” or a right against digital resurrection. The phrase can sound melodramatic, but it names something real. The interest at stake is not just in secrecy, ownership, or anti-fraud protection. It is an interest in non-animation: in not being turned into an active first-person identity system after death without unusually strong, specific, and bounded authorization.

Such a right would need careful drafting to avoid swallowing legitimate speech. It should not ban quotation, documentary reconstruction, satire, historical interpretation, scholarship, or ordinary memorialization. Nor should it collapse all synthetic use into one category. A disclosed restoration of audio quality, or a narrow technical repair to an old recording, is not the same as generating novel statements. The crucial line is between preserving an actual expression and producing a new attributable one.

If I were writing the norm in practical terms, I would start with a presumption against high-agency posthumous simulation. Systems that answer open-ended questions in the first person as the deceased, generate novel advice, converse freely, endorse things, or appear publicly under the dead person’s identity should face the strongest restrictions. Premortem consent should have to be explicit, specific, and revocable while alive; a buried clause in platform terms should not suffice. Family or estate consent should not be enough by itself, because survivors may have interests that diverge from the dead person’s values. Uses should be purpose-limited, access-limited, and time-limited. And design should default toward provenance: clips, transcripts, tagged recordings, source-grounded stories, clear boundaries between actual material and any synthetic interpolation.

In short: preserve the record; distrust the performer.

Dignity matters even when measurable harm is hard to prove

One temptation in technology policy is to wait for cleaner evidence. Do griefbots increase distress? For whom? Under what conditions? Over what period? Those are good empirical questions, and the current literature remains thin. Some users may indeed find comfort. Others may become confused, dependent, or distressed. Families may disagree internally. Longitudinal evidence is sparse.

But moral judgment here does not have to wait for a randomized controlled trial. Not every line of respect depends on a quantified therapeutic effect. Societies prohibit many forms of mistreatment of the dead because they violate dignity and shared norms of reverence, not because we can always measure downstream psychological damage with precision. The dead are in a special position: they are identifiable, emotionally potent, and unable to defend themselves. That combination has always justified constraint.

Posthumous AI belongs partly in that register. The deepest objection is not simply that some griefbots might confuse mourners or facilitate scams. It is that they treat a human life’s remnants as material for continued output under a name that can no longer authorize it. That is a dignity problem even when everyone involved is sincere. Affection does not eliminate the risk of misuse. Family love can itself become the medium through which a dead person is over-scripted.

In 2025, backlash followed the use of an That case matters precisely because it was not a clear fraud or obvious theft. It was a reminder that authorization from survivors does not automatically settle whether new speech in the dead person’s voice should have been made at all.

The same lesson appears in entertainment law’s recent evolution. California’s 2024 digital-replica legislation for deceased performers and Tennessee’s ELVIS Act both reflect a growing recognition that synthetic voice and likeness are not just another species of copyrighted asset. They touch personhood. Yet the legal frame still leans toward labor markets, commercial exploitation, and celebrity estates. The philosophical challenge is wider. Ordinary people, too, can be wronged by posthumous fabrication.

A better ambition: memorial infrastructure without synthetic availability

The humane alternative is not a digitally puritanical refusal of all technological help in grief. It is better memorial infrastructure. Families need clearer tools for preserving actual recordings, stories, passwords, photographs, and context before crisis arrives. Estate planning should treat digital remains as a routine matter rather than an improvisation. Platforms should make export, succession, and memorialization legible without nudging people toward endless interaction products. Journalists, museums, and documentary makers should adopt stricter norms for any synthetic reconstruction of deceased people, especially when first-person narration is involved.

Design matters here. If a person with a terminal diagnosis wants to leave future birthday messages, the system should preserve messages, not convert them into a generalized conversational agent. If a family wants children to access stories later, the archive should return source-grounded clips, transcripts, and provenance rather than improvise in the dead person’s voice. If restoration is needed for a historical recording, the line between repair and invention should be made explicit. The design ideal should be bounded remembrance, not simulated ongoing presence.

This approach also fits a wider social need. In an era of cheap generative persuasion, we need more spaces governed by testimonial restraint rather than by frictionless fabrication. Death is one of those spaces. A person who can no longer answer back should not become easier, rather than harder, to script.

The case against giving the dead new lines is therefore not a plea for emotional austerity. It is a plea for moral accuracy. Love after death is real. Continuing bonds are real. The desire to hear one more sentence in a familiar voice is among the most human desires imaginable. But desire is not the same thing as permission, and comfort is not the same thing as fidelity. Our tools are getting better at simulating presence. That is exactly why our norms must become stricter about what should remain absent.

The dead can still teach, comfort, and matter without being made to perform. Their words, where they left them, are enough. Our task is not to squeeze more output from them. It is to keep faith with what they actually said, and with the silence they can no longer break.
