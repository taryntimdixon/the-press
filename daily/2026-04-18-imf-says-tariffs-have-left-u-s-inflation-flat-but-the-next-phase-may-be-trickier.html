IMF says tariffs have left U.S. inflation flat, but the next phase may be trickier

A new IMF review says tariff-driven goods inflation has offset softer services prices, keeping inflation stuck in place as growth holds up — for now.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 18, 2026 • 2:25 p.m. • 980 words

The International Monetary Fund’s new assessment of the U.S. economy lands at an awkward moment for policymakers: growth has held up better than many feared, but tariffs are now shaping both prices and the outlook in ways that make the next few quarters harder to read.

In its April 2026 Article IV consultation, the IMF said U.S. growth reached 2% in 2025 despite a fourth-quarter government shutdown and a series of policy shifts. Inflation, meanwhile, “moved sideways” last year because tariff-driven goods inflation offset declines in services inflation. The fund expects growth to tick up to 2.4% in 2026, but it also says the effective tariff rate on imports into the United States is likely to settle in a range of 7% to 8.5% after recent changes to tariff authority take effect. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

That combination — sturdier growth, stubborn inflation and tariffs that are not yet fully reflected in the data — is why the report is being watched so closely on Wall Street and in Washington. The IMF argues that the tariff impulse should fade over time and help core PCE inflation return to 2% in the first half of 2027, but it also warns that higher energy prices could keep inflation risk tilted to the upside. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

Why tariffs are showing up in prices, not just politics

The IMF’s core message is that tariffs are no longer just a trade-policy story; they are now part of the inflation story. The fund says U.S. inflation stayed flat through 2025 even as goods prices were pushed up by tariffs and services inflation eased. That means tariff costs are working less like a one-time shock and more like a persistent offset to the disinflation that policymakers had hoped would continue after the post-pandemic price surge.

The effect is especially important because tariff pressure tends to be uneven. Businesses can absorb some of the cost, delay passing it on, or shift sourcing over time. But when tariffs are broad and trade policy is changing quickly, companies often have little choice but to protect margins with price increases. That is one reason the IMF expects the tariff impulse to remain visible in the data even as the overall economy stays relatively resilient. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

The IMF’s latest world outlook also reinforces that broader point. In its April 2026 forecast, it said the global economy is again facing stress from war in the Middle East, elevated trade barriers and tighter financial conditions, with global growth projected to slow to 3.1% in 2026. The report described renewed trade tensions as one of the risks that could damage growth and financial stability. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

The Federal Reserve’s difficult middle ground

For the Federal Reserve, the IMF’s report highlights a familiar problem: the economy is not weak enough to demand emergency easing, but inflation is not settled enough to make policy easy. The IMF says unemployment should remain near 4% in 2026 and 2027, suggesting the labor market is cooling only gradually. At the same time, employment growth is expected to slow to less than half the pace seen in the five years before the pandemic, reflecting both slower population growth and weaker labor demand. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

That leaves the Fed in a narrow corridor. If inflation remains above target because tariffs keep feeding into goods prices, rate cuts become harder to justify. If growth weakens more sharply than expected, the central bank could be forced to respond to a slowdown it did not cause. The IMF’s forecast is unusually useful because it implies both outcomes remain possible: near-term risks to activity and unemployment are “broadly balanced,” even as inflation risks remain skewed upward. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

Recent government data suggest the picture is not deteriorating all at once, but it is far from settled. The Census Bureau said retail trade sales were up 0.6% in February from January and up 3.5% from a year earlier, indicating that consumer spending has not fallen off a cliff. Still, fresh trade and inflation data are likely to be scrutinized for signs that tariff costs are starting to filter more clearly into household budgets. ([census.gov](https://www.census.gov/retail/marts/www/marts_current.pdf?utm_source=openai))

A stronger economy, but with more debt and more uncertainty

The IMF’s U.S. review is not a recession warning. In fact, it is a reminder that the U.S. has entered 2026 with more momentum than many peers. But the fund also sees strain building beneath the surface. It says the federal deficit narrowed slightly in 2025, to 5.9% of GDP from 6.3%, yet general government debt rose to 123.9% of GDP and is projected to keep climbing. The IMF expects the deficit to remain in the 7% to 7.5% range over the medium term, with debt exceeding 140% of GDP by 2031. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

Those numbers matter because tariffs are being layered onto an already delicate fiscal and monetary environment. When inflation is sticky, growth is modest and debt is high, every policy shock has a bigger ripple effect. Tariffs can add revenue, but they also tend to raise costs for consumers and businesses. That can make the political case for protectionism easier while making the economic case for stability harder. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

The global backdrop is not helping. The IMF’s April outlook says war-related commodity price increases, lingering trade barriers and uncertainty are all clouding the path ahead. Even if the U.S. remains stronger than many advanced economies, the rest of the world matters: weaker global demand can feed back into U.S. exporters, supply chains and corporate earnings. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

That is why the key question now is not whether tariffs are moving the economy, but how long it takes for those effects to show up in full. The IMF’s answer is that the lag is still playing out. The U.S. economy has been resilient enough to absorb the first round. The harder test may be whether it can keep growing once the second round arrives in prices, wages and profits. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))
