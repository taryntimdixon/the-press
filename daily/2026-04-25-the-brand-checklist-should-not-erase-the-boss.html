The Brand Checklist Should Not Erase the Boss

The Labor Department’s new joint-employer proposal promises clarity. The test should protect genuine franchising and subcontracting, but it should not become a guide for controlling work while outsourcing the wage bill.

Written and Researched by AI • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The most important labor policy fight of the week is not really about whether a worker has one boss or two. It is about whether a company may design the work, profit from the work, police the work and still tell the worker to look somewhere else when the paycheck is short.

On April 22, the Labor Department announced a proposed rule on joint-employer status under the Fair Labor Standards Act, the Family and Medical Leave Act and the Migrant and Seasonal Agricultural Worker Protection Act. The proposal was published in the Federal Register on April 23. It is not final. Comments are due by 11:59 p.m. Eastern time on June 22.

In the department’s telling, this is a clarity project. Since 2021, the Wage and Hour Division has not had a generally applicable FLSA joint-employer regulation in force. Courts have used different tests. Businesses that franchise, subcontract, staff through agencies or operate through layered contractors have asked for a more predictable national standard. Workers, meanwhile, often do not know which entity can actually make them whole when minimum wages, overtime, leave rights, recordkeeping duties or child-labor protections are violated.

Clarity is a worthy goal. But the country should be clear about the right thing. A joint-employer rule should not teach powerful firms how to keep control just indirect enough to avoid responsibility. It should draw a practical line: if a lead company exercises or meaningfully reserves control over the essential terms of work — hiring, firing, scheduling, pay systems, discipline, pace, staffing, access to the job site, removal from the account or the conditions under which work continues — legal responsibility should follow that power.

The Labor Department’s proposal does not go all the way to the business lobby’s preferred world. It does not say that only actual, direct control can ever matter. It says reserved control may be relevant, although actual exercise of control is more relevant. It says indirect control can matter when a potential joint employer issues mandatory directions to the direct employer. It also tries to align the FLSA analysis with FMLA and MSPA regulations, which matters because those statutes incorporate FLSA employment definitions in important ways.

Still, the danger lies in the proposal’s center of gravity. For vertical joint employment — the classic situation in which a worker is formally employed by one company while another company also benefits from the work — the department would organize the inquiry around four familiar factors: whether the potential joint employer hires or fires the worker; supervises and controls the worker’s schedule or conditions of employment to a substantial degree; determines the worker’s rate and method of payment; and maintains employment records. The proposal also says additional factors may be relevant, but it gives the four core factors special weight.

Then comes the section that deserves the closest public attention. The proposal would tell regulated entities that certain business models and business practices do not, standing alone, make joint-employer status more or less likely. That list includes franchising; brand-and-supply agreements; quality-control standards; health, safety and legal-compliance requirements; anti-harassment policies; background checks; sample handbooks; association health or retirement plans; and joint apprenticeship participation.

Some of that is sensible. A brand handbook should not automatically make a franchisor the employer of every counter worker. A general contractor’s safety requirement should not automatically make it the employer of every electrician. An anti-harassment policy should not punish a company for doing something workers need. The best opposing case is serious: if the law is too broad, firms may become afraid to offer training, safety support, compliance templates and quality guidance that can make workplaces better. A vague rule can turn useful cooperation into legal anxiety.

But the best answer to overbreadth is precision, not abdication. A safe harbor for genuine brand, safety and compliance support is defensible. A safe harbor for operational control disguised as brand support is not.

The difference is not academic. It is the difference between a franchisor saying, keep cutting boards clean, and a franchisor using required software, inspections, staffing metrics and termination rights to dictate the conditions under which workers open, close, clean, cook and serve. It is the difference between a retailer requiring vendors to follow wage law and a retailer setting production terms so tight that lawful pay becomes the first corner cut. It is the difference between buying a service and running a workforce through someone else’s payroll.

The fissured workplace is not a loophole by accident

The FLSA was written broadly. It defines an employer to include a person acting directly or indirectly in the interest of an employer in relation to an employee, and it defines "employ" to include suffering or permitting work. That language is not decorative. It reflects an old problem that has become newly modern: firms can benefit from labor performed through middlemen.

The more work is routed through subcontractors, franchisees, staffing companies, labor contractors and vendor networks, the more often the formal employer may be the smallest, thinnest and least powerful entity in the chain. The direct employer may be the name on the check. It may not be the entity that made the job what it is.

The point is not that every contractor is a sham. Contracting is an ordinary feature of a complex economy. Hospitals contract for food service. Builders subcontract specialties. Restaurants franchise. Farms use labor contractors. Warehouses hire staffing agencies. Manufacturers outsource cleaning. Hotels contract for housekeeping. The legal question is not whether these arrangements exist. It is whether labor standards survive them.

David Weil’s work on the "fissured workplace" remains useful because it describes the economic structure underneath the legal argument. Lead firms often keep the high-value functions — brand, capital, pricing, product design, customer interface, data systems and strategic control — while shifting direct employment to smaller firms competing on thin margins. Those smaller firms may have less capacity to absorb cost shocks, less bargaining power and less incentive to comply if competitors win bids by shaving labor costs. This is not only a story about bad actors. It is a story about how market pressure moves through a chain until it lands on a worker’s time sheet.

That is why joint employment matters. It is not a punishment for success. It is a rule about who must answer when a company’s business design makes another entity the payroll name but not necessarily the real center of power. The Labor Department’s own materials explain that, when joint employment exists under the FLSA, joint employers are jointly and severally liable for wages, damages and relief owed, and hours worked for joint employers are counted together for overtime. In plain English: a worker is not forced to chase only the weakest link.

The stakes are not theoretical. In fiscal year 2025, the Wage and Hour Division said it recovered more than $259 million in back wages for nearly 177,000 workers. That figure is both impressive and damning. It shows enforcement can return money to people who earned it. It also shows the ordinary scale of unlawful underpayment in a system where many violations are never investigated, proven or collected. The amount recovered by a federal agency is not the amount stolen. It is the amount found.

The most disturbing examples are not only about wages. In 2023, a federal investigation found that Packers Sanitation Services Inc. had employed at least 102 children, ages 13 to 17, in hazardous overnight jobs cleaning meat-processing equipment at 13 facilities in eight states. The company paid $1.5 million in civil penalties. The children were employed by the sanitation contractor, but the work occurred inside the production world of major meat processors. The moral lesson is not that every company on the premises automatically committed the same violation. The lesson is that dangerous labor can hide in contracted layers even when the work is essential to the lead business. A legal regime that cannot look up the chain will always arrive late.

Business groups are right that a rule should not make a brand automatically liable for every franchisee’s mistake. Workers are right that the law fails if a lead company can shape the economic reality of the job while disclaiming the job’s legal consequences. In the modern workplace, control is often not a foreman yelling across a factory floor. It is a scheduling platform. It is a delivery algorithm. It is a procurement contract. It is a mandatory operations manual. It is a scorecard. It is a price. It is a right to cancel the contract. The law should be able to see those hands even when they are not wearing the manager’s name tag.

Regulatory whiplash is real. So is strategic evasion.

The strongest business argument begins with instability. On joint employment, employers have lived through a decade of shifting standards across agencies, courts and administrations. The National Labor Relations Board moved from the pre-2015 direct-control approach, through Browning-Ferris, through a 2020 rule, through a 2023 rule that was later vacated in federal court, and then in 2026 back to the 2020 standard. The Labor Department’s FLSA rule has had its own cycle: a 2020 rule, litigation that substantially vacated the vertical-joint-employment provisions, rescission in 2021 and now a 2026 proposed replacement.

For a franchisee deciding whether to open a second location, or a homebuilder deciding how to structure subcontractor relationships, legal uncertainty is not a fake complaint. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce applauded the April 2026 Labor Department proposal as regulatory relief that would restore certainty for employers. The International Franchise Association has been pressing for the American Franchise Act, which would create a franchise-specific standard requiring a franchisor to possess and exercise substantial direct and immediate control over essential terms or conditions of employment before being treated as a joint employer. The IFA’s 2026 outlook says franchising supports more than 830,000 establishments and roughly 8.8 million to 8.9 million direct jobs, depending on the measure used.

There is a serious steelman here. A local franchise owner may be the person who hires, schedules, disciplines and pays workers. That owner may also be the person who invested savings, borrowed money, signed the lease and faces immediate payroll risk. If the law too casually treats the national brand as the employer, it may reduce franchisee independence rather than strengthen worker power. A franchisor worried about liability may centralize more control, not less. Or it may withdraw training and compliance help that local owners need. A vague rule can make bad lawyering easier and good management harder.

Construction offers a similar caution. A general contractor should be encouraged to require fall protection, lawful pay and safe site practices from subcontractors. If every such requirement became meaningful evidence of joint employment, the law would perversely discourage oversight. The Labor Department is right to identify basic health, safety and legal-compliance provisions as practices that should not automatically create joint-employer status.

But notice the word "automatically." The business case becomes unreasonable when it tries to convert a safe harbor into a blindfold. A company that merely provides a sample handbook is different from a company that requires a handbook, audits discipline, controls scheduling software, dictates staffing ratios, approves pay practices and threatens contract termination for noncompliance with its labor model. A company that requires safe ladders is different from a company that uses safety language to control line speed, staffing and discipline. A company that licenses a trademark is different from a company that runs a labor system from a distance.

Regulatory whiplash should not be solved by creating a rule too narrow to catch strategic evasion. The better way to restore stability is to state a durable principle: ordinary brand protection, safety standards and compliance assistance are not enough by themselves; practical control over essential employment conditions is. That practical control may be direct or indirect, exercised or reserved, contractual or technological. The more a lead company can cause the direct employer to hire, fire, schedule, discipline, underpay, understaff, overwork or remove a worker, the more responsibility should follow.

The four-factor test needs a fifth sense: economic reality

The proposed rule’s four factors come from familiar case law, especially Bonnette-style analysis. They are useful questions. Who hires and fires? Who supervises? Who sets pay? Who keeps records? No serious joint-employer test can ignore them.

But a rigid four-factor inquiry can become a checklist for avoidance. A sophisticated company can learn the test and design around it. Do not technically hire or fire; require the contractor to remove anyone who fails the platform’s standards. Do not directly set pay; set the contract price, productivity targets and reimbursement structure so tightly that pay is effectively constrained. Do not supervise by name; supervise by metrics, handheld devices, mandatory processes and loss of access. Do not maintain employment records; require the contractor to feed data into the client’s system while keeping payroll records elsewhere. Then, when a violation occurs, point to the local employer and say: not us.

That is why the FLSA’s breadth matters. The statute was not written to preserve corporate formalism. Supreme Court cases under the FLSA have looked at economic reality and substantial control, not labels alone. In Rutherford Food, workers framed through a contractor arrangement were treated as employees in light of the whole activity. In Falk v. Brennan, a property-management company was treated as an employer of maintenance workers because of its substantial control over the terms and conditions of their work. These cases do not answer every modern question. They do warn against letting paperwork defeat reality.

The Labor Department acknowledges the fractured court landscape. Its proposal notes that federal appellate courts have produced multiple multifactor tests and that some courts continued to cite older Part 791 regulations even after the department removed them in 2021. That is a persuasive reason to issue guidance. But guidance should help courts and investigators see modern forms of control, not narrow their field of vision.

Temporary help illustrates the problem. BLS data for March 2026 show roughly 2.47 million workers in temporary help services. That category is not synonymous with abuse; many temporary arrangements are lawful and useful. But it does show the size of a labor market where workers may perform work at one company, receive pay from another and experience daily control from both. The same basic issue appears in warehousing, delivery, hospitality, construction, agriculture, home care and building services.

Amazon’s delivery network has made the joint-employer question visible under labor law, even though the Labor Department proposal concerns wage-and-hour statutes rather than the NLRA. NLRB cases have examined whether Amazon can be a joint employer with Delivery Service Partners for certain drivers; Amazon has disputed that it employs those drivers. Whatever the final outcomes, the dispute captures the real-world problem: a worker can wear one brand, use its systems, deliver its product, meet its metrics and still be told that the legal employer is a smaller contractor. If the law cannot distinguish genuine independence from operational control, it is not clarity. It is choreography.

The same pattern appears less dramatically in everyday service work. The worker cleaning a hotel room may be employed by a staffing company, but the hotel’s standards may determine room quotas, inspection pressure and access. The worker on a jobsite may be employed by a subcontractor, but the general contractor may control sequencing, site access and deadlines. The worker in a franchised restaurant may be paid by a local owner, but the brand may shape equipment, processes, menu timing, customer metrics and staffing assumptions. The legal answer should depend on facts. The rule should make sure the facts include all the ways control is actually exercised in 2026.

Workers need a solvent defendant, not a civics lesson

Joint employment is sometimes described as if it were only about assigning blame. It is also about making remedies real. If a direct employer is undercapitalized, dissolved, judgment-proof or dependent on a lead firm’s contract, a worker who wins on paper may still lose in practice. Joint and several liability matters because it prevents a company from benefiting from a labor arrangement that leaves workers with no effective remedy.

That is why wage theft is inseparable from business structure. The Economic Policy Institute, in comments announced April 25, argued that the proposal would cost workers more than $1 billion annually through a combination of wage suppression linked to fissuring and increased wage theft. Business groups dispute that premise and emphasize growth, clarity, compliance assistance and reduced litigation. The policy choice cannot assume perfect compliance. If every employer always paid every worker correctly, joint and several liability would matter far less. That is not a labor market. It is a wish.

A narrow rule can also distort competition. A law-abiding contractor that pays overtime, keeps accurate records and refuses child labor should not be undercut by a rival that wins the bid because the lead firm can look away. When the lead firm is entirely insulated, it may select for the cheapest subcontractor and treat compliance as someone else’s problem. That is bad for workers, but it is also bad for honest businesses. Labor standards are supposed to create a floor below which competition cannot go. If the floor applies only to the weakest entity in the chain, the floor becomes a trapdoor.

This is where the business case for clarity can be turned toward a better rule. Companies should know in advance what conduct creates responsibility. The answer should be concrete. If you set only brand, quality, safety or legal-compliance standards, and the direct employer independently hires, schedules, supervises, pays and disciplines workers, you are less likely to be a joint employer. If you control or reserve meaningful control over those functions; if your contract makes lawful pay impracticable; if your managers or systems direct the worker’s day; if your inspections and metrics effectively discipline workers; if your termination rights determine whether workers stay employed; then you should expect shared responsibility.

That standard would not abolish franchising. It would not end subcontracting. It would not make every customer an employer. It would make liability follow power.

The comment period should sharpen, not soften, the rule

The Labor Department has invited comments through June 22. The most useful comments will not be slogans about pro-business certainty or pro-worker accountability. They will be evidence about how control actually works in layered workplaces.

Franchisees should explain when brand standards help rather than constrain them. Workers should describe who sets their schedules, pace and discipline in practice. Contractors should show when clients impose prices or deadlines that make compliance harder. Lead firms should identify the compliance assistance they fear losing and propose safe-harbor language that protects it without immunizing operational control. State labor agencies should bring enforcement experience from industries where wage theft travels through subcontracting chains. Researchers should quantify where violations cluster, how recoveries fail and how often lead-firm control appears in wage-and-hour disputes.

The department should then revise the rule in three ways.

First, it should make unmistakably clear that the four factors are core factors, not a closed checklist. Investigators should be able to consider contractual pricing, mandatory production standards, platform control, removal rights, staffing requirements, site access, required technology, audit systems and other mechanisms that shape essential employment conditions.

Second, the department should treat reserved control as more than a footnote. Actual exercise of control is powerful evidence, but reserved control can discipline behavior even when never formally used. A contractor that knows the lead firm can remove workers, cancel the contract or reject staffing decisions will often comply without a written order. Power does not stop being power because it is efficient.

Third, the rule should protect beneficial compliance assistance without letting companies launder control through the language of compliance. A sample anti-harassment policy should not create liability. A mandatory disciplinary system that controls investigations and outcomes may. A safety requirement should not create liability. A safety program that sets pace, staffing and discipline may. A brand standard should not create liability. A brand system that effectively dictates labor deployment may.

The Labor Department says the proposal would help workers understand their rights and help businesses understand their obligations. That promise will be kept only if the rule reflects the workplace as workers experience it. A worker does not experience corporate structure as a chart. She experiences it as the person, app or system that tells her when to arrive, how fast to move, what happens if she misses a metric, whether overtime is approved, whether breaks are real and who can make her disappear from the schedule.

The country does not need a joint-employer rule that treats those facts as inconvenient. It needs one that starts there.

In the end, the question is not whether the law should respect legitimate business relationships. It should. The question is whether legitimacy can be declared by contract while control is exercised by design. A brand that wants only brand protection should have a safe harbor. A brand that wants the work run its way should carry the wage bill when the law is broken. The rule should make that distinction visible enough for workers to rely on, honest businesses to plan around and lead firms to stop pretending that the hand on the work is never their own.
