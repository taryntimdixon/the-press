Sudan’s war is redrawing borders, aid routes and migration across three fronts

As fighting in Sudan enters its fourth year, the conflict is spilling into Chad, South Sudan and Libya, exposing the limits of border controls and the fragility of humanitarian access.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 22, 2026 • 1:39 p.m. • 2977 words

Sudan’s war is no longer just a national tragedy. As the conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces moves into its fourth year, it has become a border story, a migration story, a logistics story and, increasingly, a regional systems story. The World Health Organization says Sudan now accounts for the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, with 34 million people needing aid, 21 million needing health assistance and 37 percent of health facilities non-functional. UN humanitarian officials say the country remains the world’s largest displacement crisis. The war’s significance now extends far beyond the fighting inside Sudan: it is reshaping crossings into Chad and South Sudan, complicating relief corridors into Darfur, feeding movement through Egypt and Libya, and testing whether neighboring states and aid agencies can keep pace with a crisis that has become both regional and protracted. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

The most immediate consequence is human movement. UN and agency reporting show that the war has driven millions from their homes inside Sudan and pushed large numbers across borders. UNHCR’s Sudan situation overview says the conflict has produced the largest displacement crisis of the year, with 1.5 million people seeking safety abroad in the period it tracks, while WHO says about 13.6 million people were displaced by early 2026. UN agencies also say Sudan has now sent more than 1.3 million people into South Sudan and another 1.3 million into Chad since the war began in April 2023. Egypt has taken in large numbers as well. This is not simply a refugee emergency. It is an administrative and security emergency in which border posts, local clinics, school systems and customs checkpoints have become frontline institutions. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/09-01-2026-sudan-1000-days-of-war-deepen-the-world-s-worst-health-and-humanitarian-crisis?utm_source=openai))

The war’s second-order effects are equally important. Once a conflict becomes regional, it changes the politics of aid. Humanitarian agencies must negotiate with armed actors, persuade neighboring governments to keep crossings usable, and secure fuel, warehousing, transport, health staff and cash programming in places where state presence is thin. UN briefings in April have emphasized severe needs inside Sudan and mounting pressure on refugees in Chad, while the International Organization for Migration has repeatedly warned that migration routes across Africa and toward the Mediterranean remain lethal. The result is a system built for short shocks being asked to absorb a long border crisis. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-09.html?utm_source=openai))

What changed in April 2026

April has brought fresh evidence that the regional footprint of the war is widening rather than stabilizing. On April 14, WHO said Sudan’s health emergency had deepened after three years of conflict, repeating the figure that 34 million people need aid, 21 million need health assistance and more than a third of health facilities are out of service. UN officials also said attacks on health care continue, with WHO recording 133 attacks since the start of the conflict. The point is not only that the crisis is severe. It is that the damage is cumulative: conflict, disease outbreaks, hunger, destroyed clinics and repeated displacement are reinforcing one another. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

That churn is visible at the borders. In Chad, humanitarian agencies say the refugee response is now under acute strain. UNHCR reported in mid-April that Chad hosted more than 1.3 million people who had fled Sudan, while more than 900,000 crossed since the war began. UNHCR and WFP warned that essential aid to refugees in Chad could be drastically scaled back unless a funding shortfall of US$428 million is filled, and UNHCR said more than 243,000 people remained in eastern border areas because the operation lacks the money to move them inland. Far from a short-term reception point, eastern Chad has become a holding zone where families wait for services that are thin, delayed or unavailable. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

South Sudan tells a similar but distinct story. It is receiving Sudanese refugees and South Sudanese returnees at the same time, while also coping with its own internal insecurity, flooding and weak public services. UNHCR says the South Sudan situation remains one of Africa’s most significant refugee crises, with 2.3 million refugees and asylum-seekers in neighboring countries and millions of people displaced at home. UNHCR’s work in South Sudan now includes supporting returnees who came back from Sudan under difficult conditions, and the agency says many return to areas with damaged infrastructure and limited services. The border is porous in both directions, so disturbances in Sudan rapidly become an internal governance problem for South Sudan: who registers arrivals, who vaccinates children, who provides water and shelter, and who protects women and girls when families are stranded far from courts or police. ([reporting.unhcr.org](https://reporting.unhcr.org/operational/situations/south-sudan-situation?utm_source=openai))

Chad: the northern safety valve with the thinnest margin for error

Chad has borne some of the heaviest spillover costs of the war. It lies closest to the fighting in Darfur and has absorbed one of the largest refugee movements in Africa. UNHCR says Chad is hosting more than 1.3 million people who fled Sudan, and that its borders have remained open despite immense strain on resources. The country’s refugee response is underfunded, and UNHCR’s own reporting says border communities continue to receive new arrivals even as aid has been cut back. In practical terms, the border with Sudan is not a line on a map but a zone of informal settlements, transit sites, local negotiation and humanitarian improvisation. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

The strain in Chad is not only about numbers; it is about location and timing. Many refugees arrive in remote eastern provinces with weak roads, limited clinics and sparse administrative capacity. The International Rescue Committee has warned that health centers near the border are under severe pressure, while Refugees International says aid, protection and accountability efforts are lagging, leaving women and children especially exposed to hunger, violence and disease. UNHCR’s own country strategy for Chad describes a long-running and complex burden that predates this war and now has to absorb a major new influx. That is why the problem is not merely humanitarian. It is institutional. When funding is delayed, plans for relocation, schooling and health services collapse into emergency triage. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/us/news/briefing-notes/unhcr-warns-crisis-reaching-breaking-point-sudanese-refugee-numbers-triple-chad?utm_source=openai))

The border itself is also a security problem. Armed groups, smugglers and traffickers exploit displacement corridors; local authorities face pressure to screen arrivals while avoiding abusive practices; and aid workers often depend on the same roads and checkpoints as military actors. UNHCR and other agencies have repeatedly stressed the need to move refugees away from the border into more stable settlements, but the number of people requiring relocation is large and the resources available are not. The consequence is that migration management and security policy are no longer separate files. They are the same file, and in eastern Chad that file is being edited in real time. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

South Sudan: returns, reverse flows and fragile reception capacity

South Sudan is not only receiving people from Sudan. It is also receiving large numbers of its own citizens who had been living in Sudan, along with other people moving in search of food, work and safety. UNHCR says the war in Sudan has pushed more than one million people into South Sudan, counting Sudanese refugees and South Sudanese returnees together. Its 2025 and 2026 planning documents warn that returnees face enormous challenges, especially when they come back to areas with damaged infrastructure and limited services. That makes the border between the two countries a pressure point where the collapse of one state’s services immediately strains the other’s. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/unhcr-and-partners-support-safe-and-sustainable-integration-returnees-south?utm_source=openai))

One underappreciated aspect of the story is the way displacement changes local political economies. When families move into border counties, they need food, housing, firewood, water, transport and paperwork. Prices rise. Labor markets shift. Local chiefs and administrators are asked to mediate disputes over land and grazing. If aid is late, host communities can turn resentful. If aid is targeted only at newcomers, existing residents feel abandoned. If aid is spread too thinly, nobody gets enough. The temptation in policy circles is to define this as a humanitarian issue only, but it is also a development issue and a legitimacy issue. Governments lose credibility when they cannot provide basic services to both hosts and arrivals. ([reporting.unhcr.org](https://reporting.unhcr.org/operational/situations/south-sudan-situation?utm_source=openai))

There is also a more politically sensitive question: whether some returns are truly voluntary. In a prolonged war, people may go back because they cannot survive where they are, not because home is safe. UNHCR’s language about safe, dignified and sustainable return reflects that risk. The agency says many returnees arrive in areas with limited services, and UNHCR’s Sudan appeal for 2026 says insecurity and the worsening humanitarian situation have pushed nearly 870,000 refugees who had been living in Sudan to return to their countries of origin, often under dangerous conditions, with the majority going to South Sudan. A return driven by exhaustion or coercion is not a durable solution; it is a change of address inside a crisis. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/sites/default/files/2026-02/UNHCR%20Sudan%20Situation%20Appeal%202026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Libya and Egypt: the wider migration corridor

Sudan’s war is also feeding into the broader migration system across northeastern Africa and the Mediterranean. Libya remains a key transit country, and Human Rights Watch says abusive detention centers and prisons continue to be controlled by armed groups nominally linked to authorities. Its 2026 Libya country chapter says the International Organization for Migration recorded 894,890 migrants in Libya as of July 2025 and that, by December 2025, more than 467,000 Sudanese refugees fleeing the conflict since April 2023 had arrived there, though only a fraction were registered. This matters because not all those on the route are moving for the same reason: some are Sudanese escaping war, others are migrants and asylum seekers from elsewhere in Africa who use the same dangerous corridors. ([hrw.org](https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2026/country-chapters/libya?utm_source=openai))

Libya is a warning about what happens when border control becomes detention policy. Human Rights Watch says migrants in Libya face arbitrary detention, torture, beatings, deprivation of food and water, forced labor and sexual violence in centers that are often outside meaningful legal oversight. When people fleeing one war end up trapped in another abusive system, the migration story becomes a rights story and a regional security story at the same time. Abuse along these routes strengthens smuggling networks, deepens mistrust of authorities and increases the likelihood that more people will attempt riskier crossings. ([hrw.org](https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2026/country-chapters/libya?utm_source=openai))

Egypt is part of the same broader equation, though its role is different. UNHCR says Egypt had 1,099,595 refugees and asylum-seekers registered as of March 31, 2026, including 845,957 Sudanese. A separate February 2026 operational update said UNHCR had provided registration appointments to more than 1.03 million people who fled Sudan since the war began, and that the country’s active registered refugee population topped 1.09 million. Egypt is therefore not simply a transit point. It is a large receiving country with its own domestic pressures, a major registration burden and a large Sudanese refugee community. That combination is likely to keep shaping migration policy well beyond the war’s immediate battlefield dynamics. ([data.unhcr.org](https://data.unhcr.org/en/country/egy?utm_source=openai))

Aid corridors, ceasefires and the politics of access

The most consequential battlefield in Sudan’s war may not be the front line but the access route. Humanitarian operations depend on roads and permissions that can be closed as easily as military positions. UN statements in April pointed to ongoing attacks on civilians and aid systems, while WHO and others keep repeating the basic demand for safe, rapid and unimpeded humanitarian access. The repetition is itself revealing. If this were only a matter of funds, money would fix more of the problem. But when roads are cut, warehouses are looted, convoys are delayed and authorization changes hands among armed actors, money cannot buy access on its own. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-09.html?utm_source=openai))

Access also sits at the intersection of diplomacy and coercion. Neighboring states have incentives to keep some corridors open because the alternative is uncontrolled displacement and deeper instability. Yet opening a corridor often requires engagement with the very actors causing the displacement. That creates political discomfort, especially when atrocities, sanctions, military support networks and accusations of outside involvement are part of the picture. The system therefore oscillates between humanitarian pragmatism and political condemnation. Both impulses are legitimate. Neither is sufficient on its own. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-14.html?utm_source=openai))

There is a temptation to treat border openings as technical gestures. In reality, they are political acts with economic consequences. The opening or closure of a crossing affects market prices, fuel supplies, vaccination campaigns, school access and the ability of separated families to reunite. It can also determine whether aid gets into an enclave before the rainy season, whether medicine arrives before an outbreak spreads, and whether people trapped inside conflict areas can cross to safety. That is why any coverage of Sudan’s war that stops at the front line misses the story’s most durable effects. The border is where war meets administration. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-09.html?utm_source=openai))

Counterargument: regional spillover does not automatically mean regional collapse

It would be wrong to suggest that the border states are simply buckling. One reason the system has not collapsed is that neighbors have, so far, absorbed immense pressure with significant resilience. Chad continues to host very large numbers of refugees; South Sudan continues to keep its frontier workable enough for movement and aid; Libya, despite grave abuses, remains a functioning if violent transit state; and international agencies still manage to move supplies, run clinics and register arrivals. Those are real capacities, not symbolic ones. They deserve recognition because crisis narratives can flatten local agency into helplessness. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

There is also a reasonable argument that regional spillover can create diplomatic leverage. Neighboring governments, donors and UN agencies have more reason to coordinate when a war threatens to spread disease, disrupt trade and increase irregular migration. In theory, a shared crisis can unlock better burden-sharing, regional planning and more coherent cross-border health and protection systems. The African Union, the UN system and bilateral donors all have structures that could support such coordination. If those structures are properly funded and politically backed, spillover could strengthen regional institutions over time rather than merely expose weakness. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-09.html?utm_source=openai))

But the counterargument has limits. Coordination is only as good as enforcement and financing. If donors remain fragmented, if governments prioritize domestic politics over regional goods and if armed actors continue to treat civilians as bargaining chips, institutional gains will remain modest. The current evidence suggests not collapse, but chronic underperformance. In that sense, the region is not failing all at once. It is failing selectively, in the places where institutions are weakest and where civilians are least visible to national politics. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

What the numbers do, and do not, tell us

The headline figures are alarming, but they do not capture everything. WHO says 34 million people need aid in Sudan, and UNHCR says the conflict has produced the largest displacement crisis in the world. UNHCR’s global appeal documents and country data also show how Sudan sits within a broader displacement landscape, including the pressure on Chad, Egypt and South Sudan. The IOM’s Missing Migrants work, meanwhile, shows how perilous migration routes remain around the world, even if the exact 2025 global total cited in earlier drafts could not be verified from a primary IOM source in this review. The broader lesson is still clear: wars feed migration systems that are already underfunded, politically contested and often deadly. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/09-01-2026-sudan-1000-days-of-war-deepen-the-world-s-worst-health-and-humanitarian-crisis?utm_source=openai))

Yet numbers can also mislead. They can obscure the difference between a formal refugee camp and an improvised host-family arrangement; between a person displaced once and someone displaced multiple times; between a person who has crossed an international border and someone who has crossed a frontline inside Sudan three times in two months. They also undercount people who avoid registration because they fear authorities, debt collectors or armed groups. In highly fluid border crises, statistical certainty is often an illusion. The correct journalistic posture is not to ignore the numbers but to read them as minimum estimates of a bigger and messier reality. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

That is why triangulation matters. WHO’s health data, UNHCR’s displacement estimates, IOM’s migration reporting, local reporting from border areas and rights groups’ documentation of detention and abuse each illuminate a different layer of the same crisis. The overlap is where the strongest conclusions emerge: Sudan’s war is driving displacement; neighboring states are absorbing it unevenly; regional routes are becoming more dangerous; and humanitarian systems are being forced to substitute for politics that has failed to prevent, contain or resolve the conflict. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

Why this matters beyond Sudan

The most important implication of Sudan’s war is not only that a country of more than 40 million people is in danger. It is that the international system’s standard response to border-driven crises is losing credibility. Wealthier states want migration contained, but they often underfund the humanitarian and development work that would reduce pressure at origin and transit points. They call for order at borders while tolerating instability in the regions that feed them. They demand safe returns while cutting the budgets that make safety possible. Sudan exposes the contradiction more sharply than many crises because the movement is so large and the border states are so fragile. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

It also matters because Sudan sits at the intersection of several strategic geographies: the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, the Red Sea, the eastern Mediterranean and the Arab world. A war that displaces millions in that space does not stay local. It alters labor markets, customs revenues, disease surveillance, arms flows, smuggling routes and the domestic politics of neighboring states. If policymakers continue to treat displacement as a humanitarian footnote rather than as a structural regional shock, they will keep arriving late to the same crises. ([emro.who.int](https://www.emro.who.int/sdn/crisis/index.html?utm_source=openai))

For now, the headline is grimly simple: Sudan’s war is not just producing refugees. It is reorganizing movement across northeastern Africa and the eastern Mediterranean faster than the institutions meant to manage that movement can adapt. The result is a region where borders are more heavily guarded, but no safer; where people travel farther for protection, but find less of it; and where the gap between security policy and humanitarian reality grows wider each month. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))
