CinemaCon’s New Deal: Theatrical Exhibition Gets a Reluctant Reprieve

At this year’s CinemaCon, studios are promising longer theatrical windows, bigger slates and more premium-screen spectacles — but the real fight is over whether the audience rebound is durable or just another tentpole bounce.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 22, 2026 • 4:18 p.m. • 3284 words

For the last five years, the movie business has been trying to decide whether moviegoing is a habit, a luxury, or a relic. In Las Vegas in April, at CinemaCon 2026, the industry answered with its usual mix of confidence and anxiety: confidence in giant event films, premium large-format screens, and theatrical exclusivity; anxiety about whether the recent uptick in ticket sales can outlast the next lull in the release calendar. That tension is not hypothetical. It is playing out in real time at the box office, where a few titles are carrying far more of the market than they once would have, and where studios are trying to use CinemaCon to argue that theaters remain indispensable to the business model. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The immediate backdrop matters. In mid-April, the North American box office was being led by The Super Mario Galaxy Movie and Project Hail Mary, while Lee Cronin’s The Mummy opened solidly but well behind those two. That is the useful context for reading CinemaCon 2026: the business is seeing proof that audiences will still show up in force for the right films, but the middle of the market remains brittle. AP reported on April 19 that Mario again topped the market and that Project Hail Mary continued to hold well in its fifth weekend, while The Mummy debuted in third place. Boxoffice Pro’s weekend chart and AMC/Cinemark reporting point in the same direction: event titles can still drive traffic, but they are doing an outsized share of the work. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/965c2fb1e54a7789362077c1733ff8d3?utm_source=openai))

That pattern — a few giant winners and a shakier middle — is the story of theatrical exhibition in 2026. It is a business increasingly built around concentration: more of the revenue, attention, and marketing oxygen go to fewer films, and the majors are responding by making those films feel more like events than products. At CinemaCon, Paramount Skydance CEO David Ellison told theater owners that his companies would guarantee 30 movie releases a year and commit to a 45-day exclusive theatrical window starting immediately. AP described that as a major promise to exhibitors, and Boxoffice Pro noted that Universal has also moved to guarantee at least five weeks of theatrical exclusivity for the remainder of its 2026 releases, expanding to seven weeks in 2027. The message from the major studios was unmistakable: theaters are not being abandoned; they are being repositioned as the launchpad for the biggest cultural products. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The question is whether that reimagining is a durable economic model or a temporary truce. The latest evidence cuts both ways. On the bullish side, AMC and Cinemark both said the 2026 Easter frame delivered record or near-record results. AMC said its five-day Easter weekend produced its best global admissions and food-and-beverage revenue for that holiday period in company history, and Boxoffice Pro reported that the chain tied part of the bump to The Super Mario Galaxy Movie and strong merchandise sales. Cinemark said the same period marked its biggest five-day Easter weekend domestic box office of all time. Those are not trivial numbers; they suggest that when audiences perceive a film as a communal event, they still respond. But they also underscore how dependent the current rebound is on a narrow set of crowd-pleasers. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/cinemark-springs-to-biggest-five-day-easter-weekend-domestic-box-office-of-all-time/?utm_source=openai))

On the skeptical side, those gains are still heavily dependent on a narrow set of crowd-pleasers. When the business is healthy, it can look robust quickly. When the tentpoles thin out, the market can look fragile just as fast. Boxoffice Pro’s early-April chart showed The Super Mario Galaxy Movie still grossing strongly in its second weekend while Project Hail Mary held well and lesser titles fell off sharply. That is excellent news for exhibitors, but it also underlines the industry’s current structure: the top two or three titles are doing much of the work that, in another era, might have been spread across several mid-budget movies. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

A box-office rebound that depends on fewer, bigger bets

Theatrical moviegoing is no longer recovering in a straight line. Instead, it is zigzagging around the release calendar, responding strongly to certain genres — family animation, sci-fi spectacle, horror, event biopics — while giving less protection to the mid-budget, adult-skewing films that once formed the ballast of the marketplace. Boxoffice Pro’s April preview anticipated exactly this kind of corridor, with Mario as the dominant spring title and Project Hail Mary positioned as a major original-film test. The fact that those expectations were broadly borne out shows that exhibitor optimism is grounded in data, but the same data also reveals how thin the overall distribution of hits has become. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

Studios, of course, are leaning into the upside. Paramount’s CinemaCon presentation emphasized a larger theatrical slate and a restored commitment to windows. Universal’s minimum-exclusivity commitment was a notable statement in a post-pandemic marketplace that had normalized shorter theatrical runs for many films. In one sense, this is a return to old-school exhibition logic: give the audience a reason to go now, or risk losing them to home release. In another sense, it is a hedged strategy. The majors are not abandoning streaming; they are trying to preserve the high-margin portion of the revenue curve for as long as possible before movies move to paid digital rental, subscription, or ad-supported services. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

That compromise is easier to announce than to police. Every studio knows that release windows are only as strong as the biggest title in the market, and the biggest title can now do more than theatrical alone ever could. Merchandise, premium formats, and global localization have become central to the economics of event films. AMC’s reporting that The Super Mario Galaxy Movie helped drive one of its strongest holiday weekends is revealing because it points to a broader truth: the movie theater is increasingly bundled with collectible culture, food-and-beverage spending, and social-media visibility. The film is not just a screen product; it is a retail occasion. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/cinemark-springs-to-biggest-five-day-easter-weekend-domestic-box-office-of-all-time/?utm_source=openai))

The studio-exhibitor détente is real, but conditional

At CinemaCon, the rhetoric from distributors and exhibitors sounded more aligned than it has in years. Theaters are once again being described as premium spaces, not just as a legacy channel. Studios are speaking the language of “big-screen experiences.” Exhibitors are speaking the language of economic impact, Main Street jobs, and community gathering places. Cinema United’s Michael O’Leary used his state-of-the-industry address to argue that concentration in distribution would shape windows, scheduling, screen placement, and access to catalogs in ways that would affect movie fans and exhibitors alike. That is a direct warning that the market is becoming more sensitive to power, not less. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

There is good reason for exhibitors to press that point. If a small number of conglomerates control most must-play titles, they can dictate terms to theaters that are already working with high fixed costs, labor pressures, and uneven attendance. Theatrical exhibition has always been cyclical, but the cycle now contains additional layers: premium-format upgrades, higher food-and-beverage dependence, more dynamic pricing, and a greater share of revenue from giant-event weekends. In that environment, a narrower slate means more volatility. A chain can have a record weekend and still face a soft quarter if there are too few movies that can drive traffic in between event releases. The record Easter and Memorial Day weekends reported by AMC and Cinemark are encouraging, but they do not prove that the demand base has fully normalized. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/cinemark-springs-to-biggest-five-day-easter-weekend-domestic-box-office-of-all-time/?utm_source=openai))

And yet the exhibitors are not simply defensive. They are adapting. Comscore has continued to expand its software and data role in theatrical operations, and AFCI’s Studio Summit 2026 made clear that production incentives, virtual workflows, and policy questions are still central to the industry’s planning. The broader point is that exhibition is now being optimized with the same logic that governs production: efficiency, premium positioning, and data-rich decision-making. The exhibition business is no longer just about showing films. It is about proving that the right auditorium can deliver an experience the living room cannot. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

Why this year’s CinemaCon felt different

CinemaCon has long been a morale convention, but this year it felt like a referendum on the industry’s post-pandemic identity. The biggest presentations were not just marketing teasers; they were arguments about what kind of movie business should exist. Universal brought Christopher Nolan back to the stage with The Odyssey, a film that has become an instant industry event because of Nolan’s track record and the promise of large-format spectacle. AP reported that Nolan showed exhibitors footage from the film, which stars Matt Damon and is set to open on July 17, 2026. Disney used its presentation to reinforce the theatricality of its franchise machine. Paramount used the stage to reassure exhibitors that its future strategy still includes theatrical release as a centerpiece. Each studio was trying to tell theater owners the same thing in different ways: trust us, and we will send you the movies that still make the trip worthwhile. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

There is a hidden tension in that promise. Bigger films can support theaters, but bigger films also magnify risk. When a studio concentrates too much of its annual strategy around a few giant releases, the downside of one misfire can be severe. The better the movie, the better the weekend; but the more the business depends on eventization, the fewer opportunities there are for films that build modestly over time. That is especially true for adult dramas, comedies, and original mid-budget films, which have not fully regained their pre-2020 box-office role. Theatrical exhibition may be getting stronger at the top, but the middle remains under pressure. The market is healthier than it was during the immediate post-strike recovery, but it is still not broad-based. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

The majors’ own language suggests they know this. Ellison’s guarantee of 30 releases a year may sound like output optimism, but it also reflects a scaling problem across Hollywood: a studio can only support so many broad-appeal releases that can be marketed globally, translated efficiently, and given enough screens to matter. That bottleneck is structural. Content abundance is not the same thing as audience concentration. A slate can be large and still be driven by a few megatitles. CinemaCon’s key promises, in other words, were less about volume than about trying to make the volume count. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Labor, production geography and the cost of making movies now

Theatrical exhibition cannot be separated from production economics. The more expensive and geographically complex movie production becomes, the more studios seek certainty on the back end. That is one reason why incentive policy, production hubs, and labor negotiations remain central to the film desk even when the headline is box office. AFCI’s Studio Summit in Los Angeles this spring framed the industry around production incentives, virtual workflows, and policy questions, while its materials emphasize how film commissions help regions pitch themselves to studios and producers. The broader point is that films are made in a global competition for labor, infrastructure, and public subsidy. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

The Motion Picture Association’s updated economic materials likewise emphasize the scale of the industry. Its 2026 infographic says the U.S. film and television industry supports 2.01 million jobs, pays $202 billion in total wages and includes more than 162,000 businesses. That kind of industry-level accounting is useful not because it settles every policy debate, but because it reminds readers that the film business is not just red carpets and reviews; it is a sprawling labor market spanning crew, postproduction, exhibition, marketing, music, and technical services. At the same time, labor advocates and local officials often dispute how evenly the gains are distributed, especially when incentives are offered to productions that may move away after a subsidy expires. The overall employment picture is large, but it does not guarantee stability for any individual worker or region. ([motionpictures.org](https://www.motionpictures.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/MPA_Economic_Contribution_US_Infographic_020326.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That uncertainty matters now because the industry is still absorbing the aftereffects of the 2023 strikes, the repositioning of streaming platforms, and the ongoing migration of work across states and countries. Film commissioners continue to sell the virtues of local infrastructure and tax policy because producers remain highly mobile. Yet the greater the competition among jurisdictions, the more incentives become a race to the bottom or a race to the most generous subsidy, depending on your point of view. Supporters argue that the jobs, travel, and small-business spending justify the policy. Critics argue that the state often underwrites activity that would have occurred anyway or leaves less public value than promised. Both arguments are alive in 2026, and neither can be dismissed as rhetorical noise. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

The awards race still matters, but mostly as a signal

Even in an industry obsessed with commercial performance, awards remain a signal of prestige, future bargaining power, and audience discovery. The 98th Academy Awards were held on March 15, 2026, and the Academy’s own materials show how nominations and voting were scheduled in January, making awards season a winter-to-spring attention machine rather than a box-office engine. The DGA’s 78th annual awards, held on February 7, 2026, crowned Paul Thomas Anderson for One Battle After Another. These are not just vanity prizes. They influence the life cycle of films, especially for titles that depend on long-tail revenue, repertory value, streaming leverage, and future financing. ([oscars.org](https://www.oscars.org/oscars/ceremonies/2026?utm_source=openai))

But awards have also become more bifurcated from the theatrical market. In previous decades, Oscar attention could extend the box-office run of a drama or prestige release. Today, that effect is less reliable, in part because the theatrical audience for awards fare is more specialized and in part because prestige films live in a more crowded ecosystem of streaming and social conversation. That means awards still matter — to talent, to marketers, to distributors — but their ability to transform an individual film into a box-office phenomenon is diminished. The bigger commercial drivers are now franchises, adaptations, and event branding. The awards race is a visibility machine, not a universal box-office engine. ([oscars.org](https://www.oscars.org/oscars/ceremonies/2026?utm_source=openai))

This is one reason Nolan’s continued gravitational pull matters. A filmmaker who can make a three-hour epic feel like a theatrical event in its own right is increasingly valuable to exhibitors. Likewise, studios that can turn IP into a social occasion — Mario, Marvel, a long-awaited sequel, a crowd-pleasing horror concept — can still make theaters feel essential. The trouble is that there are only so many Nolan-scale or Mario-scale projects available in a given year. The industry’s health therefore depends on whether it can rebuild a middle class of movies, not just preserve the top tier. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

Technology is making theaters smarter, not necessarily safer

The current wave of cinema technology is less about replacing projection than about persuading audiences that a trip to the theater will feel differentiated. Premium audio, laser projection, and branded premium formats are the obvious parts of that pitch, but the operational layer is just as important. AFCI’s Studio Summit underscores how heavily the business now leans on virtual production, remote workflows and other technology partners, while exhibitors continue to rely on data-driven scheduling and circuit management. That can improve efficiency and reduce friction. It can also deepen dependence on tech vendors and on the same hit-driven logic that already shapes programming decisions. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a subtle change in how studios are treating theaters as part of the marketing pipeline. CinemaCon presentations increasingly position the best screens as a distinct product category, one that can justify a higher ticket price and steer consumers toward premium auditoriums rather than toward at-home substitutes. In practical terms, that can help exhibitors monetize premium seating and justify higher prices. In cultural terms, it reinforces the notion that the best way to see certain films is not only in a theater, but in a specific kind of theater. The long-term effect may be to strengthen exhibition as a premium niche while making ordinary multiplex screens more vulnerable to price-sensitive demand. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

That potential split is important. A healthy film culture has historically depended on a range of viewing contexts: the neighborhood multiplex, the art house, the drive-in, the repertory screen, the premium venue. As the business concentrates around premium experiences, some of those contexts become less economically viable. The audience may get a better image and louder sound in a flagship auditorium, but it may also get fewer choices and higher prices. The marketplace can look healthier while becoming less diverse. That is not necessarily a contradiction; it is the defining tradeoff of the current era. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))

The counterargument: maybe this is how recovery actually works

The optimistic reading is straightforward and not frivolous. Theatrical exhibition was never going to bounce back by recreating 2019 exactly. The pandemic accelerated changes that were already underway: streaming expansion, home entertainment competition, shrinking tolerance for mediocre releases, and a deeper dependence on franchises. If 2026 is showing that a smaller number of bigger movies can still deliver strong weekends, then perhaps the business is merely becoming more efficient. On this view, theaters do not need every movie to be a hit. They need a steady cadence of event titles, strong premium-screen programming, and a few breakout surprises each quarter. The current slate seems to be doing that. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

There is evidence for this argument. AMC’s holiday weekend results, Cinemark’s record Easter performance, Universal’s longer exclusivity promise, and the strong early performance of several 2026 titles all suggest that moviegoing still has a powerful social pull when the film is right. AP’s reporting from CinemaCon also indicates that major filmmakers and stars still regard theatrical exhibition as the highest-status launch environment for the biggest projects. If the industry’s best work is concentrated in cinemas, then perhaps the theatrical model is not broken so much as sharpened. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

But the counterargument has limits. A healthier-looking top line does not answer the question of market resilience. If the business depends on a handful of giant titles and a few holiday windows, it may be more exposed, not less. If theatrical is becoming a premium special-occasion format, then the total addressable audience may be smaller than the industry hopes. And if the middle class of films continues to erode, the cultural role of theaters could narrow even as the biggest weekends rebound. The theater business may survive; what is less clear is whether it will continue to support a broad ecosystem of films, critics, and careers. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

The next few months will test whether this spring’s box-office heat is the start of a broader trend or a sequence of unusually well-positioned releases. If The Super Mario Galaxy Movie and Project Hail Mary keep drawing audiences into midweek and late-run play, exhibitors will have an argument that moviegoing has regained more than just opening-weekend energy. If the titles after them fail to keep up momentum, the industry may conclude that 2026 is another year of spikes without a durable base. The key indicators will be holdovers, premium-format occupancy, and whether the month-to-month floor of attendance rises when there is no giant family title in the marketplace. ([boxofficepro.com](https://www.boxofficepro.com/weekend-box-office-super-mario-galaxy-jumps-to-the-top-again/?utm_source=openai))

There will also be a policy test. If studios continue to extend windows and exhibitors continue to invest in premium technology, the next debate will be about leverage: who benefits most from longer exclusivity, and whether the gains accrue to cinemas, studios, or only to the top tier of releases. Labor and production policy will remain part of that conversation, because the industry’s real geography is still being redrawn by incentives, international competition, and the costs of making films in a market that demands both spectacle and efficiency. The current rebound is real, but it is also incomplete. The theatrical business has not died. It has become more conditional, more concentrated, and more dependent on a few movies that can make everyone else believe the future has arrived. ([afci.org](https://afci.org/studio-summit-2026/?utm_source=openai))
