IMF warns Middle East war is pushing the world toward slower growth and higher inflation

The fund says the conflict has become the biggest energy shock in decades, threatening vulnerable economies and raising the risk of food stress if fertilizer shipments are disrupted.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 17, 2026 • 1:18 a.m. • 1011 words

The International Monetary Fund has sharply darkened its outlook for the global economy, warning that the war in the Middle East is already pushing the world toward slower growth, higher inflation and a deeper squeeze on poorer countries. In remarks ahead of the IMF and World Bank spring meetings, Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva said the conflict has created the worst disruption to global energy supply in years, with knock-on effects that are spreading far beyond the battlefield. The fund’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook says conflicts produce large and persistent output losses, and that the damage to trade, investment and confidence can linger long after the guns fall silent. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

The warning lands at a moment when diplomats and finance ministers are trying to assess whether the region’s fighting can be contained. The IMF said even a quick end to hostilities would likely still force a downward revision in growth and an upward revision in inflation. If the war drags on, the effects would be worse, particularly for countries that rely on imported fuel and have little fiscal room to cushion consumers from higher prices. Georgieva said “all roads now lead to higher prices and slower growth,” a blunt assessment that reflects how quickly a regional war can become a global macroeconomic event. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

Energy shock ripples across the global economy

The fund’s concern centers on the Strait of Hormuz, a critical passage for oil and gas shipments. Reuters reported that the war has shuttered millions of barrels of production and effectively blocked much of the traffic through the waterway, helping send Brent crude toward the $110 range and tightening markets for liquefied natural gas, fertilizers and related industrial inputs. The IMF said the disruption has cut global oil supply by 13%, a major shock for an economy that was already coping with stubborn inflation and elevated uncertainty. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

That matters well beyond energy traders. Higher fuel prices quickly raise transport costs, power costs and the price of goods shipped across oceans. They also increase pressure on central banks, which may have less room to lower interest rates even if growth slows. The IMF’s message is that the war is not only a geopolitical crisis but a broad inflation shock that could weigh on business investment and household demand across many economies at once. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

Georgieva also warned that the pain is asymmetric. Energy exporters may see some gains from higher prices, but import-dependent economies are much more exposed to the squeeze. The IMF said about 85% of its members are energy importers, leaving a large share of the world vulnerable to imported inflation and balance-of-payments pressure. Poorer countries with limited reserves and little fiscal space are likely to be hit hardest, the fund said, because they cannot easily subsidize fuel or absorb higher import bills without cutting other services. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

Why poorer countries could feel the worst effects

The IMF’s warning is especially acute for emerging markets and low-income economies that depend on imported fuel for electricity, transport and food distribution. When oil and gas prices rise, governments often face a brutal choice: let prices rise for consumers, or spend scarce public money to soften the blow. Georgieva argued that broad energy subsidies are not the answer, saying they can further fuel inflation and drain public finances. Instead, she urged policymakers to target support carefully and preserve room for essential spending. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

That advice reflects a wider concern inside the fund: once a commodity shock starts feeding into food and transport costs, the people who feel it first are often those already living close to the edge. The World Food Programme said in March that millions could face acute hunger if the war continues into June, and the IMF said it is working with WFP and the Food and Agriculture Organization to monitor food-security risks. Georgieva said the fund does not yet see a full-blown food crisis, but warned that one could emerge if fertilizer shipments are disrupted for long enough. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

That risk is not abstract. Fertilizer is one of the hidden links between war and dinner tables: when energy prices jump, fertilizer production gets more expensive, harvest costs rise and food inflation can accelerate months later. For governments in Africa, the Middle East and parts of Asia that already spend heavily on food imports, a prolonged shock could force new aid requests, budget cuts or emergency borrowing. The IMF said some countries have already sought help, though it did not identify them. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

A war with no easy economic escape

The IMF’s broader outlook on conflict is stark. In its April 2026 report, the fund says wars do not simply destroy capital and lives; they create long-lasting scars in productivity, labor markets and institutions. Recoveries tend to be slow and uneven even after the fighting stops, and they depend heavily on sustained peace. The report also argues that early stabilization, debt restructuring, international support and domestic reforms are crucial if countries are to rebuild after war rather than remain trapped in stagnation. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))

That analysis helps explain why markets have been so sensitive to every ceasefire rumor and diplomatic signal in recent weeks. A temporary pause in fighting can ease immediate pressure on oil and shipping, but it does not instantly restore lost supply, reopen damaged infrastructure or erase the uncertainty that companies use to set prices and investment plans. Reuters reported that even a rapid end to hostilities would still leave a lingering negative impact on the rest of the world. ([wsau.com](https://wsau.com/2026/04/06/war-in-middle-east-will-lead-to-slower-growth-higher-inflation-imf-chief-tells-reuters/))

For policymakers gathering in Washington, the message is that the global economy has entered a more fragile phase. Growth had been expected to improve modestly this year and next, but the war has changed the calculus. The IMF says countries now have to prepare for a world in which geopolitical shocks, climate shocks and technological disruptions can all strike at once. In that environment, the question is no longer whether the conflict affects the global economy. It already does. The question is how much more damage it will do before diplomacy can catch up. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026))
