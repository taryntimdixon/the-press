AI’s power problem turns political as data centers hit the grid

A wave of megaprojects, utility fights, and new federal scrutiny is forcing the U.S. to confront a basic question: how much electricity should the AI boom get, and who pays for it?

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 1:39 p.m.

The AI boom has moved past the stage where the bottleneck was merely chips. By April 22, 2026, the harder constraint is increasingly power: not just generating enough electricity, but delivering it to the right place, at the right voltage, with the transformers, substations, transmission lines and permits to match. That shift is turning data centers from a corporate real-estate story into a national infrastructure fight. The public argument is no longer about whether AI is real. It is about who gets to build the machinery that feeds it, how quickly, and at what cost to households, communities and the grid.

The latest signs are arriving from several directions at once. Federal forecasters have been warning for more than a year that data centers are a major driver of U.S. electricity demand growth, and the warning has sharpened in 2026. In January, the Energy Information Administration said U.S. electricity demand was headed for the strongest four-year growth stretch since 2000, with large computing facilities among the drivers. In its April 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook, EIA projected total U.S. electricity generation would rise again this year and next. The International Energy Agency says data centers accounted for around 1.5% of global electricity consumption in 2024, with the United States responsible for the largest share, and expects demand to keep rising through 2030. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the federal government is actively promoting more AI infrastructure. The Department of Energy has opened public land for reuse at sites such as Portsmouth in Ohio, where it says a public-private partnership is helping prepare land for an AI data center. Regulators and grid operators are also beginning to push back on the pace and structure of the buildout. The result is an infrastructure squeeze with political consequences. On one side are hyperscalers and their partners, who say the country needs faster permitting, bigger substations, more transmission and flexible contracting if it wants to lead in AI. On the other are residents, state lawmakers and utility watchdogs who say the costs are being socialized, the water use is undercounted and the benefits are too uncertain. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/em/articles/partnership-ensures-affordable-energy-powers-ai-future-portsmouth-site?utm_source=openai))

The new bottleneck is electricity, not imagination

For years, the technology industry treated compute as if it could be summoned on demand: more servers, more GPUs, more cloud contracts. That mindset is colliding with the reality of the power system. Large AI facilities can consume enough electricity to resemble heavy industry rather than office space. The Department of Energy says data centers are among the most energy-intensive building types, and its current materials repeat the estimate that U.S. data centers used about 4.4% of total electricity in 2023, with a projected rise to between 6.7% and 12% by 2028. DOE also notes that data centers require reliable power and cooling, and that the broader energy system must adapt if the country is going to keep deploying them at scale. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/buildings/data-centers-and-servers?utm_source=openai))

The IEA’s current framing is even starker. Its Energy and AI analysis says data centers consumed around 415 TWh globally in 2024, or about 1.5% of world electricity, and that the United States accounted for 45% of that global data-center electricity consumption. Its electricity update says U.S. demand growth in 2025 and 2026 is being pushed by data centers, which consumed around 180 TWh in 2024. Those are not speculative numbers about a far-off future. They are embedded in current planning assumptions by utilities, regulators and developers trying to connect projects already on the books. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

That is why the debate has shifted from “how many megawatts?” to “how do you deliver them?” Grid operators and regulators are now dealing with an intersection of huge loads, slow infrastructure and long queues. FERC said on April 16 that it will act by June 2026 on a large-load interconnection docket aimed at improving the timely and equitable integration of major loads such as data centers into transmission systems. The commission also noted its January approval of the Southwest Power Pool’s High Impact Large Load initiative, a sign that the grid is being redesigned under pressure. ([ferc.gov](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-act-large-load-interconnection-docket-june-2026?utm_source=openai))

Utilities, meanwhile, are adjusting in real time. Google said in March that it had signed 1 GW of data-center demand-response capacity with utility partners, meaning some machine-learning workloads can be shifted or curtailed to help balance the grid. It also announced a partnership in Michigan that it says will enable 2.7 GW of new clean resources for the local grid, and in Minnesota it said it would pay all costs associated with electric service for a new Pine Island data center. These are not just marketing flourishes; they are evidence that hyperscalers have understood a basic fact of the current moment: if they want power, they increasingly have to help build the power system. ([blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/global-network/demand-response-data-center-milestone/?utm_source=openai))

The boom is being financed like a utility expansion

The scale of corporate spending tells the story. The biggest cloud and platform companies are pouring money into servers, buildings, substations, fiber and generation at a pace that would have seemed implausible a few years ago. The IEA says global investment in data centers nearly doubled since 2022 and reached about half a trillion dollars in 2024. Meta has disclosed a series of aggressive infrastructure bets: a March 2026 statement about data centers and local communities stressed that it pays the full costs of its data-center energy and water use and funds new and upgraded infrastructure, while a January 2026 Corning agreement was presented as an up to $6 billion fiber-optic supply deal for Meta’s data-center infrastructure. The company also announced in February that it would work with AMD on up to 6 GW of AI infrastructure. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

That money is flowing through a market that increasingly looks like a hybrid of telecom, industrial construction and energy finance. Specialized operators are raising debt and equity to build AI campuses; public companies are signing long-term power commitments; and some developers are pairing cloud capacity with direct financing for chips and power equipment. IREN, for example, disclosed in its February 2026 results that it had secured $3.6 billion in GPU financing tied to its Microsoft contract and was advancing a new 1.6 GW Oklahoma campus. Its earlier Microsoft agreement, disclosed in November 2025, described a five-year GPU cloud services contract with a total value of about $9.7 billion. The filings show how tightly compute, financing and power have become linked. ([sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001878848/000187884826000014/a991irenreportsq2fy26res.htm?utm_source=openai))

There is also a new kind of capex diplomacy. Google’s state-by-state announcements in Michigan and Minnesota make clear that the pitch to local communities is no longer just jobs and tax revenue. It is also that the company will help pay for the grid upgrades its demand requires. Meta has been making a similar argument, saying it pays the full costs of its energy and water use and funds new and upgraded infrastructure. The public is hearing a different story from the one told during the first wave of data-center expansion, when the industry often sold itself as invisible digital infrastructure rather than an industrial customer with a huge physical footprint. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/03/meta-data-centers-support-energy-jobs-environment-local-communities/?utm_source=openai))

Local resistance is no longer symbolic

The backlash against data centers has become concrete enough to slow projects. In Maine, lawmakers passed what reporting described as the nation’s first statewide moratorium on large data centers, halting new facilities above 20 megawatts until fall 2027 while the state studies their impact on the grid. In Virginia, a Washington Post-Schar School poll found voters had turned sharply against the facilities after previously being broadly comfortable with them. In Seattle, Axios reported that five large-scale data centers were being proposed amid concerns about the city’s electricity supply. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2026/04/14/maine-bans-data-centers/?utm_source=openai))

In Maryland, local opposition has become organized enough to dominate county politics. Washington Post reporting from Calvert County described residents attending protests, scrutinizing sound and water impacts, and questioning what exactly is being proposed on a large parcel near the county’s nuclear plant. The story is not unique to one county or one state. It is a sign that the age of automatic approval is ending. Even when a project is large enough to promise construction jobs and tax revenue, residents are increasingly asking for specifics: how much water, how much power, whose transmission lines, whose ratepayers and whose risk. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/dc-md-va/2026/04/13/data-center-opposition-calvert-county/?utm_source=openai))

This matters because infrastructure wins or loses locally. A company can announce a multibillion-dollar AI campus in a press release, but it still needs zoning changes, road access, water permits, transformer deliveries, substations, interconnection studies and, in many cases, a community willing to tolerate years of construction. That is why some of the most consequential technology policy now happens not in Washington or Brussels, but in county commission meetings and utility hearings. The coalition that once treated data centers as clean, invisible infrastructure is breaking apart under the weight of visible costs. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/dc-md-va/2026/04/13/data-center-opposition-calvert-county/?utm_source=openai))

Who pays for the grid?

The central policy question is not whether data centers need power. They do. The question is whether other customers subsidize the expansion. Utilities and advocates have spent the past year arguing over whether new large-load projects should pay fully allocated costs, whether rate structures should be redesigned and whether demand-response commitments are real enough to count as firm capacity. FERC’s new docket is partly a response to this problem: the grid was not originally designed for clusters of hyperscale campuses requesting gigawatts of power in a single region. ([ferc.gov](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-act-large-load-interconnection-docket-june-2026?utm_source=openai))

There are reasons for caution on both sides. Data-center advocates argue that the projects can anchor new generation, improve grid utilization and support local tax bases. Google says its demand-response and clean-energy contracts can make its facilities assets for utilities, not liabilities. Meta says its energy agreements are designed so consumers do not bear those expenses. The company’s March 2026 statement and its January nuclear-energy announcement are examples of a more collaborative model that tries to align private expansion with public grid planning. But critics say these arrangements can still leave ordinary ratepayers exposed if assumptions about load growth, project timing or generation costs prove too optimistic. A contract that looks beneficial in a press release can look different when it is translated into tariff language. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/03/meta-data-centers-support-energy-jobs-environment-local-communities/?utm_source=openai))

That is one reason the industry’s public-relations strategy is shifting. Meta’s recent statements emphasize jobs, energy, environmental stewardship and community investment. Google’s announcements stress paying directly for electricity and adding resources to the system. Microsoft has likewise stressed the scale of its infrastructure commitments in its SEC filings and public positioning. The phrase “pay our way,” now common in utility and policy debates, captures the new political equilibrium: if AI is going to keep expanding, then the infrastructure bill cannot be hidden inside everyone else’s electric bill. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/03/meta-data-centers-support-energy-jobs-environment-local-communities/?utm_source=openai))

Still, there is no consensus that the new deals are enough. The Washington Post reported that some state officials and local residents believe the cost-shifting problem remains unresolved. The tension is especially sharp in regions with already strained grids or high power prices. Maine’s freeze is one end of the spectrum; public-private load-building agreements in states that want to attract investment are the other. The national outcome may turn out to be a patchwork: permissive states become compute hubs, restrictive states slow or block new campuses, and the grid operator’s job becomes managing a lopsided map of demand. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2026/04/14/maine-bans-data-centers/?utm_source=openai))

Chips are still central, but the supply chain has changed shape

The infrastructure crisis is not separate from the chip business. It is the chip business. Modern AI facilities are built around GPUs and specialized accelerators, but the value chain now extends well beyond the silicon itself to packaging, networking, power delivery, cooling and software orchestration. That is why partnerships like Meta’s with AMD, Meta’s silicon work with Arm, and Google’s custom infrastructure efforts matter. The fight is over who can secure enough efficient compute to turn electricity into usable model training and inference capacity. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/02/meta-amd-partner-longterm-ai-infrastructure-agreement/?utm_source=openai))

Some of the clearest pressure points are becoming visible in the market for alternatives to Nvidia and in efforts to squeeze more performance out of each watt. Recent reporting from TechCrunch has pointed to cloud buyers diversifying their compute stacks and to startups pitching power-optimization silicon and optical processors as a response to data-center limits. Those are early signals, not settled winners. But they suggest a broad industry recognition that the economics of AI will be judged as much by power efficiency as by benchmark scores. ([music.amazon.com](https://music.amazon.com/podcasts/3539f412-b953-45da-bee5-621a287e7f9a/episodes/d15aaf0f-f2cf-45fc-97bc-ee94572dde5f/the-money-lab-amazon-q4-2025-ai-innovation-and-infrastructure-expansion?utm_source=openai))

The policy side is catching up unevenly. In the United States, the White House in January 2026 imposed tariffs on certain semiconductor imports, while carving out several categories tied to U.S. data centers and domestic technology supply chains. In Europe, the AI Act entered into force in 2024 and is now in its phased implementation period, with the majority of rules applying on August 2, 2026 and the full framework rolling out by August 2, 2027. The two regions are not just regulating AI differently; they are building the physical foundations of AI under different assumptions about speed, risk and public oversight. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/01/adjusting-imports-of-semiconductors-semiconductor-manufacturing-equipment-and-their-derivative-products-into-the-united-states/?query-11-page=96&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block&utm_source=openai))

The environmental argument is getting harder to dismiss

In the early stages of the AI infrastructure boom, industry executives often framed energy worries as lagging skepticism. That framing is harder to sustain now. The IEA says data-center electricity demand is rising rapidly, the U.S. share is dominant and the growth is unlikely to be flat in the near term. DOE’s long-range estimates are even more alarming for utilities: by 2028, data centers could consume a share of U.S. electricity large enough to materially affect generation planning, transmission buildouts and fuel mix. Those are system-level consequences, not local inconveniences. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

Water is the other obvious pressure point. Cooling requirements vary by facility and climate, but communities are increasingly asking for precise numbers before approving projects. Meta has said its goal is to be water positive by 2030 and has highlighted investments in water stewardship and local infrastructure in communities where it builds. Even so, the basic question remains: how much water, during what seasons, for what cooling design, and with what fallback if the system is stressed? That kind of detail is often missing at the point where projects are first announced. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2025/12/advancing-water-stewardship-in-metas-data-center-communities/?utm_source=openai))

There is a counterargument worth taking seriously, however. A large-scale move from fossil-heavy, inefficient compute to modern, power-aware data centers could be better for the grid in the long run if it is paired with clean generation, better load management and more disciplined siting. Google’s demand-response program and its utility contracts are examples of what that future could look like. The clearest pro-infrastructure case is that the world is already using more compute, and suppressing the buildout would not eliminate demand; it would displace it, potentially to places with less transparency and weaker standards. That is the strongest argument against blanket moratoriums. It is not that the public concerns are fake. It is that a total freeze risks pushing the problem underground. ([blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/global-network/demand-response-data-center-milestone/?utm_source=openai))

Even so, the benefits are not automatic. The industry’s promise that data centers will produce jobs, tax revenue and local investment is often true, but the scale is frequently modest relative to the land, water and grid footprint. That mismatch is what drives opposition: communities see a large industrial footprint whose main benefit accrues to distant shareholders and customers. Unless operators are willing to write much larger checks for grid upgrades, water systems, emergency planning and rate stabilization, the political backlash is likely to grow. Some of the most useful reporting now comes from places where that math is being tested in public. ([about.fb.com](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/02/metas-new-data-center-lebanon-indiana-marks-milestone-ai-investment/?utm_source=openai))

What comes next: build, delay or redesign

There are three plausible paths for the next phase of the AI infrastructure boom. The first is straight-line growth: hyperscalers keep building, utilities keep adding capacity and the grid absorbs the shock with some pain but no structural break. The second is a slowdown caused by supply-chain and permitting friction, in which the number of announced projects outpaces the number of energized projects. The third is redesign: operators and utilities make demand flexibility, on-site generation, storage and stricter cost allocation central to every deal. Reality will probably contain all three, depending on geography. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

Evidence from the past few months suggests that redesign is already underway, but unevenly. Some projects are moving faster because developers can pair them with generation, transmission or direct utility agreements. Others are stalling because transformer lead times are long, interconnection studies are complicated and local governments are less willing to race than they were two years ago. In that sense, the current boom is less a single market cycle than a renegotiation of the social contract around digital infrastructure. AI is still expanding. The machinery supporting it is being forced to explain itself. ([ferc.gov](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-act-large-load-interconnection-docket-june-2026?utm_source=openai))

The most important thing to watch over the next few months is whether the new political resistance changes the economics enough to alter where the next wave of compute gets built. If Maine-style freezes spread, if FERC and state regulators require fuller cost recovery from big-load customers and if utilities standardize demand-response and self-supply terms, the AI buildout may become more selective and more expensive. If not, the U.S. is likely to keep concentrating AI infrastructure in a handful of power-rich regions and shifting more of the system risk to grid operators and consumers. Either way, the era of assuming that digital growth is weightless is over. The grid now sets the pace. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2026/04/14/maine-bans-data-centers/?utm_source=openai))

Bottom line

The current AI story is not just about models, chips or even cloud contracts. It is about the real-world systems that make computation possible: electricity, water, land, permits, transformers and local consent. Those systems are now visible enough to provoke backlash, rich enough to attract enormous capital and fragile enough to shape national policy. The industry can still expand. But it can no longer pretend the expansion is invisible.
