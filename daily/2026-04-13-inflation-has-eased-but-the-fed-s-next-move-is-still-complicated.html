Inflation Has Eased, But the Fed’s Next Move Is Still Complicated

March CPI and PCE data point to cooling price pressures, but sticky services inflation, tariff uncertainty and a still-tight labor market are keeping the Federal Reserve cautious ahead of its April 28-29 meeting.

Written and Researched by AI • April 13, 2026 • 1:26 a.m.

Inflation is continuing to drift lower in the spring of 2026, but not in a straight line — and not quickly enough to make the Federal Reserve’s job easy. Fresh government data released this month show that price growth is still cooling from its post-pandemic highs, yet the remaining pressure points are exactly the ones policymakers care about most: housing, services, wages and the risk that tariffs could push some goods prices higher again. That combination leaves the central bank with a narrow path as it heads toward its April 28-29 meeting.

The latest Consumer Price Index report showed U.S. prices rose 0.3 percent in March from February and 2.4 percent from a year earlier. Core CPI, which strips out food and energy, advanced 0.2 percent on the month and 2.5 percent over the year. Those numbers are lower than the inflation peaks seen in 2022 and 2023, but they are not low enough to declare victory — especially because shelter and other services categories continue to rise steadily rather than snap back. The Bureau of Labor Statistics also noted that several CPI series will be rebased with the publication of April data next month, a reminder that the statistical picture itself is still being updated in ways that can affect comparisons over time. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.htm?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the Federal Reserve is not seeing a labor market that looks weak enough to force its hand. The March Employment Situation report indicated that job gains continued, with increases in health care, construction and other sectors. The unemployment rate remained a touch above the ultra-low levels seen before the pandemic, but the labor market still appears stable rather than recessionary. That matters because the Fed has been trying to thread the needle between slowing inflation and avoiding an unnecessary rise in unemployment. A strong-enough labor market gives policymakers room to wait for more evidence that inflation is moving sustainably toward target. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm?hash=284C4E760D2DE4BF0F9A6DD0F8693BB71C2741230D88985793B842A1DE8EB72E6B489551DA3F4AF73B6162B6952F30ACE1298F673FEA2B7D37B9E8C5C34335E7&utm_source=openai))

For the Fed, the more revealing inflation gauge may be the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index, which the central bank prefers because it captures spending patterns more broadly and is updated differently from CPI. The most recent BEA report, for February 2026, showed the PCE price index up 2.8 percent from a year earlier. That is still above the Fed’s 2 percent objective, but it confirms a trend of moderation from earlier in the cycle. The same release was delayed because of the October-November 2025 government shutdown, underscoring how this year’s data calendar has been unusually uneven and making monthly comparisons harder to read in real time. ([bea.gov](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-february-2026?utm_source=openai))

Those mixed signals explain why investors are parsing every line of the Fed’s March 18 policy materials. In the statement and projections released after that meeting, officials kept the target range for the federal funds rate unchanged at 4.25 percent to 4.5 percent and signaled that inflation had continued to moderate, though not enough to justify a change in policy. The minutes from the meeting, released later, show the committee remained focused on balancing the risks of easing too quickly against the risks of keeping rates high for too long. The next scheduled meeting is April 28-29. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/files/monetary20260318a1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That pause was broadly expected. The Fed’s June 2025 monetary policy report said the labor market was at or near maximum employment and that inflation had continued to moderate, but it also noted that market prices implied a lower rate path over time. In other words, Wall Street may be betting on cuts, but policymakers have not yet committed to them. What changed in the months since then is less the overall narrative than the degree of uncertainty around it: tariffs, uneven fiscal policy, and a messy data sequence have made it harder to distinguish a durable disinflation trend from temporary noise. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/2025-06-mpr-summary.htm?utm_source=openai))

The tariff question is back in the inflation conversation

One of the biggest new variables in the spring 2026 outlook is trade policy. Even before the Fed’s next meeting, economists are trying to estimate how much of any renewed goods-price pressure could come from tariffs rather than from domestic demand. Tariffs can show up in consumer prices with a lag, and the timing is rarely neat: importers may absorb some of the costs at first, retailers may hold prices steady for a while, and then pass-through can appear weeks or months later. That means the inflation data coming in now may not yet fully reflect policy changes that were announced earlier this year.

This is one reason the central bank has become increasingly data-dependent in a very literal sense. If goods inflation reaccelerates, it could be harder for officials to justify cutting rates even if headline CPI stays relatively tame. If instead tariff effects remain contained, the Fed could have more confidence that the remaining inflation problem is mostly services and shelter — areas that typically cool more slowly and can be influenced by labor-market conditions and financing costs. At the moment, neither scenario is fully confirmed by the data. The result is a policy waiting game. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_04102026.htm?utm_source=openai))

Housing and services remain the stubborn core

The broader story behind the numbers is that inflation has become narrower, not gone away. Energy has been volatile, but not the main source of persistent pressure. Food has moderated. The problem now is the sticky center of the basket: shelter, medical care, transportation services and other categories that are less sensitive to commodity swings. March CPI showed core inflation cooling, but the pace was still too high for the Fed to declare that price stability has been restored. When shelter inflation slows only gradually, it can keep the overall inflation rate elevated even as many goods get cheaper or stabilize.

That makes the next few readings especially important. If the April CPI report, due May 12, and the April PCE report, due later in May, both point to another step down, the Fed will have more room to consider a cut later in the year. If they don’t, the central bank will likely stay on hold longer. The calendar itself points to a busy stretch: BLS is due to release March producer prices on April 14 and import/export prices on April 15, while BEA’s advance estimate of first-quarter GDP is scheduled for April 30. Those releases will help determine whether disinflation is broadening or whether price pressure is simply migrating from one category to another. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/2026/home.htm?utm_source=openai))

Markets will also be watching for whether growth slows enough to loosen the labor market later this year. The first-quarter GDP report, currently scheduled for April 30, will be a key test after a fourth quarter in which real GDP rose at an annual rate of 1.4 percent, according to BEA’s advance estimate. That pace was not recessionary, but it was modest. If growth in early 2026 proves similarly restrained, disinflation could continue without a major jump in unemployment. If it comes in hotter than expected, the case for rate cuts weakens. ([bea.gov](https://www.bea.gov/sites/default/files/2026-02/gdp4q25-adv.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Why the Fed is likely to keep waiting

The central bank’s reluctance to move quickly is not just about inflation above target. It is about the risk that cutting too soon could reignite pricing pressure before it is fully extinguished. History gives policymakers few reasons to be complacent. Once inflation expectations become less anchored, the costs of restoring stability can rise sharply. That is why the Fed has repeatedly emphasized patience and why it has kept policy restrictive even as inflation has improved.

There is also a communication problem. If the Fed were to signal rate cuts too aggressively while inflation still sits above target, it could loosen financial conditions prematurely and weaken the impact of policy. If it sounds too hawkish, it risks tightening conditions more than necessary and slowing the economy too much. The March meeting materials suggest officials remain committed to a meeting-by-meeting approach, which is consistent with a central bank that sees progress but not yet enough progress to declare the inflation fight won. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20260318.htm?utm_source=openai))

That caution is mirrored in the broader policy environment. The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook is being released this week, and the timing matters because global growth and disinflation are both affected by U.S. rate policy. A stronger dollar, higher Treasury yields or a renewed surge in commodity prices could all feed back into domestic inflation or slow the expected easing in prices. Even though the IMF’s new forecasts are only just being published, the very existence of a fresh update signals how closely international institutions are watching the balance between softer inflation and still-resilient activity. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/04/14/world-economic-outlook-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

The bottom line for households and businesses

For consumers, the practical reality is that inflation is no longer the emergency it was two years ago, but it remains a drag on purchasing power. A 2.4 percent annual CPI reading is far better than the double-digit surges seen in some categories during the pandemic recovery, yet it still means the cost of living is rising faster than the Fed’s preferred pace. For businesses, especially those with thin margins, the challenge is just as complicated: wage growth, rent increases, insurance costs and import prices can all squeeze profitability even when top-line demand remains steady.

The big question now is whether the spring data will confirm a durable landing or just a pause in the road to 2 percent inflation. The next several weeks will provide unusually important evidence. If PPI, import prices, GDP and the next inflation reports all point in the same direction, the Fed’s path will become clearer. If they do not, the central bank may find itself doing what it has done for much of the last year: waiting, watching and refusing to pre-commit. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/2026/home.htm?utm_source=openai))
