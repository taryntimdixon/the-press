White House pushes Congress to set federal AI rules as states race ahead

The Trump administration says a patchwork of state AI laws could slow U.S. innovation, but its new blueprint leaves lawmakers with the hard work of turning broad principles into a real national standard.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 4:56 a.m.

WASHINGTON — The Trump administration has thrown its weight behind a national framework for artificial intelligence, urging Congress to step in with federal rules that would override some state-level laws and give the industry a single playbook to follow. The move, outlined in a new White House blueprint released Friday, March 20, reflects how quickly AI has become a front-burner political issue in Washington — and how difficult it may be to turn broad agreement on “innovation” into legislation that can survive Congress.

The administration’s message is straightforward: the U.S. should not let 50 different state regimes slow what it sees as a strategically important technology race. White House officials said the plan is intended to protect children, limit fraud, address copyright concerns, and avoid spikes in electricity costs tied to the buildout of AI infrastructure, while also preventing a confusing patchwork of rules for developers and deployers of AI systems. White House AI czar David Sacks said the effort responds to “a growing patchwork of 50 different state regulatory regimes” that could weaken America’s lead in AI. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

A federal push, but no easy path

The White House is asking Congress to “preempt state AI laws” it views as overly burdensome, a sharp signal that the administration wants federal authority to set the tone on AI governance. The framework’s goals — child safety, consumer protection, intellectual property, energy, and anti-censorship guardrails — are broad enough to attract support from both parties, but they are not specific enough to settle the hardest disputes. AP reports that House Republican leaders quickly backed the framework, but also notes that any sweeping bill would need cooperation from Senate Democrats, where divisions over AI remain deep. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

That political reality matters because the states are not waiting. California, Colorado, Texas, and Utah have all already enacted some AI rules, and Texas’s new law, which took effect this year, requires government agencies and health care providers to disclose when they use AI to interact with consumers or answer questions. Colorado’s law, meanwhile, aims to stop AI systems from discriminating in consequential decisions such as hiring and medical care, and it is set to take effect later this year. Any federal law broad enough to override state rules would force lawmakers to decide which protections stay local and which get absorbed into federal policy. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

Why energy and infrastructure are suddenly part of the AI debate

One reason the White House is framing AI regulation around energy costs is that the economics of the technology have changed dramatically. AI systems now depend on huge amounts of computing power, and the data-center buildout supporting them is becoming a major political issue in its own right. AP says the administration’s blueprint specifically calls out the risk of electricity prices surging, and Reuters-linked reporting in March described Nvidia chief executive Jensen Huang saying the market for AI chips could represent at least $1 trillion in revenue through 2027, underscoring just how much money is flowing into the infrastructure behind AI. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

That demand is rippling beyond chipmakers. A Reuters Connect image report from late March described AI growth driving demand for advanced semiconductors and high-bandwidth memory, with prices rising as geopolitical instability keeps supply chains tight. In other words, the White House is not trying to regulate a niche software category; it is trying to shape an industrial ecosystem that now spans chip fabrication, cloud computing, electric power, and the physical footprint of giant data centers. ([reutersconnect.com](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/ai-rapid-growth-drives-demand-for-semiconductor-storage-chips/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjY6bmV3c21sX01UMUFOQURMMDAwTFhKNlRZ))

The stakes for Washington and Silicon Valley

The administration’s proposal also reveals a deeper tension in the AI debate. The White House wants to make it easier for U.S. companies to build and deploy AI systems quickly, but many of the concerns driving public skepticism — unsafe chatbot interactions, copyright disputes, and unaccountable automated decision-making — are the same issues that state legislatures have been trying to address on their own. AP reports that the plan explicitly references protecting children and copyright, both of which could become flashpoints if federal legislation is written too narrowly. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

For AI companies, a national standard could be attractive if it replaces a patchwork of state compliance obligations. For critics, that same standard could become a ceiling instead of a floor, weakening stricter state protections before a robust federal substitute exists. That is why the next phase of the debate may be less about whether Washington should act, and more about how much authority it should take from states, how strongly it should police high-risk uses, and whether Congress can move fast enough to keep up with a technology advancing faster than the law. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))

What happens next will depend on whether congressional leaders can turn the White House’s broad principles into actual text. If they can, the result would likely become the first real national AI framework in the U.S. If they cannot, states will keep filling the vacuum — and the industry will continue navigating a regulatory map that changes every time it crosses a border. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a))
