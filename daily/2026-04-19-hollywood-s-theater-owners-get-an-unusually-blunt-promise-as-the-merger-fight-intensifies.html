Hollywood’s theater owners get an unusually blunt promise as the merger fight intensifies

At CinemaCon, Paramount Skydance chief David Ellison pledged 30 annual releases with Warner Bros. if his company’s proposed acquisition wins approval, even as more than 1,000 film professionals condemn the deal.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 4:56 a.m.

LAS VEGAS — Hollywood’s biggest sales pitch this week was also its biggest warning. At CinemaCon, the annual convention for theater owners, Paramount Skydance chairman and chief executive David Ellison used a marquee-stage appearance to promise a flood of movies, a firm theatrical window and a future in which his company and Warner Bros. would together deliver 30 releases a year. The message was meant to reassure exhibitors rattled by the proposed $111 billion merger now awaiting shareholder and regulatory approval. Instead, it underscored how much of Hollywood’s future is being negotiated in real time, on and off the conference stage.

The stakes are unusually high because CinemaCon has become more than a trade-show showroom. It is where studios test how aggressively they can court theater owners after a bruising stretch for moviegoing, a weak 2025 box office and an industry still trying to define the post-pandemic theatrical experience. This year’s convention has also become a referendum on consolidation. Paramount’s pitch to owners comes just days after more than 1,000 actors, directors, writers and other film workers signed an open letter opposing the Warner Bros. deal, warning that it would reduce competition, jobs and the number of major U.S. studios. Together, the public promise and the public backlash capture the central tension of the film business in April 2026: Hollywood still depends on theaters, but the companies that feed those theaters are becoming fewer and larger. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Ellison’s theater pledge is also a power play

Ellison’s promise was notable not only for its size but for its timing. He told exhibitors that Paramount would commit to a 45-day exclusive theatrical window “starting today,” a pledge designed to reassure owners who have long argued that shorter windows push audiences to wait for home viewing and weaken the economics of moviegoing. He also said the combined Paramount-Warner Bros. operation would guarantee 30 movie releases annually, a volume that would make the merged company one of the most prolific suppliers in the market. The pitch is simple: if theaters want more dependable product, a bigger studio can provide it.

But the same logic is what worries critics of the deal. An industry with fewer independent greenlight centers can become an industry with fewer distinct voices, less bargaining leverage for talent and more pressure to prioritize franchise fare over riskier films. That concern was laid out explicitly in the open letter, which framed the merger as a threat to opportunities across the production ecosystem. Even at CinemaCon, where optimism is part of the programming, the deal has become impossible to ignore. The merger would reshape release calendars, bargaining dynamics and likely the flow of midbudget films that still need theatrical muscle to find audiences. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Ellison’s appearance also reflected how much studios are trying to reclaim the language of moviegoing. He invoked theater exclusivity at a moment when exhibitors are hungry for certainty, and he wrapped the promise in classic big-studio confidence. For owners, that combination matters. A reliable pipeline of films can help fill auditoriums and justify investment in premium formats, concessions and local marketing. For filmmakers and artists, the same promise can read differently: as a sign that the industry may be moving toward even more centralized control over what gets made and how it reaches audiences. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

CinemaCon is already revealing the next box-office battleground

The Paramount announcement did not happen in a vacuum. This week’s CinemaCon presentations have been stacked with footage and first looks from the studios that are still independently deciding what the box office will look like over the next year. Warner Bros. brought stars including Tom Cruise, Nicole Kidman, Timothée Chalamet, Zendaya, Sandra Bullock and Jason Momoa to Las Vegas, teasing projects that range from J.J. Abrams’ original sci-fi film “The Great Beyond” to Denis Villeneuve’s “Dune: Part Three.” Christopher Nolan also used the conference to show an extended clip from “The Odyssey,” which will arrive in theaters on July 17. Disney, meanwhile, unveiled the opening of “The Mandalorian and Grogu” and fresh footage from “Avengers: Doomsday.” ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/644b63a58677396cced445659df289a4?utm_source=openai))

The upshot is that theaters are being asked to plan around a pipeline that still depends heavily on a handful of event films. That is why the conference feels especially consequential now. Every marquee title matters more when owners are trying to recover audiences and studios are trying to prove theatrical exclusivity still has value. Universal’s spring hit “The Super Mario Galaxy Movie,” which has already powered a strong April box office, is the kind of crowd-pleaser exhibitors want more of — the sort of title that fills seats across formats and age groups. But the market’s dependence on a smaller number of wide releases also makes the industry more vulnerable to any disruption in studio strategy, mergers or labor conflicts. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32128b87e44ba4853829a8ff7fbc437f?utm_source=openai))

Why the merger fight matters to filmgoers, not just executives

For movie audiences, the Warner Bros.-Paramount fight may sound like corporate chess. In practice, it could affect what ends up playing at the multiplex. Consolidation can alter how many films get made, which directors get financing and how aggressively studios support midlevel dramas, thrillers and original stories that do not automatically come with brand recognition. If a merged Paramount-Warner Bros. prioritizes scale and franchise efficiency, the theatrical marketplace could tilt even further toward sequels, spin-offs and IP-driven spectacles. That would leave fewer slots for the kind of films that used to give theaters a steady middle class of releases between the tentpoles.

At the same time, the box office is still recovering from the uneven demand that has defined much of the post-strike, post-pandemic era. Studio leaders know that theaters want stronger guarantees because attendance remains fragile. They also know that audiences still turn out in huge numbers when a film feels like an event. CinemaCon this week has demonstrated both realities at once. On one side are the spectacular previews, first looks and star-studded appearances designed to persuade exhibitors that theatrical moviegoing still matters. On the other is the fight over who gets to control the marketplace that delivers those movies. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

That is why Ellison’s words landed so heavily in Las Vegas. They were not only a commitment to theaters; they were a preview of a Hollywood where scale, leverage and access to screens may be decided by fewer hands. For exhibitors, that may sound like stability. For many filmmakers, it sounds like less room to breathe. And for audiences, the question is whether a more concentrated studio system will mean a more reliable stream of blockbusters — or a narrower definition of what a movie can be. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))
