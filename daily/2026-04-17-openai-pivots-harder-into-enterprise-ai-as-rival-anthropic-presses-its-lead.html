OpenAI pivots harder into enterprise AI as rival Anthropic presses its lead

OpenAI is re-centering its business around enterprise customers, agents, and infrastructure after a string of April announcements underscored how quickly the AI market has shifted from consumer novelty to workplace utility.

By The Press • April 17, 2026 • 1:54 a.m.

OpenAI spent the first half of April making a pointed case for where the AI market is headed next: deeper into enterprise workflows, more tightly integrated with workplace software, and increasingly measured by how much real work its systems can do. In a note on April 8, the company said enterprise now accounts for more than 40% of revenue and is on track to reach parity with consumer revenue by the end of 2026. Four days later, OpenAI announced a deeper integration with Cloudflare’s Agent Cloud, giving millions of enterprise customers access to frontier models for production agents that can handle tasks such as customer responses, system updates and report generation. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))

Those announcements arrived against the backdrop of a more intense commercial fight with Anthropic, which has spent the past several months widening its international footprint, sharpening its safety positioning and pushing hard on enterprise products. Anthropic opened its first office in India late last year, announced a Sydney office in March, and updated its Responsible Scaling Policy on April 2. The company has argued that frontier AI should be paired with stronger safeguards and governance, a message that increasingly doubles as a competitive differentiator with large customers and policymakers. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/expanding-global-operations-to-india?utm_source=openai))

The enterprise turn

OpenAI’s April messaging suggests the company believes the next phase of AI adoption will be less about flashy consumer features and more about embedding models into the day-to-day machinery of businesses. In the April 8 post, Chief Revenue Officer Denise Dresser said she had seen “immense sense of urgency and readiness” from customers, and described a strategy built around a “frontier” intelligence layer that governs company-wide agents alongside a unified AI interface for employees. The same post said Codex had reached 3 million weekly active users and that OpenAI’s APIs were processing more than 15 billion tokens per minute. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))

The Cloudflare partnership extends that strategy into a distribution channel that already sits close to enterprise infrastructure. By making OpenAI models available in Agent Cloud, the companies are betting that the next corporate software upgrade will not be a chatbot window but a fleet of agents that can run behind the scenes. OpenAI said the integration would let customers deploy agents powered by GPT-5.4 and Codex into secure, production-ready environments. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/cloudflare-openai-agent-cloud?utm_source=openai))

That positioning matters because enterprise buyers have become the most lucrative and most demanding audience in AI. They want systems that are reliable, auditable and controllable, not just impressive in demos. OpenAI’s recent posts repeatedly stress security, administrative controls and deployment support, while Anthropic continues to market Claude as a trusted workplace assistant for regulated industries and international markets. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/academy/applications-of-ai/?utm_source=openai))

Anthropic’s safety-first counteroffer

Anthropic’s answer to the enterprise boom has been to lean harder into safety, governance and transparency. Its updated Responsible Scaling Policy, effective April 2, formalized the company’s latest safety roadmap and noted that it had already completed some prior safety goals. The policy describes frontier AI risk governance as something that should be “proportional, iterative, and exportable,” language that reflects a company trying to reassure both regulators and large buyers that powerful systems can be developed with clear guardrails. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy?utm_source=openai))

That message is not only philosophical; it is commercial. Anthropic has been expanding in the regions and industries most likely to care about compliance, from India to Australia and New Zealand. In March, the company said it was opening a Sydney office and cited demand from businesses in financial services, agriculture, healthcare, clean energy and scientific research. In February, it announced a collaboration with Infosys to build AI agents for telecommunications and other regulated sectors. Those moves suggest Anthropic is using safety and enterprise credibility as twin pillars of its international growth strategy. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/sydney-fourth-office-asia-pacific?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the company’s public stance has become part of the broader policy debate around how much power frontier labs should hold. Anthropic has recently sued the U.S. government over a Defense Department designation it disputes, and it has also argued publicly for stronger constraints on military and surveillance uses of AI. Whether one sees that as principled safety advocacy or brand positioning, it has helped define Anthropic as the lab most willing to challenge the industry’s default move fast, ship faster mentality. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/09/anthropic-sues-pentagon-supply-chain-risk-label?utm_source=openai))

Why this matters now

The most important shift in AI right now is not a new benchmark or a single model release. It is the speed at which frontier companies are reorganizing around the enterprise. OpenAI’s April posts show a company trying to turn its consumer reach into a broader business platform, with agents, APIs, infrastructure partnerships and workplace software all pulling in the same direction. Anthropic, meanwhile, is trying to win the same customers by emphasizing trust, controls and a more explicit doctrine for where the technology should not go. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))

That tension is likely to shape the next phase of competition. Enterprise customers do not just want access to powerful models; they want predictable pricing, dependable uptime, clear data boundaries and a vendor they believe will survive regulatory scrutiny. The firms that can offer all of that will have a stronger claim to the most durable revenue in AI. OpenAI’s recent revenue claim and Anthropic’s continued international expansion both point to a market that is no longer experimental. It is becoming structural. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a policy story embedded in the business one. OpenAI has recently published a raft of public-interest documents, from an industrial policy proposal to a child safety blueprint and a safety fellowship, while Anthropic has kept refining its scaling policy and related safeguards. Both companies know that the winners in AI will be judged not only by what they can build, but by what they can justify. In that sense, the battle between OpenAI and Anthropic is increasingly about the rules of the road as much as the products on it. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

For the AI desk, the takeaway is straightforward: the center of gravity in the industry has moved. The question is no longer whether businesses will adopt AI, but which company’s definition of enterprise AI — OpenAI’s agentic platform or Anthropic’s safety-led pitch — will become the default. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))

As of mid-April, the answer remains unsettled. But the latest round of announcements makes one thing clear: the race is now being fought in the boardroom, the compliance office and the cloud infrastructure stack, not just in the consumer app store. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/next-phase-of-enterprise-ai/?utm_source=openai))
