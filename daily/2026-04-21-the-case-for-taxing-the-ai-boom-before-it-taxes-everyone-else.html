The case for taxing the AI boom before it taxes everyone else

Data centers are no longer a private tech story. They are a public utilities story—and lawmakers should stop pretending the bill can be hidden.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 21, 2026 • 10:43 a.m. • 3031 words

America is making a dangerous mistake with artificial intelligence: it is treating the physical buildout behind the boom as if it were a private matter between hyperscale companies and their vendors. It is not. The new factories of the AI era are data centers, and data centers are electricity, water, land use, transmission lines, tax policy, and local consent wrapped into a single political problem. That is why the most important AI fight of 2026 is no longer only about model safety, deepfakes, or copyright. It is about who pays for the power, who bears the risk, and whether lawmakers let the costs of the AI race spill onto ordinary households while corporate balance sheets keep the upside.

The sharp thesis is this: states should not stop at disclosure rules or symbolic moratoriums. They should impose a hard public-interest bargain on the AI buildout—new data-center taxes where needed, utility-cost safeguards, mandatory grid and water transparency, and automatic ratepayer protections—because the current model socializes the costs of a private investment boom. The opposing case is real and deserves a steelman: America’s AI leadership is strategically valuable, regulation is fragmented, and overreaction could push investment, jobs, and innovation elsewhere. But the better answer is not to pretend the infrastructure has no externalities. The answer is to price them honestly and regulate them early, before utilities, courts, and public anger force worse outcomes later.

The story in 2026 is not just “AI regulation.” It is infrastructure backlash.

If the phrase “AI policy” still conjures up chatbots, content moderation, or model benchmarks, the political reality of 2026 is already out of date. The most visible resistance is now happening where the physical footprint of AI shows up: in electric bills, transmission corridors, and local water systems. Maine lawmakers approved a yearlong freeze on new large data centers, a move described by the Associated Press as the nation’s first statewide moratorium on energy-hungry facilities above a 20-megawatt threshold, while the state studies impacts on blackouts, water use, and electricity prices. That is not a fringe gesture. It is a warning shot from a place where the politics of power prices have crossed into explicit restraint on AI infrastructure. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/aa63ba087d5ad53ab0735893646e7357?utm_source=openai))

Virginia, which became a data-center powerhouse, is now debating whether its tax breaks have gone too far. AP reported in March that state senators voted to end a projected $1.6 billion annual sales-tax break by requiring the industry to resume paying a minimum 5.3% sales tax. In Indiana, AP reported that a councilman said shots were fired at his home and a “No Data Centers” note was left behind, a disturbing episode that showed how fast local disputes have become charged symbols of broader resentment. And in Pennsylvania, AP reported that AI is spurring a rapid expansion of high-voltage power lines, with landowners and locals fighting back against long-distance transmission built to serve enormous data centers. The point is not that all opposition is rational or that every project should be blocked. The point is that the political system is already telling us the current bargain is brittle. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fb9e6dbe61fbf03c467d1301f00bafb7?utm_source=openai))

At the federal level, the signal is just as clear. AP reported in late March that two high-profile progressive lawmakers introduced a bill to pause new data centers until national safeguards are in place. Separately, AP and Brookings have documented the continuing fight over whether Congress should preempt state AI laws, with the White House arguing that a patchwork of state rules can burden innovation while states insist on their own authority to protect consumers and communities. The result is a genuine federalism conflict, but beneath the constitutional drama is a simpler question: if the federal government wants a lighter touch on software policy, does that mean ratepayers should also subsidize the hardware buildout? That is the hidden issue no one in Washington seems eager to answer head-on. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/65651bd28c3d911d18eeb46cd54f4c75?utm_source=openai))

Why the electricity numbers matter more than the rhetoric.

The data are no longer speculative. The International Energy Agency says data centers accounted for about 1.5 percent of global electricity consumption in 2024, or 415 terawatt-hours, and projects that consumption will more than double to around 945 TWh by 2030. The same IEA material says data centers account for roughly one-tenth of global electricity demand growth to 2030. In a separate IEA update, the agency says U.S. data centers consumed around 180 TWh in 2024 and that their electricity demand is expected to rise by roughly 240 TWh relative to 2024 levels by 2030. Those are not niche figures. They are the scale of a new industrial load. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

The Department of Energy’s own analysis says U.S. data centers consumed about 4.4 percent of total electricity in 2023 and could rise to between 6.7 percent and 12 percent by 2028. DOE has also warned that the country is returning to a period of rising electricity demand, with data center expansion among the drivers. That is not wrong. But an opportunity for whom? The agency’s own materials also point to the need for clean firm power, storage, and better transmission planning if the grid is going to absorb the boom without shifting costs onto everyone else. If the AI race depends on the power system acting as a valet for private compute, then the public should negotiate hard over the terms. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

Water makes the case even stronger. UC Riverside researchers reported in March 2026 that spikes in data-center water demand could cost communities billions in new infrastructure, with evaporative cooling systems sometimes driving daily water demand to six to ten times average usage. That matters because the worst thing about data-center externalities is not that they are dramatic; it is that they are cumulative and locally concentrated. A single campus may look like a source of jobs and property-tax revenue. But the utility system has to plan for peak loads, not press releases. So do water systems, zoning boards, and schools that must absorb growth without a matching tax base. Even if one is skeptical of the most alarming projections, the direction of travel is enough to justify precaution. ([news.ucr.edu](https://news.ucr.edu/articles/2026/03/09/data-center-water-spikes-could-cost-billions?utm_source=openai))

The steelman: why a hard clampdown could backfire.

The best opposing argument begins with a legitimate strategic concern. AI is not a luxury. It is becoming a general-purpose technology with implications for productivity, medicine, science, cybersecurity, logistics, and defense. The IEA says AI’s rapid development has made it central to corporate strategies, economic policies, and geopolitics. If the United States slows infrastructure buildout too aggressively, the argument goes, it could hand an advantage to rivals while pushing investment to states or countries with looser standards and weaker environmental rules. That is not fantasy. It is a real risk. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

There is also a strong case against a chaotic patchwork of state rules. Brookings argued on April 20, 2026, that regulatory uncertainty is more disruptive to innovation than regulation itself, but the same debate cuts the other way too: fragmented state rules can create compliance costs and uncertainty for builders. A uniform federal framework can reduce compliance costs, simplify deployment, and make it easier for both large and small companies to plan investments. That argument has force, particularly for a technology whose value often depends on scale. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/regulatory-uncertainty-is-what-actually-holds-back-innovation/?utm_source=openai))

Even opponents of aggressive regulation should be wary of blunt instruments. A moratorium, like Maine’s, may buy time for study, but it can also function as a growth freeze that simply relocates the problem. A tax that is too high can distort site selection without actually shrinking demand, especially if developers can shift projects to neighboring states or countries. And some local resistance is plainly NIMBY politics in energy clothing. Communities benefit from jobs, infrastructure upgrades, and tax-base expansion as well as suffering the downsides. Any serious policy must acknowledge that not every data center is a predation machine. Some are worth hosting. Some are not. The challenge is distinguishing between them. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/aa63ba087d5ad53ab0735893646e7357?utm_source=openai))

That steelman should not be dismissed. It is the reason the right answer is not a blanket anti-data-center crusade. It is a framework that separates socially valuable projects from opportunistic ones, and makes developers internalize the costs they impose. The issue is not whether America should build AI infrastructure. It is whether the public gets a fair return for the privilege of hosting it.

There is a better policy than either laissez-faire or panic.

The case for action starts with simple economics. If a data center drives up peak demand, strengthens the case for new transmission, adds strain to water systems, or forces utilities to buy emergency capacity, those costs do not disappear just because a company says the project is “strategic.” They land somewhere. Often they land on households, municipalities, and small businesses that never chose to enter the AI race. That is the definition of an externality. When the public pays, the public should regulate. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

So what would a serious policy look like? First, states and utilities should require real-time transparency on load forecasts, water use, back-up generation, and interconnection terms for large AI facilities. Secret power deals are especially toxic because they let companies negotiate private arrangements while ratepayers bear the uncertainty. AP’s reporting on data-center backlash in general, and its January report on rising opposition in communities, underscore how much public frustration is driven by opacity about who pays for grid upgrades and who absorbs the downside. Large-load customers should not be allowed to hide behind confidentiality when their footprint reshapes the grid. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3c6988c44455d34c0e8db6dd63fdfd57?utm_source=openai))

Second, states should adopt cost-causation rules that make new facilities contribute to the grid upgrades they require. That does not mean punishing all data centers equally. It means distinguishing between facilities that bring their own generation, storage, or flexible load management and those that simply demand immediate, fossil-heavy reliability from everyone else. DOE materials and IEA analysis both point to the importance of storage, grid flexibility, and new generation in handling the load growth. That suggests a policy path: reward flexible operations, penalize inflexible ones, and stop pretending the cheapest kilowatt-hour is the only relevant metric. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/oe/clean-energy-resources-meet-data-center-electricity-demand?utm_source=openai))

Third, local governments should be allowed, even encouraged, to negotiate community-benefit agreements and tax structures tailored to the actual burden imposed. Virginia’s tax fight is instructive. If a state is giving away substantial revenue to attract projects that already need a state-approved utility footprint, voters are right to ask what exactly they are buying. A fair bargain might include property-tax stabilization, workforce commitments, water-efficiency standards, and mandatory contributions to grid upgrades. The public should not subsidize private land grabs in the name of progress. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/fb9e6dbe61fbf03c467d1301f00bafb7?utm_source=openai))

Fourth, policymakers should stop treating all AI infrastructure as equally urgent. There is a difference between experimental compute, inferencing for consumer products, and the giant campuses built to support frontier model training. Some facilities may warrant fast-track treatment if they are paired with clean firm power and genuine local benefits. Others should be slowed or rejected if they are designed to consume scarce capacity without adding resilience. DOE’s own work on clean energy resources, geothermal, and on-site energy solutions suggests government can guide siting toward better outcomes rather than simply rubber-stamp every proposal that arrives with a venture-capital deck. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/oe/clean-energy-resources-meet-data-center-electricity-demand?utm_source=openai))

The federal government has already admitted this is a public-power problem.

One reason the debate is so uneven is that Washington wants the benefits of AI without the political burden of the infrastructure. That is hard to sustain. The White House has pressed for national rules that would preempt state AI laws, but the same administration and agencies also know that electricity and grid reliability are central constraints. NIST’s April 7, 2026 concept note launching an AI RMF profile for trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure is an explicit acknowledgment that AI policy is no longer purely abstract. It is operational. If the government is serious about AI adoption in power-critical sectors, it has to be serious about power itself. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

The federal government has also already shown a willingness to intervene when the public sees AI harms as concrete. Congress passed the TAKE IT DOWN Act in 2025, and the law’s notice-and-removal requirements for platforms take effect in May 2026. FTC guidance says the agency will begin civil enforcement in May 2026. That law is a reminder that even a pro-innovation political environment will regulate when a technology’s harms become legible enough to mobilize voters and lawmakers. The current controversy is just broader and slower-moving. Instead of a deepfake that ruins one life, we have a buildout that can quietly reshape utility rates and local infrastructure for millions. The case for policy is at least as strong. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11314?utm_source=openai))

Indeed, the political lesson of 2026 is that AI regulation is no longer only about what the software can do to people. It is about what the hardware does to communities. That shift matters because traditional tech-policy debates often abstract away the physical economy. But the AI era is dragging policy back to first principles. A server farm is not a cloud. It is a power customer. And power customers are subject to regulation because they shape a shared system. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/buildings/data-centers-and-servers?utm_source=openai))

Why the “innovation first” camp underestimates local democracy.

Industry defenders often say that local resistance is emotional and parochial, while national-scale AI infrastructure is too strategically important to be held hostage by neighborhood politics. There is some truth in that. A country cannot be run entirely by zoning hearings. But the innovation-first camp often mistakes inconvenience for illegitimacy. Local governments are not merely obstacles. They are the institutions that must absorb the consequences when the grid strains, when construction traffic piles up, when transmission corridors cut across farms, and when water systems are forced to expand. The fact that the costs are diffuse does not make them imaginary. It makes them politically vulnerable to capture. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f1c6b1251054d2a7e193297e4111b8d1?utm_source=openai))

That is why the strongest policy answer is not to eliminate local power but to discipline it with national standards. If every state had to require transparent impact disclosures, minimum cost-sharing, and clear consumer safeguards, the worst race-to-the-bottom dynamics would ease. Brookings’ coverage of state AI lawmaking argues that states are already experimenting with notice requirements and broader accountability frameworks, and that a moratorium on state action could stall consumer protection and innovation at once. The federal government should not crush that experimentation; it should ensure the public is not left to pay for it. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/states-are-legislating-ai-but-a-moratorium-could-stall-their-progress/?utm_source=openai))

At minimum, governors and legislatures should treat large data centers the way they treat major industrial projects with significant externalities: as conditional privileges, not automatic entitlements. That means environmental review that includes peak-load modeling, not just construction permits. It means public utility commissions publishing the assumptions behind demand forecasts. It means refusing to sign off on sweetheart tax deals unless there is hard evidence that the project’s benefits exceed its systemwide cost. And it means acknowledging that AI enthusiasm does not repeal the laws of thermodynamics or public finance.

The counterargument’s strongest point is also its weakness.

Supporters of the current boom are right that the United States should not become a hostile place to build the technologies that may shape the next decade. They are right that overregulation could freeze experimentation or create a handoff to less transparent jurisdictions. They are right that not every community complaint reflects a genuine public harm. But they are wrong to infer from those truths that the proper policy is minimal interference. The scale of projected electricity growth makes that position unsustainable. The public is already bearing the burden, whether or not the accounting is honest enough to show it immediately. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

In practice, the “let the market handle it” view tends to mean letting utilities socialize risk, letting municipalities bargain from weakness, and letting developers pocket gains while the system absorbs the downside. The result is not a free market; it is a subsidized one. And because the subsidy arrives as deferred grid costs, opaque power contracts, and creeping infrastructure strain, it is easy for proponents to deny it until the bills come due. That is exactly how bad public finance happens.

A sane AI agenda would price the boom, not ban it.

There is a difference between hostility to AI and hostility to being exploited by it. The public does not need to choose between open-ended deregulation and reflexive prohibition. It can insist on a bargain that keeps the upsides while forcing the industry to pay for the actual inputs it consumes. That means transparent utility contracts, data-center-specific cost allocation, water and grid disclosure, and local benefit requirements. It also means recognizing that “innovation” is not a magic word that cancels public obligations. If the sector is producing immense private value, then it can afford to help fund the public systems that make that value possible. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/oe/clean-energy-resources-meet-data-center-electricity-demand?utm_source=openai))

The choice before lawmakers is not whether AI will continue. It will. The choice is whether the buildout happens under a regime that makes costs visible and shared, or under one that lets them sneak onto everyone else’s bill. Maine’s freeze, Virginia’s tax revolt, the Pennsylvania transmission backlash, and the Indiana shooting are all telling us the same thing in different accents: the public is no longer willing to subsidize a private compute boom on faith. Lawmakers should listen before the backlash hardens into something more destructive than a tax or a disclosure rule. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/aa63ba087d5ad53ab0735893646e7357?utm_source=openai))

The most defensible path is also the least glamorous: make the AI companies prove they are good grid citizens before they get the keys to the substation. In 2026, that is not anti-innovation. It is basic democratic housekeeping.

What the best policymaking should do next

Congress should abandon fantasies of a blanket moratorium on state AI laws and instead create a narrow baseline for large-load disclosure, cost allocation, and consumer protection. States should not wait for Washington to stop acting as if utilities are invisible. Regulators should require proof that data centers will not shift undue costs to households. And local governments should stop handing out tax concessions without binding, measurable public returns. If the AI industry wants to be treated like a strategic sector, it should accept strategic-sector obligations. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/states-are-legislating-ai-but-a-moratorium-could-stall-their-progress/?utm_source=openai))

That is the editorial bottom line: regulate the infrastructure of AI as infrastructure. Not because the technology is evil, but because the bill is real.
