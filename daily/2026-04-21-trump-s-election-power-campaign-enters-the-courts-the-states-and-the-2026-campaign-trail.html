Trump’s election-power campaign enters the courts, the states, and the 2026 campaign trail

A new push by the Justice Department, the White House and allied Republicans to reshape election rules is colliding with lawsuits, state resistance and looming midterm deadlines.

By The Press • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m.

The most consequential fight in American politics right now is not taking place on a debate stage or in a campaign ad buy. It is unfolding through letters, lawsuits, executive orders, rule changes, state election offices and the federal courts. By April 2026, the central question is no longer whether the Trump administration wants a much larger role in setting the rules of American elections. It does. The question is how far that effort can go before courts, states and election officials stop it.

That conflict has become especially visible in the past few months. The Justice Department has sued a long list of states over voter-registration data and list-maintenance records, while the White House has pressed an executive agenda that seeks to tighten federal control over voting procedures, including documentary proof-of-citizenship demands and limits on mail ballots. Republican officials in several states are exploring new redistricting moves. Meanwhile, a Supreme Court case on the Voting Rights Act could alter the legal terrain beneath all of it. The result is a sprawling power struggle over who gets to define the mechanics of democracy before the 2026 midterms. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-arizona-and-connecticut-failure-produce-voter-rolls?utm_source=openai))

This is not a single story with one headline. It is a cluster of related stories converging at once: litigation over presidential power, disputes over voter rolls, preparations for the 2026 election calendar, state-by-state fights over congressional maps, and a possible judicial reworking of voting-rights law. Each piece matters on its own. Together, they amount to a durable attempt to move election administration away from states and toward the executive branch, even if the administration’s lawyers describe their work as ordinary enforcement of federal law. Supporters frame that effort as a long-overdue cleanup of federal elections. Critics see a bid to centralize power in ways that could reshape turnout, ballot access and the competitive balance of the midterms. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/crt/voting-section?utm_source=openai))

What is happening now

The immediate trigger is the administration’s election agenda, which has been tested in court almost as quickly as it has been announced. In January, a federal judge blocked most of President Trump’s elections executive order from being enforced against Washington and Oregon, two vote-by-mail states, after finding the administration had exceeded presidential authority. In a separate January ruling, the same judge blocked additional citizenship-related provisions. Those decisions did not end the issue. They merely shifted the fight to the next round of injunctions, appeals and administrative maneuvers. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the Justice Department has pressed states for voter-registration rolls, list-maintenance records and related materials. In January, the department sued Oregon and Maine. Days later it sued Virginia. By late January and into April, it had also sued Arizona, Connecticut, Idaho and several other states, while a separate suit targeted Fulton County, Georgia, for election records. The department has cast the dispute as an effort to ensure accurate, well-maintained voter lists. State officials and voting-rights groups have answered that the department is demanding information beyond what federal law requires, or at minimum seeking it on a timeline and with a scope that could chill legitimate state practices. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-oregon-and-maine-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

On April 20, 2026, AP reported that the Justice Department demanded 2024 Detroit-area ballots in a fresh move aimed at local election records. The request is notable not just because it is directed at a swing-state jurisdiction, but because it shows how the administration’s election push is not limited to future rules; it is also mining recent elections for evidence that can support broader claims about fraud, list accuracy and federal oversight. Michigan officials called the move an attempt to weaponize the Justice Department and interfere in state elections. That response reflects a larger pattern: the administration is using federal civil-rights and elections authority in a way that state Democrats say is unprecedentedly aggressive, while its own officials argue they are simply enforcing the law neutrally and transparently. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4341df00ea8a2814a9fd42f2225d4495?utm_source=openai))

Overlaying this is the hard calendar of 2026. The Federal Election Commission has issued its April reporting reminders and deadline notices for quarterly, monthly and congressional filings, underscoring how quickly the 2026 cycle is moving from theory to operational reality. Those deadlines matter because campaign-finance disclosure is one of the few windows into how the parties are positioning themselves in a year when election administration itself is a front-line issue. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-six-additional-states-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The White House strategy: centralize, nationalize, litigate

The administration’s election approach appears to have three overlapping goals. First, it seeks to create a national baseline of rules, especially around citizenship verification and mail ballots. Second, it seeks to use federal law enforcement and civil-rights tools to police state election administration. Third, it seeks to pressure courts and Congress into accepting a more expansive reading of presidential authority over elections than has traditionally existed.

The first goal is visible in the executive-order fight. The second is visible in the Justice Department’s spate of records demands and lawsuits. The third is visible in the administration’s willingness to push legal theories that have been rejected or sharply narrowed by lower courts. AP reported in January that a federal judge found the Constitution gives the power to regulate federal elections to states and Congress, not the president, a theme echoed in later rulings that blocked parts of the executive order. That matters because the administration’s gains, if any, would not come from a single decisive win; they would come from incremental relief, narrower appeals rulings, and pressure on state agencies to conform before final judgments arrive. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

There is also a political logic here. Elections are one of the few national issues where an incumbent president can claim an anti-system reform agenda even while in office. Tightening voter-roll maintenance, requiring proof of citizenship and limiting the timing of ballot receipt are all measures that can be sold as common-sense safeguards to suspicious voters. They can also be framed as responses to post-2020 distrust. For Trump and his allies, that framing is useful because it casts the administration not as changing the rules for partisan gain, but as restoring confidence in a system they argue has become too permissive or too decentralized. Whether that claim is convincing is another matter. But it explains why the White House sees political opportunity in a fight that legal scholars view as constitutionally risky. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

Even the language used by the administration signals the broader ambition. The Justice Department’s press releases repeatedly describe “accurate” and “secure” elections, but they also describe states as if they were potentially resistant local actors rather than coequal sovereigns. That framing is not merely rhetorical. It reflects a view that federal enforcement should be proactive, data-intensive and aggressive, especially where state practices diverge from the administration’s preferred model. In normal circumstances, that kind of pressure is likely to be defended as standard election oversight. In this moment, though, it is being deployed alongside a White House effort to command the field through presidential directives. Together, the pieces amount to a nationalization project. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-arizona-and-connecticut-failure-produce-voter-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The state-level resistance is real, but uneven

The pushback is equally important, because America’s election system remains heavily state-driven. Secretaries of state, county officials and state courts still control the mechanics of registration, ballot access, counting and certification in most circumstances. That is why the administration’s strategy runs into such forceful resistance when it meets states with different voting traditions, especially vote-by-mail states such as Oregon and Washington. Federal judges have already been willing to block at least parts of the executive-order effort, which means the administration has not achieved the kind of legal momentum that would let it standardize the system quickly. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

But the resistance is uneven. Some states are likely to litigate aggressively. Others may compromise. Still others may quietly adopt pieces of the administration’s agenda if doing so helps them avoid conflict or win federal favor. That matters because a national election fight does not require a single sweeping federal victory. It can advance through dozens of smaller administrative bargains and state-level adjustments. The most consequential changes are often not the public ones; they are the implementation details, the deadlines, the list-maintenance standards and the disclosure requirements that determine whether a voter is automatically kept on the rolls, how easily a ballot can be rejected, or whether election offices must divert staff to comply with a federal demand.

Wisconsin offers an example of the uncertainty. AP reported that a judge put on hold an order requiring citizenship checks for all 3.6 million registered voters in the state, and that the dispute could remain unresolved beyond the 2026 midterm election. The case matters because it shows how election litigation can drag out long enough to affect actual voting, even when the underlying legal questions are not settled. It also demonstrates how courts may be reluctant to force massive procedural changes on the eve of major contests when the evidence is contested and the practical burden is enormous. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/e2cf2ce48fd8f78bc2ac038b9fcffe51?utm_source=openai))

New Jersey’s special election calendar is another reminder that state-level election decisions are shaping the 2026 landscape in real time. AP reported that Democratic Gov.-elect Mikie Sherrill resigned her House seat and that the state set a special primary for Feb. 5 and a special general election for April 16. The race was later won by Democrat Analilia Mejía. The episode matters not only for one House seat but because it shows how mid-2026 politics are already being defined by an unusually dense election calendar. Redistricting, special elections, Supreme Court litigation and federal-election-rule disputes are all colliding before the summer has even started. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cea3e9549d6d83613150119cd98a6357?utm_source=openai))

Why the Justice Department’s records demands matter

At first glance, voter-roll lawsuits can sound technocratic. They are anything but. Who controls the data can determine who gets contacted, who gets purged, who gets flagged for investigation and who gets to vote without friction. That makes records demands more than an administrative dispute; they are a battle over the informational foundation of elections.

The administration’s defenders say the federal government has both authority and obligation to ensure that voter rolls are accurate. That is a serious argument. List maintenance is necessary. Duplicate registrations, outdated addresses and inactive entries are real problems in every state. The Justice Department’s Voting Section exists precisely to enforce federal voting statutes, including the National Voter Registration Act, the Help America Vote Act and the Voting Rights Act. If a state refuses to produce materials that the law requires, the federal government is entitled to sue. That is the pro-enforcement case, and it is not frivolous. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/crt/voting-section?utm_source=openai))

But there is a sharp limit: the federal government does not get to treat every disagreement about election administration as proof of bad faith. The National Voter Registration Act, the Help America Vote Act and related laws impose obligations, but they do not create a blank check for federal officials to rummage through state files or use voter-registration disputes as a pretext for broader political aims. That is why a federal judge in the CREW v. DOJ matter found the public interest in understanding the details of DOJ’s effort to compile a national voter list to be substantial. In other words, the legal fight is already a live one, not a future hypothetical. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/crt/voting-section?utm_source=openai))

There is also a practical risk: aggressive list-cleanup programs can make eligible voters nervous or, worse, remove them by mistake. That is why the quiet-period logic in federal election law matters so much. DOJ’s past litigation against Virginia, for example, centered on whether systematic list maintenance too close to an election risked error and confusion. The law’s architecture recognizes that the wrong kind of cleanup can suppress the vote as easily as it can protect ballot integrity. That tension is now at the center of the current struggle. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-virginia-failure-produce-voter-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The redistricting arms race is part of the same story

Election rules are not only about who can register and how ballots are counted. They are also about which voters are grouped together in which districts. That is why the redistricting moves now underway in states such as Florida are politically inseparable from the legal battles over election administration. AP reported that Gov. Ron DeSantis said he planned to call a special session in April to redraw Florida’s congressional districts, explicitly tying the move to a possible Supreme Court ruling in Louisiana v. Callais. The governor said one or two districts in Florida could be affected. That is not a small technical adjustment. It is a bid to shift the partisan composition of the House before the midterms. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b10b743019ba7f25a2f26d3ccdaf9a67?utm_source=openai))

The Callais case is therefore one of the most important unresolved pieces in the broader puzzle. The Supreme Court docket shows the case remains active, and the public record reflects continuing attention to its effect on Louisiana’s 2026 election calendar. If the Court weakens Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, Republican-led states may have more room to redraw districts without creating minority-opportunity seats. If the Court preserves current precedent, the redraw effort becomes much harder. Either way, the case could change the map of power in Congress more directly than almost any other pending dispute. ([supremecourt.gov](https://www.supremecourt.gov/docket/docketfiles/html/public/24-109.html?utm_source=openai))

That is why the redistricting fight and the election-administration fight are really one story. Both concern how much democratic competition the system should permit, and both are being shaped by institutions that are not directly electoral themselves: the White House, the Justice Department and the Supreme Court. If those institutions lean in the same direction, the cumulative effect can be dramatic. If they check one another, the result is a slower, more contested process. The current evidence suggests both are happening at once: heavy pressure from the executive branch and real institutional resistance from the courts and states. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

What the campaign-finance data can and cannot tell us

The FEC’s campaign-finance calendar is useful because it shows when the political class is forced to reveal more of its machinery. The April reports due in 2026 capture spending and fundraising through March 31 or March activity, depending on filer type. They are not a full picture of political momentum, but they provide a disciplined baseline for seeing whether committees are ramping up field operations, legal spending or ballot-access efforts around the election fight. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-six-additional-states-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The broader FEC data also show the scale of the 2025-26 cycle so far. The commission’s statistical summaries show a cycle already rich with resources, which matters because election-law wars are expensive. Lawsuits cost money. Redistricting campaigns cost money. Ballot-access fights and voter-roll challenges cost money. The money trail will not tell us who is right, but it will tell us who is investing in the fight and where the pressure points are. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-settles-lawsuit-challenging-biden-state-departments-alleged-social-1?utm_source=openai))

Still, campaign-finance data have limits. They are lagging indicators. They tell us what committees have already reported, not what the White House is planning tomorrow or what a judge may order next week. That is why the most useful reading of the FEC data is as context rather than as proof. They confirm that the 2026 cycle is active, flush with money and increasingly organized around election administration as a core issue. They do not settle the larger constitutional question of who should control that administration. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-six-additional-states-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

What complicates the anti-Trump narrative

A strong news story cannot be one-dimensional. There are real arguments on the other side. One is that the United States does have a longstanding problem with fragmented election rules, inconsistent list maintenance and uneven standards across states. Another is that confidence in elections has been badly damaged by years of false claims, legal challenges and public skepticism. In that environment, a more assertive federal posture may seem not only understandable but necessary. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/crt/voting-section?utm_source=openai))

There is also a factual distinction between enforcing existing law and changing the law. The administration and DOJ can plausibly argue that they are doing the former. If states are out of compliance with federal records requirements, the federal government is not only allowed to act; it may be obligated to act. If the White House is merely pressing for a tighter interpretation of existing authority, supporters can say that is how constitutional litigation works. The courts decide the boundary. The administration argues for its view. The states argue back. From that perspective, the current fight is a normal legal process, albeit one with unusually high stakes. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-arizona-and-connecticut-failure-produce-voter-rolls?utm_source=openai))

Some election scholars and election administrators also worry that after years of public distrust, the status quo is not sustainable. If states cannot demonstrate that rolls are current, ballot rules are clear and citizenship requirements are enforceable, they risk fueling more suspicion. Even critics of the administration’s methods may concede that one reason the Trump agenda has traction is that many voters believe the system is too easy to game. That is a political fact, not a legal conclusion. But it helps explain why the administration’s message resonates beyond its base.

Then there is the Supreme Court. However much lower courts have blocked parts of the administration’s elections order, the legal landscape can still shift if the Court ultimately narrows voting-rights protections or expands executive latitude. The judiciary is not a passive backdrop. It is one of the main arbiters. That makes the current moment less a clash between a president and the states than a larger constitutional test of how much centralized authority America’s election system can absorb. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4b683fe2e1106316fdb05621be9b7d0e?utm_source=openai))

The likely impact on the 2026 midterms

If the administration’s election agenda succeeds in even part, the midterms could become harder to administer and easier to contest. Some voters may face more paperwork. Some states may have to change registration workflows on short notice. Some election offices may become more cautious about list maintenance for fear of litigation. That could suppress confusion in some places and create it in others. In a tight House cycle, even small shifts can matter.

If the agenda fails in the courts, the political damage may be different but still significant. A series of losses would further entrench the idea that the president is overreaching into a domain reserved to the states. That could motivate Republican officials to seek legislative workarounds or more state-level experiments in redistricting, ballot access or election-law reform. In other words, even defeat in court may not end the story; it may simply move the contest from the federal executive to the states and Congress.

There is no reason to expect a neat conclusion before November. The more likely outcome is fragmented implementation: some rulings, some injunctions, some state compliance, some state defiance and a lot of legal ambiguity. That is a dangerous place to be in a democracy heading into a high-stakes election year. The system can withstand disagreement. It struggles when the rules themselves become the campaign. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Three developments will tell us whether this is a passing skirmish or a structural shift. First, watch the federal appellate courts and any emergency Supreme Court action on the elections executive-order cases. If the administration starts winning relief, the pace of federal intervention could accelerate sharply. Second, watch the DOJ’s next round of records demands and state responses. The more states comply, negotiate or resist, the clearer the emerging national pattern will be. Third, watch Louisiana v. Callais. A ruling that narrows Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act would reverberate through redistricting fights in Florida and elsewhere, changing the partisan map before ballots are even printed. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/574546e2940c82154f5a5bd050d6dccf?utm_source=openai))

What is already clear is that election administration has moved from the back office of democracy to its front line. The administration’s effort to expand federal power over elections is not a side issue, and it is not likely to fade. It is now intertwined with redistricting, voter-roll enforcement, campaign finance and the Supreme Court’s next major voting-rights decision. That is why this story matters in politics, not just in law. Whoever controls the rules controls the terms of competition. And right now, those rules are still being fought over in real time.
