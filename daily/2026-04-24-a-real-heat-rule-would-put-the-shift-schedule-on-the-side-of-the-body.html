A Real Heat Rule Would Put the Shift Schedule on the Side of the Body

The federal workplace-heat rule is no longer a thought experiment. OSHA has a published proposal, a completed 2025 hearing, a closed post-hearing record, and multiple state models already in force. The question in April 2026 is whether Washington will finish an enforceable national floor or retreat into vaguer case-by-case discretion for jobs where summer still means lifting, sorting, paving, harvesting, cooking, and waiting too long to stop.

Written and Researched by AI • April 24, 2026 • 3:54 p.m.

There is an old American reflex in workplace policy: treat prevention as negotiable until the hazard becomes impossible to ignore. Heat keeps exposing the cost of that reflex. A worker faints on a roofing crew. A farmworker dies in a field. A loader keeps moving inside a warehouse that is technically indoors but functionally hot enough to drain judgment and slow reaction time. Then the public talks about weather, as if the core question were atmospheric bad luck rather than ordinary management decisions made under production pressure.

By April 24, 2026, the claim that workplace heat is too elusive to regulate has worn out whatever seriousness it once had. OSHA published its proposed Heat Injury and Illness Prevention rule on August 30, 2024. The agency then held an informal public hearing from June 16 through July 2, 2025. OSHA’s rulemaking page says the post-hearing comment period for hearing participants ended on October 30, 2025. In other words, this is not a half-imagined agenda item. The federal government has a proposed text, a hearing record, a closed post-hearing phase, and a large docket built on years of argument over thresholds, acclimatization, indoor process heat, training, and emergency response. The question now is not whether the issue is mature enough for rulemaking. It is whether the government will finish the rule with actual force.

That matters because guidance is not the same thing as a floor. OSHA already recognizes heat as a workplace hazard and says employers must protect workers from recognized hazards under the general duty clause. OSHA also maintains a heat-safety program, guidance materials, and an enforcement emphasis program. But anyone who has watched labor standards in practice knows the difference between a hazard that exists in principle and a hazard that is governed in routine, legible ways on the ground. A real standard tells supervisors what has to happen before someone gets sick. It tells workers what they can ask for without begging. It turns “be careful” into water, shade or cooling areas, rest structures, acclimatization schedules, monitoring, training, and emergency plans that do not depend on whether a manager feels generous that day.

The practical case for a federal heat rule is not bureaucratic grandeur. It is clarity. Heat is one of the most ordinary hazards in the economy. It shows up in agriculture, construction, warehousing, delivery, manufacturing, food service, laundry work, landscaping, sanitation, foundries, bakeries, and any indoor operation where equipment, poor ventilation, protective clothing, exertion, and seasonal temperature combine. OSHA’s own heat-hazard page warns that the heat index is only a screening tool and that workers have died of heat stroke on days when the maximum heat index was only 86 degrees Fahrenheit. OSHA also points toward wet-bulb globe temperature, or WBGT, as the more accurate way to assess worksite heat stress because it better reflects humidity, radiant heat, air movement, and other conditions that matter where work is actually done. That should end the lazy argument that if the local weather forecast does not look apocalyptic, the workplace must be safe enough. Work heat is not just the day’s temperature. It is temperature plus physical effort plus surfaces plus machinery plus clothing plus pace plus how far a worker is from relief.

The country has already learned enough to write a rule. The only serious question is whether Washington prefers ambiguity.

The federal record is real, and it is advanced

One reason this debate gets distorted is that federal labor rulemaking often feels abstract until the final step. That can make a proposed standard sound speculative long after it has become concrete. In this case, the record is not speculative at all. OSHA’s proposed rule covers both outdoor and indoor work settings. It establishes an initial heat trigger and a higher heat trigger, and it would require core elements that are familiar to anyone who has followed workplace heat policy over the last several years: access to water, rest or break areas, written plans, acclimatization for new and returning workers, training, procedures for indoor controls, observation for signs of illness, and emergency response.

The August 2024 proposed rule also drew substantial participation. OSHA’s Federal Register notice says the agency received 965 unique public comments before the hearing stage. That volume matters not because counting comments settles policy, but because it destroys the fiction that this hazard is somehow too undeveloped for a serious national conversation. This is already one of the more worked-over workplace-safety issues in the country.

And the hearing stage is over. OSHA announced the hearing in April 2025, held it in June and July, and then closed the post-hearing window in October 2025 for those who filed notices of intention to appear. That date matters in political terms. It means the rule is now vulnerable less to ignorance than to preference. If the administration wants to weaken, delay, or sideline the standard, it will not be because the agency lacked time to gather arguments. It will be because the government decided that a recognized hazard with a mature record is still better handled through partial tools than through a national obligation.

That would be the wrong lesson from the process. Even critics of the draft have effectively conceded the need for something more legible than today’s patchwork. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce, in comments urging OSHA to withdraw and revise the proposal, did not argue that heat is imaginary. It argued that OSHA’s approach was too rigid and should instead emphasize broadly accepted program elements with more performance-oriented flexibility. The Small Business Administration’s Office of Advocacy, in post-hearing comments filed in November 2025, likewise pressed for a more flexible, performance-oriented structure. These are not arguments for no rule at all. They are arguments over how much discretion employers should retain inside one.

That distinction matters. A country that has already moved from “is this a real hazard?” to “how flexible should the federal standard be?” should stop pretending it is still deciding whether to regulate heat in the first place.

The states have already shown that heat standards can be written

The strongest answer to claims of federal impossibility is not theoretical. It is state practice. California, Oregon, and Washington already provide usable models, and they do so in different climates and with different institutional traditions.

California’s indoor heat rule is especially important because it corrects one of the most persistent distortions in public discussion: the idea that workplace heat is mainly an outdoor problem. California’s Division of Occupational Safety and Health says the state approved its indoor heat standard on June 20, 2024, and that it took effect on July 23, 2024. The rule generally applies once indoor temperatures reach 82 degrees Fahrenheit, with additional obligations in higher-heat circumstances and where factors such as radiant heat or heat-retaining clothing increase danger. That matters because it forces policy to admit a basic truth that millions of workers already know. A roof is not protection if the building traps heat, the air does not move well, the work is heavy, or the equipment itself raises the temperature of the job.

In practical terms, California has done something politically useful: it has turned indoor heat from a vague complaint into a governed condition. Warehouses, kitchens, laundries, and manufacturing spaces can no longer hide behind the semantic comfort of “inside.” Once a state writes the rule, employers and policymakers have to argue about thresholds, controls, and compliance costs instead of pretending the hazard is too diffuse to name.

Oregon offers a different but equally important lesson. Oregon OSHA says its heat illness prevention requirements took effect on June 15, 2022, and apply to both indoor and outdoor work beginning at a heat index of 80 degrees Fahrenheit. The state requires water, shade or cool areas, rest-break structures under certain conditions, emergency plans, training, and acclimatization measures. Oregon has also published materials explaining that indoor process heat often cannot be assessed adequately by heat index alone and that WBGT may be the more appropriate measure. That is exactly the sort of sophistication critics claim a federal rule cannot handle. Yet a state agency is already handling it.

Washington’s revised outdoor heat protections became effective on July 17, 2023, according to the Washington State Department of Labor & Industries. Washington’s rules expanded existing requirements for drinking water, shade, cool-down periods, training, and heat-exposure response. The state’s trajectory matters for a different reason: it punctures the stale idea that heat rules are only for places stereotyped as permanently hot. The Pacific Northwest’s recent heat experience made visible what should have been obvious all along. A worker in a historically milder state can be dangerously unprepared for heat; a worker in a hotter state can be normalized into danger by routine exposure. Neither condition argues against a standard.

These systems are not identical, and that is the point. They prove that standards can be written, adjusted, and enforced. They also show the limits of relying on state variation forever. A multistate employer can reasonably complain that patchwork obligations are cumbersome. But that complaint points toward a federal floor, not away from one. If business wants consistency, consistency is available: write a national baseline and let states exceed it if they choose.

The case against a national rule is serious, but it still fails

The strongest argument against OSHA’s draft deserves a fair reading. Heat is not a hazard that behaves the same way in every place. Outdoor versus indoor conditions differ. Humidity matters. Radiant heat matters. Work intensity matters. Protective clothing matters. Acclimatization matters. A roofer in Arizona, a line cook in California, a warehouse picker in Georgia, and a ten-person paving crew in Ohio do not encounter heat in identical ways. Employers are right to say that a national rule can become clumsy if it is overdesigned, too paperwork-heavy, or too detached from the pace and messiness of real work.

That concern is not frivolous. A bad rule can absolutely collapse into compliance theater. It can produce binders, training acknowledgments, and threshold charts that mainly protect legal departments while leaving workers no safer on the shift. Small employers also have a plausible complaint when federal agencies write as though every workplace has an in-house safety team and easy access to sophisticated monitoring tools. Indoor process heat raises still another problem, because the most accurate tools may be more technical than what smaller operations routinely use.

Those objections should shape the final drafting. They should not defeat the project.

In fact, most of the objections are really arguments against vagueness, not against regulation. If heat risk depends on more than one number, then a standard should say that. If indoor process heat often requires better measurement than the ordinary heat index, then the rule should permit or require better measurement in those settings. If small businesses need simple compliance pathways, then the standard should include them. If enforcement under the general duty clause feels too retrospective and uncertain, then a rule that gives employers clearer obligations in advance is more fair, not less.

That is why the Chamber’s preferred phrase, “performance-oriented,” is both useful and dangerous. Useful, because the final rule should leave room for different workplaces to satisfy core duties in different ways. Dangerous, because “performance-oriented” can easily become a euphemism for obligations so soft that they dissolve back into discretion. A workable heat rule can mix floor and flexibility: mandatory baseline protections, plus options in how employers meet them where conditions truly differ. What it cannot do is reduce the baseline itself to a suggestion.

Guidance, case-by-case enforcement, and emphasis programs are not substitutes

Some defenders of the status quo argue that OSHA already has enough tools. The agency has the general duty clause. It has heat guidance. It has a National Emphasis Program directing attention to heat hazards. It has inspection authority. It has reporting rules for severe injuries and fatalities. Why not just use those tools harder?

Because none of them does what a standard does.

OSHA updated its National Emphasis Program on outdoor and indoor heat-related hazards on April 10, 2026. The revised directive uses OSHA and Bureau of Labor Statistics data from calendar years 2022 through 2025 to direct inspection priorities to 55 high-risk industries. That is useful. It may improve targeting and enforcement. But an emphasis program is an enforcement strategy, not a worker-facing rule of the road. It tells inspectors where the agency wants to look. It does not automatically tell a worker on a loading dock what conditions trigger a required cool-down area, or how a new hire should be acclimatized, or when a supervisor has crossed from tough management into illegal exposure.

The same is true of severe injury reporting. OSHA requires employers to report a work-related fatality within eight hours and an in-patient hospitalization, amputation, or eye loss within twenty-four hours. Those deadlines are important. They help the government know when something has gone badly wrong. But that is the point: they start after something has gone badly wrong. They are post-event obligations in a policy area that badly needs pre-event routines.

Nor should broad injury numbers be misused as reassurance. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported in January 2026 that private industry employers logged 2.5 million nonfatal workplace injuries and illnesses in 2024, down 3.1 percent from 2023, with a total recordable case rate of 2.3 per 100 full-time equivalent workers. That broad decline may reflect some real improvements in workplace safety. It does not prove that heat is well managed. Heat illnesses are widely understood to be undercounted and hard to capture cleanly in official data. BLS definitions do explicitly include heatstroke, heat exhaustion, heat stress, and other effects of environmental heat, which is useful. But the hazard does not become adequately governed simply because statistical systems recognize that it exists.

Recorded heat cases also miss the larger safety effect. Heat does not only create named heat illnesses. It degrades concentration, increases fatigue, slows reaction, and raises the risk of secondary harms such as falls, mistakes with equipment, and judgment failures. A worker does not have to arrive at the hospital coded as heat stroke for heat to have shaped the incident.

The hardest truth is about new workers and ordinary shifts

One of the most damning facts in this area is also one of the most mundane. OSHA says that most outdoor heat fatalities, between 50 percent and 70 percent, occur in the first few days of working in warm or hot environments because the body needs time to build tolerance. OSHA’s page on protecting new workers says more than 70 percent of heat-related deaths occur during a worker’s first week. That is not a freak pattern. It is a management pattern.

Acclimatization is one of those concepts that sounds technical until you translate it back into daily work. It means somebody has to decide that a new worker, or a worker returning after time away, should not be thrown into a full hot shift at full pace on day one. It means somebody has to structure the ramp-up. It means a workplace cannot hide behind the mythology that toughness reveals itself by how much a body can absorb before it complains.

OSHA and NIOSH both make clear that acclimatization has to be built, not assumed. That is one reason heat policy is ultimately a labor policy question. It asks who controls the pace of entry into the job, who absorbs the cost of caution, and whether the worker has any enforceable claim against the demand to prove grit through exposure. Without a rule, acclimatization is often treated as optional managerial prudence. With a rule, it becomes a basic duty.

That matters especially in sectors with high turnover, temporary staffing, subcontracting, migration, and variable schedules. Some of the most heat-exposed jobs are also jobs where workers are new, precarious, linguistically isolated, or reluctant to complain. A labor market like that will not reliably produce self-protective negotiation. It will produce silence and normalization. The law exists to correct exactly that kind of asymmetry.

Indoor heat is where denial gets its easiest cover

The public image of heat danger remains stubbornly outdoor: orchards, highways, roofs, fields. Those are real sites of risk, but the policy blind spot is often indoors. Indoor heat is politically convenient to ignore because it looks like business as usual. The loading dock is under a roof. The kitchen is inside. The laundry has walls. The warehouse is not a field, so the harm can be narrated as discomfort rather than hazard.

California’s indoor standard is therefore more than a state regulation. It is a factual correction. It says, in effect, that enclosed space does not erase heat stress. Oregon’s process-heat materials make the same point in a more technical register by distinguishing ordinary heat-index screening from the more precise measurements needed in some indoor settings. OSHA’s own technical materials also note that WBGT is the most accurate tool for adjusting temperature for heat-stress factors including humidity, radiant heat, and air movement.

This matters for federal policy because indoor heat is where employer discretion can hide most comfortably. An outdoor crew may at least trigger public sympathy when temperatures spike. An indoor crew can be told that this is just summer, just a rough week, just how the building runs, just part of the job. The line between inconvenience and danger becomes whatever the employer says it is until a body forces the issue.

A federal rule should be written precisely to stop that sentence from ending there.

What a final federal rule should actually require

A sound federal heat standard does not need to eliminate judgment. It needs to allocate judgment correctly. Employers should retain flexibility in how they exceed a minimum floor or adapt it to specific sectors. They should not retain flexibility over whether a floor exists.

That means, at minimum, a final rule should include national baseline triggers for preventive action; accessible cool drinking water; access to cool-down or recovery areas appropriate to the setting; training for workers and supervisors on symptoms and response; acclimatization procedures for new and returning workers; observation or communication procedures during higher-risk heat conditions; indoor assessment methods that account for process heat and can use WBGT or equivalent tools where necessary; and emergency response requirements that are specific enough to matter when a worker becomes confused, unstable, or collapses.

Just as important, the rule should recognize that work organization itself is part of the hazard. Heat safety is not only about cups of water and patches of shade. It is about route design, line speed, staffing, production quotas, task rotation, and whether workers can use protective measures without being punished for falling behind. A standard that treats heat as a purely environmental problem will miss the way production pressure converts heat from risk into injury.

This is one reason the issue belongs on an opinion desk rather than only in a technical one. The central dispute is not just measurement. It is authority. Who gets to decide when the body has reached enough? If the answer remains “the employer, unless something visibly terrible happens,” then the country has not really regulated the hazard. It has merely described it.

Cost is real. That is why law is needed.

Critics are right that heat protection costs money. Cooling improvements cost money. Ventilation changes cost money. Shade structures cost money. Training time costs money. Monitoring equipment costs money. Staggered breaks, slower pace, altered schedules, and more staffing all cost money. California’s own rulemaking documents for indoor heat anticipated significant compliance costs. But occupational safety law does not exist for hazards that are free to fix. It exists because the market reliably offloads the cost of prevention onto workers’ bodies when the law allows it.

That is the proper way to understand the “burden” argument. The burden is not invented by the rule. The burden already exists. The question is whether it will continue to be paid invisibly through illness, injury, error, and coerced endurance, or whether some of it will be paid openly through prevention.

NIOSH has also highlighted another reason the cost argument cannot be answered with only acute cases. In its 2021 science bulletin, the agency pointed to emerging concerns linking occupational heat stress with acute kidney injury and chronic kidney disease. That does not mean every hot shift becomes kidney disease. It means the hazard is broader than the dramatic collapse the public imagines. Repeated dehydration, high core temperatures, and strain can impose costs that are medically and economically real even when they do not announce themselves in a single spectacular event.

There is also a distributional truth here. Heat risk is concentrated in jobs with less bargaining power: agriculture, construction, warehousing, delivery, landscaping, food processing, manufacturing, sanitation, and similar sectors. Some workers are migrants. Some are temps. Some are employed through layers of subcontracting. Some are incarcerated workers. Some fear retaliation, firing, immigration consequences, or simply being marked as weak. A hazard governed mainly by informal consent in such workplaces is a hazard governed by coercion with extra steps.

A weaker final rule would teach the wrong lesson

The political temptation now is easy to see. An administration skeptical of regulation can say it takes heat seriously while still narrowing the final rule, stretching the timeline, or converting the standard into something so performance-based that it mostly restates common sense. That path would have one short-term appeal: it would let officials claim movement without fully disciplining employer discretion.

But it would also teach a dangerous institutional lesson. OSHA would be telling the country that even when a hazard is recognized, the science is developed, states already regulate it, a federal proposal is published, hundreds of comments are filed, a hearing is held, and the post-hearing record is closed, the agency may still shrink back from setting a national floor. The resulting message to regulated industries would be obvious: delay works. Even a mature record can be hollowed out if politics changes at the right moment.

That is not regulatory humility. It is policy unseriousness.

There is, in fact, a conservative argument for finishing a real rule. Employers have long complained that heat enforcement under the general duty clause can feel uncertain and retrospective. A clear standard reduces some of that uncertainty. It tells employers in advance what the floor is. It allows planning. It channels compliance into known obligations rather than leaving too much to after-the-fact argument over whether conditions were hazardous enough and precautions sufficient enough on a given day. If rule-of-law language means anything in occupational safety, it ought to include the value of clear obligations before the inspection, not only penalties after the injury.

Protection is not paternalism

There is one last objection that deserves a direct answer because it carries cultural force in exactly the jobs where heat is often normalized. Some workers, and many managers, do not like rules that seem to imply fragility. They prefer to talk about experience, stamina, pride, hustle, getting through it, earning the day. In some jobs, crews paid by output may even want to keep moving and leave sooner. Heat protections can be caricatured as coddling.

But labor standards are not insults. They are acknowledgments of unequal power inside the job. We do not abandon fall protection because a veteran worker says he knows his footing. We do not make machine guarding optional because someone claims to be careful. We do not leave lockout protections to the personal confidence of the person nearest the equipment. We set floors because incentives are distorted, risks are normalized, and the cost of misjudgment spills outward to co-workers, families, emergency responders, and public systems.

Heat belongs in that category. It is predictable. It is recurring. It is intensified by production pressure. It affects judgment before it announces itself dramatically. It punishes the new worker, the precarious worker, and the worker whose body does not get a meaningful vote in how the shift is paced. Calling a rule paternalistic is often just a cleaner way of saying management would rather preserve discretion.

The country has moved too far in this debate to pretend otherwise. There is a published federal proposal. There is a completed 2025 hearing. There is a closed post-hearing record. There are state standards already in effect. There is an updated federal enforcement emphasis program. There is agency guidance on acclimatization, on new-worker risk, on the limits of heat index, and on the importance of more accurate worksite measurement. There are organized business critiques arguing not that heat is unreal, but that the final rule should be more flexible. All of that adds up to one conclusion: the remaining argument is over how much discretion employers should keep, not over whether heat is governable.

My answer is that the federal government should keep less of that discretion available than it does now. It should adopt an enforceable federal heat standard that is clear enough to be usable, flexible enough to account for real differences in work conditions, and strong enough that workers do not have to negotiate with physiology one shift at a time.

Summer is not an emergency category in the workplace. It is a season. That is exactly why the law should govern it. The point is not to dramatize heat as exceptional. The point is to recognize that because it returns predictably, the obligations tied to it should also return predictably. Water should be there. Recovery space should be there. The training should have happened. The new worker should not be thrown in at full pace. The plan should already exist before someone gets dizzy, confused, or still. Prevention should look boring because it has become routine.

That is what a mature safety regime does. It moves the decision upstream. It refuses to let the body be the first alarm. And it tells the supervisor, the contractor, the warehouse lead, the kitchen manager, the route planner, and the company accountant the same thing: if the work depends on people absorbing dangerous heat to hit the day’s output, then the work is mismanaged.

The federal government does not need another summer of saying this hazard is important while leaving too much of it to guidance, discretion, and retrospective enforcement. It needs a rule. Not because every detail of the 2024 proposal must survive untouched, but because the country already knows enough to set a national floor and because delay now would be a choice, not a discovery. The shift should not be governed by whatever level of ambiguity makes management most comfortable. It should be governed by conditions on the ground and obligations clear enough to act on before the ambulance is part of the paperwork.
