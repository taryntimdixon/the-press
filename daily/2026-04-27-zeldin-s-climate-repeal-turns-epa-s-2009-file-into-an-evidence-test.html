Zeldin’s Climate Repeal Turns EPA’s 2009 File Into an Evidence Test

EPA’s rescission of the greenhouse-gas endangerment finding is a legal fight with a scientific core: when climate evidence is strong, uneven and costly to act on, what kind of uncertainty is enough to make an agency change course?

By The Press • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

The most important climate argument in Washington this spring is not happening on a glacier, at a hurricane forecast desk or beside a smoking tailpipe. It is happening in a file.

The file has a dry name, as powerful files often do: the Endangerment and Cause or Contribute Findings for Greenhouse Gases Under Section 202(a) of the Clean Air Act. In 2009, the Environmental Protection Agency found that six heat-trapping gases, including carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide, could reasonably be expected to endanger public health and welfare, and that emissions from new motor vehicles and new motor-vehicle engines contributed to that pollution.

For 16 years, that finding served as a hinge. Once EPA made the judgment that greenhouse gases endangered people, the agency had a legal basis to limit greenhouse-gas emissions from cars and trucks. The finding also sat beneath later climate rules for other sectors, even when those rules relied on different parts of the Clean Air Act.

Now EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin has tried to pull that hinge out. EPA signed a final rule on Feb. 12, 2026, published it in the Federal Register on Feb. 18, and made it effective on April 20. The rule rescinds the 2009 endangerment and contribution findings for motor vehicles and repeals federal greenhouse-gas standards for light-duty, medium-duty and heavy-duty highway vehicles and engines. EPA says Section 202(a) of the Clean Air Act is not the right tool for regulating greenhouse gases in response to global climate change.

That makes the story sound legal. It is. But it is also a science story, because the legal fight keeps circling back to the same practical question: how should government treat evidence when the evidence is strong, imperfect, global, cumulative and politically expensive?

EPA’s new rule does not simply announce that carbon dioxide is harmless. It leans mainly on statutory interpretation. The agency says Congress did not authorize EPA to use the motor-vehicle section of the Clean Air Act to address a worldwide climate problem. It also argues that vehicle greenhouse-gas standards do not materially change global temperature or sea-level trends enough to justify the program. Opponents, including states, cities, public-health groups, environmental organizations and scientists, argue that EPA is trying to walk away from a scientific record that has grown stronger, not weaker, since 2009.

The unresolved question is not whether every model gets every storm right. No serious climate scientist claims that. The sharper question is whether the remaining uncertainties are large enough to erase EPA’s earlier judgment that greenhouse gases pose a danger to people.

A boring name with a giant hinge

The 2009 finding was born after Massachusetts v. EPA, the 2007 Supreme Court decision that held greenhouse gases fit within the Clean Air Act’s broad definition of air pollutant. The court did not order EPA to regulate every source of greenhouse gases. It told the agency to make a reasoned judgment under Section 202(a): do emissions from new motor vehicles cause or contribute to air pollution that may reasonably be anticipated to endanger public health or welfare?

EPA answered yes in December 2009. The agency did not say carbon dioxide poisons the body in the way carbon monoxide can. It did not say one family sedan can be tied neatly to one flooded basement. It made a cumulative-risk judgment. Greenhouse gases build up in the atmosphere. That buildup changes Earth’s energy balance. The warming that follows changes heat, rainfall, drought, sea level, wildfire conditions, air quality, infectious-disease ranges and other risks. Those changes can harm people, ecosystems, crops, infrastructure and economies.

That is why the finding mattered far beyond its bureaucratic title. Once EPA made the finding, vehicle greenhouse-gas rules followed. Those rules mattered directly for automakers and consumers. The finding also became a foundation stone in a much larger federal climate structure. Remove it, and the question is not merely whether one tailpipe rule survives. The question is how much of the federal government’s climate-regulatory authority is left standing.

Zeldin’s EPA says the agency’s old reading was wrong. In the final rule, EPA argues that Section 202(a) is better understood as a provision for pollution that harms health and welfare through local or regional exposure, not for a global atmospheric problem whose risks depend on emissions from many sectors and many countries. EPA also invokes the modern Supreme Court’s skepticism toward sweeping agency actions under old statutes, especially after West Virginia v. EPA in 2022.

That argument should not be dismissed as a trick. There is a real legal debate over whether a 1970 statute, written before climate policy became a central national fight, is the best vehicle for an economy-wide greenhouse-gas program. Congress could write a clearer climate law. It has not done so. Courts have become more willing to ask whether agencies are claiming authority over questions of major economic and political significance without clear congressional authorization.

But that legal argument is not the same as a scientific argument. Saying Congress should speak more clearly is different from saying greenhouse gases do not endanger health and welfare. EPA’s 2026 rule tries to separate those questions. The lawsuits will test whether courts let it.

EPA stepped away from a full science reversal

One of the most important features of the final rule is what it did not do. EPA did not finalize its proposed alternative science rationale as an independent basis for rescinding the finding. In plainer English: the agency did not issue a new scientific determination that the 2009 climate judgment was wrong.

The final rule says the administrator continues to have concerns about the scientific analysis behind the 2009 finding. It also discusses modeling, uncertainty, causal chains and the size of the U.S. vehicle contribution. But EPA says its legal conclusions are sufficient, and that it does not need to resolve the underlying climate-science disputes in this rulemaking.

That choice may be strategic. A full scientific reversal would force EPA to confront a heavy record: international assessments, U.S. national assessments, NOAA measurements, NASA temperature records, health studies, attribution research and the National Academies’ 2025 review. By resting mainly on legal authority and what EPA calls futility, the agency reduces the number of scientific claims it must defend.

It may also make the rule vulnerable. Agencies can change policy when administrations change. They cannot usually behave as if the old record never existed. If an agency once relied on a large technical record, it must explain why it now treats the same problem differently. If the evidence has changed, it must say how. If the law is doing the real work, it must explain why the new reading is allowed despite prior court decisions.

That is why the administrative record is the star witness. It includes old findings, new modeling, public comments, state filings, industry concerns, expert reports and EPA’s responses. It is less cinematic than an ice shelf collapse, but it may shape U.S. climate policy more directly.

The science record did not freeze in 2009

In 2009, climate science was already strong. The basic greenhouse effect had been understood for more than a century. Carbon dioxide concentrations were rising. Global temperature datasets showed warming. Major assessments concluded that human-caused greenhouse-gas emissions were the dominant driver of modern warming.

Since then, the evidence has thickened. That does not mean every uncertainty disappeared. It means the core chain has been tested with more observations, more satellites, more ocean measurements, more paleoclimate work, more extreme-event studies and more health research.

NOAA’s Global Monitoring Laboratory continues to report rising global carbon dioxide concentrations from a distributed network of air-sampling sites. NASA’s 2026 temperature update found that 2024 remained the hottest year in its record and that 2025 was effectively tied with 2023 in NASA’s analysis, while several other major temperature groups ranked 2025 third. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s Sixth Assessment Report concluded that human activities, principally through greenhouse-gas emissions, have unequivocally caused global warming. The Fifth National Climate Assessment concluded that climate change is already affecting every U.S. region, with risks to health, infrastructure, ecosystems and economic activity.

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering and Medicine added a pointed review in September 2025. The committee reviewed evidence since 2009 and concluded that EPA’s original judgment was accurate, had stood the test of time and was reinforced by stronger evidence. Republicans on the House Oversight Committee criticized the National Academies’ decision to conduct a fast-track review without a government request, which is relevant to the politics and process of the report. But the report’s scientific conclusion does not stand alone. It sits beside a much larger body of assessment literature.

For climate scientists, the central picture is not delicate. Carbon dioxide traps heat. Human activity has raised atmospheric concentrations. The planet has warmed. Warmer air and oceans alter extremes and increase risks. The hardest questions are often the local ones: how much rain one valley will get, how much smoke one county will breathe, how fast one coastal road will flood, how many deaths a specific heat wave caused. Those are difficult, consequential questions. They do not erase the broader pattern.

Endangerment is a risk word, not a crater word

Endangerment can sound like a demand for proof of catastrophe. In law and public health, it is usually about risk.

Imagine a building inspector looking at a bridge. The inspector does not need to know the exact minute the bridge will fail to say the structure is unsafe. Cracks, corrosion, load, weather and engineering models can be enough. A good inspector still has to be honest about uncertainty. Maybe the bridge can last years. Maybe a repair changes the risk. Maybe another engineer disagrees about the most likely failure point. But uncertainty does not automatically mean safety.

Climate risk works in a similar way, except the bridge is everywhere and the cracks do not appear evenly. A hotter average world changes the odds of extremes. Some harms are direct, such as heat illness. Some are indirect, such as wildfire smoke, flood damage, water contamination, food-price shocks, longer allergy seasons or pressure on emergency services. Some are easier to count than others. Death certificates do not always say climate change. Insurance claims do not list atmospheric physics as the cause.

The Clean Air Act’s phrase public health and welfare matters here. Welfare is broad. It includes effects on weather, climate, property, economic values, agriculture and ecosystems. EPA’s 2026 rule argues that this breadth became too broad when applied to global climate change through the motor-vehicle section. Challengers answer that Congress wrote the term broadly, and that the Supreme Court already recognized greenhouse gases as air pollutants under the Act.

The scientific problem underneath is attribution. Attribution means estimating how much a cause contributed to an outcome. For global warming overall, attribution to human greenhouse-gas emissions is very strong. For particular impacts, confidence varies. Heat extremes are generally easier to connect to warming than some other phenomena. Tornado behavior is more complicated. Wildfire risk depends on heat, dryness, wind, vegetation, ignition, land management and firefighting. Sea-level rise has multiple contributors, including warming seawater and melting land ice.

That nuance can be misused from both sides. Advocates can overclaim by turning every fire or flood into a simple climate verdict. Opponents can underclaim by pointing to complexity as if complexity were innocence. The honest middle is less punchy: climate change loads many dice, not all dice, and not by the same amount everywhere.

The vehicle question is a bathtub problem

EPA’s rescission focuses on motor vehicles because Section 202(a) deals with emissions from new motor vehicles and engines. That focus creates the rule’s central math fight.

Transportation is a major source of U.S. greenhouse-gas emissions. EPA’s transportation emissions materials show the sector as a large share of U.S. emissions, with passenger cars, light-duty trucks and medium- and heavy-duty trucks making up much of the transportation total. EIA’s 2024 energy-related carbon dioxide report likewise shows transportation as the largest end-use sector for energy-related CO2 emissions.

EPA’s final rule asks a narrower question: what do these vehicle standards do to global surface temperature or global sea level by a given year? When a global, cumulative climate problem is divided into individual policy pieces, the effect of any one piece can look small. That is not fake math. It is bathtub math. No single faucet fills the tub alone, but all the faucets matter to the water line.

The counterargument to EPA is that almost every climate policy looks small when isolated. A city bus rule looks small. A methane rule looks small. A power-plant rule looks small. A shipping rule looks small. The climate system adds emissions; policy adds reductions. Scale can be used to design a portfolio, or it can be used to declare each piece too small to count.

EPA’s futility argument says vehicle greenhouse-gas standards impose large costs while producing no material value toward the statutory health and welfare objective. Challengers are likely to answer that the agency has chosen the wrong yardstick, the wrong time horizon or the wrong legal threshold for contribution. An April 2026 preprint on sea-level modeling, not yet peer reviewed, illustrates the live methodological dispute by challenging how EPA-style analysis treats time horizons and regional sea-level effects. Because it is a preprint, it should not be treated as settled evidence. But it shows the kind of technical question the courts will now see dressed in legal clothing: if carbon dioxide persists for a long time, how far into the future should regulators look?

That is partly science and partly values. A 2050 endpoint emphasizes near-term measurable effects. A 2100 or 2150 endpoint gives more weight to today’s children and future coastlines. Discount rates, baseline assumptions and model choices can make the same emissions cut look modest or important. Methods are not neutral plumbing. They are where judgments hide.

Costs are real, and so are the costs of bad accounting

EPA says rescinding the rules will save money. The agency’s regulatory impact analysis estimated a wide range of net effects depending on scenarios and discount rates, including large potential savings under some assumptions. That claim matters. Regulations can raise upfront vehicle costs, change product design, affect automakers and suppliers, shift jobs and influence what families pay.

But cost accounting is also a science-adjacent battlefield. If a rule raises the sticker price of a vehicle but lowers fuel costs over time, how should that be counted? If it reduces greenhouse gases and conventional pollutants together, how should health benefits be valued? If it speeds electric-vehicle adoption, how should charging access, battery supply chains, grid emissions and mineral extraction enter the ledger? If it avoids some future heat deaths, crop losses or flood damage, how do those benefits enter today’s balance sheet?

EPA’s final rule emphasizes consumer and industry savings and the limited climate effect of vehicle standards. Public-health and environmental challengers argue the agency discounted or sidestepped climate and health harms. Industry interests are not perfectly aligned either. Some companies welcome less stringent rules. Others have invested in electric vehicles, compliance credits, export strategies and product plans built around years of greenhouse-gas standards. Regulatory rollback can reduce near-term obligations and increase long-term uncertainty at the same time.

This is where the story leaves the lab and enters the showroom. A family buying a car may care first about monthly payments. A child with asthma may care about local air quality. A coastal town may care about drainage pumps. An automaker may care about one national market. A climate modeler may care about cumulative carbon. The law has to decide how those interests fit into one record.

The DOE report is still in the room

The rescission also carries baggage from a 2025 Department of Energy report produced by a Climate Working Group assembled by Energy Secretary Chris Wright. DOE described the report as a critical review of the conventional climate narrative and said it found that CO2-induced warming appeared less economically damaging than commonly believed and that aggressive mitigation could be misdirected. Critics saw something else: a selective document designed to support EPA’s proposed repeal.

More than 85 climate experts produced a detailed rebuttal of the DOE report, arguing that it cherry-picked literature, misrepresented citations and overstated uncertainty. In January 2026, a federal court in Massachusetts declared that the Climate Working Group was subject to the Federal Advisory Committee Act and that DOE had violated requirements involving open meetings, open records and balance. That ruling did not automatically erase every sentence in the DOE report, but it complicated the report’s usefulness as a foundation for federal rulemaking.

EPA’s final rule appears to have reduced its reliance on a direct scientific reversal, including the DOE-adjacent critique. That may protect the agency from one line of attack. It also sharpens another. If EPA is not making a new scientific finding, and if major scientific assessments still support endangerment, then the agency’s case depends heavily on law, policy judgment and its futility analysis.

There is a reasonable debate about whether the Clean Air Act is the best tool for climate policy. There is a much weaker debate about whether greenhouse gases warm the planet and create serious risks. EPA is trying to win the first debate without fully fighting the second. Its challengers will argue the agency cannot separate them so neatly.

Why the current lawsuits matter

The legal map is now crowded. Public-health and environmental groups sued in February. A coalition of 25 attorneys general, Pennsylvania’s governor, and cities and counties filed a D.C. Circuit petition for review in March. In April, state and local challengers asked EPA to reconsider the rule, arguing in part that the final version relied on new methodologies and assumptions that were not properly aired for public comment. Environmental and public-health groups also filed a reconsideration petition in April.

The cases will likely turn on administrative law as much as climate science. Did EPA explain its reversal? Did it respond to major comments? Did it rely on factors Congress allowed? Did it ignore evidence? Did it properly estimate costs and benefits? Did it overread West Virginia v. EPA? Did it underread Massachusetts v. EPA and the D.C. Circuit’s 2012 decision upholding the original finding?

For science, the danger is that a court opinion can flatten evidence into a yes-or-no slogan. Judges are not climate modelers. They rely on records, briefs, precedent and standards of review. The best opinions in technical cases do not pretend to become textbooks. They ask whether the agency behaved reasonably with the expertise and record before it.

That is why this story is local-to-global in the strangest possible way. It begins with new U.S. cars and trucks. It ends with the atmosphere. A tailpipe is local. Carbon dioxide is global. A federal rule is national. The harm is uneven. The law is old. The evidence is modern.

What would change the conclusion?

A good science story should say what could change its mind. Here, several things could.

Scientifically, the case for endangerment would weaken if independent temperature datasets stopped showing long-term warming, if greenhouse-gas concentrations stopped rising without emissions cuts, if attribution studies found a non-human driver that better explained modern warming, or if health and welfare impacts consistently came in far below projected risks across regions and methods. That is not what the current evidence shows.

The conclusion can still change at the edges. New research may lower estimates for some harms, raise them for others, or show that adaptation works better in certain places than expected. Better heat-warning systems, stronger building codes, cleaner electricity, improved wildfire management, better flood defenses and more resilient health systems can reduce harm. Adaptation matters. It can save lives. But adaptation is not the same as proving no danger exists. A seat belt does not prove crashes are harmless.

Legally, the result could change if courts decide that Section 202(a) simply cannot carry this kind of greenhouse-gas regulation, no matter what the science says. That would not make carbon dioxide safe. It would mean Congress must act more clearly, or states and other agencies must carry more of the load. Courts can decide authority. They cannot repeal atmospheric chemistry.

Economically, the balance could shift if clean-vehicle technologies became much more expensive, unreliable or environmentally damaging than expected, or if alternatives became cheaper and cleaner faster than expected. Technology will keep moving while the case proceeds. Battery prices, charging networks, fuel prices, consumer preferences, state policies and international markets will all affect the real-world stakes.

The file is not closed

There is no tidy ending available on April 27, 2026. EPA’s rule is final and effective. The lawsuits are underway. Petitions for reconsideration are pending. The science record keeps growing. The vehicles keep rolling. The atmosphere keeps doing arithmetic.

The most useful way to read the fight is not as certainty versus doubt. Science is almost never certainty in the everyday sense. It is disciplined confidence built from measurements, models, error bars, arguments and revision. Climate science has uncertainties, and some are large enough to matter for local planning. But uncertainty can point both ways. It can mean an outcome is milder than expected. It can also mean it is worse.

The 2009 endangerment finding was never a claim that scientists could predict every consequence of every ton of carbon dioxide. It was a judgment that the risk was serious enough for government to act. EPA now says that judgment cannot stand under this part of the law, and that the vehicle standards are too futile to justify their costs. The next question is whether judges see that as a lawful change in interpretation or as an agency trying to turn a live scientific record into a closed file.

Either way, the fight has already revealed something important. In climate policy, the argument over evidence is not only about what scientists know. It is about how institutions decide when knowledge is strong enough to matter.
