NASA’s Lucy Finds a Surprising Asteroid with a Strange Double-Lobed Past

New data from the Lucy mission’s flyby of asteroid Donaldjohanson suggest the object may be a contact binary with a more complex formation history than scientists expected.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 18, 2026 • 2:25 p.m. • 991 words

NASA’s Lucy mission has added an unexpectedly complicated chapter to the story of asteroid Donaldjohanson, a small rocky body in the main asteroid belt that the spacecraft flew past last year as part of its long journey toward Jupiter’s Trojan asteroids. Early analysis of the flyby suggests Donaldjohanson is not just an odd, double-lobed object, but one whose shape and surface details may preserve clues about how small bodies in the early solar system collided, merged, and reassembled.

The find matters because asteroids are leftovers from planet formation. They are time capsules from the era when dust, ice, and rock were being assembled into planets, moons, and the other bodies that fill the solar system today. Lucy’s target list is designed to help scientists read those records. Donaldjohanson was never one of the mission’s ultimate destinations, but as a main-belt encounter it offered a useful preview: a chance to test the spacecraft’s instruments and to inspect a body whose shape already hinted that it might have formed in a dramatic way.

That hint proved important. The object’s two linked lobes make it look like a contact binary, the kind of body thought to arise when two smaller asteroids drift together and stick at low speed. But the new imagery and mission analysis suggest the connection between the lobes may be more complex than a simple gentle collision. Instead of a neat merger, the asteroid appears to have a twisted, irregular bridge and a surface that may reflect a multi-stage history of break-up and reassembly.

A practice run with scientific payoff

Lucy’s main scientific targets are the Jupiter Trojan asteroids, a population of ancient objects trapped near Jupiter’s orbit. The spacecraft’s 2025 flyby of Donaldjohanson was meant to calibrate instruments and refine procedures before those more ambitious encounters. That role should not obscure the scientific value of the pass: every close-up asteroid image helps researchers compare one object with another and build a broader theory of how small bodies behave over billions of years.

What makes Donaldjohanson especially interesting is the mismatch between its simple silhouette and the complexity implied by the data. Asteroids are often classified by broad shape or composition, but those categories can hide a lot of history. A body that looks like a single object may actually be the fossil of multiple bodies that came together under the weak gravity typical of small worlds. If Donaldjohanson is a contact binary, it may have formed in a way that was less tidy than scientists usually imagine, with repeated collisions and reshaping events leaving behind the odd structure Lucy saw.

That kind of complexity is not a footnote. It affects how scientists model the evolution of asteroid families, how they estimate internal strength and porosity, and how they think about the early solar system’s collision environment. Even small changes in assumptions about asteroid structure can influence future planetary-defense planning, since how an object breaks apart or deforms under impact depends heavily on what lies beneath the surface.

Why asteroid shape tells a bigger story

There is a tendency to think of asteroids as static rocks floating in space, but many are anything but static. They tumble, spin, fracture, shed material, and occasionally reassemble. Some are rubble piles, loosely held together by gravity and friction. Others may be monolithic fragments from larger parent bodies. Still others, like Donaldjohanson appears to be, occupy a middle ground that scientists are still trying to understand.

The mission’s close-up data should help answer several questions. How sharply do the lobes meet? Is the bridge between them made of material that was compressed in a merger, or could it be the scar of a later event? Are the surface ridges and grooves signs of stress, impact, or slow reshaping by sunlight-driven forces such as the YORP effect, which can alter spin over long periods? Each of those clues would point to a different formation path.

There is also a naming story woven into the science. Donaldjohanson is named for the paleoanthropologist who discovered the famous hominin fossil Lucy, after which NASA’s spacecraft takes its own name. That naming link is more than a coincidence; it reflects how exploration connects different kinds of origins research. One Lucy helps study human ancestry on Earth, while the other helps study the ancestry of planets and asteroids in space.

What comes next for Lucy

The big scientific payoff from Donaldjohanson will not come from one image alone. It will come from comparison: between this asteroid and the mission’s later targets, between the spacecraft’s measurements and laboratory models of impact and rubble-pile formation, and between Donaldjohanson’s shape and the shapes of other known asteroids. If scientists can determine whether it is a simple contact binary, a reworked fragment, or something more unusual, they will have a better handle on how common each pathway is.

That matters because asteroid origins are solar-system origins. The same collisions and accretions that shaped small bodies also influenced the early building blocks of the terrestrial planets. Understanding those processes can improve models of how Earth formed, how volatile-rich material was delivered to the inner solar system, and how a chaotic youth evolved into the relatively stable system we inhabit now.

Lucy is still early in its journey, and the most famous encounters are ahead. But Donaldjohanson already shows why mission planners build room for surprise into exploration. A flyby intended as a rehearsal can still produce a real scientific result. In this case, it suggests that a seemingly simple asteroid may be a layered remnant of a far messier past, one that began long before planets looked anything like they do today.

For planetary scientists, that is exactly the point. The solar system’s smallest objects are not leftovers in any trivial sense. They are records, and sometimes the records are complicated. Donaldjohanson appears to be one of those cases: a modest-looking rock with a story that may be richer, older, and stranger than it first appeared.
