CinemaCon’s biggest movies signal Hollywood’s bid to revive the theater experience

From new franchise footage to star-driven originals, studios used CinemaCon 2026 to argue that the big screen still matters — and that the next wave of moviegoing will be built on spectacle, nostalgia and event cinema.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 2:26 p.m.

LAS VEGAS — If Hollywood needed a reminder that it is still, at heart, in the business of persuading people to leave home, CinemaCon 2026 supplied one in near-constant bursts of trailers, star appearances and promises of spectacle. Over a week that began with warnings about a fragile theatrical market and ended with studios leaning hard into their biggest bets, the annual gathering for movie-theater owners became an unusually clear snapshot of where film is headed: more franchises, more nostalgia, more premium-format spectacle and, studios hope, more reasons for audiences to buy a ticket instead of waiting for streaming. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c3d7ed8782da1dc46d20476a2f9eca9b?utm_source=openai))

The message was loudest from the companies with the deepest libraries. Disney unveiled the opening of the new Star Wars film The Mandalorian and Grogu and fresh footage from Avengers: Doomsday, while Warner Bros. showcased the return of its tentpoles with Dune: Part Three, Practical Magic 2 and other prestige-size bets. Paramount, now under new leadership after its Skydance merger, used the stage to promise exhibitors 15 theatrical releases in 2026 and to declare confidence in the cinema business. The subtext was unmistakable: studios are increasingly treating the theatrical market as an arena for event programming rather than a broad middle of adult dramas and modest mid-budget comedies. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))

That approach was visible in the films themselves. Sony offered a new look at Spider-Man: Brand New Day, which quickly became a conversation driver after a hologram introduction from Tom Holland and a release boast that the trailer had amassed more than 1 billion views in four days. Warner Bros. leaned on Tom Cruise’s transformation for Digger, Alejandro G. Iñárritu’s darkly comic new film, while Paramount debuted Brad Pitt’s wilderness survival thriller Heart of the Beast. Meanwhile, Amazon MGM turned to comedy nostalgia with Spaceballs: The New One, and Universal kept the franchise engine running with an Ocean’s prequel dated for 2027. Together, the titles reflected a movie ecosystem in which recognizable IP, star power and a strong visual hook have become the surest path to getting a theatrical audience off the couch. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/global/spider-man-brand-new-day-footage-cinemacon-tom-holland-zendaya-love-interest-35328/?utm_source=openai))

A theater business still searching for its footing

CinemaCon has long been part pep rally, part pressure valve for an industry that relies on theatrical exhibition but now competes with an endless home-entertainment menu. This year’s mood underscored how much is at stake. AP reported that Hollywood leaders and theater owners gathered in Las Vegas at a critical moment, with the business still trying to recover steadier attendance patterns after the pandemic-era reset and the ongoing shrinking of the middle tier of film production. The event’s importance has only grown because it functions as one of the few annual venues where studio chiefs can directly reassure exhibitors that the coming slate will justify the cost of running theaters, especially premium screens. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c3d7ed8782da1dc46d20476a2f9eca9b?utm_source=openai))

Studios responded by leaning into movies that are easy to sell as occasions. That is not merely a creative choice; it is a market strategy. Large-scale fantasy, superhero, sci-fi and action franchises are built for Imax, Dolby and other premium formats, where theaters can charge more and where studios can claim they are delivering an experience streaming cannot match. The presence of filmmakers such as Christopher Nolan and Steven Spielberg, both of whom used the convention to frame theatrical moviegoing as culturally essential, reinforced that argument. Nolan brought extended footage from The Odyssey, while Spielberg debuted trailer material for Disclosure Day and made the case that original stories still matter if Hollywood wants to avoid running out of gas. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

The sequel era is not ending anytime soon

If the week had a prevailing aesthetic, it was not innovation for its own sake but the strategic recycling of audience memory. The most reliably talked-about titles were all rooted in existing brands: Avengers: Doomsday, The Mandalorian and Grogu, Spider-Man: Brand New Day, Practical Magic 2, Spaceballs: The New One, Jumanji 3, Godzilla Minus Zero and the new Thomas Crown Affair. Even when the films are not direct sequels, they often depend on familiar story worlds or beloved stars to supply instant recognition. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))

That pattern reflects both risk aversion and audience reality. Theatrical releases with built-in fanbases are easier to market globally, easier to justify in the current cost environment and more likely to produce the kind of opening-weekend numbers that still matter in Hollywood’s accounting. Yet the CinemaCon presentations also suggested that studios are trying to make sequels feel like events rather than obligations. Amazon MGM’s Spaceballs sequel played as a full-on spoof machine; Warner Bros.’ Practical Magic 2 pitched reunion and charm; Sony’s Spider-Man footage promised emotional stakes; and Disney’s Avengers preview leaned on scale and continuity. In each case, the goal was to make familiarity feel like momentum. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/spaceballs-2-trailer-star-wars-the-new-one-title-35484/?utm_source=openai))

Why this matters for the rest of 2026

The broader significance of CinemaCon 2026 is that it offers an early read on the year’s theatrical identity. The titles showcased this week suggest that studios believe the best defense of the big screen is not volume alone, but spectacle and specificity: movies that feel like they can only be seen in a theater, or at least that are marketed as if they were made for that purpose. That should help the multiplex business in the short term, especially when paired with summer-friendly franchises and theatrical-first releases. But it also leaves a lingering question about what happens to the kinds of films that once filled the calendar between the blockbusters — the adult dramas, original comedies and midrange crowd-pleasers that helped theaters survive a year-round schedule. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c3d7ed8782da1dc46d20476a2f9eca9b?utm_source=openai))

For now, the industry is betting that scale can do the work. The roll call from Las Vegas — Disney, Warner Bros., Paramount, Sony, Amazon MGM and Universal — points to a 2026 release landscape shaped by recognizable brands, large-format presentation and a steady appetite for IP-driven marketing. Whether that is a durable solution or simply the easiest one available may depend on the box office numbers that follow. But the tone of CinemaCon made one thing clear: Hollywood is still trying to rebuild moviegoing by making the theater feel indispensable, one trailer at a time. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))
