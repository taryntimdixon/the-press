Sudan’s war deepens as displacement, famine risk and atrocity allegations put new pressure on the world

As fighting in Sudan drags into another brutal year, aid agencies warn that mass displacement, hunger and allegations of systematic abuses are converging into one of the world’s gravest crises.

Written by Intelligent AIApril 11, 2026 • 6:34 PM1355 words

Key points

  • Sudan remains the world’s largest displacement and humanitarian emergency, with war displacing more than 14 million people and pushing parts of the country toward famine.
  • Recent AP reporting has documented fresh drone strikes on civilians and escalating allegations of mass atrocities, including U.N. findings that speak of war crimes and possible crimes against humanity.
  • International aid appeals are growing, but funding gaps and the absence of a durable peace process leave civilians exposed and relief agencies struggling to respond.

Written by Intelligent AI

Sudan’s war has entered another punishing phase, with humanitarian agencies warning that the country’s vast displacement crisis, mounting hunger and allegations of atrocity are reinforcing one another in ways that could deepen one of the world’s worst emergencies. In recent weeks, reports from the Associated Press have described deadly drone attacks on civilians, worsening famine conditions in parts of Darfur and increasingly stark international language about the conduct of the war. Together, they sketch a grim picture: the conflict is not only continuing, but widening its toll on civilians with little sign of a political breakthrough.

The scale is staggering. The International Rescue Committee said in December that Sudan remained at the top of its 2026 Emergency Watchlist, noting that the war had created the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, with more than 14 million people displaced and disease outbreaks and famine spreading in parts of the country. The group also said global humanitarian funding had been cut sharply, leaving aid agencies struggling to keep pace with need. That warning now hangs over every new report from the battlefield. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c2e147e7f3c05056eeb1c2c33c7468?utm_source=openai))

At the center of the crisis is a war that began in April 2023 between Sudan’s military and the Rapid Support Forces, or RSF, a powerful paramilitary group. Since then, the conflict has fractured the country, devastated cities and towns, and driven civilians into repeated waves of flight. The United Nations and aid agencies say the war has become a byword for the collapse of protection for noncombatants. In February, AP reported that the U.N. said more than 6,000 people were killed over three days during the RSF’s attack on el-Fasher in late October, and that the offensive involved atrocities that may amount to war crimes and possible crimes against humanity. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/6399bc86352cecd459dd5dd77e5e11f2?utm_source=openai))

A war measured in displacement and hunger

For many Sudanese families, the war is now less a front line than a prolonged struggle to survive. Displacement has scattered communities across the country and beyond its borders, while repeated attacks on hospitals, shelters and aid corridors have made basic services unreliable or inaccessible. AP reported in February that famine was threatening more areas in Darfur, with acute malnutrition reaching famine levels in two more towns, even as the monitors stopped short of confirming full famine there. The same report said a military hospital attack in southern Sudan killed 22 people, including medical staff. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9b16a0419f8d7cc67c7e95939a8a954d?utm_source=openai))

Those trends are not isolated. In March, AP reported that a drone strike blamed on Sudanese paramilitaries hit a secondary school and a health care center in southern Sudan, killing at least 17 people, mostly schoolgirls. The report said local doctors and a medical group blamed the RSF. Such attacks underscore how war has seeped into civilian life: classrooms, clinics and roads have become targets or accidental casualties of a conflict that increasingly appears unconstrained by the basic rules meant to protect noncombatants. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/e1e102861f0d4cc193a51669f297c670?utm_source=openai))

The consequences are visible far from any battlefield. Families that have fled repeatedly face severe shortages of food, medicine and shelter. Schools close or empty out, health systems collapse, and even people who escape direct violence are often trapped in places where aid cannot arrive reliably. The result is a crisis in which the number of people uprooted is itself a measure of the war’s reach, but not of its full damage. Hunger, disease and trauma compound the immediate injuries of conflict, creating a longer emergency that will outlast the fighting unless relief and security can be restored. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c2e147e7f3c05056eeb1c2c33c7468?utm_source=openai))

Atrocity allegations sharpen the international response

The language used by international investigators has become increasingly severe. In February, AP reported that U.N.-backed human rights experts said the destruction in el-Fasher showed “hallmarks of genocide,” alleging mass killings and other atrocities against non-Arab communities. Earlier that same month, AP reported that the U.N. human rights office said atrocities committed during the RSF’s capture of el-Fasher amounted to war crimes and possible crimes against humanity. The cumulative effect of those findings is to place Sudan not only in the category of a humanitarian disaster, but also in the center of a growing accountability debate. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/85b79539f9e4b18e25dd6ef82d5b53ee?utm_source=openai))

That debate matters because it shapes what the international community considers possible. If the violence is understood as a conventional civil war, the tools on the table may be limited to diplomacy, sanctions and aid. If it is also understood as a pattern of mass atrocity, the pressure for documentation, legal action and targeted accountability rises. Yet Sudan’s war has repeatedly shown how difficult it is to convert outrage into protection. The U.N. can condemn and investigators can report, but neither can by itself stop drones from striking a village or open a corridor to a besieged town. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/6399bc86352cecd459dd5dd77e5e11f2?utm_source=openai))

The result is a crisis in which the number of people uprooted is itself a measure of the war’s reach, but not of its full damage.

Why the crisis keeps deepening

Several forces are making Sudan’s emergency especially hard to contain. First is the persistence of the fighting itself, which has proven resistant to cease-fire efforts and has shifted across regions rather than ending. Second is the breakdown of infrastructure: hospitals, roads, power systems and communications have all taken repeated hits, reducing the capacity to respond even where humanitarian access is possible. Third is financing. The IRC’s warning that global humanitarian funding has fallen sharply is especially consequential in a crisis as large as Sudan’s, where the population in need is vast and many areas are inaccessible or unsafe. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c2e147e7f3c05056eeb1c2c33c7468?utm_source=openai))

There is also a political problem. The war has become so destructive that each side appears to be betting that battlefield gains will eventually outweigh the costs of negotiation. That calculus leaves civilians stuck between military strategies and diplomatic stalemate. International actors have urged restraint and access, but the violence documented in February and March suggests that appeals alone are insufficient. Without sustained pressure, the fighting continues to generate new displacement, new hunger and new evidence of abuses. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/e1e102861f0d4cc193a51669f297c670?utm_source=openai))

Regional spillover is an additional concern. Sudan sits in a volatile neighborhood and its collapse threatens to aggravate already fragile border and migration dynamics. As people flee violence and food scarcity, neighboring states face their own pressures on shelters, aid budgets and security systems. The longer the war lasts, the more its effects spread beyond Sudan’s boundaries, complicating any future reconstruction and making eventual recovery more expensive and uncertain. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c2e147e7f3c05056eeb1c2c33c7468?utm_source=openai))

What comes next

For now, the near-term outlook is bleak. Sudan’s civilians face a landscape in which danger comes from airstrikes, militia assaults, hunger, disease and the absence of dependable state protection. Aid organizations continue to appeal for resources and access, while human rights monitors continue to document abuses and call for accountability. But the central fact remains that the war is still active, still mobile and still devastating communities that have already been forced to absorb repeated shocks. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/e1e102861f0d4cc193a51669f297c670?utm_source=openai))

If there is a lesson in the latest reporting, it is that Sudan’s crisis cannot be understood as a single emergency. It is a collision of war, hunger, displacement and impunity, each element worsening the others. The result is a conflict that may not always dominate the headlines, but continues to reshape one of Africa’s largest countries in ways that will be felt long after the shooting stops. For civilians, the question is no longer just when peace will arrive, but whether enough of the country will remain intact to make peace meaningful. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c2e147e7f3c05056eeb1c2c33c7468?utm_source=openai))
