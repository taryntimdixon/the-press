CinemaCon 2026 puts theatrical movies back at the center of Hollywood’s fight for the future

At this week’s CinemaCon in Las Vegas, studios pitched a bigger theatrical future even as consolidation, shrinking slates, and streaming habits keep the business under pressure.

By The Press • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m.

LAS VEGAS — Hollywood’s annual theater-owner summit opened this month with a familiar ritual: studio chiefs promising that movies still matter, and exhibitors hoping the promises will survive the quarter. But CinemaCon 2026 landed in a different business climate than the one that greeted the post-strike recovery two years ago. The convention ran from April 13 to April 16 at Caesars Palace in Las Vegas, and it arrived as studio executives, theater operators and investors were still debating how much of the movie business should remain theatrical, how much should be reserved for streaming, and how much the market can absorb after years of contraction and reset. ([cinemacon.com](https://www.cinemacon.com/en/exhibitor-faq?utm_source=openai))

The immediate news hook was not subtle. At CinemaCon, Paramount Skydance chief David Ellison told theater owners he would support a minimum of 30 theatrical releases a year across Paramount and Warner Bros. if Paramount’s proposed acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery goes through, and he said he would commit to a 45-day exclusive theatrical window starting immediately. Paramount’s own recap of the event and AP’s reporting both confirmed the pledge. Ellison also said Paramount would maintain a 45-day window for its own titles beginning that day, a promise aimed squarely at reassuring exhibitors who fear fewer films, shorter runs and more corporate concentration if the deal closes. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

That pledge matters because the Warner Bros. Discovery transaction is still live and still central to exhibition anxiety. Warner Bros. Discovery has set a shareholder meeting for April 23, 2026, to approve the transaction with Paramount Skydance, meaning the sale is not a done deal but a pending one. The timing gave CinemaCon a distinctly political feel: exhibitors were not simply watching studio marketing pitches; they were listening for clues about what the next studio structure might mean for release volume, theatrical windows and the overall bargaining power of theaters. ([wbd.com](https://www.wbd.com/news/warner-bros-discovery-sets-shareholder-meeting-date-april-23-2026-approve-transaction?utm_source=openai))

CinemaCon is built for that kind of signaling. The event markets itself as the official convention of Cinema United and the world’s largest gathering for the motion picture theater industry. The 2026 show was expected to draw thousands of industry professionals from dozens of countries, and the annual studio presentations have become the industry’s most visible stage for announcing release slates, showing footage and telegraphing how the majors see theatrical exhibition in the year ahead. In that sense, CinemaCon is no longer just a marketing confab. It is a live referendum on whether theatrical moviegoing is still the center of Hollywood’s business model or merely one premium channel among several. ([cinemacon.com](https://cinemacon.com/?utm_source=openai))

A convention about confidence, held in a year of anxiety

This year’s presentations made the case for theatrical urgency with unusual force. Warner Bros. brought out Tom Cruise, Nicole Kidman, Sandra Bullock, Zendaya, Timothée Chalamet and Jason Momoa in a star-heavy showcase that emphasized a mix of franchises, prestige films and familiar brands. Disney, meanwhile, showed theater owners new footage from Avengers: Doomsday and the opening scene of The Mandalorian and Grogu, with AP reporting that the clip featured the return of Chris Evans as Captain America and that Robert Downey Jr. joined the presentation in Doctor Doom’s costume. Christopher Nolan also appeared with an extended clip from The Odyssey, underscoring how studios continue to frame giant-screen spectacle as something theaters still do better than living rooms do. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/644b63a58677396cced445659df289a4?utm_source=openai))

The point was obvious: if studios can make theatrical releases feel eventful enough, audiences will still show up. But that optimism was layered over real concern. AP described the week as taking place at a critical time for the industry, and the broader backdrop supports that view. The domestic box office remains below pre-pandemic norms, streaming is still the default home for many viewers, and studios continue to cut, reshuffle or delay films rather than flood the market with releases. In other words, the problem is no longer whether one title can break out. It is whether the calendar has enough films, in enough categories, to keep moviegoing a habit rather than an occasional splurge. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

That concern was visible in the room because the industry’s own release strategy has become more selective. The majors still treat tentpoles as cultural events, and their CinemaCon presentations leaned hard on spectacle, star power and the idea of communal viewing. Yet those same companies are making business decisions that narrow the slate, concentrate on recognizable IP and rely more heavily on franchise pictures that can justify premium-ticket pricing and global marketing. The result is not a dead theater business, but a more selective one: theaters are being asked to carry fewer kinds of movies and to do so with more intensity. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/644b63a58677396cced445659df289a4?utm_source=openai))

Why the Warner Bros. sale became an exhibition story

Warner Bros. Discovery’s proposed sale became a theater-policy story because it implicates the number of movies, the length of theatrical windows and the bargaining power of exhibitors at the same time. AP reported earlier this month that Warner Bros. Discovery reopened takeover talks with Paramount after receiving a waiver from Netflix, and the transaction remained pending as of the April 23 shareholder date. That makes the deal a live test case for what consolidation means in practice. If one merged company controls more of the release calendar, exhibitors worry it will prioritize a smaller number of larger pictures, reduce overlap in marketing and distribution, and leave less room for the kinds of midbudget films that used to fill theaters between franchise peaks. ([wbd.com](https://www.wbd.com/news/warner-bros-discovery-sets-shareholder-meeting-date-april-23-2026-approve-transaction?utm_source=openai))

Ellison’s 30-film pledge is therefore doing double duty. It is a reassurance to theater owners, and it is also a message to regulators, investors and Wall Street that a combined company would still feed the exhibition pipeline. But a promise is not a schedule. Paramount’s own CinemaCon materials make clear that the company is trying to present itself as aggressively theatrical in the present tense, even as the merger logic points toward long-term cost cutting and integration. The tension is hard to miss: studio leaders say they value theaters, but the business incentives of consolidation often push in the opposite direction. ([paramount.com](https://www.paramount.com/news/paramount-pictures-goes-all-in-on-theatrical-at-cinemaconr?utm_source=openai))

There is also a larger structural point. Paramount’s own messaging at CinemaCon emphasized that the company already has 15 films dated for 2026, up from eight in 2025, and framed that as evidence that output can grow when a studio chooses to do so. That is a useful reminder that theatrical scarcity is not inevitable. It is often a management decision. But it also means that any merged-company promise should be judged by actual release calendars, not by rhetoric delivered on a Las Vegas stage. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/more/global/david-ellison-cinemacon-paramount-windows-streaming-35518/?utm_source=openai))

Theatrical windows are back, but only partly

The post-pandemic debate over windowing used to sound ideological. Now it is operational. Major studios have experimented with 17-day, 30-day and 45-day exclusives, often changing tactics title by title. The latest public commitments are more theater-friendly than the earliest streaming-era experiments, but they still fall far short of the old 90-day norm. At CinemaCon, Ellison said all Paramount films would have a 45-day theatrical window beginning immediately. That matters because exhibitors have long argued that short windows train audiences to wait for home viewing and erode the value of opening-weekend urgency. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Still, the economics remain brittle. A shorter window can help films retain urgency in the first weeks, but it also speeds the transition to home platforms and compresses the time theaters have to recoup their share. That is especially difficult for non-franchise films, which often rely on longer legs rather than enormous openings. If the theatrical proposition is built almost entirely around opening weekend, then the model stops serving most adult dramas, specialty releases and smaller genre titles. The more studios define theaters as the home of “events” only, the more ordinary movies get pushed out of that definition. ([thewrap.com](https://www.thewrap.com/industry-news/business/david-ellison-cinemacon-warner-merger-speech/?utm_source=openai))

There is a counterargument worth taking seriously. A stricter focus on event films can produce clarity. Some executives argue that it is better to give theaters fewer titles but make sure those titles are films audiences actively want to leave home for. That logic has helped franchise pictures and star-driven spectacle retain theatrical relevance, and it was visible all week in how the studios talked about their slates. The trouble is that such a strategy can become self-defeating if it is the only strategy. A cinema culture built on tentpoles alone is more vulnerable to fatigue, release-date clustering and audience drift than one that offers a broader menu of reasons to go out. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/644b63a58677396cced445659df289a4?utm_source=openai))

What the box-office numbers say — and do not say

The latest numbers show a market recovering unevenly rather than snapping back. AP’s CinemaCon reporting said the domestic box office is still about 20% below pre-pandemic levels. In Britain, the British Film Institute’s latest statistics show a similar pattern: total UK admissions in 2024 reached 126.5 million, up 2% from 2023 but still 28% below 2019, and the 2024 box office remained below pre-pandemic norms. Those figures do not prove the same thing in every market, but they do show that the big-screen business has not returned to the old baseline, even if strong titles still draw substantial crowds. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The composition of the box office is changing as much as the total. The BFI reported that the last three months of 2024 saw a surge in cinema attendances driven by family-targeted franchise releases, sequels and musical adaptations, and that the top-grossing UK independent releases were still concentrated in a relatively small number of titles. That concentration is a warning sign for some observers and a reassurance for others. It suggests that theaters remain highly dependent on a narrow set of attention-grabbing releases, and that the middle of the market — adult dramas, smaller comedies, original concepts — remains vulnerable. ([bfi.org.uk](https://www.bfi.org.uk/news/official-bfi-statistics-2024?utm_source=openai))

What box-office data does not tell you is whether the public has permanently rejected theaters. It does not. It tells you the market is more top-heavy. The BFI’s statistics show admissions can rise year over year without returning to 2019 levels, which means the business can stabilize in a smaller form. That matters because it changes the debate. The question is no longer whether theaters will survive at all. It is what sort of theaters, showing what kind of films, at what scale, and under what ownership structures. ([bfi.org.uk](https://www.bfi.org.uk/news/official-bfi-statistics-2024?utm_source=openai))

Comscore’s own 50-year anniversary release this week is a reminder that the industry still treats the box office as a shared measuring stick. Comscore said on April 15 that it was celebrating 50 years of movie measurement, with theatrical performance tracked in more than 70 countries. That continuity matters because theaters, studios and advertisers still need common data to negotiate, even as the business around the box office shifts. But the persistence of the measuring stick should not be mistaken for stability in the market it measures. Reliable data can describe a volatile ecosystem just as accurately as a stable one. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

Labor, production and the geography of film work

CinemaCon’s focus is exhibition, but the theater story is inseparable from production geography and labor. One reason the slate is getting narrower is that the broader production system has been under pressure since the 2023 strikes and the post-strike slowdown. California has been trying to counter runaway production through an expanded film and television tax credit program, and the California Film Commission’s production alerts page shows active 2026 application windows. Entertainment Partners reported that the California Film Commission opened the sixth window of the revamped program on March 2, 2026, while the commission’s own page confirms current alerts and timing for 2026 windows. ([ep.com](https://www.ep.com/industry-news/california-film-tax-credit-spring-2026-application-windows-feature-films-television/?utm_source=openai))

The policy race is global, not just domestic. The BFI said the UK reached £5.6 billion in film and high-end television production spend in 2024, and the same industry data points to continued competition for shoots and crews across countries. California, Britain and other production centers are all trying to make sure they remain attractive to projects that can travel anywhere. Those incentives do not directly solve the theater problem, but they shape what gets made, where it gets made and whether local crew ecosystems can survive long enough to support a healthy theatrical pipeline. ([bfi.org.uk](https://www.bfi.org.uk/news/official-bfi-statistics-2024?utm_source=openai))

Labor is a central part of that equation. The American Federation of Musicians said in 2024 that its new motion picture agreements with the AMPTP included protections against generative AI encroachment and compensation provisions when musicians’ performances are used with AI tools. That does not settle the broader labor question, but it is a sign that the post-strike settlement has not ended the industry’s negotiation over technology, rights and pay. Even as studios praise theatrical cinema in public, they are still reorganizing production around cost control, digital workflows and artificial intelligence tools that can change budgets, schedules and the kinds of films that remain economically viable to make. ([afm.org](https://www.afm.org/2024/04/afm-musicians-ratify-motion-picture-agreements/?utm_source=openai))

There is also an audience-side labor story. The people who make theaters function are part of local service economies, and their fortunes rise or fall with box-office health. When big studios push fewer but larger releases, theaters can often survive on premium pricing and event margins, but smaller venues, art houses and independent chains feel the gap quickly. That is why public support for audience development and exhibition matters more than it used to. The BFI’s screen-culture funding and audience-projects programs are examples of how public institutions increasingly treat exhibition not as a passive retail channel but as civic infrastructure that needs maintenance. ([film.ca.gov](https://film.ca.gov/news-notices/production-alerts/?utm_source=openai))

What the awards season tells us about theatrical value

One reason studios still court theaters so aggressively is that awards prestige remains tied to theatrical prestige. The 98th Academy Awards took place on March 15, 2026, and the Academy’s official materials show that the awards calendar still revolves around theatrical releases, screenings, shortlists and a public ceremony that keeps films in the cultural conversation. CinemaCon’s 2026 presentation lineup did the same thing in reverse: it made theatrical exhibition look like the path to cultural relevance rather than the residue of an older system. ([thewaltdisneycompany.com](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/news/cinemacon-2026-recap/?utm_source=openai))

That connection is not just symbolic. Awards can extend a film’s theatrical life, which helps justify premium screens, second weekends and word-of-mouth campaigns. But the prestige model can also obscure the broader market. A few acclaimed titles may get longer runs, yet that does not fix the economics of midrange theatrical filmmaking. In many cases, the awards system simply redistributes scarcity: a handful of contenders get extended lives while the overall slate remains constrained. That is why exhibitors keep insisting on volume, not just quality. ([thewaltdisneycompany.com](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/news/cinemacon-2026-recap/?utm_source=openai))

The better evidence that theaters still matter is that the biggest companies continue to treat theatrical release as the launch pad for almost everything they care about. Disney’s 2025 box-office strength, as referenced in its CinemaCon recap, helped give the studio confidence to lean into giant-screen marketing. Warner Bros. used its presentation to tie star power to future releases. Paramount used its stage time to promise more films, not fewer. That pattern suggests the industry has not abandoned theatrical value; it is simply trying to extract that value more selectively. ([thewaltdisneycompany.com](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/news/cinemacon-2026-recap/?utm_source=openai))

Technology is changing the theater bargain, but not always in the way studios suggest

Studios increasingly describe theatrical cinema through the language of immersion, premium formats and spectacle. At CinemaCon, Disney leaned on the promise of blockbuster-scale experiences; Warner Bros. emphasized star power and franchise depth; Nolan’s The Odyssey footage was framed as the sort of giant-screen event only theaters can properly sell. There is truth in that pitch. Large-format presentation, sound and scale still give theatrical movies advantages that home viewing cannot fully replicate. The larger question is whether the industry can build a sustainable release calendar around those advantages alone. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))

Technology also cuts the other way. Streaming interfaces have conditioned audiences to expect instant access and low-friction viewing. Social media has trained younger viewers to discover films in fragments rather than through months-long theatrical campaigns. And AI is beginning to affect marketing, localization, dubbing, effects workflows and other parts of the chain that shape theatrical output without always being visible to audiences. The net result is not that theaters have lost all technical relevance, but that a theater visit now has to justify itself against a much broader set of digitally enabled alternatives. ([afm.org](https://www.afm.org/2024/04/afm-musicians-ratify-motion-picture-agreements/?utm_source=openai))

The strongest argument for theaters is still social rather than technological. Moviegoing remains one of the few mass entertainment experiences that is both public and synchronized. A good opening weekend can still become a collective event, and in some cases theatrical attendance can transform a movie into a news item rather than just content. But that social function only works if the business keeps offering occasions worth leaving home for. Theatrical exhibition does not need every film; it needs a healthy enough flow of films that audiences stay in the habit. ([cinemacon.com](https://cinemacon.com/?utm_source=openai))

The countervailing evidence: audiences still turn out when the product is right

It would be wrong to write an obituary for theaters. The evidence from 2025 and early 2026 argues against that. Disney’s CinemaCon materials said the studio’s films dominated the 2025 box office with nearly $2.5 billion in domestic ticket sales and $6.6 billion globally, showing that the biggest studios can still generate enormous theatrical revenue when the slate lands. Paramount used CinemaCon to make the opposite argument: that more theatrical output, not less, is the right answer to industry fragility. And Comscore’s 50-year measurement milestone is a sign that the box office remains central enough to plan around. ([thewaltdisneycompany.com](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/news/cinemacon-2026-recap/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a deeper market reason to avoid fatalism. Theatrical movies still anchor downstream value: they create franchise identity, generate marketing awareness and can boost subscription and transactional platforms later. Theaters are not merely a legacy channel; they are an early-value engine. That helps explain why studios continue to spend heavily on tentpoles even while cutting back elsewhere. A theatrical hit can still power the rest of the ecosystem. ([thewaltdisneycompany.com](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/news/cinemacon-2026-recap/?utm_source=openai))

But the existence of hits does not answer the policy question facing the industry. If fewer companies control more of the release calendar, if those companies concentrate on franchise titles, and if theatrical windows stay shorter than they once were, then the market may remain profitable for a small number of winners while becoming less robust for everyone else. That is the real debate behind the CinemaCon cheerleading. The issue is not whether movies can still succeed in theaters. It is whether the business will keep supporting enough kinds of movies, in enough places, for theatrical culture to remain broad rather than elite. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Over the next few months, the most important indicators will be practical, not rhetorical. First, how the Warner Bros. Discovery sale proceeds after the April 23 shareholder meeting, and whether regulatory or shareholder concerns alter the theatrical commitments now being advertised. Second, whether the 45-day windows hold across the slate or become exceptions for only the biggest films. Third, whether studios announce enough midbudget and original titles to give theaters a steadier release cadence. Fourth, whether state incentive programs in California and abroad continue to stabilize the supply of films as crews and vendors try to recover from the post-strike slowdown. ([wbd.com](https://www.wbd.com/news/warner-bros-discovery-sets-shareholder-meeting-date-april-23-2026-approve-transaction?utm_source=openai))

The larger question is whether Hollywood can move from reassurance to reconstruction. For the moment, CinemaCon 2026 suggests a business that still believes in theaters but is not yet willing to restructure itself fully around them. Theatrical exhibition is being celebrated as an experience while also being narrowed as a strategy. If that continues, movie theaters may survive as premium venues for the industry’s biggest bets, but the older idea of theaters as the default first home for film culture will keep eroding. That is the tension the week in Las Vegas made impossible to ignore. ([cinemacon.com](https://cinemacon.com/?utm_source=openai))
