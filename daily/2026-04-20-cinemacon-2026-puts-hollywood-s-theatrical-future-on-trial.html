CinemaCon 2026 puts Hollywood’s theatrical future on trial

At this year’s theater-owner confab in Las Vegas, studio chiefs are promising more movies, firmer windows and a reset for a battered exhibition business — but the box office, labor pipeline and production geography still suggest the recovery is fragile.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 20, 2026 • 4:20 p.m. • 2887 words

LAS VEGAS — If you want to know what Hollywood thinks about movie theaters in April 2026, listen to the speeches it makes to exhibitors in Las Vegas. CinemaCon, the annual gathering of theater owners, studio chiefs and vendors, has become less a trade show than a referendum on the business model itself: how many movies studios will truly release in theaters, how long those movies will stay exclusive, who will pay for the screens, and whether audiences still want the ritual of seeing a film together in a room with strangers. CinemaCon 2026 is taking place at Caesars Palace from April 13 to 16, under the umbrella of Cinema United, the exhibition trade group formerly known as NATO. ([cinemacon.com](https://cinemacon.com/?utm_source=openai))

This year’s edition arrived at a moment when the industry’s contradictions are impossible to ignore. On the one hand, the box office has continued to prove that theatrical still matters for event films and franchise pictures, and Comscore chose this month to mark 50 years of movie measurement, underscoring how central a common box-office scoreboard remains to the industry’s self-argument. On the other hand, Los Angeles production remained depressed through 2025, California’s incentive regime is still being revised and sold politically as a retention tool, and theater owners continue to warn that the supply of big-screen product is not yet robust enough to restore pre-pandemic business. FilmLA’s year-end report said Greater Los Angeles finished 2025 with 19,694 shoot days, down 16.1% from 2024, while reporting in early 2026 said feature filming and other categories remained weak despite a late-year uptick. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

The headline-making sound bite from this year’s CinemaCon was David Ellison’s promise, as Paramount Skydance chairman and CEO, to keep faith with theatrical release. At the conference, Ellison said the combined company would guarantee 30 theatrical releases a year across Paramount and Warner Bros. and would maintain a 45-day exclusive theatrical window starting immediately. The pledge matters because it cuts against the direction many executives have taken over the last five years, as studios have increasingly treated theatrical runs as marketing events for streaming rather than as the core of the business. That shift is one reason exhibitors listened so carefully: if an owner of two major studios says “long live the movies” and commits to a standard window, the statement becomes both promise and pressure test. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

But the more interesting story is not the speech itself; it is what it reveals about the bargaining position of every player in the film ecosystem. Studios need theaters to launch most large-scale films with cultural impact. Theaters need studios to fill seats and concession stands. Streaming companies need both, because even when they bypass theaters they still borrow theatrical prestige to market awards contenders and franchise entries. Labor, from writers to crews to projection and exhibition workers, sits inside that triangle, waiting to see whether promises of renewed production will produce jobs or merely repackage old distribution habits. The industry’s apparent consensus about the value of theaters hides a deeper dispute about who shoulders the risk of making movies and who captures the upside. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-16/david-ellison-cinemacon-pledges-30-films-paramount-warner-bros?utm_source=openai))

Why CinemaCon 2026 matters now

CinemaCon has always been partly theater, partly market research and partly political theater. But in 2026 it lands at a particularly consequential point. Hollywood is still trying to find its post-strike equilibrium after the 2023 writers’ and actors’ stoppages; California policymakers spent much of 2025 and early 2026 trying to keep production in-state; and exhibitors are coping with a public that is going to the movies selectively rather than reflexively. Box office is no longer collapsing, but it has not yet returned to the kind of reliably broad performance that supported the old studio cadence of tentpoles, mid-budget dramas, comedies and adult-skewing prestige pictures all flowing into theaters on a steady rhythm. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-16/david-ellison-cinemacon-pledges-30-films-paramount-warner-bros?utm_source=openai))

The recent news cycle underscores that tension. Paramount and Warner Bros. have both been tied to a major strategic question: whether studios should commit to theaters as the first and best place to see movies, or treat them as one node in a larger content supply chain. Netflix, long the symbol of theatrical disruption, has recently signaled a more pragmatic stance in public remarks about the Warner Bros. acquisition debate, saying the company would preserve a 45-day theatrical window for Warner Bros. films if the deal goes through. Sony, meanwhile, has continued to use a conventional studio distribution model while also making downstream streaming arrangements part of its rights stack. Those patterns suggest a film economy in which the theatrical window is no longer disappearing, but is being renegotiated transaction by transaction. ([en.wikipedia.org](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposed_acquisition_of_Warner_Bros._Discovery_by_Paramount_Skydance?utm_source=openai))

That is exactly why exhibitors care so much about the language of exclusivity. A 45-day window is not just a number; it is a signal that the theatrical run still has meaningful commercial value beyond opening weekend. The AP reported that Ellison’s pledge was paired with a claim that all films would remain in theaters exclusively for 45 days, beginning that day, and Bloomberg Law later reported that Paramount’s plan also includes a 90-day path before streaming for its films. Even with those assurances, the broader industry remains in flux, and no single promise has settled the long-running argument over whether consumers should get faster access or theaters should get more breathing room. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Theatrical is still a business, not a metaphor

For all the cultural language about movie magic, theatrical exhibition remains a capital-intensive business with narrow margins and fixed costs. Screens must be staffed, leased, insured, maintained and programmed. A theater owner cannot cash in on a film’s second-week audience if the studio sends the movie to premium video on demand or a streaming platform too quickly. Exhibitors therefore read every statement about windows as if it were a budget line, because it is. The AP noted that Ellison’s promise came amid industry hand-wringing over the pending Paramount-Warner Bros. deal, and the concern among theater owners was not abstract nostalgia but the fear that fewer films and shorter windows could hollow out the release calendar. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

Even the data people are signaling that the business is worth defending. Comscore’s anniversary release framed standardized box-office measurement as foundational to the industry’s decision-making. That may sound self-congratulatory, but it also points to something real: theatrical still functions as a shared, independently monitored marketplace. When the industry argues over which films are hits, which audiences are turning out and which territories are recovering fastest, Comscore remains part of the common language. The existence of that language is one reason theatrical can still act as a prestige and financial benchmark, even when overall attendance is uneven. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

Recent box-office reporting suggests the theatrical market can still surprise on the upside. AP reported in March that “Scream 7” opened with a franchise-best $64.1 million, and the same run of recent coverage from trade outlets has continued to show that event films and recognizable brands can still pull audiences into theaters. But strong openings do not solve the structural problems. A hit weekend can prove demand exists; it does not prove that the industry has solved the release-calendar problem, the production problem or the habit-shifting problem. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/6b2f6d5137e8f3d01768812f8c3a576e?utm_source=openai))

The labor story under the box-office story

Film coverage often treats labor as a separate beat, but in 2026 it is central to the theatrical question. The WGA and studios reached a tentative deal in early April 2026, an important marker of détente after the shocks of the past few years. That does not mean labor tensions are gone. It means the industry is trying to stabilize enough to make and release more work. For a theatrical recovery, that distinction matters. A healthy release slate depends on writers, crews, post-production staff, performers and a production base that is not constantly in motion between jurisdictions. When labor negotiations are unsettled, financing and scheduling become more fragile. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

California’s production debate makes the point more sharply. In Los Angeles, filming fell 16.1% in 2025, reaching 19,694 shoot days, according to FilmLA data reported by the Los Angeles Times. FilmLA’s own March 2026 update said Los Angeles County still has the world’s largest concentration of sound-stage space but faces intense competition from the U.K. and New York, whose inventories have expanded. The implication is straightforward: theatrical can only thrive if production itself is healthy enough to generate a pipeline of movies worth seeing in theaters. Distribution strategy cannot save an anemic production base. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-01-15/filming-in-la-drops-16-1-compared-to-2024?utm_source=openai))

That is why the labor and policy side of the story complicates any simple “theaters are back” narrative. California lawmakers expanded and revised the state’s film and TV tax credit program in 2025, and Los Angeles Times reporting in 2026 described how the state continued trying to use incentives to keep production local. At the same time, local reporting has documented how post-production workers are pushing for a dedicated incentive because work is leaving California even when filming does not. The point is not merely where cameras roll, but where editing, visual effects, sound and finishing happen. If that work continues to migrate, theatrical releases may still arrive, but the jobs and local infrastructure that support them will keep eroding. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-01-15/filming-in-la-drops-16-1-compared-to-2024?utm_source=openai))

Los Angeles is still the symbolic center — and still losing ground

The industry’s habit of treating Los Angeles as both myth and factory is under strain. FilmLA’s March 2026 sound-stage update said the county remains the world leader in available stage space and that new facilities opened in the first quarter of 2026, but it also said the market is contending with highly competitive incentives elsewhere. The local permitting system remains a live issue too: FilmLA has been emphasizing faster permit turnaround and streamlined procedures, while the city continues to adjust filming rules and executive actions to make production less cumbersome. These changes matter because every extra day of friction adds cost, and every cost increase encourages producers to test another region. ([nbcpalmsprings.com](https://www.nbcpalmsprings.com/2026/01/15/filmla-reports-late2025-production-uptick-despite-overall-decline-across-greater-los-angeles?utm_source=openai))

That local decline should worry anyone who cares about the theatrical future, because theaters are downstream from production. No matter how effective a marketing campaign may be, exhibitors cannot sell tickets to films that are not being made or are being made somewhere else for someone else’s platform. A production flight does not only affect union jobs and local vendors. It changes the kind of movies that get financed. Lower-cost environments and incentive-driven ecosystems tend to favor certain sorts of projects over others, which can alter the balance between blockbuster spectacle, adult drama, regional stories and mid-budget crowd-pleasers. In that sense, the film-production map is also a criticism map: it shapes what the public gets to see. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-01-15/filming-in-la-drops-16-1-compared-to-2024?utm_source=openai))

There is also a less romantic but important dimension: the physical plant of moviegoing. Sound-stage expansion, theater retrofits, premium formats and the maintenance of older multiplexes all require investment. FilmLA’s updated stage report and Comscore’s anniversary note both point toward an industry that increasingly values measurement, optimization and infrastructure discipline. But those are not substitutes for product. A modern theater can have laser projection, luxury seats and immersive sound, yet still struggle if release slates are thin or skewed heavily toward franchise IP. The infrastructure is only as useful as the content pipeline behind it. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

What the studios are really promising

When studio leaders speak to exhibitors about a theatrical comeback, they are not simply declaring faith in the big screen. They are making a financial argument that theatrical can still do three jobs at once: generate revenue, create cultural visibility and anchor downstream monetization. The current crop of industry deals suggests that this model is now much more selective than it once was. Netflix can support certain theatrical runs while remaining fundamentally streaming-first. Sony can preserve a conventional release-and-pay-one pathway while using downstream platforms as part of its rights strategy. Paramount and Warner Bros. can pledge theatrical volume and windows while still being shaped by corporate restructuring and merger logic. In each case, theatrical is less a universal policy than a portfolio strategy. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/more/news/david-ellison-cinemacon-paramount-windows-streaming-35518/?utm_source=openai))

That means promises should be read as evidence of bargaining power rather than sentimental conversion. A 45-day window is credible partly because the studio making the promise wants to reassure theater owners, and because theatrical still delivers some combination of premium grosses, public attention and award-season momentum. But the industry has learned that a theatrical release can also be a flexible lever: a way to create event status for a few weeks before a title enters a broader distribution lifecycle. The argument over whether that helps or hurts cinemas is really an argument over whether the first week or the first six weeks of a release should be the primary unit of value. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

The counterargument is that moviegoing is not just a commercial category but a social one, and that theatrical’s value cannot be reduced to window arithmetic. Industry observers and critics have long argued that audiences still seek large-screen spectacle, communal laughter and a sense of occasion. CinemaCon itself is built around that belief. The event’s schedule includes a big-screen achievement awards gala, trade-show demonstrations and studio presentations designed to make future releases feel like shared events before they ever open. In that reading, the theater business is not dying; it is being refined into a more selective, higher-commitment ritual. ([cinemacon.com](https://cinemacon.com/?utm_source=openai))

Yet the strongest rebuttal to that hopeful version is that selectivity itself may be the problem. If audiences only show up for sequels, branded worlds, horror franchises, musicals and the occasional event film, then theaters can survive, but they may become narrower in what they program and what they can afford to take risks on. That in turn affects criticism, taste and the ecology of smaller releases. Recent box-office stories have shown that strong openings increasingly belong to recognizable IP or aggressively positioned titles, while original or older-skewing films often need longer runs, better reviews or specialty positioning to build. Theatrical may remain culturally vital, but it may do so in a more unequal way than it did before the streaming wars. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/6b2f6d5137e8f3d01768812f8c3a576e?utm_source=openai))

The awards circuit still points back to theaters

Another reason CinemaCon matters is that prestige still routes through theatrical even when the industry pretends otherwise. The awards ecosystem continues to reward films that can sustain a narrative over months rather than over a weekend, and studios understand that a theatrical release still helps turn a project into a public event. That is one reason major companies keep investing in theatrical launches even when they also rely on streaming, premium video on demand or international licensing. Theatrical is not only where audiences pay; it is where films become legible to the broader culture as movies rather than just content. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

That prestige function explains why the debate over windows has never really been about timing alone. It is about meaning. A film that opens in theaters acquires a different kind of public life: reviews matter more, exit polls matter more, box-office tracking matters more, and the film gets a chance to become an event rather than a file. Studios that remain committed to theatrical are therefore buying not just ticket revenue but cultural framing. In a fragmented media environment, that framing can still be valuable enough to justify the cost of exclusivity. The question is whether studios will continue to value that framing when short-term distribution efficiencies tempt them to shrink theatrical runs even further. ([comscore.com](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Press-Releases/2026/4/Five-Decades-of-Box-Office-Intelligence?utm_source=openai))

The uncertainty that still hangs over the industry

It would be premature to say the theatrical business has turned a corner. The current evidence supports a more careful conclusion: the industry is stabilizing around a smaller and more conditional idea of moviegoing. The best-case scenario is not a full restoration of the pre-pandemic order. It is a hybrid system in which theaters retain the highest-value premieres, prestige titles and event releases, while streaming and digital platforms absorb more of the volume business. For exhibitors, that is better than collapse but worse than the old norm. For studios, it can be profitable but operationally complicated. For workers, it may mean some gains without a full return of the production ecosystem that once supported more abundant employment. ([latimes.com](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-04-16/david-ellison-cinemacon-pledges-30-films-paramount-warner-bros?utm_source=openai))

That uncertainty is what made this year’s CinemaCon feel so consequential. The industry is not merely celebrating movies; it is negotiating the terms on which movies will exist in public. Ellison’s pledge of more theatrical releases and a 45-day window is a meaningful signal, but it is only one signal among many. California is still trying to keep shoots at home. Streaming companies are still balancing prestige and efficiency. Theater owners are still counting on consistent supply. Labor is still seeking stable employment and protection from the next disruption. And audiences, who have gained habits formed during the streaming era, are still deciding when a movie is worth leaving the house for. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

In that sense, CinemaCon 2026 is less a coronation than a stress test. The industry is saying it believes in theaters. The real question is whether it will build a film economy that allows that belief to survive the next few years of merger fallout, labor negotiation, incentive competition and audience fragmentation. If it does, the theatrical business may not return to its old self, but it could remain a central part of film culture. If it does not, the promises made in Las Vegas will read like the last bright-lit assurances before a more permanent rearrangement. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))
