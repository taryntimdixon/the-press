Ohio’s 10-gigawatt AI campus signals a new phase in the data-center boom

The federal government’s plan for a massive data center and power plant in southern Ohio shows how the AI buildout is becoming an infrastructure project, not just a software story.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 17, 2026 • 1:18 a.m. • 922 words

The artificial intelligence race is increasingly being fought with land, steel and fuel, not just code. In southern Ohio, the U.S. Department of Energy has announced a public-private partnership to turn the decommissioned Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant into what officials are branding the “PORTS Technology Campus,” a vast development that would pair a data center with its own power supply. The plan envisions a 10-gigawatt data center alongside up to 10 gigawatts of new generation, including 9.2 gigawatts of natural gas, according to the department. It is one of the clearest signs yet that the AI boom is colliding with a far older constraint: electricity. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

For technology companies, the message is blunt. It is no longer enough to secure chips, cloud contracts and talent. The next bottleneck is power, and the scale of demand is changing how companies think about data-center development. At the Ohio site, officials said the project would include both on-site and grid-connected generation, along with billions of dollars in transmission upgrades. That makes the project less like a conventional server farm and more like a self-contained industrial campus built around AI workloads. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

A 10-gigawatt campus is bigger than a normal cloud buildout

The size of the Ohio proposal helps explain why it matters. A 10-gigawatt data center is not just large by technology standards; it is huge by energy-infrastructure standards. The Department of Energy said the project would sit on a site previously used for uranium enrichment, with the campus designed to support a major push into artificial intelligence. Energy Secretary Chris Wright, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Interior Secretary Doug Burgum attended the announcement, alongside representatives from SoftBank and SB Energy. The project’s scale puts it in the same conversation as utility planning, industrial policy and national competitiveness. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

The announcement also reflects the government’s eagerness to speed up domestic AI capacity. Federal officials have already identified former government sites as possible locations for technology companies to build data and storage facilities. The Ohio plan goes further by linking the computing campus directly to new power generation, a model that may appeal to companies worried about grid delays, rate disputes or opposition from local communities. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

This is also a political signal. The Trump administration has pressed technology companies to build their own generation alongside electricity-intensive sites, arguing that the AI buildout should not leave households and businesses absorbing the costs. In that sense, the Ohio project looks like an attempt to preempt the criticism now surrounding large data centers: that they consume enormous amounts of electricity while shifting costs onto everyone else. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

States are starting to push back on data-center expansion

The Ohio project lands as state lawmakers and regulators are becoming far more aggressive about data-center oversight. In Illinois, a proposed bill would require large data centers to bring their own renewable energy and disclose water and electricity use. The proposal, called the POWER Act, would also force hyperscale facilities to report and pay for water usage, create efficient cooling systems and submit impact assessments before they are sited. Supporters frame it as a consumer-protection measure; critics say it would impose exceptional burdens on an industry that has become central to economic development. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/local/chicago/2026/04/10/illinois-bill-pushes-data-center-accountability))

Illinois is hardly alone. Georgia regulators last year approved a large increase in electric generation capacity to meet projected data-center demand, while warning about the long-term cost implications. Across the country, local officials are trying to balance the lure of investment against the strain on power systems, water resources and household bills. The Ohio campus is a reminder that the tension is no longer theoretical. It is showing up in legislation, utility planning and public debate right now. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0b377d6a4a57c9353c0eb577b8951af3?utm_source=openai))

The political argument around data centers has shifted quickly. A year ago, the story was about how many new facilities cloud companies could build. Now it is about where those facilities can get electricity, how much water they will use and who should pay for the grid upgrades they require. That is a major change for an industry that has long benefited from local incentives and low-key siting. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/local/chicago/2026/04/10/illinois-bill-pushes-data-center-accountability))

AI infrastructure is becoming a national-security issue

The Ohio project is not the only sign that AI infrastructure is being folded into national strategy. At the same time that federal officials are backing new campuses, U.S. lawmakers are moving to tighten export controls on semiconductor manufacturing equipment sold to China. A bipartisan proposal would widen restrictions on advanced wafer-fabrication tools, reflecting concern that supply-chain choke points still matter in an AI era dominated by hardware, not just software. The logic is clear: if compute power is strategic, then the tools that produce chips and the energy that runs them are strategic too. ([tomshardware.com](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/semiconductors/u-s-lawmakers-aim-to-ban-export-of-duv-chipmaking-and-etching-tools-to-leading-firms-in-china-bipartisan-proposal-would-ban-lithography-equipment-for-huawei-smic-and-others))

That convergence helps explain the pace of announcements around data centers, power plants and chip controls. Governments are no longer treating AI as a niche software category. They are treating it as an industrial base with physical dependencies that can be regulated, subsidized or blocked. The result is a much broader policy fight over who gets to build, where it gets built and how the costs are allocated. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))

For tech executives, the message is that the era of lightweight cloud expansion is giving way to something heavier and more capital-intensive. The next frontier of AI growth depends on grid interconnections, generation contracts, permitting timelines and community acceptance. The most important technology story of the spring may not be a model launch or a new app. It may be the infrastructure race now unfolding beneath them. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4667fa1442ec1c652228337ab4eb68ee))
