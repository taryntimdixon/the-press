The Housing Bill Keeps Growing Even When the Mortgage Doesn’t

New federal data, regional Fed reporting and housing research suggest the affordability problem has shifted. In many places, softer rents and slightly lower mortgage rates are being offset by insurance, utilities, taxes and other costs that households cannot easily refinance, defer or negotiate away.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m. • 3724 words

For most of the past two years, the housing-affordability argument has been dominated by a familiar trio: mortgage rates, asking rents and home prices. Those measures still matter. But the latest round of public data and field reporting suggests they no longer tell the whole household story. In many parts of the country, the listed cost of shelter has become only the beginning of the bill. The harder part for families is increasingly the cost of keeping a home insurable, powered, cooled, taxed and functional.

That shift is economically important because it changes where relief can come from. A household can benefit when mortgage rates fall. A renter can catch a break when a wave of new apartments slows rent growth or brings back concessions. But a family cannot refinance a homeowners-insurance renewal, negotiate much with a local utility monopoly, or do very much about a property-tax assessment tied to local budgets and infrastructure needs. These are not peripheral expenses. They are increasingly central to whether a home feels affordable after the lease is signed or the closing documents are done.

The April 23 release from the Census Bureau offers a timely entry point. The bureau’s new Household Trends and Outlook Pulse Survey, or HTOPS, published fresh data on employment, housing status, difficulty paying usual household expenses, energy use and spending, and stress related to higher prices. The survey does not compress the household economy into one clean housing headline. Its value is almost the opposite: it shows how expenses travel together. Housing pressure is entwined with energy bills, transportation, insurance status and the broader question of whether households can cover their usual expenses at all.

That same broadening of the affordability story appears in the Federal Reserve’s April Beige Book. The Fed’s national summary said housing market activity softened across several districts as heightened uncertainty and rising mortgage rates damped buyer demand. In the same report, the Fed also said insurance premiums and health-care costs continued to climb, while district reports described additional pressure from utilities, transportation and energy. The point is not that every extra dollar belongs in the housing column. It is that households experience affordability as an all-in monthly burden, not as a single rent quote or mortgage advertisement.

The result is a less visible but more stubborn version of the housing problem. Sticker-price relief has become possible in some markets. Total occupancy relief is much harder to find. That matters for renters deciding whether to move, for owners who thought a low fixed mortgage rate had stabilized their housing costs, and for policymakers still speaking as if the affordability crisis can be summarized by the mortgage market alone.

The listed payment is no longer the whole payment

Mortgage rates remain the most legible number in housing. They are national, frequent and easy to compare over time. Freddie Mac’s weekly survey put the average 30-year fixed-rate mortgage at 6.23 percent as of April 23, 2026, down from 6.88 percent a year earlier. The Mortgage Bankers Association reported that mortgage applications rose 7.9 percent in the week ending April 17, with the purchase index up 10 percent from the previous week and 14 percent from a year earlier. By the standards of the last two years, that is real improvement.

But even that improvement has not translated into broad confidence that housing is suddenly manageable. Part of the reason is straightforward: rates remain high relative to the pandemic era, and monthly principal-and-interest payments are still expensive in much of the country. Yet the bigger change may be that families increasingly understand the mortgage as only one component of ownership. Escrow bills, insurance premiums, taxes, utilities, association fees and repair costs now loom larger in household decision-making than they did when money was cheaper and insurance markets were calmer.

Census data released with the 2024 American Community Survey underscore the point. Median monthly owner costs for homeowners with a mortgage rose to $2,035 in 2024 from $1,960 in 2023, after adjusting for inflation. Census explicitly said the increase was driven primarily by higher mortgage costs and insurance fees. That phrase deserves attention. Insurance is not a side detail. It is part of the mechanism through which homeownership becomes less affordable even before a borrower falls behind.

The same ACS release found median gross rent rose to $1,487 in 2024 from $1,448 a year earlier. Gross rent includes utilities, which makes it a better measure of lived affordability than an advertised base rent. A listing site can show moderation or concessions while the tenant’s actual monthly burden rises because the air conditioning runs constantly, the building is inefficient, or fees and utility pass-throughs keep accumulating. A cooler rent market is not automatically a cheaper housing life.

This distinction helps explain why so many households still report strain even when some headline metrics are moving in a friendlier direction. Housing affordability has become less about the quoted price of entry and more about the carrying cost of staying put.

Rent relief is real, but it is not the whole story

There is genuine evidence of cooling in the rental market. Zillow said the typical U.S. asking rent in March was $1,910, up 1.8 percent from a year earlier, the slowest annual increase since 2020. Zillow also said income growth has been outpacing rent growth nationally, leaving the typical renter household with about $193 more in monthly breathing room than a year earlier. In March, 39.8 percent of rental listings on Zillow offered concessions, roughly tying the highest March share in the company’s series.

For movers, that is meaningful relief. It is one reason the 2026 housing story should not be flattened into pure deterioration. In many metros, the asking-rent surge that defined the early post-pandemic years has cooled materially. Harvard’s Joint Center for Housing Studies likewise reported that professionally managed apartment rents for new leases declined on average late in 2025 while vacancy rates edged higher as supply came online.

But the same Harvard report makes clear why the affordability crisis has not gone away. In 2024, 22.7 million renter households, or 49 percent of all renters, were cost burdened, meaning they spent more than 30 percent of income on rent and utilities. Some 12.1 million were severely burdened, paying more than half their income for housing. Cost burdens have risen over the past five years in 44 states and in 88 of the 100 largest metro areas.

That is the paradox of the current housing economy. Market temperature can cool while household burden remains punishing. Asking rents can rise more slowly than incomes while millions of renters remain stuck on a much higher rung of the staircase than they occupied before the pandemic run-up. The slowdown in new-lease inflation does not undo the earlier reset in housing costs. And because cost burden includes rent and utilities together, a softer leasing market does not eliminate the affordability consequences of higher summer electric bills or older, less efficient buildings.

The numbers point to a housing system in which relief is partial and uneven. New supply helps. Slower rent growth helps. Concessions help. But those gains often improve the market chiefly for households that are moving, bargaining or entering at the margin. They do less for families whose budgets are already structurally tight, whose income has not kept pace, or whose housing-related costs extend far beyond the base rent.

Utilities are no longer a side issue to housing

Utility costs are often treated as an energy story rather than a housing story. For households, that distinction is artificial. A family choosing between homes is also choosing between building efficiency, insulation quality, local climate, rate structures and the age of the cooling and heating systems. A home that looks affordable in spring can become budget-breaking by midsummer if the electricity bill surges.

The Energy Information Administration has warned that retail electricity prices have been rising faster than inflation since 2022 and are expected to keep increasing through 2026. In a January outlook, EIA said the United States is on track for its strongest four-year growth in electricity demand since 2000. A later EIA analysis said electricity load is forecast to rise again in 2026 and 2027, with large computing facilities and industrial expansion among the drivers.

It would be too simple to blame household utility strain on data centers alone. Power prices depend on a wide mix of forces: fuel costs, transmission and distribution spending, weather, regional generation mixes, storm hardening, wildfire mitigation and the long cost recovery process built into utility regulation. But the broader point still matters. The same grid must absorb rising demands and large capital needs, and those system costs do not remain abstract forever. They move, unevenly and with lags, into monthly bills.

That is one reason affordability is becoming more infrastructural. The housing question is no longer just how much a landlord charges or what rate a bank offers. It is also what kind of energy system a household is exposed to. It is whether the unit leaks cold air in winter or cool air in summer. It is whether rapid population growth requires utility investment and whether those costs are spread over customers in ways that raise the baseline price of staying comfortable.

Harvard’s rental-housing report flags this problem directly through the lens of energy insecurity. The report argues that renters, especially lower-income renters, face mounting pressure from climate-related risks and the need for significant investment in resilience and efficiency even as rental assistance fails to keep pace. In practical terms, the issue is not a theoretical debate about decarbonization. It is whether a household that can barely clear the rent can also survive the utility bill that comes with an aging unit in a hotter climate.

That helps explain why energy and housing can no longer be cleanly separated on an economics desk. The bill arrives as one household budget, not as discrete policy silos.

Insurance has moved from background cost to frontline burden

If utilities are the underdiscussed housing expense, insurance may be the expense that can no longer be ignored. For years, homeowners insurance functioned in most public discussion as a relatively boring escrow item. That is no longer a safe assumption. The Treasury Department’s Federal Insurance Office said in January 2025 that homeowners insurance was becoming more costly and harder to obtain as climate-related events placed growing strain on insurers and customers. Treasury’s analysis covered more than 330 insurers and more than 246 million policies from 2018 through 2022, aggregated at the ZIP-code level.

The official finding was notable not just because premiums were rising, but because availability was worsening in riskier places. Consumers in the highest-risk ZIP codes faced nonrenewal rates about 80 percent higher than those in the lowest-risk ZIP codes, according to Treasury. That matters for affordability in a deeper sense than a single premium increase. A household can sometimes stretch to meet a larger bill. It has much less leverage when coverage becomes harder to find at all.

Private-sector estimates differ in method and should be treated carefully, but they reinforce the direction of travel. Bankrate’s April 2026 analysis said a standard homeowners policy with $300,000 in dwelling coverage could cost around $1,000 a year in some states and well above $6,000 in states such as Nebraska and Louisiana. The exact figures will vary by home and policy. The broader point is the same one seen in Treasury’s data: insurance costs are diverging sharply by geography and becoming a much larger budget variable than many households had built into their expectations.

That change is especially consequential for owners who bought when mortgage rates were much lower and are often described as protected by lock-in. They are protected from having to refinance at today’s rates. They are not protected from a new premium notice, a deductible change, a shrinking set of carriers, or a lender’s insurance requirements. An owner with a 3 percent mortgage can still feel financially squeezed if insurance, taxes and utilities keep climbing. In that sense, the low-rate cohort is less insulated than standard market commentary implies.

The idea of lock-in itself is changing. The old version was simple: households did not want to trade a very low mortgage rate for a much higher one. The newer version is broader and more unsettling. Many households may not want to move because they are uncertain what the next property will cost to insure, tax and cool, even if the price or mortgage payment appears tolerable at first glance. Affordability has become more unpredictable, and unpredictability is itself a deterrent to moving.

Why a softer sales market still feels unaffordable

On the surface, the for-sale market offers reasons for optimism. Mortgage rates are below year-ago levels. Inventory has improved in many markets. Redfin’s recent reports have described a buyer’s market in more places than during the pandemic frenzy. Zillow has said affordability for prime homebuying-age renters stabilized in 2024 after two years of sharp deterioration.

Yet actual demand remains subdued. Redfin reported on April 16 that pending home sales fell 4.1 percent from a year earlier during the four weeks ending April 12, the biggest decline in more than a year, while touring activity was much weaker than at the same point in 2025. The Federal Reserve’s April Beige Book gave a similar national impression, saying housing activity softened across several districts as uncertainty and rising mortgage rates damped buyer demand.

The district-level details are revealing. In Richmond, the Fed said residential real estate initially showed signs of a stronger spring market when long-term rates dipped below 6 percent, but increased uncertainty and rates moving back over 6.5 percent wilted buyer optimism. In Philadelphia, business contacts reported continued cost pressure from tariffs, insurance, health care and utilities. In Atlanta, contacts said land costs and property insurance continued to rise even as some construction costs stabilized.

Taken together, those reports suggest that households are reacting not just to financing costs but to the all-in uncertainty of ownership. Buyers know the monthly spreadsheet does not end with principal and interest. Property taxes may reset. Insurance may be repriced. Utility bills may run higher than expected. Repairs remain costly. In a market like that, even some improvement in rates does not automatically restore confidence.

This is one reason a calmer dashboard can coexist with a sour consumer mood. The market may be offering slightly more room to negotiate. It may not be offering much more certainty about what the home will actually cost to carry.

Regional growth does not cancel local cost pressure

The places that build the most housing are often expected to solve affordability fastest. More supply does matter. But recent reporting suggests that supply growth alone does not neutralize insurance, tax and utility burdens. Texas is a useful case study precisely because it has long been treated as a high-growth, high-building counterexample to the constraints seen in coastal markets.

The Texas Tribune reported in January that housing costs in the state have risen faster than incomes, even as educational attainment increased and poverty fell. The Tribune noted that homeowners have seen higher total ownership costs, including insurance and utilities, and that 29 percent of Texas homeowners with a mortgage were spending more than 30 percent of their income on housing as of 2024. More than half of the state’s renters were cost burdened.

That does not mean supply reforms are irrelevant. It means they are not sufficient by themselves. A state can build aggressively and still leave households exposed to rising operating costs once weather risk, utility needs, insurance pricing and local taxation are added to the equation. The economics of occupancy are more complicated than the economics of unit counts.

The same tension appears in metro-level rental trends. Zillow’s national concession data are good news for households able to shop around. But a concession on the first lease term does not guarantee a lower long-run housing burden if the building’s operating costs rise or if those costs are passed through over time. Likewise, modest declines in asking rents for professionally managed apartments do not erase what long-term renters pay when gross rent and utilities remain high relative to income.

The affordability conversation, in other words, has become more local and more systems-based. It runs through state insurance regulation, local tax capacity, utility commissions, weather exposure, drainage and grid investment, building quality and the age of housing stock. Those are harder stories to summarize in a single national metric, which is one reason they are easy to underplay even as households feel them acutely.

Households are still holding up, but the margin is thinner

This is not yet a story of universal household collapse. The New York Fed’s latest household debt report said total household debt rose to $18.8 trillion at the end of 2025. Mortgage balances increased to $13.17 trillion, credit-card balances to $1.28 trillion and auto loans to $1.67 trillion. Those numbers are large, but by themselves they do not prove a generalized payment crisis. Labor-market conditions also remain far from recessionary extremes.

Wage data tell a similarly mixed story. The Bureau of Labor Statistics said real average hourly earnings in March 2026 were up 0.3 percent from a year earlier. That means wage gains have not disappeared. But the cushion is not large. When inflation runs hotter for a month or when a few fixed costs jump at once, a household can quickly feel poorer even if annual wage growth still looks positive on paper.

That is where the cumulative burden matters. Affordability problems do not need to produce immediate delinquency to be economically real. Households often adapt before they miss payments. They delay repairs, shop down on groceries, keep the thermostat higher, postpone moving, carry a balance elsewhere or cut discretionary spending to preserve housing stability.

Some outside-housing data make this point visible. KFF’s March follow-up survey of Affordable Care Act marketplace enrollees found that half of returning enrollees said their health-care costs were a lot higher in 2026 than a year earlier, and 55 percent said they had already cut back or planned to cut back on food or basic household items to cover health-related costs. CMS said the average HealthCare.gov premium after tax credits for the lowest-cost plan in 2026 was projected at $50 per month for eligible enrollees, up $13 from 2025. Health insurance is not a housing bill, but in household budgeting it can crowd out the same discretionary dollars that would otherwise absorb a rent increase, a repair bill or a spike in summer electricity costs.

That interconnectedness is exactly what the Census pulse framework is capturing. Affordability is not experienced in isolated categories. It is experienced as the narrowing or widening of slack.

What this means for policy and for the next housing fight

The political awkwardness of this moment is that the standard affordability tools are better at changing sticker prices than changing the total cost of occupancy. Lower rates would help. More supply would help. Faster income growth would help. But none of those guarantees cheaper insurance in a hurricane-prone county, lower utility rates in a heat-stressed fast-growth corridor, or lighter property-tax pressure in a locality trying to fund roads, schools, drainage and other infrastructure for a larger population.

That does not make those policies unimportant. It makes them incomplete. The next affordability debate is likely to run through a wider set of institutions: insurance regulators, utility commissions, local governments, resilience planners, building-code officials and agencies that shape efficiency retrofits or disaster mitigation. It will also run through the quality of the housing stock itself. A home’s affordability increasingly depends on whether it is cheap to operate, not just cheap to acquire.

For renters, this means that a softening market may still leave gross rent burdens high if utilities remain expensive or if landlords pass through higher operating costs. For owners, it means a fixed mortgage rate no longer guarantees a stable housing bill. For homebuilders and local officials, it means that the economics of location and infrastructure matter more than a simple count of units built. For economists, it means the household-cost story is migrating out of the mortgage series and into a more fragmented landscape of insurance, energy, taxes and resilience.

There are fair cautions here. The biggest national swings in affordability still often begin with rates, rents and prices. Insurance and utility data are less standardized and often laggier than the most cited housing indicators. Geographic variation is large. The total-cost problem is more acute in some states and metros than others. And if mortgage rates were to fall meaningfully from here while rent growth stayed subdued, households would probably feel noticeably better even if insurance and utilities remained elevated.

Still, the direction of the recent evidence is difficult to miss. Census says owner costs rose in part because of insurance fees. Harvard says renter burdens remain near record highs even as market rents cool. EIA says electricity prices have been rising faster than inflation and are expected to keep climbing. Treasury says homeowners insurance is becoming both more expensive and less available in riskier places. The Fed says housing has softened while insurance, health care and related costs continue to rise. These are not identical datasets, but they point in the same direction.

The simplest way to put it is this: the housing bill keeps growing even when the mortgage does not. The market can look calmer while the household feels tighter. Rents may cool, rates may ease a bit and buyers may gain negotiating power, yet the cost of living in a home can still rise through bills that are more local, more infrastructural and less negotiable than the headline housing numbers.

That is the affordability story worth watching now. The crisis has not disappeared. It has become more distributed across the systems that surround a home: the grid, the insurance market, the tax base, the climate risk map and the monthly expenses that families cannot refinance away. For an economics desk trying to read the country from the household up, that is the more revealing measure of strain.

Housing is still about shelter. But it is increasingly also about the cost of the systems that make shelter livable. And that is why the next phase of the affordability fight will be harder to solve with one lever, one headline number or one market thaw.
