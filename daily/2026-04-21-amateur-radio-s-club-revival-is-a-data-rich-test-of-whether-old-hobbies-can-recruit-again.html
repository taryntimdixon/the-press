Amateur radio’s club revival is a data-rich test of whether old hobbies can recruit again

Inside the 2026 push to refresh ham radio clubs, new spectrum rules, youth programs, and the argument over whether the hobby is growing or merely reorganizing.

By The Press • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m.

For a hobby that many outsiders still picture as a basement full of beige boxes and Morse code, amateur radio is having a surprisingly contemporary year. In 2026, the nation’s largest amateur radio association has declared a “Year of the Club,” a campaign built around club newsletters, club websites, club maps, and a renewed emphasis on recruiting, mentoring, and local public service. At the same time, the Federal Communications Commission updated 60-meter amateur privileges, adding a new secondary allocation and new operating rules that briefly jolted a community that pays close attention to spectrum changes because rules are part of the pastime itself. The result is a useful window into a subculture that is both older and more adaptive than its stereotypes suggest. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

The big question is not whether amateur radio still exists. It does. The harder question is what kind of community it has become. Is it a shrinking fraternity of retirees preserving a mid-20th-century identity? Or is it a distributed technical culture, increasingly organized through clubs, school programs, emergency communications groups, digital logging systems, contesting, and portable operating at parks and summits? The evidence supports both answers at once. The hobby’s public face is older and more club-centered than it was a generation ago, but its internal culture has become more networked, more data-driven, and in some corners more experimental. ARRL’s 2026 club campaign itself is a signal that the organization sees clubs as a core organizing structure rather than an afterthought. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

A hobby with rules, licenses, and a surprisingly active calendar

Amateur radio is unusual because the hobby is inseparable from regulation. It is not just about talking to strangers over the air; it is about doing so within a licensed service governed by the FCC’s Part 97 rules, with specific privileges, band plans, power limits, and operating traditions. That gives the hobby a strong institutional spine. In 2026, that spine is visible in the FCC’s updated 60-meter rules, in ARRL’s campaign materials, in club affiliation systems, and in a steady calendar of contests, roundups, operating awards, and special-event stations. The hobby’s culture is part social network, part engineering practice, and part civic ritual. ([docs.fcc.gov](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-18-115A1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That structure matters because amateur radio has always depended on local institutions. Clubs run exam sessions, teach newcomers how to build antennas, explain propagation, and keep people involved long enough to move past the first awkward steps. ARRL’s 2026 campaign explicitly treats clubs as the hobby’s backbone, and it has paired that message with practical tools: a club map, an online club-application system, club update help, and a member-verification workflow designed to help clubs demonstrate value to members and prospective members. The campaign is not just branding. It is infrastructure. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

There is also a public-service argument embedded in the club revival. Amateur radio clubs are often the group you call when a marathon needs communications volunteers, when storms take down cell service, or when a school wants a hands-on STEM demo that is not just another slide deck. That pitch is not new, but in 2026 it is being wrapped in a more modern message: clubs are not merely sentimental relics; they are the on-ramps where technical literacy, emergency readiness, and social belonging overlap. FCC and ARRL materials both continue to frame amateur radio as socially useful, and ARRL’s club campaign explicitly links club vitality to community service and mentoring. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/icom-america-named-official-sponsor-of-the-arrl-year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

Why 2026 matters: a rule change, a club campaign, and a hobby that likes anniversaries

The club campaign alone would be enough to make this a notable year in amateur radio. But the FCC’s 60-meter decision makes it more newsworthy. In late 2025, the commission approved a new secondary amateur allocation at 5351.5-5366.5 kHz and retained the four existing 60-meter channels. ARRL said the new privileges would take effect at 0000 EST on February 13, 2026, and explained that the change followed the WRC-15 worldwide amateur allocation. For most Americans, that is arcana. For hams, it is the kind of arcana that can animate months of discussion because frequency privileges are both technical resource and identity marker. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/new-60-meter-frequencies-available-as-of-february-13?utm_source=openai))

This is one of the reasons amateur radio remains newsworthy as a niche. Very few hobbies can turn a regulatory footnote into a major cultural event. But in ham radio, spectrum changes ripple through operating strategy, portable work, emergency procedures, antenna design, and club presentations. The new 60-meter rules do not transform the hobby. They do, however, show that the service remains alive enough for regulators to update its boundaries and for operators to care deeply about the details. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/new-60-meter-frequencies-available-as-of-february-13?utm_source=openai))

There is a second reason 2026 stands out: the hobby loves anniversaries, and this year is stacked with them. ARRL has tied club-focused programming to the organization’s broader field structure and to America’s semiquincentennial, layering the Year of the Club onto a wider calendar of special operating events and recognition programs. That kind of layered celebration is not just pageantry. It is a strategy for keeping an aging hobby legible to newcomers who may never have heard a shortwave signal but understand gamified goals, badges, maps, and communities of practice. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/files/file/America250/ARRL%20and%20America250%20Press%20Release%20-%205%20Feb%202025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

What the source map says: the hobby is older than average, but not frozen in time

Publicly available counts and club tools show two truths at once. First, amateur radio remains a regulated national service with a large ecosystem of licensees, clubs, and affiliated organizations. Second, many of its public-facing institutions are trying hard to adapt to demographic headwinds. ARRL’s own materials and affiliated club resources show a hobby that understands it must recruit, modernize, and explain itself better than it did before. Its FCC license-count page, updated in March 2026, provides a concrete reminder that the service is measurable, geographically widespread, and still large enough to track state by state. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/fcc-license-counts/?utm_source=openai))

That matters because hobby writing often collapses into nostalgia. There is a familiar story: young people are absent, clubs are fading, and everything was better when people soldered more and scrolled less. The problem is that the record is more mixed. ARRL’s 2026 club efforts would not exist if clubs were irrelevant. School Club Roundup still has scheduled sessions in February and October 2026, which suggests a deliberate attempt to pull students into the service. Local ARRL section pages continue to list club meetings, nets, and operating events. The hobby may be older on average, but it is not static, and it is not only old men in garages. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/school-club-roundup?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the counterevidence is real. Recruitment remains a concern, especially among younger operators, and the very fact that ARRL has branded 2026 around clubs is a sign that institutions see a need to defend the pipeline. The organization’s student coding competition, which ran from January 1 through March 31, 2026, is another clue: ARRL is trying to connect youth participation to hands-on technical work rather than waiting for interest to appear organically. Community forums and surveys are not perfect evidence, but they repeatedly reveal the tension between enthusiasm and aging demographics. The hobby often looks vibrant inside its strongest local clusters while feeling thin in the aggregate. That tension is not unique to amateur radio, but it is particularly sharp here because clubs serve both as social glue and as the main recruitment mechanism. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/student-coding-competition-is-underway-with-25-000-award?utm_source=openai))

What clubs actually do, beyond the stereotype

If the average outsider thinks of amateur radio as solitary, clubs show the opposite. Clubs are where the hobby reproduces itself. They host license classes, coordinate exam sessions, run field days, staff public-service events, manage repeaters, lend gear, and provide the social pressure to keep learning. A club can be the difference between a license that expires quietly and a habit that becomes a lifelong technical identity. ARRL’s club resources make that explicit by organizing clubs not just as social groups but as operational units with maps, affiliation categories, newsletters, websites, and update tools. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/affiliated-club-resources?utm_source=openai))

Clubs also solve a problem that internet communities rarely do well: they create a place where beginners can be confused in public. That sounds small, but in technical hobbies it is decisive. Antenna theory is easier to absorb when a mentor can point at your backyard and say why the wire should go there instead of there. Propagation is less abstract when someone shows you how changing conditions affect the bands during a club net. The social fact of being around other people who care about the same esoterica makes the hobby durable. ARRL’s own club materials and leader resources stress exactly this kind of mentoring and retention. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/affiliated-club-coordinator?utm_source=openai))

Clubs are also where amateur radio intersects with civic life. Emergency-communications groups, ARES-style volunteers, and public-service event crews are often club-adjacent or club-driven. That overlap is one reason local officials and emergency managers still know what a ham club is even if they do not understand the whole hobby. In disasters, redundancy matters. Amateur radio’s value proposition is less that it is fancy than that it is decentralized, locally knowledgeable, and able to operate when normal infrastructure is compromised. FCC materials continue to acknowledge the amateur service’s socially beneficial and emergency-communications roles. ([docs.fcc.gov](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-22-181A1_Rcd.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The new 60-meter allocation: a technical change that exposed old divides

The 60-meter change is a good example of how the hobby’s internal debates work. On paper, the update is modest: a new allocation, some retained channels, a new power limit, and a new effective date. In practice, it becomes a miniature referendum on operating philosophy. Should amateurs prefer channelized simplicity or contiguous spectrum? How should they balance interference concerns against usable bandwidth? How much does a narrow slice of new spectrum matter compared with the broader question of access to HF operating in a crowded communications landscape? The FCC’s rule change answers only the first question, and even then only partially. The rest is left to the community. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/new-60-meter-frequencies-available-as-of-february-13?utm_source=openai))

That is important because the hobby’s best arguments are not really about nostalgia or novelty. They are about utility. The 60-meter allocation appeals to operators who value emergency communications, regional coverage, and a band that can be useful when higher bands are quiet. But some operators will always ask whether small regulatory tweaks move the needle at all in a world where the bigger challenge is participation, not frequency. That skepticism is healthy. It prevents the service from mistaking policy victories for cultural renewal. ARRL’s own 60-meter FAQ underscores the band’s limited spectrum and the need for careful operating behavior. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/60-meter-faq?utm_source=openai))

Still, the rule change has symbolic force. Amateur radio has spent years in a regulatory environment where some of its most visible moments have been about defending old privileges rather than gaining new ones. A new allocation, however small, reminds participants that the service is still negotiated, still evolving, and still part of the broader communications order. That matters to a community that likes to see itself as technically literate and politically relevant. The FCC’s final rule and ARRL’s implementation notes both make clear that this is not just a social-media talking point; it is a formal change in operating conditions. ([docs.fcc.gov](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/FCC-23-26A1_Rcd.pdf?utm_source=openai))

What complicates the rosy version of the story

The main pro-club narrative is easy to love: clubs build community, grow the hobby, teach STEM, and support emergencies. But serious coverage has to make room for the weak spots. One is burnout. Clubs can be welcoming and still be dominated by the same small cadre of volunteers. Another is gatekeeping. Any technical hobby can become harder to enter than it needs to be, especially when jargon accumulates faster than onboarding. A third is geographic inequality: urban clubs, rural clubs, university clubs, and contest clubs do not face the same constraints, and a national campaign can flatten those differences if it is not careful. ARRL’s own affiliation categories make clear how varied club structures already are. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/affiliated-club-resources?utm_source=openai))

Another complication is that “growth” can be defined in several ways. License counts, club memberships, event participation, youth involvement, and on-air activity do not always move together. A hobby can attract more casual participants while failing to retain them. It can have more digital visibility while losing physical gathering places. It can generate enthusiasm around special events and still struggle with the mundane work of mentoring, reimbursement, and keeping repeaters maintained. That is why club-centered reporting is so valuable: it makes the hobby legible at the scale where people actually experience it. ARRL’s FCC license-count reporting gives one picture; its club-update system gives another. Neither is sufficient alone. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/fcc-license-counts/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a broader cultural question. Amateur radio has always sold itself as a technical playground with civic benefits. In 2026, that pitch has to compete with maker culture, online communities, inexpensive SDR gear, satellite apps, mesh networking projects, and all the other ways people can dabble in radio without joining a club. Some of those alternatives are not competitors so much as adjacent worlds. But they do change the expectation of what a hobby should feel like: immediate, searchable, and less dependent on local gatekeepers. Clubs that thrive this year are likely the ones that use that shift instead of resisting it. ARRL’s newsletter and website contests are a tacit acknowledgment that communication style now matters as much as meeting minutes. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/news/arrl-year-of-the-club-newsletter-contest-call-for-submissions?utm_source=openai))

Why the undercovered story is not “ham radio is dying”

The lazy version of the story says amateur radio is a fading relic. That is too simple to be useful. A better frame is that amateur radio is a midlife institution trying to remain legible to the next generation without abandoning the habits that made it resilient in the first place. The hobby’s club structure, its licensing system, its emergency role, and its event calendar are all evidence of continuity. The ARRL’s 2026 campaign, with its maps and contests and club-website push, is evidence of self-conscious adaptation. The FCC’s new 60-meter rules are evidence that the regulatory environment still sees the service as live. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

What is newsworthy here is not simple survival. It is institutional improvisation. Amateur radio is trying to solve a common 21st-century problem: how to keep an analog-era community meaningful when attention, learning, and friendship increasingly arrive through digital channels. The answer may be less about preserving an identity than about making the club itself more portable. In that sense, a ham club in 2026 looks less like a static organization and more like a local node in a broader communications culture. Local section pages and club update systems reinforce that picture: clubs are expected to be active, current, and visible. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/club-update-help?utm_source=openai))

That is why the hobby deserves coverage from a Niche desk. It is not merely quaint. It is an ecosystem where regulation, volunteerism, technology, and friendship overlap in ways that illuminate bigger questions about institutions under strain. If a community built around antennas and frequency bands can modernize its recruitment without losing its soul, it has something to teach other local organizations doing the same kind of work. If it cannot, that failure will also be instructive. Either way, amateur radio is not just surviving in 2026. It is testing itself in public. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

The immediate markers of whether this club revival matters will be boring in the best possible way. Are more clubs updating their information and using the new map? Do the newsletter and website contests surface clubs that have real outreach energy? Does School Club Roundup keep bringing in students? Does the 60-meter rule change create more operating chatter than confusion? Do local sections report healthier attendance and more visible public-service participation by late 2026? These are not glamorous indicators, but they are the right ones. In amateur radio, the health of the hobby is often visible in its habits before it shows up in its headlines. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/club-update-help?utm_source=openai))

If the Year of the Club works, the lesson will not be that old hobbies can be revived by marketing alone. It will be that communities with real technical substance still need institutions that feel human at the local level. Amateur radio has always known that. The question in 2026 is whether the rest of us will notice. ([arrl.org](https://www.arrl.org/year-of-the-club?utm_source=openai))
