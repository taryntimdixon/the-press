Sudan’s war enters a fourth year as a Berlin conference tests whether diplomacy can catch up

As fighting between the Sudanese army and the Rapid Support Forces grinds on, diplomats in Berlin are trying to convert a deepening humanitarian catastrophe into coordinated pressure for a ceasefire.

By The Press • April 17, 2026 • 12:43 p.m.

Sudan’s war has entered its fourth year with no sign that the battlefield or the diplomatic track is moving toward a decisive turning point. On April 15, international officials and aid agencies warned that the conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces has pushed nearly 34 million people into urgent need of assistance, displaced about 14 million, and left large parts of the country dependent on emergency relief. The warnings coincided with a Berlin conference hosted by Germany, the European Union and the African Union that aimed to mobilize aid and press for a ceasefire. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

The conference matters because Sudan’s war has become more than a domestic struggle for power. It is now a regional emergency that is straining neighbors such as Chad, Egypt and South Sudan, while also exposing the limits of outside leverage. The UN said 4.4 million people have crossed borders to escape the fighting, and officials described surrounding states as being at breaking point. That is a geopolitically significant warning: the war is no longer contained within Sudan’s borders, and every month of stalemate increases pressure on the wider Horn of Africa and Sahel corridors. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

A conflict that has outlasted every ceasefire attempt

The war erupted on April 15, 2023, after a power struggle between the army and the RSF collapsed into open combat. In the three years since, neither side has achieved a knockout victory, but both have sustained enough strength to keep the war going, feeding a cycle of drone attacks, artillery strikes and urban fighting that has devastated civilian life. The UN said the crisis is “continuing to deepen with no end in sight,” and aid workers warned that famine conditions have already persisted in parts of the country for two years. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

That endurance is what makes Sudan so difficult for foreign capitals. The conflict is not frozen, but it is not resolving either. Each side has reason to believe it can still improve its position on the ground, especially in a war where territorial gains can be reversed and where the front lines can shift without producing a political settlement. The result is a war of attrition that punishes civilians far more than commanders. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

Recent reporting also underscores how the fighting has become deadlier and more technologically diffuse. Médecins Sans Frontières said this week that it recorded two deaths and treated 56 wounded people after a series of drone attacks in Darfur, a reminder that aerial warfare is now central to the conflict’s civilian toll. Earlier this month, an AP report said a drone strike on a wedding in Darfur killed at least 30 people. These attacks are not just tactical events; they signal the widening reach of the war into areas once thought to be outside the main military contest. ([investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/world-news/drone-attacks-in-sudan-kill-two-injure-56-msf-says-4612832?utm_source=openai))

Berlin’s diplomatic bet

The Berlin meeting brought together a dozen foreign ministers and more than 60 delegations, according to Reuters. Its immediate goal was to mobilize humanitarian aid, but the larger purpose was political: to keep Sudan on the agenda of governments that are increasingly distracted by other wars and crises. That is not a small challenge. Sudan is competing for attention with conflicts in Ukraine and the Middle East, while the humanitarian response still depends on donor fatigue-prone pledges and access that armed actors can deny at will. ([reutersconnect.com](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/international-sudan-conference-in-berlin/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjY6bmV3c21sX01UMUFOQURMMDAwTFNGUFdB))

At the conference, donors pledged more than £1 billion for Sudan, according to The Guardian, with the total described as exceeding the organizers’ target. AP and UN updates also pointed to significant new pledges and renewed calls for access. But the deeper question is whether money can still alter incentives on the ground. Sudan’s experience suggests that humanitarian conferences can relieve immediate suffering without changing the strategic calculations of the combatants. ([theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/global-development/2026/apr/15/more-than-1bn-pledged-for-sudan-as-humanitarian-crisis-deepens?utm_source=openai))

Still, the meeting had geopolitical value. Germany, the EU and the AU are effectively trying to assemble a contact point for a fragmented crisis management effort: donors for the aid pipeline, regional actors for border stability, and multilateral institutions for political pressure. If Berlin produces any shared language on civilian access, prisoner releases or renewed talks, it would at least create a framework for future diplomacy. If not, it will mainly stand as another warning that Sudan’s war is drifting beyond the reach of traditional mediation. ([reutersconnect.com](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/international-sudan-conference-in-berlin/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjY6bmV3c21sX01UMUFOQURMMDAwTFNGUFdB))

The regional spillover is the real strategic danger

The most consequential aspect of the war may be its spillover effects. The UN said millions have fled to neighboring countries, while famine, sexual violence and the collapse of basic services have made Sudan the world’s largest humanitarian crisis. Those are not only humanitarians’ concerns. They are border-security, migration, and state-capacity problems for the region. When millions move, informal markets, armed networks and smuggling routes move with them. When food systems collapse, local authorities in neighboring states face price shocks and pressure on already fragile infrastructure. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

The war’s regional impact also complicates international diplomacy. Neighboring states have their own security calculations, and external powers see Sudan through competing lenses: Red Sea security, mineral access, migration management and influence in Africa’s shifting political map. That makes consensus on pressure tactics hard to sustain. It also means that even a pause in the fighting would not restore stability quickly, because the war has already altered border economies, displaced communities and local power balances. This is why the UN warnings about Chad, Egypt and South Sudan are so important: Sudan is exporting instability, not just suffering. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

The war also lands at a moment when the international system is showing signs of stress elsewhere. The IMF’s April 2026 outlook says Europe’s planned defense buildup will have macroeconomic trade-offs, a sign of how much strategic attention and fiscal space are being absorbed by security competition around the world. Sudan is not the main driver of those trends, but it is part of the same pattern: more states are prioritizing hard security, while conflicts that do not directly threaten major powers struggle to hold the center of the diplomatic stage. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/weo/2026/april/english/ch2.pdf))

What to watch next

For now, the immediate question is whether the Berlin pledges translate into sustained humanitarian access and political follow-through. If donors can keep aid corridors open and preserve pressure for negotiations, they may slow the war’s worst effects even without ending it. If the parties continue to weaponize access and believe battlefield gains remain possible, the conflict is likely to grind on, with more drone warfare, more displacement and more regional destabilization. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns))

Sudan’s tragedy is that the war has become both deeply local and profoundly international. Its combatants are fighting for state power, but the costs are being paid across borders. Berlin did not resolve that contradiction. It did, however, make plain that the world is still trying to catch up with a war that has already remade one of Africa’s most strategically important countries. ([reutersconnect.com](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/international-sudan-conference-in-berlin/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjY6bmV3c21sX01UMUFOQURMMDAwTFNGUFdB))
