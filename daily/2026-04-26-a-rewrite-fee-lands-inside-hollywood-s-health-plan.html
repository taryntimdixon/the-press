A Rewrite Fee Lands Inside Hollywood’s Health Plan

The Writers Guild’s new studio contract avoided another shutdown. Its sharper lesson is that a screenplay deal now turns on medical inflation, streaming residuals, AI licenses and the price of staying insured between jobs.

Written and Researched by AI • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most revealing prop in Hollywood this week is not a clapperboard, a red-carpet backdrop or a giant popcorn tub. It is a contract page with a deeply unromantic phrase on it: page-one rewrite.

In studio language, that phrase does not mean a small polish. It means the writer has been asked to replace all, or substantially all, of an existing screenplay. The old draft is not being touched up. The house is being rebuilt from the slab.

Under the new Writers Guild of America agreement ratified on Friday, that kind of work now has its own minimum: $57,500 for a high-budget feature and $31,500 for a low-budget feature in the first year of the agreement, rising with applicable minimums after that. The contract language also matters in a second way: the minimum applies only when the writer’s contract actually says the writer is being hired for a page-one rewrite. That is a narrow tool, not a revolution. But narrow tools can reveal where the pressure is.

The Writers Guild of America East and West approved the 2026 Minimum Basic Agreement with 90.38 percent voting yes, 4,282 votes in favor and 456 against. The deal runs from May 2, 2026, through May 1, 2030. The tentative agreement arrived on April 4, weeks before the old contract was set to expire, and its ratification spared Hollywood a writers’ strike in a year when SAG-AFTRA and the Directors Guild of America still have their own contract clocks running toward June 30.

That is the easy headline: a shutdown was avoided. The harder story sits beneath it. This contract is not only a labor truce. It is a map of the film business after the streaming boom stopped feeling like a boom. Look closely and the glamorous words fade. Film. Studio. Premiere. Franchise. Auteur. What remains are the ordinary objects that keep a freelance creative life from collapsing: an eligibility threshold, a contribution cap, a residual base, a plan premium, a written notice about artificial-intelligence licensing, a rewrite minimum.

Hollywood still sells stories as magic. The people who make them are now negotiating over plumbing.

The deal that arrived without the thunder

The striking thing about this agreement is not only what it contains. It is how calmly it arrived.

In 2023, the WGA strike lasted 148 days. Actors struck too, and the dual stoppages froze much of the American film and television business. That year taught a broad public lesson in streaming economics: the platforms had changed the way audiences watched, but writers argued that pay, residuals and job stability had not kept pace with the new distribution system. The 2023 deal brought gains in minimums, streaming residuals, staffing terms and artificial-intelligence language. It also left a bruise. Productions were delayed. Workers far beyond writers’ rooms lost income. The industry that returned from the strike was not the same industry that had entered it.

The 2026 talks opened in a more defensive atmosphere. The guild’s own materials framed the bargaining problem around contraction and health-plan pressure, not just around headline wages. The AMPTP, representing the major studios and streamers, described the tentative agreement as part of a path toward long-term stability. The WGA said the deal put the health plan on a more sustainable path, built on 2023 gains, raised minimums, improved residuals and addressed free-work problems.

The free-work language is important because a screenplay is not merely a file on a writer’s laptop. It is also an industrial document. It tells financiers what they might be buying, directors what they might shoot, actors what they might play, production managers what may have to be built and editors what may someday be assembled. A payment rule for screenwriting does not affect only the writer. It affects how honestly a movie can be developed.

If a company can repeatedly extract unpaid revisions, the hidden budget of development moves onto the writer’s rent, child care, savings and patience. If a total rewrite is treated as if it were a small polish, the contract pretends a demolition job is a paint touch-up. That is why the page-one rewrite minimum matters beyond its dollar figure. It is a small attempt to name the work correctly.

It is also a film-specific provision in a contract often discussed through the more familiar vocabulary of television writers’ rooms. The feature business has its own version of instability: long development periods, multiple writers, producer passes, notes from executives and financiers, and projects that can live for years without ever being greenlit. In that system, uncertainty can become a business model. The writer is often the cheapest place to keep uncertainty alive.

The health fund became the plot

The center of the new contract, however, is not the page-one rewrite. It is the Writers’ Guild-Industry Health Fund.

The fund is a portable benefit system. Portable means it is built for people who move from job to job rather than remaining with one employer for decades. That is essential in film and television. A writer may work on a feature, then a limited series, then nothing for months, then a development room, then another gap. The benefit structure has to travel with the worker because the worker’s employment often does not.

The fund’s money comes largely from employer contributions on writer earnings, subject to caps. That last phrase is where the arithmetic becomes important. If a writer earns above a cap on a particular kind of work, the extra earnings do not keep generating health contributions. Some caps had not meaningfully increased for many years even as medical costs and pay patterns changed. The guild’s pre-bargaining bulletin said the screen compensation limit had last increased in 2004 and the overall-deal contribution limit in 2018.

The guild described a double squeeze: fewer jobs because of industry contraction and higher medical costs because of broader health-care inflation. Its February bulletin said plan expenses had exceeded contributions since 2022. It listed operating deficits of $2.3 million in 2022, $90.4 million in the strike year of 2023, $62.6 million in 2024 and $61.9 million in 2025, before investment income. The same bulletin said in-network plan costs had risen 13 percent per year since 2019 and warned that, at the then-current rate, reserves would run out during the next MBA term.

That is the strange arithmetic behind a Hollywood labor deal in 2026. A writer’s health insurance is not funded by applause, reviews or a movie’s cultural afterlife. It depends on enough covered work happening, at enough pay, with enough employer contribution flowing into the fund.

When peak television was expanding, more writers and dependents entered the system. When studios and streamers pulled back, fewer jobs meant less contribution income. At the same time, many writers relied on extended coverage points — banked credits that can help maintain coverage between jobs. Those points are valuable because creative work is uneven. They also cost a health fund money when too many people need to use them at once.

The new agreement raises the employer health contribution rate from 13 percent to 16.25 percent in the first year and then to 16.75 percent in the second year. It also raises several health contribution caps. For screen work, the cap rises from $250,000 to $325,000 in 2026, $375,000 in 2027 and $400,000 in 2028. The WGA says the package is projected to generate $321 million in additional health-fund contributions over the term, including $280 million in new money from the companies. It also includes a one-time $25 million transfer from the Paid Parental Leave Fund to the general Health Fund, while reducing the parental-leave contribution rate.

That is the win side of the ledger. The other side is that writers will pay more too.

Beginning Jan. 1, 2027, active and pre-65 participants in the broad Anthem PPO option will pay monthly premiums of $75 for a single participant, $150 for a participant plus one dependent and $200 for a participant with multiple dependents, with annual increases after that. The health-fund FAQ also lists higher deductibles, higher in-network out-of-pocket maximums and lower in-network coinsurance, moving from the plan covering 85 percent to 80 percent. In July 2027, the earnings threshold to qualify for coverage will rise to 110 percent of the one-hour network primetime story and teleplay minimum, listed by the guild as $53,773.

The guild is also adding a lower-premium Centivo option for active participants and pre-Medicare retirees. That plan has lower listed premiums — $25 for an individual, $50 for an individual plus one dependent and $75 for a family at launch — but is built around a different network and primary-care coordination. The FAQ says the network includes Centivo partners such as UCLA Health and Mount Sinai, and that referrals are required. For some members, that may be a sensible way to preserve lower out-of-pocket costs. For others, especially people with existing specialists, mental-health providers or complex family care, a narrower network is not just a spreadsheet choice. It is an anxiety choice.

This is why the 90 percent yes vote should not be read as 90 percent joy. It was ratification under pressure. The contract brings a historically large employer-side contribution package and protects the fund from a worse immediate crisis. It also accepts higher participant costs in a guild that has long treated strong health coverage as one of the few reliable rewards in an unreliable business.

A better box office does not mean everyone is working

One reason the deal can be misunderstood is that theaters are having a better year.

The 2026 domestic box office opened with its strongest post-pandemic start, helped by titles including Project Hail Mary and The Super Mario Galaxy Movie. April box-office reporting has treated those films as proof that moviegoing still has power when the release calendar gives audiences enough reason to show up. Theater owners have had a more plausible case for optimism than they did in the thinnest post-pandemic stretches.

But box-office recovery and employment recovery are not the same creature. A hit movie can fill auditoriums while the development pipeline remains thin. A studio can profit from a few giant releases while ordering fewer scripts. A streaming company can improve margins by spending less. A theatrical rebound can help exhibitors, distributors and some producers without restoring the broad job volume that supported thousands of writers, crew members and support workers during the peak-TV years.

That is the paradox under the WGA agreement. Hollywood can look busy from the lobby and quiet from the payroll office.

Production data out of Los Angeles remains sobering. FilmLA reported that total on-location filming in Greater Los Angeles fell 16.1 percent in 2025 compared with 2024, to 19,694 shoot days. Otis College’s 2026 creative economy work describes structural shifts in film, television and traditional media that have reshaped employment patterns, with a pronounced impact on Los Angeles County. Federal labor data for motion picture and sound recording also show a smaller workforce than the boom-time industry once implied.

The WGA’s own employment snapshot helps explain why writers approached the health fund as a structural problem. The guild said approximately 37 percent fewer WGA-covered episodic series aired in the shortened 2023-24 season than in the prior season, with 1,319 fewer television writing jobs. For screenwriting, the snapshot said the number of WGA-covered films released was mostly stable through 2024, but screenwriter earnings for the first three quarters of 2024 were down 6 percent from the comparable period in 2022 and the number of screenwriters working was down 15 percent.

Some of the pressure comes from local competition. Georgia, New York, New Jersey, New Mexico, the United Kingdom and other jurisdictions have courted productions with tax credits, facilities and crews. Some of it is the aftermath of the 2023 strikes. Some of it is streaming discipline after years of overspending. Some of it may be more permanent: shorter seasons, fewer episodes, longer pauses between seasons, more global production and more caution around development.

For writers, the cause matters less than the gap. The old Hollywood model was never stable, but it had rhythms: development seasons, broadcast orders, studio slates, cable expansion, then streaming expansion. The current model is less legible. More projects are announced, developed, paused, rethought, sold, merged, shelved or written off. A portable health fund can survive gaps. It cannot thrive if the gaps become the system.

The AI clause is a notice, not a wall

The artificial-intelligence language in the new agreement is narrower than a slogan, which makes it more interesting.

In 2023, the WGA won language providing that generative AI could not be used to write or rewrite literary material under the contract and that AI-generated material could not be treated as source material in a way that undermined a writer’s credit or pay. The guild also preserved its position that the use of writers’ work to train AI may violate rights under the MBA or other law.

The 2026 agreement adds a reporting mechanism. If a company grants a paid license to a non-affiliated third party to use covered literary material to train a public-facing, commercially available generative AI system for creating new outputs, the company must give written notice to the guild. At the guild’s request, the company must meet to discuss the license, including possible remuneration, subject to confidentiality agreements. The agreement also keeps semiannual meetings available for the guild to review information about a company’s use and intended use of generative AI in motion-picture development and production.

That is not a ban. It is not a simple permission slip either. It is a tripwire.

In labor terms, tripwires matter. They create records, meetings and chances to ask for information. In technology terms, they may not be enough. Writers who wanted stronger AI language can reasonably ask what happens after notice. How much will be hidden by confidentiality? What if a company uses an affiliate, a vendor relationship, an internal system or a model that does not fit the exact wording? What happens when training, retrieval, adaptation and internal development tools blur together?

Those are not paranoid questions. They are what happens when a fast-moving technology meets contract language written in paragraphs.

Still, the provision shows where the bargaining frontier is moving. The fight is no longer only whether AI may spit out a script. It is whether past scripts, drafts, treatments and show documents become training material for systems that may later compete with the labor that created them. In the old residual fight, the question was: when a work is reused, who gets paid? In the AI fight, the question becomes: when a work is absorbed into a tool, what counts as use?

Hollywood has spent a century arguing over credits. It may spend the next decade arguing over inputs.

The no votes have a case worth hearing

A contract can be ratified overwhelmingly and still contain real reasons for discomfort.

The strongest argument for the deal is practical. The health fund needed money. The studios were willing to put in a large employer-side contribution package. Another writers’ strike could have further reduced work, damaged the fund and delayed recovery for people far beyond the bargaining table. A four-year contract gives studios and writers a longer planning horizon in a fragile market. It also lets SAG-AFTRA and the DGA negotiate without a simultaneous WGA crisis.

But the no side has serious points.

First, the four-year term means writers will not return to the main table until 2030. In a period of streaming consolidation, AI acceleration and production shrinkage, one extra year can be a long time. A three-year contract is a recurring pressure valve. Four years trades flexibility for stability, and stability is not distributed evenly.

Second, some health-plan changes fall hardest on writers who are already less secure. A premium that looks low compared with the average American employer plan can still hurt a person who qualified one year, missed the next room, spent down savings and is trying to keep a family covered. Raising the eligibility threshold may be financially logical for the fund, but it changes the lived meaning of membership for people near the line.

Third, the deal does not solve the central employment problem. It can improve terms for work that exists. It cannot force studios to order more movies, develop more original scripts or staff more rooms. A better bucket does not make it rain.

Fourth, the WGA West has been dealing with its own staff union strike, an awkward and painful conflict for an organization whose public authority rests on labor solidarity. Staff represented by the Writers Guild Staff Union have said their health coverage was affected during the strike; WGA West confirmed to the Los Angeles Times that month-to-month covered employees were no longer eligible as of April 1. WGA West has said it respects the staff union’s right to strike and disputes allegations of bad-faith bargaining. Whatever the final resolution, the dispute complicates any simple story about Hollywood labor unity.

The ratification vote settled the contract. It did not settle the mood.

Why this belongs on the film page

It is tempting to treat guild contracts as television news because writers’ rooms are easy to picture and streaming residuals dominate the debate. But this is a film story too.

The theatrical feature provisions in the deal go straight to the fragile middle of movie development. The page-one rewrite minimum, the expanded second-step protection and the rule requiring companies to notify producers that only the named people in a writer’s deal memorandum or personal service contract may request rewrites or accept delivery all concern the place where many films are made or quietly die: the script stage.

Second-step protection means that when a company hires a writer to write a first draft, or buys a screenplay from a professional writer, the writer may be guaranteed a paid rewrite step under certain conditions. The new deal expands that protection by raising the compensation ceiling from 200 percent to 225 percent of the applicable minimum. In plainer terms: more screenwriters hired near minimum rates should have a better chance of being paid for the next real round of work.

That matters because screenwriting is often invisible even when it succeeds. The public sees the director, the star, the trailer, the poster, the score. It does not see the abandoned drafts, the replaced writers, the notes calls, the producer passes, the weeks spent solving a second act that may later be credited to someone else. The screenplay is the first architecture of a movie and often the least publicly understood.

When studios make fewer bets, the pressure on that stage intensifies. Development wants more certainty before money is spent. Producers want more versions before greenlight. Executives want a script that can satisfy talent, budget, marketability and risk committees. A writer can become the place where everyone parks risk for the least money.

The new feature language does not end that pattern. It makes some of the risk less free.

The contract as a weather report

Contracts are not predictions, but they are weather reports. They tell you what powerful institutions think is coming.

This one says studios want labor peace, longer planning horizons and fewer shutdown risks. It says writers want to preserve the 2023 gains while rescuing the benefit system that makes freelance work survivable. It says health-care inflation is now a central force in film economics. National data support that broader context: KFF’s 2025 employer survey put average annual family premiums for employer coverage near $27,000, while Mercer projected the highest employer health-benefit cost increase in 15 years for 2026. The WGA version of the problem has its own structure, but it is not occurring in a vacuum.

The agreement also says streaming residuals remain unfinished business. The new contract raises certain high-budget SVOD residual bases, increases the top foreign subscriber factor and improves the performance-metric bonus for high-performing streaming programs beginning in 2027. Those are gains, but they still sit inside a system that depends on formulas, subscriber tiers and data controlled by platforms. Writers have moved the residual fight forward without ending it.

Most of all, the contract says Hollywood’s recovery is uneven. Theaters can be more crowded while writers are less secure. Studios can promise more movies while keeping development lean. A guild can win $321 million in added health-fund contributions and still ask members to pay more. A contract can avert a strike and still feel like a warning.

The old Hollywood myth was that movies were made by dreamers. The newer, harsher myth says they are made by intellectual-property managers and franchise engineers. Both are incomplete. Movies are also made by people who need to see a doctor between jobs.

That is what the health card on the writer’s desk says. That is what the page-one rewrite minimum says. That is what the residual tables say. The future of film is not only whether audiences will leave the couch for a big screen. It is whether the people who build the story can afford to stay in the business long enough to write the next one.

The WGA deal buys time. It does not buy certainty. In Hollywood, that may be the most realistic ending available for now: not fade-out, not cliffhanger, but a stack of revised pages waiting for the next note.
