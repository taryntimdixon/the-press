Philippines deepens South China Sea push with new base and allied drills

Manila is hardening its maritime posture in the disputed South China Sea as it opens a new coast guard command on Thitu Island and prepares for expanded U.S.-led exercises with Japan and other partners.

Written and Researched by AI • April 16, 2026 • 1:22 p.m.

The Philippines is accelerating a two-track strategy in the South China Sea: building harder on-the-ground presence in disputed waters while widening its network of security partners. In the space of a week, Manila opened a major coast guard base on Thitu Island, one of the most sensitive outposts in the Spratly chain, and prepared for a large-scale round of joint military exercises with the United States, Japan and other allies. Together, the moves signal that the Philippines is no longer treating its maritime contest with China as a purely bilateral problem. It is trying to turn the dispute into a regional balancing act.

That shift matters because the South China Sea remains one of Asia’s most dangerous flashpoints. China claims almost the entire waterway, while the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei and Taiwan all press overlapping claims. The area is not only strategically vital for shipping lanes and military access. It is also rich in fisheries and believed to hold significant energy resources. For Manila, the dispute has become a test of sovereignty and deterrence; for Beijing, it is a test of resolve and regional dominance.

The latest Philippine base opening on Thitu Island, also known locally as Pag-asa, is designed to make that contest harder for China to win by attrition. Thitu is already the largest and most important Philippine-held feature in the Spratlys, with a runway, village, port facilities and a small civilian population. By establishing a coast guard district command there, Manila is adding an institutional layer to its presence, not just a military one. The goal is to improve surveillance, law enforcement, search and rescue, and the day-to-day ability to respond to Chinese coast guard and maritime militia activity nearby. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0880f88576273345a9efe850e0dbfef6?utm_source=openai))

Manila’s answer to gray-zone pressure

The Philippine calculation is shaped by years of what officials and analysts often describe as gray-zone pressure: actions below the threshold of war that steadily erode a rival’s position. In the South China Sea, that has included water cannoning, blocking maneuvers, close-quarters encounters, lasers, ramming incidents and the constant shadowing of Philippine vessels by Chinese ships. These are not headline-grabbing battles, but they are the kind of persistent pressure that can gradually normalise control on the water. Manila’s response is to make its own presence more permanent, more visible and more difficult to sideline.

Thitu is especially significant because it sits near Chinese-built installations in the Spratlys, including Subi Reef, which Beijing transformed into a fortified island base with a runway and other military infrastructure. That proximity means Philippine activity there is both symbolic and practical: symbolic, because it defends a foothold; practical, because it gives coast guard and civilian authorities a better platform to monitor events across the surrounding waters. AP reported that the new command is meant to back law enforcement, environmental protection and search and rescue, underscoring that Manila is framing its push as state administration, not militarisation alone. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0880f88576273345a9efe850e0dbfef6?utm_source=openai))

China has not yet issued a major public response to the opening of the Thitu command, but the silence should not be mistaken for indifference. Beijing has consistently objected to moves that strengthen Philippine positions in the disputed sea, whether through construction, resupply operations or closer security ties with outside powers. The broader pattern suggests that every Philippine advance on the ground is likely to invite more Chinese patrols, more presence operations or more diplomatic pushback later. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0880f88576273345a9efe850e0dbfef6?utm_source=openai))

Allies move closer to Manila

Manila is also drawing strength from the fact that it is no longer operating alone. The upcoming Balikatan exercises will involve more than 17,000 American and Filipino personnel and will expand to include Japanese forces, along with smaller contributions from France and Canada. The drills are set to run from April 20 to May 8 and will include a live-fire ship-sinking exercise in waters facing the disputed South China Sea. Japan’s defense chief has been invited to observe the drill, a sign of how much the exercise now functions as a regional message, not just a bilateral training event. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/68894d30572389809694853088695334?utm_source=openai))

This matters because the Philippines has spent years building a wider security architecture to offset China’s maritime advantage. It has signed or deepened military arrangements with partners including Japan, New Zealand and Australia, and it has discussed similar cooperation with Canada and France. The diplomatic logic is straightforward: if China can pressure Manila by exploiting asymmetry, Manila can respond by making that pressure visible to more capitals and by creating a broader coalition of countries with a stake in maintaining maritime law and freedom of navigation. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b695ff83f5021e1569b2245229dfe4d3?utm_source=openai))

For Washington, the stakes go beyond treaty language. The United States has repeatedly signaled that an armed attack on Philippine military forces, public vessels or aircraft, including coast guard units, would activate its mutual defense commitments. That creates a deterrent floor beneath the Philippines’ confrontation with China, even if it does not eliminate the risk of escalation. Manila’s new posture suggests it believes the combination of local fortification and allied backing offers the best chance of holding ground without provoking a crisis it cannot control. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/53af205efe7de5a37237302dd7e093a9?utm_source=openai))

A regional test of resolve

The larger geopolitical question is whether the South China Sea is becoming a model for how smaller states resist pressure from a much larger power. The Philippine approach is a hybrid: physical presence, international law, alliance-building and selective deterrence. It does not seek to match China ship for ship. Instead, it aims to make coercion more costly and to keep each confrontation from becoming a fait accompli. That is a durable strategy only if partners stay engaged and if Manila can sustain the political will and resources required to hold remote outposts over time.

There are also limits. More allied drills and more coast guard infrastructure may reassure Manila, but they also raise the probability of contact at sea. The more crowded the waterway becomes, the more room there is for accidents, miscalculation or a deliberate provocation that gets out of hand. The region has seen that before. A recent AP report noted that a Chinese coast guard and navy operation near Scarborough Shoal was tied to a ship capsizing incident that left people dead and missing, a reminder that even noncombat encounters in these waters can quickly become humanitarian and diplomatic crises. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/7789025da5bde719900279e1b85912d1?utm_source=openai))

Still, the direction of travel is clear. Manila is betting that the best way to preserve its claims is to make them operational: a coast guard command on the ground, stronger logistics, more allied exercises and a steadier international spotlight. In a sea defined by incremental pressure, the Philippines is answering with incremental permanence. Whether that is enough to alter China’s calculations remains uncertain. But it does show that the contest for the South China Sea is entering a more organized, more multinational and possibly more dangerous phase.
