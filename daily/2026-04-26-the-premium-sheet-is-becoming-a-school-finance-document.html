The Premium Sheet Is Becoming a School-Finance Document

California’s new school-healthcare workgroup follows a run of teacher labor fights in which the costly classroom item is not a smart board, a bus or a textbook. It is the promise that the adults in the building can take their families to the doctor without turning a raise into a deduction.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m. • 3416 words

The latest hard thing in public education can fit inside an envelope.

It is not a state test booklet, a suspension notice, a bus route or a college-aid form. It is a health-insurance premium sheet: the paper that asks a school employee to figure out whether a raise is really a raise after coverage for a spouse, a child or a retiree is counted.

That paper has been showing up in places that usually sound more like school stories: picket lines, school-board meetings, substitute plans, family text chains, and the emergency schedules principals assemble when adults are not sure who will be in the building tomorrow morning.

On April 2, California State Superintendent of Public Instruction Tony Thurmond announced a new TK–12 Education Healthcare Cost Workgroup. The Department of Education said the group would bring educators, economists and policy experts together to develop policy solutions for the effect of rising healthcare costs on school districts and educators. The announcement came after the Twin Rivers Unified School District strike in the Sacramento area ended, and the state release explicitly tied the new workgroup to a conflict in which rising healthcare costs had been cited by both labor and management as a significant fiscal stressor.

By itself, a workgroup can sound like the softest possible government response: people in a room, perhaps with binders, listening sessions and careful minutes. But the timing tells a sharper story. Health coverage is no longer a quiet appendix to the teacher contract. In a growing number of school systems, it is one of the main events.

That does not make the story simple. For educators, health coverage is compensation. If a district shifts hundreds of dollars a month in family premiums to employees, a paycheck may appear larger while the household has less money to spend. For districts, health coverage is also a budget line that can rise faster than state funding, faster than enrollment and faster than the public notices that explain why a school is cutting a counselor, a librarian, an aide, a campus supervisor or an after-school hour.

In other words: the doctor bill has walked into homeroom.

The April peg is small. The pressure behind it is not.

California’s new workgroup is not a law, not a strike settlement and not a new pot of money. The Education Department described it as a venue to identify policy solutions and new sources of revenue that could help affected districts offset rising healthcare costs. That wording matters. It recognizes that the issue is not only a dispute between one union and one superintendent. It is a recurring mismatch between the way schools are funded and the way medical premiums move.

The immediate example was Twin Rivers. The district and Twin Rivers United Educators reached a tentative agreement on March 22, and teachers were expected back in classrooms on March 23. Local reporting described a 12-day walkout and a two-year deal that included 7 percent raises over two years, a $4,000 bonus and 100 percent employer-paid Kaiser HMO family healthcare. The district’s own negotiations page also showed how quickly the next fight can grow inside the current settlement: based on an estimated 2026–27 monthly premium of $3,348, Twin Rivers said a 7 to 9 percent increase in 2027–28 would amount to roughly $234 to $301 more per month before any new bargaining over that increase.

That is the strange arithmetic of school benefits. A union can win, a district can reopen classrooms, families can exhale — and the next plan renewal can already be waiting.

Twin Rivers was not alone. In February, San Francisco Unified and United Educators of San Francisco reached a tentative two-year agreement after the city’s first teachers strike in nearly 50 years. The district said the contract included salary increases and fully funded family health benefits at Kaiser rates beginning Jan. 1, 2027, with meaningful relief for eligible employees beginning July 1, 2026. Superintendent Maria Su later emphasized the other half of the story: the district said it would use one-time funds strategically, rely on parcel-tax revenue for family healthcare and leverage reserves while trying to demonstrate long-term sustainability.

That sentence should make readers pause. One-time funds can help a district cross a bridge. They cannot, by themselves, build a permanent road. If a benefit promise lasts longer than the revenue source, the next bargaining cycle may inherit the problem with interest.

Los Angeles Unified offers a different version of the same pressure. In December 2025, the district and labor partners announced an agreement to fully fund employee health benefits through the 2026 and 2027 plan years, covering 100 percent of estimated healthcare premiums for eligible employees, spouses and dependents. In April 2026, the district reached a separate tentative two-year agreement with United Teachers Los Angeles that increased salary scales by 11.65 percent and raised the beginning teacher salary to $77,000. By April 24 and 25, local reports said UTLA and administrators had overwhelmingly ratified their new contracts, while voting was still pending for SEIU Local 99, which represents tens of thousands of classified workers.

Then there is Little Lake City School District in southeast Los Angeles County. Teachers there began striking April 16 over health care, class sizes and student supports. ABC7 reported that union officials said some employees’ monthly costs could rise as high as $1,400 under proposed midyear healthcare changes; the district said it had to balance employee compensation and student supports with fiscal solvency. A district update on April 19 said Little Lake had provided fully funded healthcare for employees and families, but that declining enrollment, exhausted one-time funds and depleted reserves meant it could not continue the practice on the same basis. The district described an 85.5 percent ongoing contribution offer and warned that, absent negotiated changes, the current contract’s default would be a 60/40 split on health-benefit contributions. By late April, local reporting still described the strike as active.

Beyond California, Sheridan School District No. 2 in Colorado showed how fast benefits can become part of strike pressure even when the formal dispute is different. Sheridan educators went on strike April 1 after working without an active contract. The Colorado Sun reported that, during the strike, staff received notice that their health coverage could lapse if employee premium shares were not paid while paychecks were interrupted; district leaders later described the notice as a misunderstanding. CBS Colorado reported April 23 that a tentative agreement had been reached to end what it called the longest Colorado teachers strike in 40 years.

Different states, different labor laws, different district sizes — but the same fragile hinge: when the health plan wobbles, school operations wobble with it.

What counts as pay?

Ask a child what a teacher earns, and the child will probably think of salary. Ask a budget officer, and the answer is salary plus pension contributions, payroll taxes, workers’ compensation, health insurance, retiree health obligations and other benefits. Ask a teacher trying to stay in a high-cost city, and the answer becomes more practical: after rent, childcare, student loans, gas, groceries and medical premiums, can I live here?

This is why benefit fights can look confusing from the outside. A district may say it has offered a raise. A union may say employees are still losing ground. Both can be describing real pieces of the same pay stub.

In California, the pressure is especially visible because many school districts bargain locally while healthcare prices move through regional insurance markets, hospital systems, pharmacy costs and plan design choices that local school boards do not control. The state’s Local Control Funding Formula sends money to districts based largely on attendance, with supplemental and concentration funding for students who are low-income, English learners or in foster care. For 2026–27, the California Department of Education lists the governor’s-budget estimate for the LCFF cost-of-living adjustment at 2.41 percent, with the statutory and funded figures still to be determined later in the budget cycle. That is an increase, but it is not a shield against medical trend. If healthcare premiums rise 6, 7, 9 or more percent, a district can receive more state funding and still feel squeezed.

National health-benefit data help explain why local disputes are multiplying. KFF’s 2025 Employer Health Benefits Survey found that the average annual family premium for employer-sponsored coverage reached $26,993, up 6 percent from 2024, with workers contributing $6,850 on average toward family coverage. KFF’s California Health Benefits Survey, conducted with the California Health Care Foundation, found an average annual family premium of $28,397 in California in 2025, higher than the national figure. Mercer projected a 6.5 percent average increase in total health benefit cost per employee in 2026 even after planned cost-reduction measures; without plan changes, Mercer said the increase would have been nearly 9 percent. The Business Group on Health, surveying large employers, reported that members expected a median 9 percent cost trend for 2026, reduced to 7.6 percent after plan-design changes.

Those numbers are not school-specific, and that matters. A school district is not uniquely bad at buying healthcare just because its premiums rise. It is living inside the same national system as factories, universities, city governments, hospitals and private companies. But schools have one difference that makes the strain more public: when the budget breaks, children notice.

The classroom is not the only place with a staffing shortage.

Health benefits matter partly because schools are trying to hold onto workers in a labor market that is already thin.

The Learning Policy Institute’s 2025 scan of state data estimated that about 1 in 8 teaching positions nationally was either unfilled or filled by a teacher who was not fully certified for the role. The institute cautioned that the figures undercount shortages because states do not report the same data in the same way. Still, the signal is clear enough: many districts are competing for adults at the same time they are trying to contain the cost of those adults’ benefits.

That competition includes teachers, but not only teachers. Schools run on paraeducators, bus drivers, cafeteria workers, counselors, social workers, psychologists, custodians, clerks, nurses, campus aides and substitutes. Many of those workers earn far less than classroom teachers. For a lower-paid school employee, affordable family coverage can be the difference between a school job that is barely workable and one that is impossible.

Los Angeles Unified’s December health-benefits agreement is revealing for that reason. It did not only preserve a benefit for teachers. The district said the agreement covered eligible employees, spouses and dependents, and it described medical, dental and vision coverage for the district’s workforce, retirees and families. UTLA celebrated the agreement as a health-care victory for active and retired employees. In a school system, benefit design is part of the hidden architecture of staffing. If aides, cafeteria workers, campus supervisors or substitutes cannot afford coverage, a district may still have buildings, buses and classrooms — but fewer people to make them work.

The Economic Policy Institute’s 2025 analysis of the teacher pay penalty found that teachers generally receive a larger share of total compensation as benefits than comparable professionals, but said that advantage did not erase the wage penalty. EPI estimated a 17.1 percent total compensation gap for teachers in 2024 after accounting for benefits. The National Council on Teacher Quality, looking at teacher health-insurance costs, found that average public-school employee premium contributions had risen faster than average teacher salaries over a recent period, and that family premiums can place a much larger monthly burden on educators than single coverage does.

Those two organizations often approach teacher compensation from different angles. That is useful. One emphasizes how far teacher pay has fallen behind comparable college-educated workers. The other shows how district-by-district benefit costs can eat into take-home pay in ways salary schedules do not reveal. Together, they point to the same warning: a salary table alone does not tell a family whether a school job pays enough.

Why districts say the math scares them

It is easy to frame every healthcare dispute as a simple fight between workers who need coverage and administrators who do not want to pay. Sometimes the public rhetoric sounds that way. But district officials face a real constraint that unions cannot wish away: they are not supposed to sign contracts they cannot afford.

California’s interim-report rules require districts to identify new labor agreements and new commitments, compare those commitments with projected ongoing revenues and explain how they will be funded in future fiscal years. If salary and benefit negotiations are not finalized, a district must determine the cost of any settlement when it is reached, including salaries, benefits and other cost-changing agreements, and provide the county office of education with an analysis of the settlement’s effect on the operating budget. That is bureaucratic language, but the stakes are practical. A district that cannot meet its obligations can face more intensive oversight, emergency cuts, state loans and a long loss of local control.

Little Lake’s April 19 update put the management argument plainly, even if the union disputes the district’s priorities. The district said declining enrollment had reduced funding, one-time resources had been exhausted and reserves had been spent down. It said rising operating costs were outpacing resources and that legal obligations to maintain fiscal solvency limited what it could promise. The union’s case, as reported by local outlets, focuses on the suddenness and size of proposed healthcare cost shifts, class-size concerns, special-education supports and spending priorities. The conflict is not merely over whether healthcare matters. It is over who should absorb the cost when the bill rises and the enrollment base shrinks.

San Francisco’s settlement carried a similar warning label in softer language. The district described the agreement as a major milestone for dependent healthcare and retention, while also saying it had to remain focused on long-term fiscal stability. That is the central tension. Fully funded dependent coverage may help keep educators in one of the nation’s most expensive housing markets. It also has to be paid for again next year, and the year after that.

Los Angeles Unified, because of its scale, shows another version of the dilemma. The district’s benefits package is unusually comprehensive, but large promises can create large long-term obligations. A 2017 Legislative Analyst’s Office report found that Los Angeles Unified accounted for 9 percent of statewide student attendance but 56 percent of statewide school-district unfunded retiree health-benefit liabilities, based on 2015–16 audit data. The exact liability changes over time with actuarial assumptions, staffing, eligibility rules, prefunding and market conditions. The structural point remains: retiree health promises can sit on a district’s books long after the students taught by those workers have graduated.

That does not mean retiree benefits are wrong. It means they are not free. They are deferred pay, written in the language of actuarial tables.

The healthcare market is bigger than the school board.

One reason these disputes are so frustrating is that the people fighting are often not the people setting the underlying prices.

A school board can choose among plans, negotiate with unions, join purchasing pools, change eligibility rules, adjust deductibles, increase employee contributions or ask voters for local revenue. It cannot, by itself, lower the price of a hospital stay, a specialty drug, a physician-group contract or a regional insurance trend. California has created an Office of Health Care Affordability to slow spending growth across the broader health system. Its statewide target is 3.5 percent for 2025 and 2026, 3.2 percent for 2027 and 2028, and 3 percent for 2029. That is an important attempt to bend the cost curve. It is not the same as immediate premium relief for a district at a bargaining table in April 2026.

This is where the state workgroup could matter if it does more than collect testimony. It could examine whether districts are too fragmented as healthcare purchasers. It could look at regional pools, state aid, stop-loss insurance, dependent coverage, retiree obligations, broker and consultant arrangements, plan design, reserve policy and the special vulnerability of small or shrinking districts. It could also ask an uncomfortable equity question: should a teacher’s family healthcare depend so heavily on the district where that teacher happens to work?

That question cuts both ways. A statewide solution could spread risk and reduce bargaining chaos. It could also create new state costs, reduce local flexibility or lock in benefit promises that some districts cannot sustain without new revenue. If the state pays more, taxpayers pay more. If employees pay more, school workers pay more. If districts pay more without new money, students may pay through fewer staff and services. The bill does not vanish. It changes hands.

What families see first is disruption.

Families usually meet this issue at the worst possible moment: the night before a strike, when a district sends an alert about whether schools will be open.

That is understandable. Parents and guardians need to know who will watch their children, whether special services will run, whether meals will be served, whether a teenager should report to campus, whether a kindergarten student will see a familiar adult at the door. Labor economics can sound distant until it becomes Tuesday childcare.

But treating strikes only as disruptions misses what they are warning about. When educators strike over health coverage, they are often saying the school system has made a promise it can no longer keep in a way that feels fair. When districts resist, they are often saying the promise is outrunning the budget. The family at home may not care which side has the better chart. The family needs school. That is exactly why the issue matters.

The pandemic years left many students needing more academic help, more mental-health support and more stable adult relationships in school. At the same time, federal relief dollars have expired, enrollment has declined in many districts, attendance patterns have shifted, and costs for transportation, utilities, construction, insurance and labor have climbed. A healthcare fight is not separate from those pressures. It is one of the places where they meet.

Three things to watch next

First, watch whether California’s workgroup defines the problem narrowly or broadly. A narrow version asks how to help districts pay premiums next year. A broader version asks why school districts are such vulnerable healthcare purchasers in the first place, and whether benefit design, state funding and health-cost regulation should be discussed together.

Second, watch smaller districts. Los Angeles Unified can draw headlines because of its size. San Francisco can draw attention because of its politics and cost of living. But smaller districts may have less room for error. A few hundred students lost to enrollment decline can matter. A premium jump can consume the money that was supposed to pay for a counselor, speech therapist, intervention teacher or class-size reduction. Little Lake’s strike shows that healthcare conflict is not confined to giant systems.

Third, watch the difference between active employees and retirees. Current employee premiums are the visible fight. Retiree health obligations are the basement machinery. They do not always make noise, but they shape how much room a district has. Any serious solution has to distinguish between paying this year’s premium, prefunding future promises and deciding what new employees are being promised today.

The envelope is not going away.

There is a tempting fantasy in education politics that every hard problem has one villain: wasteful administrators, unreasonable unions, stingy taxpayers, expensive consultants, greedy insurers, falling enrollment, state formulas, federal neglect, local mismanagement. Sometimes one of those is the main problem. Usually, several are in the room.

Healthcare costs are especially resistant to simple blame. Teachers did not invent premium inflation. School boards did not invent specialty-drug prices. Families did not choose to make schools the place where the country’s labor-market, childcare and health-insurance problems collide. Yet public schools are where the collision becomes visible.

The image is not dramatic: an employee at a staff-room table, checking a benefits packet before the first bell. But that quiet moment now carries a public question. If society wants schools staffed by stable adults, and if health insurance remains tied to jobs, then school finance is also healthcare finance. The promise on the premium sheet is part of the promise of school.

California’s workgroup may produce proposals, or it may produce another stack of careful language. Districts and unions will keep bargaining either way. But the story has already moved. The healthcare line is no longer hiding at the back of the budget book. It is sitting in the classroom, taking attendance.
