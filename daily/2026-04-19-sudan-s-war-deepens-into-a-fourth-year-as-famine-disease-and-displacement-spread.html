Sudan’s war deepens into a fourth year as famine, disease and displacement spread

A relentless conflict between Sudan’s army and the RSF has become the world’s largest displacement and hunger crisis, with aid access worsening and outside powers still failing to force a ceasefire.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 2:26 p.m.

Sudan’s war has entered its fourth year with no clear path to peace, and the consequences are becoming more severe by the week. The fighting between the Sudanese military and the Rapid Support Forces, or RSF, has displaced about 13 million people, pushed parts of the country into famine, and left millions more reliant on aid that is increasingly difficult to deliver. As international attention has shifted to newer crises in the Middle East, Sudan’s devastation has continued largely unchecked. The war is now widely described by aid agencies and officials as the world’s largest humanitarian emergency. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

The crisis is not only about the front lines. It is also about the collapse of services that most civilians depend on to survive. The United Nations says about 34 million people, or nearly two out of three Sudanese, need assistance, while health facilities continue to struggle under pressure from displacement, shortages and disease outbreaks. The World Health Organization says only 63 percent of health facilities remain fully or partially functional. In many places, the basic machinery of daily life has broken down alongside the fighting. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

A war that keeps spreading its damage

The conflict began in April 2023 as a power struggle between the army and the RSF, a paramilitary force with roots in the Janjaweed militias that terrorized Darfur two decades ago. Since then, the war has ravaged large parts of the country, particularly Darfur and areas around Khartoum, and has become increasingly difficult to contain. AP reported this week that Sudan is now entering its fourth year of war as famine, massacres and shortages deepen. The report also noted that the fighting has driven a humanitarian catastrophe and that outside diplomatic efforts have repeatedly stalled. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

The human toll is staggering. AP said at least 59,000 people have been killed, though the true figure is likely higher because of the scale of the fighting and the difficulty of verifying deaths in besieged areas. More than 11,000 people are missing, according to the Red Cross. In October, at least 6,000 people died over three days in el-Fasher when the RSF swept through the city’s outskirts, an assault that U.N.-backed experts said carried the defining characteristics of genocide. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

Violence has also spread through the economy and supply chains. Food prices remain elevated, transport routes are unsafe, and aid convoys face delays or denial of access. The result is a conflict that is no longer measured only in territorial gains and losses, but in the depletion of every resource civilians need to stay alive. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

Famine and disease are now part of the battlefield

Sudan’s hunger crisis has reached catastrophic levels in several areas, and the outlook is worsening. The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification, the world’s leading food-security monitor, said in February that the number of people with severe acute malnutrition is expected to rise to 800,000. UNICEF, the Food and Agriculture Organization and the World Food Programme warned in March that an additional 420,000 people in southern governorates could fall into crisis-level food insecurity or worse between September 2025 and February 2026 if help does not arrive at scale. The agencies said delayed and insufficient rainfall in April has also threatened this year’s planting season. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

That warning matters because Sudan’s hunger emergency is not limited to isolated pockets. The U.N. says about 18.3 million people are acutely food insecure across Yemen, and Sudan faces a similarly severe humanitarian map in which access, price shocks and displacement reinforce each other. In Sudan, the lack of food is intertwined with disease outbreaks, especially cholera, and with the collapse of health care. When treatment centers run short of medicine or fuel, children with malnutrition and patients with preventable disease have fewer and fewer options. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

The war’s impact has been especially brutal in areas where civilians were already vulnerable. Displaced families crowd into makeshift shelters, often with little sanitation and unreliable water. Health workers in Port Sudan, one of the relative safe havens on the Red Sea, have described the grim work of weighing crying babies and feeding them through tubes as malnutrition rises. The imagery is repeated across the country: war has become a force that destroys not only homes but the possibility of recovery. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

Diplomacy is lagging behind the emergency

The international response has struggled to keep pace. AP reported that attempts by the United States and regional powers to secure a ceasefire have failed, with attention now further distracted by the war involving Iran and other crises in the region. Meanwhile, the U.S. imposed sanctions on three people and two companies on Friday over allegations that they were recruiting and deploying Colombian mercenaries to fight with the RSF, underscoring how the conflict has drawn in outside actors and private networks. The Treasury said the sanctioned group was helping the RSF bring in former Colombian military personnel. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

That external dimension has made the conflict harder to resolve. AP noted that regional powers have backed different sides behind the scenes, while local fighting has become entangled with wider rivalries. The result is a war in which neither battlefield advances nor sanctions have yet produced decisive pressure toward peace. For civilians, the diplomatic stalemate has a direct cost: every week without a settlement means more hunger, more displacement and more disease. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))

Sudan’s crisis is also a test of global attention. As new emergencies emerge elsewhere, aid groups warn that the country can no longer be treated as a forgotten war. The numbers alone are overwhelming: 13 million displaced, tens of thousands dead, millions hungry, and a health system under sustained strain. But the deeper danger is that the emergency is normalizing. The longer the war drags on, the more Sudan’s suffering risks becoming a permanent condition rather than a crisis that the world still feels obliged to solve. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b))
