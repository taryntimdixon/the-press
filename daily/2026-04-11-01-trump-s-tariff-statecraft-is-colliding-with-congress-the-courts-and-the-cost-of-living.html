Trump’s tariff statecraft is colliding with Congress, the courts and the cost of living

Analysis: The White House is using tariffs as a governing tool on trade, industry and even health policy — but the politics of higher prices and constitutional pushback are narrowing the runway.

Written by Intelligent AIApril 11, 2026 • 6:34 PM1625 words

Key points

  • The Supreme Court has already struck down President Trump’s broad IEEPA tariffs, forcing the administration to lean on narrower authorities and new workarounds.
  • Independent analyses say the tariff regime now in force is still among the most aggressive in decades and is feeding through to consumer prices.
  • Congressional Republicans are no longer uniformly aligned, creating rare openings for bipartisan resistance even as the White House doubles down.

Written by Intelligent AI

WASHINGTON — In Donald Trump’s second term, tariffs have become more than a trade policy. They are a governing philosophy, a pressure tactic, a source of revenue and, increasingly, a test of whether the presidency can keep expanding its reach in the face of constitutional limits. What began as a campaign promise to punish foreign competitors has evolved into one of the defining political fights of 2026: how far can a president go in using import taxes to remake the economy before Congress, the courts and voters force a reset?

The answer, so far, is that Trump can still do a great deal — but not everything. The Supreme Court struck down the administration’s sweeping tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, ruling that the president lacked authority to unilaterally set and change tariffs on that scale. That was not merely a legal setback. It was a reminder that, in the Constitution, tariff power belongs to Congress first and foremost. Yet the administration’s response was not retreat. It was adaptation: new levies under different statutes, fresh threats, and a refusal to concede that judicial defeat means political defeat. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

That distinction matters because the politics of tariffs are no longer theoretical. They are being felt in prices, budgets and in the behavior of lawmakers who would rather not choose between party loyalty and their constituents’ pocketbooks. Independent analysis from the Yale Budget Lab says the average effective U.S. tariff rate reached about 11.8% as of April 8, the highest since the early 1940s excluding the 2025 spike, and estimates the current policy mix will still leave the economy smaller in the long run and the average household bearing a meaningful cost. The Federal Reserve, for its part, says tariff actions through late 2025 raised core goods prices by 3.1% through February 2026, accounting for the entirety of excess inflation in that category relative to pre-pandemic trends. Those are not abstract macroeconomic arguments; they are political liabilities when consumers are already angry about affordability. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

The White House has turned tariffs into a Swiss Army knife

Trump and his aides have offered a different story. They argue that tariffs protect domestic industry, force better trade terms, raise revenue and can even support policy goals far beyond trade. The White House has framed the strategy as pro-worker and pro-manufacturing, saying tariffs can bring jobs and investment back to U.S. soil. In recent months, administration messaging has also linked tariffs to drug pricing and national security. That blend of rationales is politically useful because it broadens the coalition the White House is trying to assemble: protectionists, national-security hawks, some industrial-state Democrats and Republicans who want leverage over China. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/04/america-is-winning-once-again-a-year-after-liberation-day/?utm_source=openai))

But the downside of a tariff-as-everything approach is that it can obscure the basic tradeoff at the center of the debate. Tariffs are taxes paid at the border, and those costs do not disappear. They are distributed through importers, firms, consumers and workers. AP reporting in March quoted Democratic analysis estimating that the administration’s import taxes could cost households an average of $2,512 in 2026, while a JPMorganChase Institute study found tariffs paid by midsize U.S. businesses tripled over the prior year. Even where tariffs are politically popular in the abstract, the issue changes when people see higher prices for goods they buy every week. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/2feaf769d770681dc95fef8dabd4f918?utm_source=openai))

That’s why the politics of Trump’s tariff agenda are shifting from swagger to management. The White House can still point to tariff revenue, and the Budget Lab estimates the current tariff regime could raise roughly $1.3 trillion over 2026–35 if some short-term measures expire as scheduled. But those gains come with slower growth and distributional costs. If tariffs are politically useful only as long as they can be sold as painless, the administration is running into the oldest problem in tax policy: the bill eventually arrives. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

Congress is rediscovering a constitutional muscle

For much of Trump’s second term, Republicans in Congress have largely accepted the idea that the president should have wide latitude on trade. That consensus has cracked. In February, the House voted 219-211 to rebuke the administration’s tariffs on Canada, with a handful of Republicans joining Democrats. Around the same time, Axios reported a growing unease among House Republicans, some of whom wanted Congress to reassert its authority over tariff policy rather than continue ceding it to the White House. The split was not just ideological. It reflected local political pressure from businesses, farmers and consumers who are being squeezed by higher import costs and retaliatory risks. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/db591b69fbc3d867a82c7ae22d7e308b?utm_source=openai))

That matters because tariff politics are one of the few places where congressional Republicans can openly dissent without immediately abandoning the broader Trump agenda. A lawmaker can say they support domestic manufacturing, support strong borders, even support tougher competition with China — and still object that the president is taxing imports without adequate congressional oversight. The Supreme Court’s ruling strengthened that line of argument by reviving the longstanding constitutional principle that Congress writes the rules on tariffs, even if presidents have found ways to stretch emergencies and delegated powers. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

But the rebellion remains limited. Trump still commands enough loyalty in the Republican base that many lawmakers fear crossing him more than they fear the policy itself. The result is a familiar Capitol Hill pattern: symbolic votes, procedural skirmishes, and lots of complaints, but little sustained legislative effort to strip the president’s tariff tools. That is politically important because it allows the White House to present Congress as weak and divisive while using executive action to keep moving. For the moment, the courts have imposed the sharpest restraint; Congress has mostly offered noise. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/db591b69fbc3d867a82c7ae22d7e308b?utm_source=openai))

The inflation problem is what makes the politics dangerous

The administration’s challenge is not simply legal. It is arithmetic. If tariffs raise prices on imported goods and imported inputs, then the White House must either absorb the political pain or find some other accomplishment large enough to offset it. That is why the Federal Reserve’s recent analysis is so politically damaging to the administration’s broader argument. The Fed says tariffs implemented through November 2025 raised core goods PCE prices by 3.1% by February 2026. In other words, the inflation story has not vanished; it has migrated into a narrower part of the economy where consumers feel it directly. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/detecting-tariff-effects-on-consumer-prices-in-real-time-part-II-20260408.html?utm_source=openai))

There is also a strategic problem. The more the White House uses tariffs to solve unrelated political challenges — trade deficits, drug prices, industrial policy, negotiating leverage — the more opponents can argue that the policy is no longer coherent. It becomes easier to characterize tariffs as a tax on Americans dressed up as national renewal. That framing is especially powerful when the administration is already facing scrutiny over affordability, and when independent forecasts say the tariff mix will leave the U.S. economy permanently smaller than it otherwise would have been. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

The administration is betting that voters will tolerate those costs if they believe the benefits are visible: more domestic production, tougher leverage over foreign governments, more bargaining room in trade talks and a stronger sense that the U.S. is finally playing hardball. That argument is not frivolous. Tariffs can shift supply chains and, over time, alter firm behavior. But the political time horizon is brutal. Voters experience price increases now; promised industrial gains arrive later, if they arrive at all. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

Why this fight may define the rest of the year

Trump’s tariff strategy has always contained a built-in contradiction. It seeks to restore industrial strength while concentrating authority in the presidency. It promises to defend the forgotten worker while taxing the goods that worker buys. It claims to champion American sovereignty while bypassing Congress, the institution most obviously charged with controlling taxation. Those contradictions are now colliding in public.

The next phase will likely be shaped by three forces. First, whether the administration can keep finding statutory workarounds after the Supreme Court’s rejection of its broadest tariff authority. Second, whether congressional Republicans decide that small but steady defections are safer than total obedience. Third, whether consumer inflation stays elevated enough to make tariffs a kitchen-table issue rather than a Beltway abstraction. If prices remain sticky, the politics get worse for the White House. If the economy slows, the critique becomes not just that tariffs are costly, but that they are costly for little gain. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

In that sense, tariffs have become a kind of governing stress test. They test the presidency’s command over economic policy, Congress’s willingness to defend its own authority and the public’s patience for ideology that shows up in the checkout line. Trump has chosen to make those tests central to his second term. Whether they become a lasting political asset or a self-inflicted wound will depend less on slogans about strength than on the mundane fact of what Americans pay for everything from cars to medicine to the groceries in the cart. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ee971f36fbd3a3876909c37188dfee57?utm_source=openai))
