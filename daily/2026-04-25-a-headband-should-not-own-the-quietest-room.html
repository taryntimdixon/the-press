Philosophy

A Headband Should Not Own the Quietest Room

Neural privacy is being written into law while the technology is still awkward, partial and medically promising. That is exactly the moment to decide that mental life is not just another data stream.

By The Press • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The fight over neural privacy has arrived before the miracle and before the catastrophe. That is the strange advantage of spring 2026. Most consumer headbands cannot read a private confession. A wrist device that senses muscle activity is not telepathy. Even the most impressive laboratory language decoders depend on cooperation, training data, specialized equipment, clinical context, implanted arrays or bulky imaging systems. The science is powerful, but it is not magic. And yet a politics of the mind is already being drafted in state capitols, international agencies, company labs, court opinions and congressional proposals because the moral boundary at stake does not begin at perfect mind reading. It begins when institutions decide that signals from the nervous system are ordinary inputs to be collected, scored, retained, inferred from, combined with other records and monetized.

The thesis should be plain: neural data deserves special legal and ethical treatment not because every brain sensor can expose a complete thought, but because it creates a new kind of power over persons. It gives companies, employers, insurers, schools, governments and medical vendors a claim on the pre-behavioral zone where attention, intention, fatigue, emotion, motor planning, disability and self-command are beginning to form. The old privacy bargain was already weak when it covered web clicks, location trails, faceprints, health apps and search histories. It becomes morally absurd when applied to the nervous system. A consent pop-up is not an adequate social contract for the quietest room a person has.

That does not mean neurotechnology should be greeted with panic. The strongest counterargument is not only plausible; it is ethically urgent. Brain-computer interfaces can return communication to people with ALS, paralysis, stroke and other severe disabilities. Implanted systems have helped research participants type, move cursors, control devices and synthesize speech. Noninvasive devices may broaden access to assistive communication, rehabilitation and daily independence. A society that treats every neural interface as a threat risks turning protection into paternalism, especially toward disabled people who have the most to gain from devices that translate intention into action. The problem is not that machines can listen to neural activity. The problem is that the legal order has not decided who gets to benefit from that listening, who may profit from it, who may refuse it, and what uses should remain off limits even when a contract says otherwise.

The law is arriving in pieces

The legal map is no longer theoretical. Colorado became the first U.S. state to put neural data directly inside consumer privacy law when Gov. Jared Polis signed HB24-1058 on April 17, 2024. The measure took effect on August 7, 2024, and added neural data to Colorado’s sensitive-data category as part of a broader definition of biological data. California followed with SB 1223, approved on September 28, 2024, adding neural data to sensitive personal information under the California Consumer Privacy Act. Montana amended its genetic-information privacy law in 2025 to cover neurotechnology data. Connecticut’s 2025 amendments to the Connecticut Data Privacy Act, effective July 1, 2026, add neural data to the state’s sensitive-data definition. By April 2026, the early U.S. pattern was clear: rather than create one freestanding mental-privacy code, states were mostly grafting neural data onto existing privacy structures.

That patchwork matters because it shows a rare regulatory instinct: lawmakers are trying to act before the market becomes too embedded to govern. Usually, privacy law arrives after extraction has been normalized. Search histories, location records, voiceprints, period-tracking data and online mental-health questionnaires all became commercially useful before the public had a serious chance to decide which uses were unacceptable. Neural data law is different in timing, if not yet in coherence. Legislatures are seeing the category form in real time.

The state bills also show why definitions are already doing moral work. Colorado and California include central and peripheral nervous-system activity. Connecticut’s enacted amendment defines neural data more narrowly as information generated by measuring activity of the central nervous system. Montana places neurotechnology data inside a genetic-privacy framework. Alabama’s 2026 HB263, which passed the House and reached the Senate calendar before the regular session ended, would have targeted health and fitness apps that disclose, transfer or use biological or neural data without express consent. Vermont’s H.791 proposal defines neural data for government data practices as information generated by measuring central or peripheral nervous-system activity and not inferred from nonneural information. Minnesota’s SF1240 proposal goes further into the language of neurodata rights and manipulative uses of neurotechnology. These measures do not agree on scope, enforcement or institutional setting. Some speak to consumer businesses, some to government entities, some to health and fitness apps, and some to broader rights. The disagreement is not a footnote. It is the substance of the debate.

At the federal level, the Management of Individuals’ Neural Data Act of 2025, or MIND Act, would not itself create a full national neural-privacy regime. It would direct the Federal Trade Commission to study neural data governance, current data-protection and cybersecurity requirements, gaps outside existing federal standards such as HIPAA, procurement guidance and possible oversight categories. That is modest. It is also revealing. Congress is not yet prepared to say exactly what neural data is owed, but it is prepared to say that the old categories may be inadequate. A 2025 Senate Commerce request to the FTC made the same basic point more directly, warning that brain-computer interface technologies could expose mental health conditions, emotional states and cognitive patterns and asking the agency to scrutinize deceptive or unfair practices in the sector.

Internationally, the frame has moved faster. UNESCO’s Recommendation on the Ethics of Neurotechnology, adopted at the organization’s 43rd General Conference in November 2025, does not stop at direct neural recordings. It also addresses indirect neural data and non-neural data capable of supporting mental-state inferences. That expansion is important. The ethical threat is not limited to an electrode on the scalp or an implant in motor cortex. A future system could infer fatigue, attention, susceptibility, stress, depression risk or intention from combinations of eye movement, muscle activity, voice, sleep, location, purchase behavior and neural signals. If lawmakers define the protected zone too narrowly, companies will learn to stand just outside it.

The danger is not a mind-reading helmet

Public argument about neurotechnology often gets trapped between two bad images. One image is the science-fiction helmet that extracts sentences from an unwilling brain. The other is the skeptical shrug that says consumer EEG is too noisy to matter. Both images are misleading. The present danger is not that a meditation headband can produce a transcript of the soul. The danger is that nervous-system data may become one more commercial input in a system already skilled at turning weak signals into consequential classifications.

Consider the difference between knowing and sorting. A company does not need to know exactly what a person thinks to sort that person into a profitable category. Online advertising did not require perfect knowledge of the self. It required enough probabilistic inference to decide who might be anxious, lonely, impulsive, pregnant, ill, persuadable, price sensitive or likely to churn. Employers do not need a window into the soul to misuse attention scores. Insurers do not need a perfect diagnosis to be tempted by risk signals. Schools do not need certainty to label a child distracted. Police agencies do not need metaphysical truth to build suspicion from behavioral indicators. The history of data power is a history of institutions acting on inferences they cannot fully justify.

Neural data intensifies that pattern because of where it comes from and what it gestures toward. A pulse rate or step count is intimate, but a brainwave, a motor-neuron signal or a speech-attempt signal carries a different social meaning. It appears closer to agency. It invites the claim that the device is not merely observing what a person does but measuring the preparation for doing, attending, feeling or intending. That claim may be exaggerated technically and still powerful socially. Once a metric is accepted as a proxy for focus, emotional response, cognitive load, credibility, alertness or engagement, the proxy can shape judgment even when the science is partial.

This is why the most responsible sentence in the debate is also the least theatrical: neural data is not necessarily thought, but it can become evidence about a person in systems that have power over that person. The moral issue is not only secrecy. It is authority. Who is allowed to turn a signal from my nervous system into a claim about my mind? Under what conditions may that claim be used against me? Can I contest it? Can I demand that it be deleted? Can I refuse collection without losing work, insurance, schooling, care or access to a device that lets me communicate?

Medicine proves the stakes are real

The counterargument deserves more than a paragraph because it is the reason simplistic bans would be cruel. Clinical brain-computer interfaces are among the most morally compelling technologies now being developed. Neuralink’s PRIME trial, registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, is a first-in-human early feasibility study of an implanted brain-computer interface and surgical robot for people with tetraparesis or tetraplegia. Neuralink’s separate VOICE study, first posted in November 2025, is aimed at communication restoration for people with severe and irreversible speech production impairment. BrainGate investigators reported in March 2026 that an investigational implanted BCI typing neuroprosthesis enabled rapid communication for two research participants, one with ALS and one with a spinal cord injury. NIH has highlighted work translating brain activity into audible words for people who cannot speak naturally. Synchron is testing an endovascular BCI intended to let people with limited hand mobility control digital devices. Paradromics announced FDA authorization in November 2025 for an early feasibility study of a fully implantable BCI for speech restoration and computer control in people with severe motor impairment.

These examples are not hype in the ordinary consumer-tech sense. For a person with ALS who is losing speech, the difference between a slow spelling board and a faster neural communication system can be the difference between being present in family life and being trapped behind an interface too slow for ordinary conversation. For a person with high spinal cord injury, cursor control, device control or speech synthesis can mean autonomy. To call all neural data collection exploitative would erase the agency of people who may rationally choose surgery, training sessions, data sharing and device risk because the alternative is silence or dependence.

That point should discipline privacy advocates. A right to mental privacy must not become a right of the able-bodied to feel pure while disabled people wait. Regulation should distinguish clinical uses governed by safety, medical ethics, informed consent, continuing support and research oversight from consumer or workplace uses sold as wellness, productivity, entertainment or convenience. It should also recognize that medical-device review is not the same as a complete data-rights regime. A device can be clinically promising and still raise questions about secondary use, long-term support, model training, corporate acquisition, cybersecurity, research access, insurance coverage and the rights of participants whose neural data helped build the system.

The best ethical framework therefore begins from dual obligations: speed help to people who need communication and mobility, and slow the conversion of neural traces into ordinary commercial property. Those duties are not opposites. Trustworthy rules may be the condition of durable medical adoption. Patients and families will be more willing to consider invasive or intimate technology if they know that the data it produces cannot quietly migrate into advertising, employment scoring, insurance underwriting or opaque model training.

Consent is necessary and insufficient

The standard privacy solution is consent: disclose the practice, obtain permission, allow deletion or opt out. Consent is necessary, especially for devices that touch the nervous system. But treating consent as the whole answer misunderstands both the nervous system and the modern data economy.

First, consent collapses under dependency. A paralyzed person who needs a BCI to speak may not have meaningful bargaining power over data terms. A worker offered an attention-monitoring headset by an employer may technically be free to decline, but the refusal may carry a cost. A student in a school experiment, a gamer inside a platform ecosystem, a patient in a rehabilitation program or a driver whose car interface depends on a neuromotor band may face a choice structured by someone else. The more useful the device becomes, the less voluntary the terms may be.

Second, consent is brittle across time. Neural data may be collected for one purpose and become valuable for another after models improve. A raw recording that seems minimally revealing today may be more revealing tomorrow when combined with better algorithms, larger training sets or other biometric data. A person cannot meaningfully consent in 2026 to all future inferences that may be drawn in 2036. That is not a defect of individual judgment. It is a structural feature of data whose meaning changes with analytic capacity.

Third, consent does not settle justice. Suppose a company obtains clear permission to use neural responses from low-income users in exchange for discounted devices, then trains models sold to wealthier users as cognitive enhancement. Even if every click is valid, the arrangement may still be exploitative. Suppose an employer obtains written consent to monitor fatigue in warehouse workers and promises safety benefits, then uses the same data to discipline workers who appear inattentive. The ethical question is not only whether consent occurred. It is whether the practice creates domination, discrimination or unfair extraction.

Fourth, consent does not solve the problem of inferred mental states. A law that protects only measured neural data may leave unprotected the mental-state inference produced by combining neural data with eye tracking, EMG, sleep metrics, keystrokes and location. UNESCO’s inclusion of indirect neural and non-neural data capable of mental-state inference points toward the harder truth: the protected interest is not merely the raw signal. It is the person’s claim not to have inner life converted into administrative leverage without strict limits.

The consumer market is the rehearsal

Consumer neurotechnology is not waiting for a perfected implant. Muse markets headbands that read brainwaves in real time for meditation, sleep and focus. Emotiv sells EEG devices and BCI software for research, wellness and experimentation. Meta’s Ray-Ban Display glasses, announced in September 2025, ship with the Meta Neural Band, an EMG wristband that translates muscle-activity signals into commands for the glasses. Meta researchers also published peer-reviewed work in Nature on a noninvasive neuromotor interface based on surface electromyography at the wrist. This is not a brain implant, but it is part of the same ethical migration: bodily and nervous-system signals are becoming everyday input methods.

That migration may be useful. A wristband that lets a person control glasses through subtle gestures could be more discreet and accessible than voice commands or touchscreens. For people with tremor, limited mobility or speech impairment, such interfaces may improve access. But consumer adoption changes the privacy baseline. A device sold as convenient can normalize collection before society has debated permissible uses. A wellness device can gather data outside HIPAA. A platform device can make the nervous system part of a broader advertising, identity and engagement ecosystem. A research-grade signal can become a product feature; a product feature can become a behavioral profile.

The point is not that neurotech companies are uniquely sinister. It is that they are entering an economy with established incentives. The FTC’s actions involving GoodRx, BetterHelp and Premom were not neural-data cases, but they are warnings from the health-adjacent data world. The agency alleged or found that sensitive health information moved into advertising or analytics channels in ways users did not reasonably expect. HHS also makes clear that HIPAA usually does not protect data a person enters into a personal app unless the app is provided by a covered entity or business associate. The lesson for neural privacy is obvious: if a device is sold as wellness, focus, gaming or convenience, the moral intimacy of the signal may exceed the legal category that currently governs it.

If data can improve a model, target a customer, measure engagement, prove efficacy, attract investors, support a subscription or create a defensible moat, someone will be tempted to retain and repurpose it. Without hard limits, neural privacy will be negotiated one unread policy at a time.

A right to mental privacy should be narrow enough to work

The phrase cognitive liberty can sound grandiose, and critics are right to ask what it adds to existing rights. Privacy, bodily integrity, freedom of thought, disability rights, consumer protection, medical ethics and anti-discrimination law already cover pieces of the terrain. Creating new rights too casually can produce symbolic law that is vague, unenforceable or hostile to beneficial research. A person cannot sue every time a company claims to measure focus. Nor should law freeze neuroscience by treating all inference about mental states as forbidden.

But the case for a mental-privacy right is stronger if it is made modestly. It need not rest on mystical claims about the brain. It can rest on four practical commitments.

First, neural and nervous-system data collected by devices should be treated as sensitive by default, with strict purpose limitation. Data collected to control a cursor should not be repurposed to target ads. Data collected to guide meditation should not be sold to brokers. Data collected for rehabilitation should not become an employment or insurance signal without a separate, narrow, meaningful authorization, and some uses should be prohibited regardless of authorization.

Second, mental-state inferences should be regulated when they are used to make consequential decisions. If an employer, school, insurer, lender, landlord, platform or government agency uses neural or neuro-adjacent data to infer attention, emotion, fatigue, impairment, credibility, productivity or risk, the affected person should have notice, explanation, contestability and a right to refuse in many contexts. The inference, not just the raw data, should be within the law’s field of vision.

Third, certain domains should be presumptively off limits. Neural data should not be used for behavioral advertising, political persuasion, price discrimination, employment discipline, law-enforcement suspicion scores or insurance underwriting. There may be narrow safety exceptions, such as fatigue monitoring in high-risk transportation, but those exceptions should be governed by independent standards, data minimization, worker participation and anti-retaliation rules. Safety is too important to become a password for surveillance.

Fourth, neurotechnology should carry duties of continuity. If a company implants or supplies a device that mediates communication, movement or perception, its obligations cannot end at the sale. Patients need support, updates, repair pathways, data portability, explantation planning where relevant, and protection if the company is acquired, pivots, fails or shuts down a product. The ethical stakes of abandonment are higher when the device is not just in a drawer but part of someone’s agency.

The mind is not a warehouse of property

It is tempting to solve neural privacy by saying, simply, that people own their brain data. The phrase is useful politically because it asserts control. Philosophically, it is incomplete. Ownership suggests alienability: what is mine, I can sell. But some interests are too closely tied to personhood to be treated as ordinary commodities. We do not usually say that a person owns the right not to be tortured, the right not to be enslaved, or the right to vote in the same way one owns a bicycle. We say these are rights that structure what may be done to persons, including what persons may be pressured to sell.

Neural data sits somewhere between property, privacy and bodily integrity. People should have strong control over it. They should be able to access, delete, port and restrict it. But society should be wary of building a market where the poor sell nervous-system access and the rich buy cognitive enhancement trained on that access. The point of mental privacy is not merely to help each person bargain better. It is to declare that there are kinds of access institutions should not normalize.

Chile’s experience shows why this is more than theoretical. After the country amended its constitution to protect brain activity and information related to it, its Supreme Court ruled in 2023 in a case involving an Emotiv EEG headset and ordered deletion of the plaintiff’s brain data. The case does not supply a ready-made U.S. template. Constitutional structures differ, and courts cannot substitute for detailed statutory design. But it does show that consumer neurotechnology has already produced a justiciable dispute over brain data, consent and deletion. This is not only a future problem. It is an early problem with future consequences.

The civic stakes are larger than any one device. A democratic society depends on some space where citizens are not yet performing for measurement. That space has already been narrowed by phones, cameras, platforms, wearables and predictive analytics. Neurotechnology threatens to narrow it further by moving measurement closer to impulse and intention. The law cannot protect an untouched inner sanctum in a literal sense; human beings are always embodied, expressive and socially legible. But law can protect a civic norm: no one should have to justify the contents, rhythms or vulnerabilities of mental life to participate in ordinary life.

The rules should be written before habits harden

The most common objection to early regulation is that it will choke innovation. That risk is real if lawmakers draft broad, fearful prohibitions that fail to distinguish a clinical speech prosthesis from a consumer attention toy. But the opposite risk is larger and more familiar: waiting until commercial practice becomes infrastructure. Once schools buy systems, employers adopt metrics, insurers price risk, platforms train models and consumers build habits, reform becomes a negotiation with sunk costs. The history of digital privacy is a warning against patience disguised as prudence.

Good early rules would not ban neurotechnology. They would make legitimate neurotechnology easier to trust. They would give medical researchers clear pathways, give patients enforceable rights, give companies predictable limits, and give the public a reason to believe that intimate signals will not be quietly routed into advertising or institutional scoring. They would also prevent the worst actors from setting expectations for everyone else.

The immediate task for lawmakers is not to write a metaphysics of the mind. It is to write practical boundaries: sensitive-data classification; bans on sale and advertising use; strict consent for collection; purpose limitation; data minimization; retention limits; security duties; audit rights; protections against discrimination; special rules for employment, education, insurance and government use; clinical continuity obligations; and enforcement strong enough that violations are not merely a cost of doing business. Federal law should eventually preempt the weakest state rules with a higher floor, not erase stronger protections. Until then, the state patchwork is an imperfect but useful laboratory.

The immediate task for ethicists is equally important: stop framing neural privacy as a choice between wonder and fear. The same technology can be emancipatory in a clinic and coercive in a workplace. The same data stream can restore speech and fuel manipulation. The same user can be both a patient seeking autonomy and a consumer exposed to extraction. The ethics must be able to hold those facts together.

A headband should not own the quietest room. Neither should an app store, an employer, a school district, a glasses platform, an insurer, a data broker or a government office. The mind has never been sealed off from the world, and neurotechnology will make that boundary even more porous. But porosity is not permission. If neural data is becoming a language that machines can partly read, then the first civic rule should be simple: the person whose nervous system is speaking is not merely the source of the data. They are the rights-holder in the room.
