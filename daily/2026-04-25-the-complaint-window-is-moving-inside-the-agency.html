The Complaint Window Is Moving Inside the Agency

Before the public can see a governmentwide list of jobs moved into Schedule Policy/Career, agencies are already writing the papers employees would have to use afterward: job-announcement warnings, at-will acknowledgements, complaint deadlines and internal decisions that replace some ordinary personnel forums with department-run channels.

By The Press • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The newest evidence of the Trump administration’s civil-service overhaul is not a mass firing notice, a courtroom argument or a campaign speech. It is an employee document: the kind of human-resources bulletin that tells workers where a complaint must go, how quickly it must be filed and who gets the last ordinary word.

At the Department of Commerce, HR Bulletin #275 carries an effective date of April 9, 2026, and creates a prohibited-personnel-practices process for employees and applicants in Schedule Policy/Career positions. The bulletin routes complaints to the department’s Office of the General Counsel, requires filing within 30 days of the alleged practice or of when the person knew or reasonably should have known about it, and says the written decision is not subject to further review except if OGC reopens it on its own initiative. At the Department of Veterans Affairs, a handbook chapter effective April 8 is more explicit about the shift. Schedule Policy/Career employees, VA says, may not seek corrective action before the Office of Special Counsel for the covered prohibited-personnel-practice allegations; instead, VA will receive, investigate and adjudicate those claims through an internal process.

Those documents do not, by themselves, convert any job. OPM’s February 2026 guidance says placement of positions into Schedule Policy/Career requires a presidential executive order, and the final rule says the President will make the final decision about which positions are placed in the schedule. But the VA and Commerce policies show that the machinery around the new category is no longer theoretical. Agencies are drafting the notices, complaint addresses, deadlines, acknowledgement language and internal decision rules that will shape what happens after a firing, demotion, reassignment, performance action, alleged retaliation episode or claimed political-pressure dispute.

That is why the Schedule Policy/Career fight is not only a fight over whether the president may remove more federal employees more quickly. It is also a fight over forum. For much of the modern civil-service system, the key promise has not been that permanent federal employees can never be disciplined. It has been that certain adverse actions and retaliation claims move through procedures that include notice, a record, appeal rights, outside adjudication, the Merit Systems Protection Board, the Office of Special Counsel, inspectors general, courts or some combination of those channels. The new department policies sketch a different arrangement for a category of career jobs close to policy: the same department that takes the personnel action may also become the first and last ordinary stop for the claim that the action was improper.

The administration’s answer is that this is the design, not a defect. OPM and the White House argue that policy-influencing career employees occupy roles with enough power over the president’s agenda that ordinary removal procedures can become a veto by delay. They say Schedule Policy/Career positions remain career positions, not political appointments; that merit hiring and veterans’ preference remain part of the framework; that political patronage, political discrimination and loyalty tests are forbidden; and that employees may not be punished for lawful whistleblowing or for political affiliation. OPM’s final rule describes the category as limited to positions of a confidential, policy-determining, policy-making or policy-advocating character and estimates that about 50,000 positions, roughly 2 percent of the executive-branch workforce outside the Postal Service, will be placed in it.

Critics’ answer is that internal rules are not the same as independent rights. Federal employee unions and public-interest organizations, in a pending Maryland lawsuit, describe Schedule Policy/Career as an unlawful attempt to strip a large group of career employees of due-process protections and to recreate patronage vulnerability under a different label. Their complaint challenges the executive order and OPM rule under the Civil Service Reform Act, the Administrative Procedure Act and constitutional due-process principles. They say the fact that agencies are instructed to prohibit the same kinds of misconduct does not cure the loss of outside enforcement.

The form is the policy

The VA’s March 31 staffing handbook amendment is a useful place to see how the change reaches the level of a job announcement. It tells VA human-resources offices that public postings for Schedule Policy/Career jobs must say the position is in the excepted service; that employment will be at-will; that MSPB appeals of adverse actions concerning performance, discipline and other matters under specified Title 5 chapters will not be available; and that prohibited personnel practices will be enforced by VA rather than the Office of Special Counsel. Applicants selected for Schedule Policy/Career jobs must sign an acknowledgement that they have read and understood the notice. Current employees whose jobs are moved into the schedule must receive notice, but VA says they do not have to sign an acknowledgement and may not be punished for refusing.

That difference matters. An applicant can decide whether to accept a posted job carrying those conditions. A current employee whose existing position is moved into the schedule is not necessarily making a new bargain. The notice becomes evidence that the agency told the employee what changed, not proof that the employee agreed to it. In later litigation, that distinction may matter if individual employees claim they lost a property interest in continued employment or were deprived of procedures they previously held.

The VA’s April 8 prohibited-personnel-practices chapter goes further. It says Schedule Policy/Career employees are excluded from the statutory remedial framework under 5 U.S.C. 2302(b), including review by OSC and MSPB, and that VA’s internal process is intended to provide a fair, impartial and timely mechanism. VA directs employees operating under Schedule Policy/Career to file alleged prohibited-personnel-practice complaints with the Office of Accountability and Whistleblower Protection in specified cases, including claims involving senior leaders and retaliation for protected disclosures. The filing deadline is 15 business days. For allegations investigated by OAWP, the office is to issue a written report or decision within 90 calendar days, though the handbook says missing that deadline does not invalidate the later decision. OAWP’s decision becomes final after 15 calendar days and is not subject to further review, except for reopening by OAWP itself within that same 15-day period.

Commerce chose a different route. Its April bulletin sends Schedule Policy/Career prohibited-personnel-practice complaints to the department’s Office of the General Counsel. The complaint must include enough facts, the remedy sought and supporting documents in the employee’s possession. OGC may investigate or delegate the investigation to another Commerce office, must ensure the investigator is an unbiased department official, and is to issue a written decision within 90 days. The decision of the designated department official is not subject to further review, except that OGC may reopen and reconsider it on its own initiative. Commerce also says the bulletin does not abridge discrimination complaints, collective-bargaining grievances where an agreement allows them, or the right to report to inspectors general, Congress or other lawful authorities.

In ordinary bureaucratic prose, the documents reveal a large institutional bet: internal agency channels can substitute for at least some outside review normally associated with federal personnel law. The question is not whether VA and Commerce copied the list of prohibited personnel practices. They largely did. The question is what it means when similar standards are enforced through a narrower, faster and more agency-controlled route.

A category built from old words

Schedule Policy/Career is the renamed and revised successor to Schedule F, the late-2020 Trump executive order that President Biden revoked before any positions were actually moved into it. President Trump revived and amended that framework on January 20, 2025, through Executive Order 14171, replacing the letter F with Schedule Policy/Career and directing OPM to undo Biden-era protections that would have made involuntary conversions much harder to use.

The key phrase is old: positions of a confidential, policy-determining, policy-making or policy-advocating character. Federal personnel law has long treated some such positions differently, especially confidential and political roles. The dispute is whether the administration can use that language to reach tens of thousands of career employees who are not political appointees and are not normally expected to depart when a president changes.

OPM’s final rule, published February 6 and effective March 9, says yes. It places Schedule Policy/Career in the excepted service and says positions moved from the competitive service will generally still be filled through merit-based competitive hiring procedures. It also says the President, rather than OPM, will place positions in Schedule Policy/Career after agencies submit recommendations for OPM review. OPM says the expected 50,000 positions represent a small minority of the workforce, not a collapse of the civil-service system.

Critics read the number differently. Fifty thousand is not the whole bureaucracy, but it is far larger than the traditional political layer. It could include attorneys, economists, budget analysts, grant officials, scientists, regulatory specialists, program managers and senior advisers whose work shapes policy but whose public legitimacy comes from expertise and continuity rather than partisan appointment. The amended complaint filed by Public Employees for Environmental Responsibility, AFGE, AFSCME, the AFL-CIO and AFGE Local 1923 argues that the administration is reaching past the political appointee structure Congress has already allowed. The complaint says administrations already have thousands of noncareer appointees to direct policy and that career experts are supposed to implement lawful directives without becoming politically disposable.

OPM answers that Schedule Policy/Career jobs are still career jobs. Its overview page says they are not political appointments. The final rule and OPM guidance say employees in the category are not required to personally or politically support the president or administration policies, but must faithfully implement administration policy to the best of their ability. The White House and OPM also point to performance-management concerns. A 2015 GAO report found that formal processes for dismissing poor-performing permanent employees are more time- and resource-intensive than probationary dismissals and that removal can take six months to a year, sometimes longer. To the administration, the existing system can protect obstruction and incompetence. To opponents, the new schedule risks making candor, expertise and lawful dissent look like obstruction whenever they inconvenience political leadership.

The list the public still cannot test

For employees, the most practical question remains unresolved in public: whose jobs are actually on the list?

OPM’s final rule says the office has conducted an initial review of agency recommendations and continues to view its 50,000-position estimate as reasonable. The rule also says the President will make the final decision through executive order. But as of April 25, the public materials The Press reviewed did not provide a governmentwide list of positions recommended for conversion, the agencies most affected, the grade levels involved or the occupational series most exposed. OPM’s resource page links to guidance, templates and reference materials, not to a public inventory of proposed or approved positions.

The earlier Schedule F experience shows why the footprint matters. GAO reported in 2022 that no agency placed positions into Schedule F before Biden revoked the order in January 2021. OPM did approve OMB’s request to place 136 positions into Schedule F, which GAO said affected 415 employees, or 68 percent of OMB’s workforce at the time. That did not show what the governmentwide scale would have become, but it did show how differently the category could land across agencies. A narrow national percentage can still be large inside a particular office.

The lack of a public list creates two kinds of uncertainty. First, employees do not know whether the risk is concentrated in obvious policy offices or could reach legal, scientific, administrative and supervisory jobs whose position descriptions use the word policy in ordinary management ways. Second, Congress and the public cannot yet measure whether Schedule Policy/Career is being used as OPM describes: limited to a defined set of employees with genuine policy influence.

This is why the VA and Commerce documents matter before a designation order is visible. They are not conversion lists. They are operating manuals for the aftermath of conversion. VA has prepared job-announcement language and employee notices. Commerce has created a complaint email route. OPM has issued templates for prohibited-personnel-practices policies, notices to applicants and employees, performance-based and adverse-action policies, compensation policies and administrative grievance processes. The architecture is being assembled before the public can inspect the full footprint.

Forum shifting as governance

Personnel law is often covered as a question of how hard it is to fire someone. The Schedule Policy/Career rollout shows that government power also turns on who hears the appeal.

The Merit Systems Protection Board describes one of its primary statutory functions as protecting federal merit systems against partisan political and other prohibited personnel practices by adjudicating employee appeals over which the board has jurisdiction. The Office of Special Counsel ordinarily receives and investigates prohibited-personnel-practice complaints, including whistleblower retaliation claims, for covered employees. Inspectors general, equal-employment offices, union grievance systems and courts occupy other parts of the landscape. The system is complicated, slow and imperfect, but its structure reflects a basic fear: if the same chain of command can hire, fire, investigate itself and close the file, merit protections may become managerial promises rather than enforceable constraints.

Schedule Policy/Career does not erase every outside path. Discrimination claims remain governed by equal-employment statutes. Some collective-bargaining grievances may survive where agreements and law allow them. Employees may still communicate with Congress and inspectors general within legal limits. Courts may later decide that some converted employees have constitutional or statutory claims. Veterans’ preference claims may also travel through existing veterans’ preference routes. But the VA and Commerce policies show that for a defined class of prohibited-personnel-practice claims, agencies are preparing to say: start here, inside the department, under this deadline, with this final internal decision.

That is a significant administrative change even if the covered share of the workforce is 2 percent. The most sensitive government disputes are rarely evenly distributed. A scientist who refuses to alter a technical finding, an attorney who warns that a proposed rule is unlawful, a budget analyst who says a spending plan violates an appropriation, or a grants official who documents political interference may be considered policy-influencing precisely because the work matters. The more consequential the position, the more important the forum becomes.

The administration’s strongest argument should be stated plainly. Presidents are elected to govern. The executive branch contains many career officials whose judgments shape national policy without Senate confirmation and without direct electoral accountability. When employees use procedural protections to slow-walk lawful directives, conceal options from political leaders or substitute their own preferences for elected leadership, democratic control suffers. A personnel system that cannot distinguish expertise from defiance can become a veto point.

There is also a real management problem. GAO and workforce managers have long described performance systems that reward caution, documentation burdens and delay. Supervisors may avoid formal processes altogether, shifting work to stronger employees while weak performance remains. Citizens experience those failures not as constitutional theory but as delayed benefits, unanswered calls, backlogs, poor inspections and services that do not arrive when promised.

OPM is trying to thread that argument through statutory language that already recognizes a category of confidential and policy-influencing work. Its rule says Schedule Policy/Career positions remain career jobs, filled on merit, subject to veterans’ preference and protected by agency-enforced rules against political coercion, nepotism, whistleblower retaliation and other prohibited practices. That is the cleanest version of the case: a narrower, more accountable career layer for roles close enough to policy to require responsiveness, but still distinct from partisan patronage.

The difficulty is that the boundary between responsiveness and political obedience is not self-enforcing. A rule can ban loyalty tests while a workplace culture rewards anticipatory compliance. A handbook can protect whistleblowing while employees doubt that an internal legal office will rule against top agency leadership. A notice can say an employee keeps the right to contact Congress while the loss of MSPB or OSC routes makes the practical risk of speaking up feel larger. The administration’s case depends on disciplined implementation, narrow designations and credible internal neutrality. Those are the points not yet proven.

Why courts may not settle the question quickly

The Maryland lawsuit is one route for review, but the legal posture is complicated. A Congressional Research Service legal sidebar notes that challenges may fall into several categories: facial attacks on the executive order, challenges to OPM or agency implementation, and claims by specific people after they are harmed by a conversion or personnel action. CRS also notes a procedural complication: the President is not ordinarily treated as an agency for Administrative Procedure Act review, while final agency actions implementing presidential directives may be reviewable. That means timing and the identity of the challenged action matter.

If courts wait for concrete employee harm, many of the most important practical decisions will happen before a merits ruling. Agencies will classify positions, issue notices, revise job announcements, train managers, build complaint channels and tell employees what risks now attach to their roles. If courts intervene earlier, they will have to decide how far Congress allowed the President and OPM to go in classifying policy-influencing career jobs and whether the new framework conflicts with the broader design of the Civil Service Reform Act.

Meanwhile, agencies have to operate. VA cannot pause all staffing systems while litigants test the outer boundary of Title 5. Commerce cannot wait to decide where a complaint should be filed if an employee in a converted position alleges political retaliation. HR offices need templates. Managers need instructions. Applicants need notices. That operational reality is how a disputed theory becomes administrative fact.

A democratic procedure story

Schedule Policy/Career is often described as a federal employee issue, which is true but incomplete. The civil service is one of the hidden operating systems of democratic government. It determines whether election winners can direct policy, whether statutes are administered consistently, whether technical findings are insulated enough to be useful, and whether workers can report abuse without wagering their careers on the benevolence of the same leadership they accuse.

The VA and Commerce documents make the next phase concrete. A job announcement will tell an applicant that MSPB appeals are unavailable. An acknowledgement will sit in a personnel file. A Commerce employee will have 30 days to submit a complaint to the general counsel. A VA employee will have 15 business days for many covered allegations. A decision may arrive within 90 days, or later, and in many instances will become final inside the agency.

That is not the whole story of the federal workforce. Most civil servants may never be touched by Schedule Policy/Career. The administration may keep the category closer to its 50,000-position estimate than critics fear. Courts may narrow, block or bless the system. Congress may intervene, especially if conversions become a visible 2026 campaign issue in federal-worker-heavy regions and among veterans, scientists, engineers, regulators, benefit administrators and public-health professionals.

But the procedural direction is already visible. The administration is not merely trying to move a set of employees from one employment label to another. It is trying to relocate disputes about loyalty, performance, retaliation and policy resistance from independent personnel forums into agency-managed systems for a category of career jobs close to power. The still life on the desk — bulletin, acknowledgement, deadline, complaint address — is where that constitutional fight becomes legible to the person who has to sign, file, object or stay quiet.
