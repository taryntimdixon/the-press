Trump’s tariffs are back in court, and the fight is really about presidential power

A federal trade court heard a fresh challenge to the White House’s latest global tariffs, reviving a broader constitutional battle over whether presidents can tax imports by emergency decree.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 12, 2026 • 12:58 a.m.

President Donald Trump’s tariffs are back in court, and so is the larger political question lurking beneath them: how much unilateral power can a president claim over trade before Congress and the courts draw a line?

On Friday, April 10, a three-judge panel at the U.S. Court of International Trade heard arguments over Trump’s latest 10% tariff on most imports, a temporary replacement after the Supreme Court in February struck down his earlier, more sweeping tariff regime. The new challenge is not just about whether the administration picked the right legal tool. It is about whether Trump has now made tariffs a standing instrument of presidential will, used first as emergency leverage and then reissued, adjusted, and defended as a flexible governing weapon. The case landed in a specialized federal court in New York, but its implications reach far beyond customs law. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

The White House’s tariff posture has become one of the defining features of Trump’s second term. When the Supreme Court ruled in February that the president could not use the International Emergency Economic Powers Act to impose sweeping tariffs on nearly all foreign countries, it delivered an important statutory check. But Trump responded within days by announcing a narrower 10% global tariff under a different authority, telling supporters he had “great alternatives” and suggesting the fight had only changed venues. That sequence matters because it shows the administration is not abandoning tariff-based governance; it is iterating on it. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

At the heart of Friday’s hearing is a familiar Washington tension with new urgency. Congress holds the power to levy taxes and regulate commerce, but presidents have long sought room to maneuver in trade policy, especially when claiming national emergency or national security concerns. Trump has pushed that boundary harder than any modern president. His team has argued that persistent trade deficits justify emergency-style action and that the White House needs fast, unilateral tools to pressure other countries. Opponents say that logic would turn an emergency statute into a blank check, letting a president tax imports whenever politics or economics demand it. The court’s skepticism this week suggested those objections remain potent. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

This is not the first time the courts have put Trump’s tariff program under a microscope. In February, the Supreme Court struck down the earlier tariffs in a 6-3 ruling, sharply limiting the president’s claim that the 1977 emergency law gave him authority to impose broad, open-ended import taxes. That decision did not end the trade war. It changed its legal architecture. Trump then pivoted to a different provision allowing more limited tariffs, but the move left critics asking whether the administration was now testing a sequence of statutes in search of the broadest surviving interpretation rather than following the boundaries Congress actually set. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

That distinction matters politically because tariffs are no longer just a policy instrument; they are a message. For Trump, they signal toughness, leverage, and the ability to force foreign governments, multinational companies, and even domestic institutions to bend to presidential demands. For his allies, they are evidence that he is willing to use executive power aggressively in pursuit of industrial and geopolitical goals. For his critics, they are a warning that the modern presidency is sliding toward something more like a national command center, where legal limits are treated as obstacles to be routed around. The current case therefore sits at the intersection of constitutional law and political style. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

The administration’s own rhetoric underscores that point. After the Supreme Court’s February ruling, Trump not only dismissed the setback but hinted at escalating again. He first announced a 10% global tariff, then floated a 15% rate. That kind of public improvisation is one reason the tariff saga has become such a headache for businesses and trading partners: the policy can shift quickly, but the legal theory behind it remains contested. In effect, the White House has treated tariffs as both policy and theater, a tool for extracting concessions and a performance of strength for a domestic audience that associates economic nationalism with presidential resolve. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f0b2a1eb3a8e23046613466ef3aa21e8?utm_source=openai))

Friday’s hearing also raises a subtler institutional issue: how much deference courts should give to executive claims about emergencies. Trump’s lawyers have argued that the president must be able to react decisively to persistent trade imbalances, foreign economic manipulation, and strategic vulnerabilities. Yet the Court of International Trade appeared willing to question whether a long-running trade deficit can plausibly be treated as the sort of emergency Congress meant to empower. If the judges conclude that the administration’s theory is too expansive, it could narrow the practical reach of emergency powers not only in trade but in any domain where presidents try to stretch old statutes to meet new ambitions. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

That is why the case is drawing attention well beyond the business press. Trump’s second term has been marked by an unusually confrontational relationship with legal and institutional constraints, whether in immigration, spending, media access, or trade. The tariff fight is part of the same pattern: an administration pressing the outer edge of the law, losing in one forum, then returning in another with a revised argument and the hope that a different statutory hook will withstand review. In that sense, the litigation is not a sideshow to Trump’s political agenda. It is a test of whether the judiciary can keep pace with an executive branch determined to keep pressing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9b44e480117fbc83adc587824efd29a4?utm_source=openai))

The political stakes are enormous because tariffs are one of the few economic policies that can be explained, applauded, and resented in the same breath. Supporters see them as a forceful answer to years of deindustrialization and unfair trade terms. Critics see them as a tax on consumers and an unpredictable drag on global commerce. Trump has leaned into the first narrative, repeatedly framing tariffs as patriotic correction and proof that he is fighting for American workers. But the court fight suggests the country may be moving toward a more basic constitutional debate: should a policy so economically consequential be controlled by a president acting alone, or by a Congress that must legislate in the open? ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

There is also a broader strategic consequence. The Supreme Court’s February decision weakened one of Trump’s favorite tools for sudden pressure on trading partners. That does not mean tariffs are off the table. It means the administration has to work harder to justify them and may need to rely on narrower statutes, more specific findings, or congressional cooperation. That could reduce the president’s ability to use tariff threats as instant diplomatic leverage. It could also force the White House to be more transparent about what it is trying to achieve: lower trade deficits, manufacturing reshoring, revenue collection, bargaining power, or punishment of adversaries. The less the administration can do by proclamation, the more it must explain in policy terms. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f0b2a1eb3a8e23046613466ef3aa21e8?utm_source=openai))

For Congress, the case is an uncomfortable reminder of its own weakness. Even though the Constitution gives lawmakers the core taxing power, Congress has often ceded trade authority to the executive branch through broad delegations and ambiguous statutes. That has made it easier for presidents of both parties to act quickly in trade disputes. But Trump’s use of those powers has exposed just how far such delegations can be stretched when a president is willing to move aggressively and litigate later. If Congress wants to reclaim authority, it will have to do more than issue complaints about overreach. It will need to write clearer limits, resist default deference, and decide whether it is willing to absorb the political costs of curbing a president who has made trade confrontation central to his identity. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

The new challenge also arrives amid a broader season of legal stress around Trump’s presidency. Just over a week before the tariff hearing, the Supreme Court heard arguments over Trump’s order seeking to end birthright citizenship for children born in the United States to parents in the country illegally or temporarily. The justices signaled skepticism, and Trump himself made the rare move of attending the argument in person, another sign that his administration sees the courts as a central arena for defining presidential power. Taken together, the two cases suggest that the biggest political story of Trump’s second term may be less about any single policy than about the administration’s effort to expand what a president can do without Congress. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/f042a0f2902958380bd8c7582030742f?utm_source=openai))

That larger pattern helps explain why this tariff dispute matters now, in April 2026. Trump has already shown that he will not treat an adverse ruling as an endpoint. He will search for another statute, another theory, another emergency rationale, and another forum. The courts, meanwhile, are being asked to decide whether this kind of iterative power claim is a legitimate part of modern governance or an abuse that leaves constitutional checks hollowed out. The answer will shape not just trade policy, but the basic rules of presidential behavior for the rest of Trump’s term and perhaps beyond it. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))

In the short run, businesses and foreign governments will keep watching for the next move. In the longer run, the tariff case may become a kind of template: a dispute that begins in trade but ends up defining the outer limit of unilateral presidential power. If the courts tighten the leash, Trump will likely cast himself as a leader constrained by a hostile legal establishment. If they do not, he will have even more room to turn the presidency into a tool for fast, unpredictable economic intervention. Either way, Friday’s hearing was not just about tariffs. It was about who gets to govern when a president decides the law should move as quickly as he does. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/612954e80e705c48c3ef82e87c6078a3?utm_source=openai))
