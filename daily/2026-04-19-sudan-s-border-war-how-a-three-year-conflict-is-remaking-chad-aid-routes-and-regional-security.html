Sudan’s border war: how a three-year conflict is remaking Chad, aid routes, and regional security

As Sudan’s war enters its fourth year, the fighting is no longer contained by the country’s frontiers: it is redrawing the humanitarian map in Chad, hardening border politics, and exposing how dependent relief still is on fragile regional corridors.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 19, 2026 • 4:00 p.m. • 2921 words

Sudan’s war has become far more than a contest over Khartoum, Darfur, and the levers of state power. By mid-April 2026, it is a regional shock that has displaced millions, pushed the humanitarian system to its limits, and turned the Sudan-Chad frontier into one of the most consequential borders in Africa. On April 15, when Sudan marked three years since the conflict erupted, the United Nations said nearly 34 million people in Sudan needed humanitarian assistance and that the crisis remained the world’s largest humanitarian emergency. UN agencies and partners also said Sudan remained the world’s largest displacement crisis, with roughly 14 million people uprooted, including millions who had crossed into neighboring countries. ([sudan.un.org](https://sudan.un.org/en?utm_source=openai))

The conflict has also pulled Chad directly into the emergency. Chad’s eastern borderlands now host a huge refugee population, most of it from Sudan’s Darfur region. UNHCR said last week that Chad hosts 1.3 million Sudanese refugees, with more than 900,000 arriving since the war began in April 2023, and that the country’s refugee-hosting regions are under intense strain. Separate UNICEF and UNHCR assessments put the number of Sudan-linked arrivals in eastern Chad even higher when returnees are included. The consistent point across the major agencies is that the conflict has created a long, grinding displacement system that is no longer confined to Sudan’s own territory. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

A border that is both a shield and a lifeline

Chad’s frontier with Sudan has never been a simple boundary. It is a corridor for commerce, kinship, grazing, displacement, and, during wartime, military spillover. Since the fighting began in April 2023, the border has also become a route of escape for civilians from Darfur and a possible path for fighters and weapons moving in both directions. On February 23, 2026, AP reported that Chad had closed its border with Sudan “until further notice” after fighters spilled into its territory and after attacks near the border area around Tine. Chad said exceptions could be granted for humanitarian reasons, but the closure underscored how deeply the war has pressed onto neighboring soil. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))

That decision was politically rational in a narrow sense. Chad has legitimate concerns about territorial integrity, armed incursions, smuggling networks, and the possibility that Sudan’s war could ignite more insecurity in its own east. But the closure also highlighted a harder reality: the same border that Chad wants to secure is also the main escape route for civilians and one of the most important corridors for aid. In eastern Chad, where refugee concentrations are dense and local services are already stretched, the line between border control and humanitarian access has become thin and unstable. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))

This tension is visible in the aid maps. Humanitarian agencies have relied heavily on crossings such as Adré and Tine to move assistance into Darfur and to sustain refugee operations in Chad. When those routes are closed or endangered, relief can be slowed or rerouted, but not easily replaced. ACAPS’ March 2026 briefing on humanitarian access in Darfur and Kordofan describes a landscape in which access is shaped by insecurity, control of roads, bureaucracy, and the practical constraints of operating through a fragmented war zone. In other words, the border is not just a policy issue. It is the operating environment itself. ([acaps.org](https://www.acaps.org/fileadmin/Data_Product/Main_media/20260322_ACAPS_Sudan_-_Analysis_of_humanitarian_access_in_the_Darfur_and_Kordofan_regions_.pdf?utm_source=openai))

AP reported in November 2024 that a key crossing with Chad had been kept open to keep aid moving into Darfur. That earlier arrangement now reads as a reminder of how temporary and reversible these openings can be. The border is not a stable humanitarian valve; it is a contested space in which any opening depends on shifting security judgments and political calculations. Aid agencies can negotiate access, but they cannot manufacture certainty. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/48c6fed77d43ce412899fe554123a436?utm_source=openai))

Sudan’s war is producing a regional displacement system

The refugee map around Sudan now reaches far beyond Chad. UNHCR, UNICEF, and the UN’s own briefing notes describe a crisis that has pushed people into Egypt, South Sudan, Ethiopia, Libya, the Central African Republic, Uganda, and Chad, while also generating secondary displacement inside those countries. In the first quarter of 2026, UN agencies continued to warn that the war’s scale and duration were overwhelming the absorption capacity of neighboring states. The total figure varies by source and date, but the direction is unmistakable: the conflict has created a multi-country displacement system that cannot be managed by Sudan alone. ([sudan.un.org](https://sudan.un.org/en/313912-aid-system-under-pressure-needs-continue-grow-three-years-sudan-war?utm_source=openai))

Chad is especially exposed because it already hosted large refugee populations from earlier crises in Darfur. UNHCR said this month that roughly 409,000 Sudanese refugees were living in Chad before the new war, and that the post-2023 influx pushed the country to 1.3 million Sudanese refugees overall. In eastern Chad, that translates into an extreme burden on schools, clinics, water systems, and local governance. UN agencies say the east now contains one of the highest concentrations of refugees per capita anywhere in the world. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

This matters because displacement is not only a humanitarian statistic; it is a political and economic event. Refugees need food, shelter, registration, health care, schooling, and protection. Host communities need roads, water, health posts, and new investment. If those systems do not expand, the arrival of civilians fleeing war can amplify preexisting fragility. That is one reason UN agencies keep describing eastern Chad as a place where humanitarian aid and development support now need to work in tandem rather than in isolation. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

The latest UNICEF material on eastern Chad makes that point even more sharply. In an April 2026 assessment, UNICEF said that by the end of March, over 918,761 refugees and 389,755 estimated returnees had crossed into eastern Chad since the war began, and that the number of Sudan-linked arrivals could rise further by the end of 2026. Even allowing for methodological differences with UNHCR’s refugee count, the combined picture is clear: eastern Chad is absorbing a massive and continuing inflow of people displaced by the Sudan war. ([unicef.org](https://www.unicef.org/chad/documents/eastern-chad-crisis-cost-inaction-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

The humanitarian corridor problem

Humanitarian access is often described as a technical problem. In Sudan, it has become a structural one. The UN, WHO, UNICEF, UNHCR, and ACAPS all describe a response dependent on sustained access, logistics, and funding at precisely the time that roads are unsafe, crossings are uncertain, and armed actors can obstruct movement. Sudan’s emergency now stretches across conflict lines and borders, making even a simple delivery chain vulnerable to delays at every step. ([sudan.un.org](https://sudan.un.org/en/313912-aid-system-under-pressure-needs-continue-grow-three-years-sudan-war?utm_source=openai))

UN agencies say the health consequences are severe. WHO’s April 14, 2026 news release said Sudan had become the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, with 34 million people needing aid, 21 million lacking health services, and a health system battered by repeated attacks. WHO also said that 37 percent of health facilities remain non-functional and that it has verified 217 attacks on health care since April 2023. These are not abstract indicators; they explain why border access is so important. When the country’s internal health system is this damaged, a single corridor can become a lifeline. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

That lifeline is fragile. In Chad, the border areas nearest Sudan are also among the most vulnerable to disease outbreaks, overcrowding, and shortages of clean water. WHO reported in 2025 that cholera was confirmed in Chad, with the first suspected case reported from Dougui camp in Ouaddaï province, along the border with Sudan. UNICEF and WHO have since tied the eastern Chad emergency to cholera, refugee crowding, and pressure on already weak health services. As displaced people move, so do health risks. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/southeastasia/outbreaks-and-emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2025-DON579?utm_source=openai))

In practical terms, humanitarian workers face a chain of vulnerabilities. A convoy can be delayed by a closure at Adré or Tine. A shipment can be slowed by customs procedures or denied passage by local insecurity. A clinic can be overwhelmed by new arrivals. A warehouse can be cut off by road conditions or fighting. A border policy intended to protect security can therefore make the border harder to govern if it pushes movement into informal routes and creates more uncertainty for civilians and aid agencies alike. That does not mean border controls are illegitimate. It means controls alone do not solve the underlying disorder. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))

Why Chad’s decision is politically rational — and still dangerous

It is easy to criticize border closures from afar, especially when the immediate image is one of families stranded between conflict and safety. But Chad’s own vulnerability matters. The country has long faced insecurity in the Lake Chad basin, pressure on public services, and repeated refugee inflows from Sudan and elsewhere. UNHCR has said Chad is on the front line of the Sudan crisis and that one in thirteen people in the country is now a refugee. In the east, the proportion is even higher. From the Chadian government’s perspective, the border is not merely a humanitarian abstraction. It is a sovereignty problem with direct security consequences. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

Yet closure brings its own risks. When formal crossings tighten, people do not necessarily stop moving. They move through less visible paths, often with more dependence on smugglers, informal guides, or armed actors who can extract rent and impose danger. For refugees, that can increase exposure to exploitation and violence. For the state, it can reduce visibility into who is crossing and why. For aid agencies, it can mean an even greater reliance on exemptions and ad hoc decisions. A policy intended to secure the frontier can make it harder to manage. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))

The strongest argument for the closure is that Chad cannot afford uncontrolled spillover from a war that is already producing drone strikes, incursions, and civilian casualties at the border. That concern is not hypothetical. MSF said in March 2026 that drone strikes by the Sudanese Armed Forces, the RSF, and allied groups were hitting civilian areas along the Chad-Sudan border, and that 457 people injured in attacks had been treated in Tine since early February. AP also reported in March that a drone strike near the border killed civilians and injured many more. In that context, Chad’s leaders may see a controlled border with humanitarian exceptions as the least-bad option. ([msf.org](https://www.msf.org/chad-tine-two-months-after-repeated-attacks-border?utm_source=openai))

But humanitarian exceptions cannot fully substitute for predictable transit. Relief systems need reliability, not only permissions. When access is granted case by case, it becomes slow, discretionary, and vulnerable to political pressure. That is why aid agencies keep stressing sustained access rather than one-off crossings. In a crisis this large, predictability is a form of humanitarian infrastructure. ([acaps.org](https://www.acaps.org/fileadmin/Data_Product/Main_media/20260322_ACAPS_Sudan_-_Analysis_of_humanitarian_access_in_the_Darfur_and_Kordofan_regions_.pdf?utm_source=openai))

From Darfur to the capital: how the war travels

One reason Sudan remains so hard to contain is that the war is not neatly divided into front lines and rear areas. Violence in Darfur reverberates in the center and capital region through displacement, price shocks, military redeployments, and bargaining among armed factions. As the conflict has entered its fourth year, the geography has become fluid, with attacks near borders affecting urban supply chains and local labor markets. That matters for Chad because the stability of eastern Sudan and western Chad are linked by movement in both directions: civilians flee, traders move goods, and armed actors exploit weak state presence. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/32a416bfbd680ea42edf6c0298d2617b?utm_source=openai))

That cross-border dynamic is one reason the conflict can be underestimated if it is measured only by battlefield gains. The more useful measure is the way it alters regional institutions and livelihood systems. A crossing opens or closes. A clinic runs out of supplies. A school becomes a shelter. A market price changes after a roadblock. A host community absorbs new arrivals without additional funding. In that sense, Sudan’s war is already a story about governance beyond Sudan. It is about whether regional institutions can manage the spillover before it becomes normal. ([unicef.org](https://www.unicef.org/chad/documents/eastern-chad-crisis-cost-inaction-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

The spillover is especially visible in the humanitarian system’s dependence on strategic nodes. Adré is one. Tine is another. Port Sudan remains a hub for UN operations even as violence elsewhere in the country continues. When those nodes are stressed, the response becomes less nimble and more politicized. That is why aid agencies continue to warn about access, funding, and the need for sustained diplomacy. It is also why regional diplomacy matters: a crossing is not just a road point, but an institution in miniature, where customs, security, sovereignty, and relief all collide. ([msf.org](https://www.msf.org/chad-tine-two-months-after-repeated-attacks-border?utm_source=openai))

The funding picture and what it means for the future

Sudan’s emergency is colliding with a broader aid-financing squeeze. AP reported in February 2026 that the United States and the United Nations launched a new Sudan Humanitarian Fund with $700 million in contributions, but that figure is far smaller than the overall requirement. UNHCR says its 2026 budget needs for Sudan and neighboring countries amount to $907 million, while UNICEF’s 2026 appeal for Sudan is $962.9 million. UNHCR’s regional Sudan response for neighboring countries has separate financial requirements that run into the hundreds of millions more, including $567.7 million for Chad alone in the 2026 regional plan. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/2a0ea03546391969bffd3ad845a6ee3f?utm_source=openai))

The result is a familiar but dangerous pattern: needs rise faster than commitments. UNHCR warned last week that more than a million Sudanese refugees in Chad face immediate cuts to food, water, shelter, protection, and health care unless a $428 million shortfall is met. The agency said the cuts could be drastic and that host communities continue to receive new arrivals despite immense strain. In Chad, the humanitarian problem is therefore no longer simply how many people are present; it is whether the system can continue to support those already there. ([unhcr.org](https://www.unhcr.org/africa/news/press-releases/funding-shortfalls-put-lifelines-risk-sudanese-refugees-chad?utm_source=openai))

Funding gaps also shape what happens after the emergency surge. Emergency food deliveries can keep people alive for a week or a month, but sustainable response requires roads, clinics, water systems, and local market recovery. If those are not funded, displacement becomes protracted. The emergency then stops being a temporary shock and becomes a baseline condition. That is the central risk along the Sudan-Chad border: not simply that more people cross, but that the region’s emergency architecture becomes permanent. UNICEF’s eastern Chad assessment makes this point by linking refugee influxes to school access, child protection, water systems, and health needs that will persist well beyond the immediate fighting. ([unicef.org](https://www.unicef.org/chad/documents/eastern-chad-crisis-cost-inaction-april-2026?utm_source=openai))

The health and disease dimension

The war’s border effects are also a public health story. WHO says Sudan’s health system is collapsing under the weight of attacks, non-functional facilities, disease outbreaks, and acute malnutrition. On April 14, WHO said more than 4 million people were estimated to be acutely malnourished in 2026. It also said outbreaks of malaria, dengue, measles, polio, hepatitis E, meningitis, and diphtheria were spreading across several states. When those conditions meet border displacement, the result is not just a refugee crisis but a regional health emergency. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-04-2026-after-three-years-of-conflict--sudan-faces-a-deeper-health-crisis?utm_source=openai))

Chad has already felt that pressure. WHO’s disease-outbreak reporting last year documented cholera in Chad, with the first suspected case in Dougui camp. UNICEF’s eastern Chad flash updates in late 2025 and April 2026 noted that cholera had affected health districts near the border and that refugees were arriving with few resources after fleeing border violence. The risk is cyclical: conflict drives displacement, displacement increases disease risk, disease weakens host communities, and weakened communities are less able to absorb the next wave of arrivals. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/southeastasia/outbreaks-and-emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2025-DON579?utm_source=openai))

That makes border policy even more sensitive. A closed crossing may reduce short-term security exposure, but it can also complicate public health logistics if it slows the movement of medical teams, supplies, and referrals. A more open border can support access but increase the challenge of screening and monitoring. The practical answer is not ideology; it is coordination. Chad, Sudan, UN agencies, and donors need mechanisms that allow security screening without strangling aid. That is a difficult balance to sustain when the broader war keeps generating new shocks. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))

The broader lesson: borders are now part of the conflict, not outside it

The most important thing to understand about Sudan in April 2026 is that the border effects are not collateral. They are central to the war’s shape and duration. Fighting generates displacement. Displacement reshapes borders. Border policy feeds back into the war by affecting supply lines, refuge, recruitment, trade, disease, and humanitarian access. That is true in Chad, but it is also true across the wider region, where Sudan’s war interacts with neighboring states’ politics and international agencies’ ability to operate. The border has become one of the war’s main theaters, even where tanks are absent. ([sudan.un.org](https://sudan.un.org/en?utm_source=openai))

There is a temptation to read this purely through a humanitarian lens, as though the only meaningful question were how to get food and medicine to civilians. That is necessary, but insufficient. The political question is whether Chad and its partners can maintain a border regime that protects sovereignty without collapsing humanitarian access. The institutional question is whether the UN system, donor governments, and regional actors can keep corridors open long enough to prevent mass suffering from turning into a permanent cross-border destabilization zone. And the strategic question is whether the parties to Sudan’s war will be allowed to externalize its costs indefinitely onto neighbors. ([sudan.un.org](https://sudan.un.org/en/313802-un-relief-chief-three-years-sudan-remains-test-world-failing?utm_source=openai))

For now, the answer is uncomfortable but clear: the war is outrunning the region’s capacity to contain it. Chad’s border closure reflects a real security concern, but it also exposes how little resilience remains in the system. Humanitarian corridors are narrowing, refugee pressures are rising, and aid budgets are not keeping pace. If the conflict continues to spill across borders, the region will face not just more displacement, but more border militarization, more aid politicization, and more local economies pulled into a war they did not start. That is the real World desk story: Sudan is not only producing a humanitarian catastrophe; it is remaking the border order of central and northeastern Africa. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/ceeccfabc33852c2aa641787a4ea2d82?utm_source=openai))
