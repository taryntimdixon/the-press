CinemaCon’s biggest takeaway: Hollywood is betting the box office still belongs to theaters

At CinemaCon in Las Vegas, Paramount’s David Ellison promised 45-day exclusivity, Disney rolled out major franchise footage, and studios used the annual gathering to argue that moviegoing still matters.

By The Press • April 17, 2026 • 1:54 a.m.

Hollywood’s annual sales pitch to theater owners is supposed to be part pep rally, part wishful thinking. This year’s CinemaCon, however, has landed at a moment when the argument for the big screen feels less theoretical than it has in years. Studios arrived in Las Vegas with a message that was unusually direct: after a long stretch of window erosion, streaming-first habits and post-pandemic uncertainty, they still believe theatrical release can be the center of the film business.

The clearest statement came from Paramount Skydance chief executive and chairman David Ellison, who told exhibitors on Thursday that the company will guarantee 30 releases a year between Paramount and Warner Bros. and will commit to a 45-day exclusive theatrical window starting immediately. Ellison framed the move as a pledge to theaters and ended with the kind of line CinemaCon is built to reward: “Long live the movies.” ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

It was more than a slogan. In an industry where release-window lengths have become a proxy for how much studios value theaters, a firm 45-day commitment is a notable signal. It suggests one major studio believes the middle ground between a brief theatrical run and a full streaming collapse still has commercial logic. It also puts pressure on rivals, especially as exhibitors continue to argue that shorter windows weaken the premium value proposition that drives ticket sales. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

A franchise parade, with a strategic point

Ellison’s pledge was only one part of a broader CinemaCon pattern. The studio presentations were packed with franchise-heavy footage and familiar titles, a reminder that Hollywood still leans on recognizable brands to motivate audiences and reassure theater owners. Disney’s presentation featured fresh footage from Avengers: Doomsday, with Robert Downey Jr. appearing to introduce the trailer, and an opening sequence from the new Star Wars film. At Sony, the crowd got an official title for the next Jumanji film, now called Jumanji: Open World. Warner Bros. previewed Practical Magic 2. Paramount also showcased an array of upcoming projects, including a concert film starring Billie Eilish and a new Ebenezer: A Christmas Carol adaptation. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b?utm_source=openai))

The common thread is obvious: studios are still betting that known intellectual property can pack auditoriums. But CinemaCon 2026 also exposed the tension inside that strategy. On one hand, the slate is increasingly franchise-driven. On the other, filmmakers and studio executives continue to make the case for original movies, suggesting that the industry knows it cannot survive on sequels alone. Steven Spielberg used his presentation for Disclosure Day to argue that Hollywood needs new stories or it will “run out of gas,” a warning that felt less like a flourish than a thesis statement for the week. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/steven-spielberg-hollywood-original-movies-disclosure-day-cinemacon-35463/))

Why the window debate matters now

The return of a firmer theatrical window matters because the box office has been showing signs of life. AP reported earlier this month that 2026’s domestic box office was off to its strongest start since the pandemic, a trend that gives theater chains a stronger hand in window negotiations. If more titles can prove they have legs in cinemas, studios have more reason to keep them there long enough to turn premium screens, repeat viewing and word of mouth into durable revenue.

That is why Ellison’s announcement resonated beyond Paramount’s own slate. A 45-day exclusivity promise is not just a distribution tweak; it is a bet that audiences still need a meaningful reason to buy a ticket rather than wait a couple of weeks for a home option. It is also a way to reassure exhibitors who have spent several years watching the theatrical experience get treated as a marketing phase rather than a distinct commercial run. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

The move comes as studios are trying to balance competing realities. They want the scale and prestige of theatrical openings for tentpoles, but they also want the downstream certainty of streaming and digital sales. A 45-day window is a compromise that aims to preserve theatrical value without delaying the wider ecosystem too long. Whether it becomes a meaningful industry norm again will depend on whether enough films can justify it at the box office. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

The bigger message from Las Vegas

CinemaCon is often dismissed as a trade-show echo chamber, but this year’s edition is arriving at a consequential moment for the business. Studios are showing off crowded slates, theater owners are looking for evidence of long-term commitment, and the biggest executives are using the stage to argue that the theatrical model is still worth defending. AP’s coverage of the Paramount presentation made clear that Ellison’s pitch was not just about one company’s schedule; it was about confidence in the moviegoing model itself. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

That confidence is being tested from both directions. On one side is the growth of streaming, which has conditioned audiences to expect fast access at home. On the other is the continuing dominance of franchises, which can make the business seem safer while narrowing the range of movies that receive major theatrical support. The best-case scenario for theaters is not simply more sequels; it is a market where studios believe audiences will still turn out for spectacle, star power and original ideas when the films are given room to build. Spielberg made that case explicitly, and Ellison backed it with a policy change. ([au.variety.com](https://au.variety.com/2026/film/news/steven-spielberg-hollywood-original-movies-disclosure-day-cinemacon-35463/))

For now, CinemaCon 2026 has produced something Hollywood rarely gets in one place: a public, high-profile statement of faith in theatrical exhibition. Whether it lasts will depend on the slate that follows, the audiences that show up, and how many studios decide that 45 days is not a relic of the past but a workable future for the movies. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))
