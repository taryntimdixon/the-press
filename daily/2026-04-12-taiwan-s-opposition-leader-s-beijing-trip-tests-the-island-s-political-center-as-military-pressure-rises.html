Taiwan’s opposition leader’s Beijing trip tests the island’s political center as military pressure rises

Cheng Li-wun’s meeting with Xi Jinping comes as Taipei struggles over defense spending, Washington worries about deterrence, and Beijing seeks to turn cross-strait politics into its own diplomatic advantage.

By The Press • April 12, 2026 • 12:58 a.m.

For years, Beijing has treated Taiwan’s politics as a battlefield of speeches, symbolism and pressure points. This month, it found a particularly useful opening: the chair of Taiwan’s main opposition party, Cheng Li-wun, traveled to mainland China and met Chinese leader Xi Jinping in Beijing, calling the trip a mission for peace even as critics in Taipei warned that it risks validating Beijing’s preferred script on unification. The timing could hardly have been more charged. Taiwan is debating a delayed defense budget, the island remains under constant Chinese military pressure, and major powers around the region are reading every cross-strait gesture for clues about the balance between deterrence and dialogue. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

The visit matters not because it changes Taiwan’s formal political status — it does not — but because it exposes a deeper struggle over who gets to define peace. In Beijing, Xi used his rare meeting with Cheng to repeat that China would never tolerate Taiwanese independence, while signaling that the Communist Party sees political outreach to opposition figures as a way to widen divisions in Taipei. In Taiwan, President Lai Ching-te has urged the opposition to support a special defense package, arguing that security is a prerequisite for any sustainable diplomacy. That clash — between a Beijing-backed narrative of “peaceful reunification” and a Taiwanese democratic debate over how to preserve de facto self-rule — is now one of Asia’s most consequential geopolitical fault lines. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9735f829b2d9d68525ad192253e47fac?utm_source=openai))

A carefully staged message from Beijing

Cheng’s mainland trip was not a casual party exchange. It was choreographed as a political signal. Chinese state media and party outlets framed the visit as evidence that there is still room for dialogue with Taiwan if Taipei’s leaders are willing to move closer to Beijing’s political red lines. The message to audiences in Taiwan was even more pointed: Beijing wants to show that cross-strait engagement can bypass the ruling Democratic Progressive Party and work through alternative channels. That is not new, but the optics are powerful when the guest is the leader of the island’s largest opposition force. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

Beijing’s strategy is familiar to anyone who watches the cross-strait file. China combines military coercion with selective politicking. Warplanes and naval vessels patrol near Taiwan on an almost daily basis; drills appear at moments of sensitivity; and then, alongside the coercion, there are invitations, meetings and carefully chosen applause lines for any Taiwanese figure willing to speak of reconciliation. That dual track aims to isolate Taiwan’s governing party domestically, weaken confidence in external backing and present Beijing as the only actor capable of setting the terms of de-escalation. The Cheng visit fits neatly into that approach. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

Xi’s comments during the meeting were unsurprising in substance but important in timing. By restating that Taiwan independence is incompatible with peace, he reaffirmed the Communist Party’s longstanding position while linking political moderation to acceptance of Beijing’s framework. The practical implication is clear: China is not offering neutral mediation. It is offering peace on conditions that amount to political subordination. That distinction matters, because it is exactly what Taiwanese critics say Cheng’s visit risks obscuring. ([lemonde.fr](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/04/12/taiwan-s-main-opposition-party-aligns-more-closely-with-beijing-in-the-name-of-peace_6752326_4.html?utm_source=openai))

Taipei’s defense debate is the real vulnerability

The larger political problem for Taiwan is not Cheng’s trip alone. It is that the trip lands in the middle of a domestic argument over defense readiness that Beijing can exploit. Taiwan’s defense ministry has said a delay in approving this year’s budget threatens tens of billions of New Taiwan dollars in procurement, maintenance and training, with officials warning that the delay would affect a large share of spending under the original schedule. President Lai has said the island must raise defense spending above 3 percent of GDP as part of a broader military overhaul. Those numbers are not abstract bookkeeping. They are a signal to Washington, Beijing and Taiwan’s own public that deterrence still matters. ([taipeitimes.com](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2026/04/03/2003854950?utm_source=openai))

That defense debate is especially sensitive because Taiwan’s security relationship with the United States depends not only on arms sales and congressional support, but also on the credibility of Taiwan’s own budgetary choices. When opposition lawmakers slow or shrink defense plans, they invite questions about whether the island is serious about its own survival strategy. The result is a political trap: a party can claim to be pursuing peace by resisting higher defense spending, but that posture can also create precisely the vulnerabilities Beijing is hoping to see. ([taiwannews.com.tw](https://www.taiwannews.com.tw/news/6293101?utm_source=openai))

For the Lai administration, the challenge is to persuade voters that deterrence is not provocation. That is always difficult in Taiwan, where the electorate is deeply divided over how much risk is worth taking to maintain the status quo. But the stakes are higher now because Beijing’s military posture has become more normalized. When pressure is constant, each political gesture is read against a background of threat. Cheng’s Beijing trip therefore resonates beyond party politics: it reinforces the sense that Taiwan’s internal divisions are now part of the regional security picture. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

Why the United States is watching closely

Washington has reason to be alert. U.S. policy toward Taiwan rests on a delicate balance: support the island’s ability to defend itself, avoid encouraging unilateral moves that could trigger crisis, and maintain enough ambiguity to deter Beijing without forcing immediate confrontation. When a major Taiwanese opposition leader meets Xi in Beijing, U.S. officials must ask whether cross-strait politics are becoming more vulnerable to manipulation. At the same time, the U.S. also wants Taiwan to invest more in its own defense, which makes the budget fight in Taipei more than a local legislative dispute. It becomes a test of whether the island can sustain the burden of deterrence. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b15c2dc87297627a2ac12ea344fe94b8?utm_source=openai))

The timing is especially sensitive because the broader Indo-Pacific environment is already under strain. China’s military activity around Taiwan has become routine enough to risk desensitizing regional audiences, while allies and partners are trying to understand how far Beijing is willing to push before the costs rise too high. Every visit, drill and budget fight can alter that calculus a little. Cheng’s trip, in that sense, is less about a single meeting than about what it tells foreign capitals: Beijing believes it can shape Taiwan’s politics from the outside, and Taiwan’s own political fractures may help it do so. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

Peace rhetoric, power politics

Cheng says her engagement is about peace and livelihoods. That argument has an audience in Taiwan. After all, many Taiwanese citizens want stability more than symbolism, and not everyone sees hard-line language as the safest route through a dangerous era. But Beijing’s definition of peace is not the same as Taiwan’s common understanding of it. For Beijing, peace means reduced resistance to unification. For Taipei’s democratic mainstream, peace means preserving the island’s autonomy without inviting war. Those are not compatible endpoints. They only overlap long enough for diplomats and politicians to claim progress. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

This is why the symbolism of Cheng’s trip has outsize importance. Beijing can present it as evidence that even Taiwanese opponents of the ruling party are willing to engage on China’s terms. Taiwan’s critics, meanwhile, see an attempt to blur the line between dialogue and capitulation. The resulting ambiguity is useful to Beijing and destabilizing for Taipei. It gives Chinese officials material to argue that Taiwanese politics are more divided than they appear, and that public opinion can be peeled away from firm resistance through selective engagement. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9735f829b2d9d68525ad192253e47fac?utm_source=openai))

The lesson for other governments is not that dialogue is dangerous. It is that dialogue without a clear strategic framework can become a tool of coercion. That is true in cross-strait politics and in many other disputes. A negotiation is not automatically a concession; nor is an outreach gesture automatically a breakthrough. Context matters. Here, the context is a military balance tilted by China’s superior force, a democratic system under pressure, and a regional power competition in which every local decision carries international consequences. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

What comes next

Over the coming weeks, the real test will be whether Cheng’s visit becomes an isolated episode or the start of a more sustained opposition-to-Beijing channel. If it is the latter, Taipei will face a harder internal debate about what constitutes legitimate engagement and where the line lies between practical communication and political capture. If the trip fizzles, it may still leave a mark by sharpening public suspicion of China-friendly messaging at a time when defense spending is already under strain. ([taipeitimes.com](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2026/04/03/2003854950?utm_source=openai))

Either way, the episode underscores a basic geopolitical truth: China’s Taiwan strategy is not only about missiles, aircraft and naval drills. It is also about persuasion, division and the management of democratic uncertainty. Cheng Li-wun’s Beijing stop gave that strategy a fresh stage. Taiwan’s response — in parliament, in the presidential office and in public opinion — will tell us whether the island can keep its political center strong enough to resist it. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/03e3a4a320cdd18152cf17639bf83be4?utm_source=openai))

For now, the region’s most important cross-strait question is not whether Taiwan and China can talk. They can. It is whether talk can be separated from pressure, and whether any peace formula that requires Taiwan to accept Beijing’s sovereignty claims can ever be sold as anything other than capitulation. Cheng’s trip did not answer that question. It made it harder to avoid. ([lemonde.fr](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/04/12/taiwan-s-main-opposition-party-aligns-more-closely-with-beijing-in-the-name-of-peace_6752326_4.html?utm_source=openai))
