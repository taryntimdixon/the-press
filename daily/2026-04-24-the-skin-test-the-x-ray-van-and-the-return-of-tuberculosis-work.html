The Skin Test, the X-Ray Van, and the Return of Tuberculosis Work

CDC’s provisional 2025 figures show U.S. tuberculosis easing slightly from 2024 while remaining above pre-pandemic levels. The sharper 2026 health story is the labor-heavy prevention system now visible again around latent infection, contact tracing, school clearance, shelter screening, chest-center care, and the slow administrative work required to keep future disease from becoming present-tense contagion.

Written and Researched by AI • April 24, 2026 • 3:54 p.m.

For months, much U.S. health coverage has treated tuberculosis as either an old disease that occasionally erupts into newsworthiness or a national line item that appears on World TB Day and then slips back out of sight. The current picture is harder than either frame. CDC’s provisional 2025 report counted 10,260 U.S. tuberculosis cases, down slightly from 10,395 in 2024, with the national rate edging down to 3.0 cases per 100,000 people. That is movement in a better direction, but not a return to the pre-pandemic trajectory. CDC was explicit that tuberculosis in 2025 remained above pre-pandemic levels after annual increases from 2021 through 2024.

That combination matters. A small national decline can be true at the same time that local health departments are doing a great deal of work because the country is still operating above the baseline it had before COVID-19 disrupted routine care, migration patterns, diagnosis, and follow-up. Tuberculosis control does not only happen in the isolation room after a person with active pulmonary disease is identified. Much of it happens earlier and elsewhere: in the blood draw for an interferon-gamma release assay, in the chest X-ray ordered to rule out active disease after a positive screening test, in the list of classroom contacts who need retesting weeks later, in the outreach event at a shelter, in the multilingual counseling visit where someone who does not feel sick is asked to start preventive treatment anyway, and in the treatment log that determines whether an apparent public-health success will hold.

That is the more revealing U.S. tuberculosis story in April 2026. It is not best understood as a single national spike, and not honestly described as a solved historical problem either. It is a story about the return of tuberculosis work: targeted screening, contact investigation, school clearance, mobile imaging, chest-center care, preventive regimens, and prolonged follow-up among people who may have infection without disease. The country’s TB burden is still low by global standards. The effort required to keep it that way is not low-effort at all.

Seen up close, tuberculosis in 2026 is less a drama of dramatic rescue than an accumulation of ordinary public-health tasks. It asks whether a local department can identify who was exposed, whether community clinicians know what to report, whether schools can manage repeat testing without panic, whether shelters and temporary housing sites can become points of diagnosis instead of delay, whether patients can complete treatment even when they feel well, and whether systems can keep people connected long enough for prevention to count. It is a disease of airborne transmission, but also of lists, forms, calls, appointments, and repeat visits.

A national decline that is not reassurance by itself

CDC’s provisional 2025 report, released March 23, 2026 and publicized around World TB Day on March 24, is important partly because it resists easy interpretation. The headline number moved down by about 1% from 2024, and CDC said rates declined slightly among both U.S.-born and non-U.S.-born people. But the same report also emphasized that the United States remained above pre-pandemic levels. That is the line that prevents the data from being read as a simple return to normal.

The agency’s framing has also remained consistent: reducing tuberculosis requires a dual approach. Active TB disease must be diagnosed and treated quickly to reduce illness and prevent transmission. Latent TB infection must also be tested for and treated among people at increased risk, because untreated latent infection is the reservoir from which many future U.S. TB cases emerge. CDC’s latent TB estimates page states that more than 80% of U.S. TB cases result from longstanding untreated latent infection. That single fact is one reason the labor of prevention remains so important even when national case counts are relatively modest.

This is what makes tuberculosis different from the way many people intuitively picture infectious-disease control. The present caseload is not the whole problem. Someone can carry latent infection for years, have no symptoms, and still face a future risk of progressing to active disease. Public health therefore has to do two things at once on different timelines: stop transmission now and interrupt tomorrow’s disease before it starts. Those are linked tasks, but they do not happen in the same places or rely on the same kind of public attention.

It also means the most consequential national number may be less dramatic than many headlines prefer. A country can post only a small decline in confirmed active cases and still be doing a vast amount of tuberculosis prevention work. Conversely, a low-incidence country can let the visibility of the disease decline faster than the infrastructure needed to control it. TB elimination is not a matter of passive background improvement. It depends on continued partnerships among health departments, clinicians, and communities, exactly as CDC says in its 2025 provisional release.

Why the harder story is latent infection

Active tuberculosis is easier to explain. It is transmissible, reportable, and often associated in the public mind with cough, weight loss, fever, and a clear need for urgent treatment. Latent TB infection is harder because it asks people to act before illness arrives. The person may feel normal. A chest X-ray may be normal. The intervention can seem abstract. Yet from a prevention perspective, latent infection is where much of the decisive work sits.

CDC’s published estimates stress that millions of people in the United States have latent TB infection and that latent infection is not evenly distributed. The agency also notes that latent TB infection is not nationally notifiable in the way active TB disease is, though some states require reporting. That mismatch is part of the problem. The public-health burden is real even when it is less visible in ordinary case dashboards.

Preventive treatment only works if people can move through a long chain of steps: targeted testing, interpretation, rule-out of active disease, counseling, treatment start, management of side effects or drug interactions, and completion. If any link breaks, the elegant logic of prevention collapses back into a missed opportunity. That is why local programs increasingly emphasize shorter regimens, low- or no-cost services, telehealth, and on-site care. They are not conveniences added at the margins. They are attempts to convert eligibility into completion.

The problem is not merely clinical. It is social and administrative. People asked to take treatment for latent infection may be balancing work schedules, childcare, transportation limits, unstable housing, language barriers, immigration fears, or distrust of institutions. Tuberculosis programs know this, which is why the work keeps returning to outreach, navigation, and repetitive follow-up. Public health often sounds abstract until one notices how concrete the obstacles are: who can get to radiology, who can answer a call from the clinic during work hours, who can return for labs, who can safely store medication, who understands that a positive TB blood test does not mean immediate contagiousness but does matter enough to treat.

CDC’s fiscal 2026 congressional justification helps show where the system still sees weakness. Among the program performance measures highlighted for TB are treatment completion and the reporting of initial drug-susceptibility results for culture-positive cases. Those metrics matter because tuberculosis control is never just about identifying people. It is about getting all the way through the response.

What local health departments are actually doing

The clearest way to understand the current U.S. TB moment is to look at what local and state programs have been saying this spring. Their statements reveal not a generalized national emergency, but a strikingly hands-on, labor-intensive pattern of prevention and follow-up.

San Francisco offered one of the most concrete examples. In a March 4, 2026 health update, the San Francisco Department of Public Health described an outbreak associated with Archbishop Riordan High School. As of February 24, the department had received results for 1,359 students and staff, about 96% of the school population. Four active TB cases had been laboratory-confirmed in people associated with the school since November 2025, and three additional suspected cases were still awaiting final culture results. Among those screened, 227 people had a positive TB test, and 207 had confirmed latent TB infection, with additional chest X-ray work in process for others with positive tests.

Those figures are notable, but the workflow is the more revealing part. San Francisco required repeat TB evaluation for all students and staff affiliated with the school during the week of March 16, 2026, regardless of prior test results or symptoms. Students and staff were encouraged to use on-campus mass screening, while outside clinicians were told they played a critical role in timely identification of active disease, evaluation for latent infection, school and work clearance, and prompt reporting. The department also stated that all confirmed or suspected active TB cases were on treatment with health department monitoring of directly observed therapy. That is tuberculosis control as administrative choreography: repeat testing, documentation, clinical triage, reporting, and a temporary but highly specific prevention bureaucracy built around one school community.

New York City shows the same pattern at metropolitan scale. In its March 24, 2026 World TB Day announcement, the city reported 743 TB cases in 2025, an 11% decrease from 2024. More important than the year-over-year decline was what the city described doing in response. The Health Department said it had begun offering a new shorter latent-TB treatment regimen at its chest centers in early 2025, reducing preventive treatment for eligible patients from 3–9 months to 1 month. It said 337 patients started that regimen in 2025 and 75% had completed treatment by mid-February 2026. The department also highlighted expanded mobile X-ray services for transient populations and other groups facing barriers to care, telehealth in many languages, and no-cost testing and treatment regardless of immigration or insurance status. More than half of the city’s confirmed TB patients in 2025 received care at a Health Department TB Chest Center.

That is not the infrastructure of a disease relegated to history. It is the infrastructure of a city that still expects tuberculosis control to depend on accessible institutions: chest centers, mobile imaging, same-day testing and medication, language access, and regular contact with people who may otherwise fall out of care. New York’s chest-center page makes that institutional logic even clearer by stating that TB-related care, including blood work, sputum collection, X-ray, and medication, is provided at no cost.

Hawaiʻi offers another angle on the same national pattern. In observance of World TB Day, the Hawaiʻi Department of Health said the state had 125 diagnosed active TB cases in 2025, up from 117 in 2024 and 92 in 2020, and that Hawaiʻi continues to have the second-highest TB case rate in the country. The state emphasized screening, treatment to prevent disease, and contact investigation, and it specifically identified immigrants, migrants, people in group shelter settings including homeless shelters and prisons, and people with diabetes, chronic kidney disease, or weakened immune systems as populations that may benefit from targeted efforts. Examinations and treatment, the department said, are available free of charge.

Other public-health agencies have made similar points, even if with less detail. Allegheny County, New Jersey, and South Carolina all used World TB Day messaging in March 2026 to underscore that testing, treatment, and awareness still matter and that vigilance remains necessary. Those releases do not all contribute major new epidemiologic detail, but together they show a broad official consensus: tuberculosis prevention still depends on active local program work, not residual historical inertia.

Why targeted screening matters more than generic alarm

Tuberculosis is not evenly distributed, and the public-health strategy reflects that fact. CDC’s provisional 2025 report again showed higher burden among non-U.S.-born people than among U.S.-born people, even as both groups saw slight declines from 2024 to 2025. The point is not to turn country of birth into a simplistic explanation. It is to recognize that TB risk clusters around particular exposure histories, arrival patterns, medical vulnerabilities, and living conditions, so testing has to be concentrated where it can actually find infection and support treatment.

San Francisco’s school outbreak guidance makes this practical rather than theoretical. Clinicians were told to assess not only school exposure but also additional epidemiologic and medical risk factors, including immunosuppression, birth or residence in a country with elevated TB rates, close contact with a confirmed or suspected case, and current or past congregate living in settings such as shelters and jails. Hawaiʻi’s health department likewise identified immigrants, migrants, group shelters, prisons, and certain chronic conditions as areas of emphasis.

This matches long-standing CDC guidance and the agency’s latent infection estimates, which point toward concentrated rather than universal screening. It also aligns with WHO and PAHO’s more recent emphasis on people-centered, primary-care-linked TB responses. PAHO said in March 2026 that an estimated 350,000 people fell ill with TB in the Americas in 2024 and around 77,000 were not diagnosed. Its argument was not for indiscriminate testing everywhere, but for bringing diagnosis and treatment closer to communities and reducing gaps in access. In U.S. terms, that often means the difference between a person reaching a chest center or mobile X-ray unit and a person never getting past the idea that TB care is bureaucratically or socially out of reach.

Targeted screening is easy to reduce to jargon, but it is really the operational heart of tuberculosis prevention. Programs have finite personnel and finite trust. They cannot test everybody, and broad untargeted screening can create noise without enough clinical follow-through. The challenge is to identify where testing has the highest yield and where the support needed for treatment completion can realistically be supplied. Tuberculosis work is therefore not merely about diagnostic technology. It is about judgment, location, and relationships.

The institutional settings where TB control becomes visible again

If tuberculosis was once popularly associated with sanatoria, its modern American settings are more fragmented and more ordinary: schools, shelters, jails, temporary housing sites, immigration-linked health screenings, chest centers, urgent care offices, and primary-care clinics that may only occasionally encounter the disease. That fragmentation is one reason so much TB control work now takes the form of coordination.

The San Francisco outbreak update is a useful case study because it shows how many institutions a single event can pull into one chain. A school must manage attendance and clearance. Families must navigate testing and repeat evaluation. Community clinicians must determine when to order blood tests or chest imaging and when to report. A health department must define the exposed population, track who has been reached, identify who needs another round of testing, and monitor active cases on therapy. The outbreak produces not only infection-control work but documentation work: result collection, lists of negatives and positives, X-ray status, return-to-school clearance, and future reassessments.

New York’s system shows what happens when that kind of tuberculosis work is institutionalized rather than improvised. Dedicated chest centers reduce friction because TB-related care is gathered in one place. Mobile X-ray services reduce friction because the imaging comes closer to the people who need it. Telehealth in multiple languages reduces friction because not every follow-up requires a new physical visit and not every patient can navigate English-only care. The city’s description of more than 30,000 people receiving on-site TB-related services between December 2022 and December 2025 in shelters, humanitarian emergency response centers, and other temporary housing sites is especially important. It suggests that some of the most consequential TB infrastructure in 2026 is built around populations whose contact with mainstream healthcare is inconsistent.

This is also why tuberculosis belongs in the same broader conversation as access to care, housing instability, and administrative burden. A disease that can be prevented through testing and treatment still becomes a systems problem when the people at higher risk are least well served by ordinary clinic routines. Public-health programs know that. Their operating choices in 2026 reflect it.

Surveillance is more than counting cases

It is tempting to think of surveillance as a national number released once a year. Tuberculosis does not allow that simplification. Effective TB surveillance is case-based, longitudinal, and deeply tied to service delivery. WHO’s 2024 surveillance guidance stresses standardized definitions, digital case-based surveillance, core data items, and data quality. That sounds technical, but the underlying reason is straightforward: TB control depends on knowing who was screened, who converted from negative to positive, who started treatment, who finished it, and where likely transmission occurred.

San Francisco’s outbreak document captures that logic in miniature. The health department asked clinicians not simply to evaluate patients, but to report screened individuals and provide supporting documentation. Negative results matter because they define who has already been reached and who may need repeat testing later. Positive results matter because they trigger the next steps in ruling out disease and pursuing treatment. In outbreak work, the list of unaffected people is also part of the control strategy.

WHO’s new guidance also emphasizes digital, case-based surveillance. The point is not digitalization for its own sake. It is to make sure the full pathway from screening to treatment outcome can be tracked and used for planning and quality improvement. Tuberculosis is especially demanding here because its timelines are long. Someone exposed today may not convert on a screening test immediately. Someone with latent infection may start a regimen but only complete it weeks later. Someone moving between jurisdictions may need continuity of care that spans institutions and, in some cases, borders. CDC’s MMWR index for 2026 includes a report on continuity of care for patients with tuberculosis relocating to other countries through the CureTB program, a reminder that TB surveillance and treatment management often extend beyond a single local setting.

CDC’s own provisional 2025 report should also be read in this context. The agency emphasizes that the provisional data reflect reports received as of February 12, 2026 and that the final annual surveillance report comes later. That is not mere bureaucratic caveat. It is a signal that TB data are produced through ongoing national-state-local reporting systems whose completeness and timing matter.

How post-pandemic disruptions still shape the picture

One reason the current TB story remains unsettled is that the pandemic period changed multiple underlying conditions at once. Care-seeking shifted. Respiratory symptoms were filtered through COVID-first assumptions. Immigration and travel patterns changed and then rebounded. Public-health staffing was strained. Tuberculosis services, like much of public health, had to recover from a period in which routine prevention did not always move smoothly.

A March 2026 CDC Emerging Infectious Diseases paper examining tuberculosis in the United States before and during the COVID-19 pandemic adds useful nuance here. The authors found that TB case counts increased annually after 2021 and noted evidence consistent with migration-related factors playing a role in post-2020 changes, including increases in diagnoses among some recently arrived non-U.S.-born populations. The paper also cited California research suggesting missed opportunities to diagnose TB when clinicians tested for COVID-19 alone in people presenting with respiratory symptoms during periods of high COVID incidence. That does not reduce the resurgence story to any single cause. It does underline that tuberculosis trends since 2020 were shaped by both epidemiology and healthcare-system behavior.

This matters because it complicates triumphal or alarmist narratives alike. Some part of the elevated post-pandemic burden may reflect delayed diagnosis or interrupted routine prevention rather than wholly new transmission patterns. Some part may reflect changes in the composition and timing of arrivals among populations with higher baseline risk. Some part may reflect better re-engagement with care. The practical implication is the same: health departments cannot simply assume that a small decline in 2025 means the harder work is over.

Shorter regimens, mobile services, and the politics of completion

When local officials highlight shorter preventive treatment regimens, mobile X-ray services, or no-cost chest-center care, they are not talking mainly about convenience. They are talking about completion. Screening that does not lead to completed preventive treatment is only partially successful. Diagnostic access that requires too many visits will lose people. A public-health system that asks high-risk populations to overcome every logistical barrier on their own is effectively selecting for noncompletion.

That is why New York City’s shorter one-month latent-TB regimen for eligible patients is more than a clinical footnote. It shrinks the burden of preventive care and may improve the odds that a person finishes what they start. It is also why mobile imaging matters. The city’s expanding mobile X-ray services target transient populations and groups facing barriers to care because a radiology order is not the same thing as a completed image. Similarly, Hawaiʻi’s emphasis on free examinations and treatment is a practical attempt to remove one major barrier from a process already full of others.

The logic is visible in federal and international guidance as well. WHO in 2025 consolidated guidance on diagnosis of TB infection, disease, and drug resistance, and in March 2026 recommended near-point-of-care tests, tongue swabs, and sputum pooling for diagnosis in some settings. Those developments are globally oriented and not directly interchangeable with U.S. local practice, but they point in the same direction: reducing delay and burden matters because speed and accessibility influence whether people are diagnosed and treated at all.

PAHO’s March 2026 World TB Day framing likewise emphasized primary care as the route to a more accessible, people-centered response. In the Americas, the organization said, tens of thousands of people with TB were not diagnosed in 2024. In a low-incidence country like the United States, that kind of regional warning translates into a local question: can public-health and clinical systems get close enough to the populations at risk to convert policy into uptake?

Access, trust, and the wider policy environment

Tuberculosis programs often provide services regardless of insurance or immigration status, and public agencies regularly stress that point. New York City’s chest centers do. Hawaiʻi does. But the surrounding policy climate still matters because patients do not experience disease-control programs in isolation from the rest of the state.

KFF’s January 2026 analysis of Medicaid issues to watch this year notes that broader immigration policies, including changes to public-charge rules, new agreements for CMS to share Medicaid data with DHS and ICE, and visible immigration enforcement activity, could result in fewer legal immigrants obtaining or maintaining Medicaid coverage. KFF also notes that the 2025 reconciliation law included restrictions affecting some lawfully present immigrants and more frequent eligibility redeterminations for ACA expansion adults. None of that proves a direct tuberculosis effect. It does, however, support a narrower and more defensible point: access to preventive care can become more fragile when people fear official systems or lose coverage continuity.

For tuberculosis, that fragility has obvious consequences. Someone who is hesitant to engage with healthcare may delay screening after exposure. Someone who loses coverage or assumes they have lost eligibility may not realize a health department chest center remains open to them. Someone worried about data-sharing or enforcement may avoid follow-up until symptoms worsen. The issue is not whether TB services nominally exist. It is whether the people who most need them feel able to use them in time.

This is one reason tuberculosis remains a revealing systems story. It sits at the intersection of communicable-disease control and social trust. The pathogen may be ancient. The practical barriers to prevention are contemporary.

Reasons for cautious optimism, and reasons not to relax

There are real reasons to resist fatalism. The United States did not post another national increase in 2025. Cities such as New York reported lower case counts while expanding lower-friction treatment and screening models. San Francisco’s school response demonstrates that local departments can still mobilize large-scale contact investigation and repeat testing. Hawaiʻi’s program messaging shows continued willingness to target resources where burden remains high. CDC continues to frame TB elimination around the linked priorities of prompt treatment for disease and preventive treatment for latent infection rather than pretending one can substitute for the other.

There is also evidence that outbreak investigation is worth the effort. A CDC Emerging Infectious Diseases analysis published in 2025 modeled the impact and cost-effectiveness of tuberculosis outbreak investigations in the United States during 2023–2032 and concluded that those activities play a critical role in curbing community transmission by promptly detecting and treating TB disease and latent infection among contacts. Outbreak investigation is laborious precisely because it does useful work.

But none of that justifies complacency. A 1% national decline after several years of increase is not restoration. Local intensity can remain high even when national numbers stabilize. The populations most affected often face the greatest administrative and social obstacles to completing care. And tuberculosis control remains vulnerable to staffing strain, reporting gaps, and the long timelines inherent in prevention. CDC’s own performance measures show that treatment completion and some reporting targets are not trivial boxes already checked.

The most realistic interpretation of the 2026 TB moment is therefore mixed. The U.S. response still has functioning tools. Some jurisdictions are adapting them in promising ways. Yet the country remains dependent on quiet institutional effort to keep a low-incidence disease from becoming a more visible one.

The broader lesson of the current TB moment

Tuberculosis is often described as a social disease with a medical treatment. In the United States in 2026, it also looks like a prevention system with a paperwork core. That is not a dismissal of medicine. It is a recognition that treatment and prevention are delivered through documentation, coordination, and persistence: the report sent to the health department, the contact list that keeps changing, the repeat-test reminder, the chest X-ray order, the school clearance form, the multilingual counseling visit, the telehealth follow-up, the directly observed therapy record, the notation that a patient completed the regimen rather than merely received it.

Other infectious-disease stories have accustomed the public to dashboards, emergency declarations, and visible surges. Tuberculosis remains instructive because it reveals a different kind of state capacity. It asks whether institutions can do disciplined, repetitive work for long enough to prevent tomorrow’s cases. It asks whether a school outbreak can be managed without losing track of exposed students who feel fine. It asks whether a city can keep a chest center accessible to people regardless of insurance or immigration status. It asks whether mobile populations can still be reached by screening and follow-up. It asks whether health departments can gather enough information to know not just how many cases they had, but which interventions actually changed the next month’s risk.

In that sense, the most important tuberculosis artifacts of spring 2026 are not dramatic ones. They are the textured materials of ordinary public health: the skin-test record, the IGRA result, the X-ray sleeve, the treatment log, the return-to-school clearance, the list of contacts to call again. They are evidence that the country is still attempting the kind of prevention that succeeds by making the visible crisis fail to arrive.

CDC’s provisional data say the United States remains above pre-pandemic tuberculosis levels even after a slight decline in 2025. Local departments are responding not with declarations of defeat, but with more screening access, more outbreak follow-up, more low-friction care, and more emphasis on latent infection treatment. The disease has not vanished into the archive. It has reappeared in the institutional places where either the quiet work continues or the later bill comes due.
