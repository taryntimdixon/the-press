A Batch Record Met an AI Agent. FDA Asked Who Checked It.

The warning letter that put AI overreliance into a drug-manufacturing deficiency is not an anti-AI manifesto. It is a labor-and-safety message: in regulated work, the machine can draft, but a qualified quality unit still owns the release.

By The Press • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The most important AI sentence in Washington this month did not come from a model company, a chipmaker or a Senate hearing. It appeared in a Food and Drug Administration warning letter to a small Michigan firm that made homeopathic drug products and cosmetics.

During an October 2025 inspection of Purolea Cosmetics Lab in Livonia, FDA investigators found insects, filth, leaves, clutter, inadequate separation from outside contamination, missing microbiological testing, inadequate component testing, weak quality-unit oversight and unapproved drug claims for products marketed for shingles and genital herpes relief. Those findings would have been enough to make the April 2, 2026, letter serious. But one section gave the case broader significance. Under the heading Inappropriate Use of Artificial Intelligence in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, FDA said the company had used AI agents to create drug product specifications, procedures and master production or control records intended to help it comply with FDA requirements. When investigators raised the absence of process validation before distribution, the firm’s answer, as described by the agency, was that it had not known validation was legally required because the AI agent had not told it so.

That is a small-company fact pattern with large-institution consequences. The Purolea letter appears, based on FDA’s own public record and early trade and legal analysis, to be the agency’s first warning letter to treat a company’s use of AI as a compliance tool as a named current good manufacturing practice deficiency. The agency did not say drugmakers may not use AI. It said something more practical and more durable: if a company uses AI to help create procedures, specifications or other cGMP records, an authorized human quality representative must review and clear the output. The legal responsibility remains where it was before the prompt was typed: with the manufacturer, its quality unit and its documented control system.

That distinction matters because AI is moving into the most paperwork-heavy layers of regulated work. The public story of artificial intelligence is often told through chatbots, creative tools, chips and power demand. Inside pharmaceutical manufacturing, the faster change is duller and more consequential: procedure drafting, deviation summaries, complaint triage, supplier qualification notes, batch-record review, inspection preparation, regulatory intelligence and quality-risk assessment. These are not glamorous tasks. They are the administrative spine that lets a firm say a product was made under control, by trained people, using qualified materials, validated processes and records that can be reconstructed months or years later.

The FDA letter is therefore not just about whether one AI agent hallucinated a rule. It is about where accountability sits when regulated organizations use software to summarize, draft and recommend. In ordinary office work, a bad AI answer can mean embarrassment, rework or a wrong slide. In drug manufacturing, a bad answer can become a missing test, an inadequate procedure, a batch released without proper evidence or a quality unit that mistakes fluent language for compliance. The new compliance question is not whether the machine sounds authoritative. It is whether the firm can prove who checked it, against what source, under which procedure, with what authority to stop production.

The letter is narrow. The signal is not.

Purolea is not Pfizer, Merck or a global contract manufacturer. The products named by FDA were homeopathic drug products, and the agency’s findings included basic problems that had little to do with AI. That is one reason to be careful about overstating the case. A single warning letter does not create a full regulatory regime. Warning letters also present the agency’s view of observed violations; they are not court judgments, and they usually give firms an opportunity to respond and correct.

But warning letters do a different kind of work in regulated industries. They tell other firms what inspectors noticed, what language the agency used and how existing rules may be applied to new behavior. Here, the agency tied AI use to familiar provisions: the quality control unit’s duty to approve or reject procedures and specifications affecting identity, strength, quality and purity; the requirement for written production and process-control procedures; and the broader statutory requirement that drugs be made under cGMP. The novelty is not the rule. The novelty is the attempted delegation.

For years, FDA has encouraged modernization in pharmaceutical quality systems. The agency’s advanced-manufacturing work and its 2023 discussion paper on AI in drug manufacturing both recognize that artificial intelligence may help with process design, process control, monitoring, fault detection, trend analysis and continual improvement. The basic model is not frozen in 1970s paper forms. A modern facility can use computerized systems, statistical tools, automation, continuous manufacturing, cloud services and advanced analytics. The current in current good manufacturing practice is supposed to move with technology. But cGMP modernization has always had a boundary: new tools do not erase the need for a scientifically justified, documented and controlled process. The point of a quality system is not to own binders. It is to know what is happening and to intervene when the system drifts.

AI agents stress that boundary because they blur three functions that regulated firms usually try to keep distinct. They can retrieve rules, draft text and suggest action in one conversational flow. A quality employee asking for help with a procedure may receive a polished paragraph, a checklist and a recommendation without seeing which rule was retrieved, whether it is current, whether the output fits the firm’s actual process or whether the model has filled a gap with plausible nonsense. In a lightly regulated setting, that may be a productivity issue. In a cGMP setting, it becomes a traceability problem.

That is why the Purolea case should be read less as an AI morality play than as a record-control case. FDA did not have to decide whether the AI agent was sophisticated, obsolete, open-source, vendor-hosted or internally built. The violation described by the agency was simpler: the firm used If a consultant, contractor or junior employee had produced the same flawed documents, the answer would have been similar. The drug owner cannot outsource responsibility for the quality of its drugs.

The quality unit is not a spell-checker

The phrase human in the loop is common in AI policy. In drug manufacturing, it is not enough. A person who clicks approve after skimming a generated document is not necessarily meaningful oversight. cGMP requires defined responsibility, written procedures, training, documentation and authority. Under 21 CFR 211.22, the quality control unit must have the responsibility and authority to approve or reject components, materials, drug products, production records, procedures and specifications that affect drug quality. Under 21 CFR 211.100, production and process-control procedures must be written, reviewed, approved, followed and documented. Under 21 CFR 211.165, appropriate laboratory testing is required before release, including testing for objectionable microorganisms when necessary.

Those duties are labor duties as much as legal duties. The quality worker who reads a deviation report, checks a certificate of analysis, verifies training records or decides whether a batch record is complete is doing safety work through documents. The job is already full of repetition, cross-referencing and tedious review. It is exactly the kind of work executives hope AI can accelerate. It is also exactly the kind of work where a false shortcut can be dangerous.

The labor effect is therefore not simply job loss or job creation. It is accountability compression. A quality manager may be told that AI will save time drafting a procedure, but the manager still signs for the procedure’s adequacy. A regulatory-affairs specialist may be told that an AI tool can scan new guidance, but the specialist still owns the submission. A plant employee may be told that a tool can answer cGMP questions, but the company still must train people on actual procedures and preserve records showing the procedure was followed. AI can reduce the time spent on first drafts. It can also increase the pressure on human reviewers to certify more outputs faster, often without giving them more staffing, authority or training.

The better standard is not human nearby. It is qualified human control. In this setting, the person reviewing an AI output needs enough subject-matter knowledge to know what may be missing; enough procedural authority to reject the output; enough time to check it; and enough independence to stop a release or require a corrective action. The quality unit is not supposed to be a passive rubber stamp. If AI turns the quality worker into a hurried proofreader of machine-generated compliance prose, the firm has not strengthened its quality system. It has created a faster way to miss things.

The best use of AI in a pharmaceutical quality system may look less like an autonomous compliance oracle and more like a controlled instrument. It should point to source documents, distinguish binding rules from guidance, identify uncertainty, preserve audit trails, avoid unsupported legal conclusions and operate within validated use cases. It should be governed by procedures that say when it may be used, when it may not, what must be independently checked, how outputs are retained, how version changes are assessed and who is authorized to approve use in cGMP activities. That is not glamorous AI. It is boring AI with a paper trail. In this field, boring is a safety feature.

The regulator is also becoming an AI user

The awkwardness is that FDA is not standing outside the AI transition. It is inside it. In May 2025, Commissioner Marty Makary announced the completion of an AI-assisted scientific-review pilot and an aggressive timeline to scale internal generative AI across the agency. In June 2025, FDA launched Elsa, a generative AI tool for employees. In December 2025, the agency announced agentic AI capabilities for all employees, saying the tool could help with meeting management, premarket reviews, review validation, postmarket surveillance, inspections, compliance and administrative work. FDA has said the tools are optional, built in a high-security GovCloud environment and not trained on data submitted by regulated industry.

That makes FDA’s message to industry both stronger and more complicated. Stronger, because the agency can credibly say it is not hostile to AI as a category. It is building its own AI workflows and has published guidance, discussion papers and research programs on AI across medical devices, drug development, biological products, pharmacovigilance and manufacturing. More complicated, because the regulator now must model the same discipline it expects from companies: human oversight, documented limits, data controls, security, bias awareness and clear responsibility for final decisions.

The internal rollout has already drawn scrutiny. NOTUS reported in July 2025 that some FDA staff had concerns about Elsa’s reliability, limited integration, broken links and hallucinated or nonexistent guidance references, while HHS defended the tool and said guardrails, disclaimers and training were in place. FDA’s own public statements present the tool as an assistant for reading, writing, summarizing, label comparison, adverse-event summaries and other efficiency tasks, not as a substitute for scientific judgment. That distinction will have to hold under pressure.

If AI helps an FDA reviewer summarize a clinical protocol, compare a submission to prior decisions or identify high-priority inspection targets, the agency will need its own version of the quality-unit question: who reviewed the output, what was the source record, how were errors detected and how could an affected sponsor understand the basis of a regulatory action? The public does not need every prompt or internal workflow. But it does need confidence that an agency charged with protecting health is not converting expert review into unchecked machine summarization, and that companies are not converting quality assurance into prompt engineering.

The small-firm problem

The most difficult part of the Purolea case is not that a sophisticated global manufacturer misunderstood AI governance. It is that a small firm appears to have used AI to compensate for a compliance gap. That possibility should worry regulators more than the flashier fear of autonomous robots running a plant.

Large manufacturers can build AI governance committees, vendor-risk reviews, validation teams and internal counsel memoranda. They can buy enterprise systems, negotiate data protections and assign experts to monitor model updates. Smaller manufacturers, compounders, packagers, laboratories and cosmetics firms often live closer to the edge of staffing and expertise. For them, an AI assistant can look like cheap regulatory help: ask it for a procedure, a specification, a checklist, a training module or a response to an FDA observation. If the output looks polished and cites plausible rules, it can appear to fill a professional gap.

That is the danger. AI can make an underbuilt quality system look written before it becomes real. A firm can generate SOPs faster than it can train people, validate processes, qualify suppliers, test components or build a culture where production stops when evidence is missing. The result is document inflation: more words, more binders, more impressive formatting, but not necessarily more control. Inspectors have long known that a procedure can exist on paper while the plant operates differently. AI makes it easier to create the paper.

There is also a market incentive. Vendors increasingly pitch AI tools for regulatory intelligence, quality management, audit preparation and submission drafting. Some tools may be genuinely useful, especially when built on curated sources with traceable retrieval and strong controls. But the buyer’s question cannot be whether the demo is smooth. It must be whether the tool can survive inspection logic. What does it know? How is the knowledge base updated? Can it separate U.S. cGMP from EU GMP, guidance from regulation, medical-device quality rules from drug manufacturing rules? Does it preserve prompts and outputs as records when those outputs support regulated decisions? Who tests it after a model update? What happens when it refuses, guesses or omits?

Those questions cost time and money. They also reintroduce the human expertise the product was often purchased to save. That is not a reason to reject AI. It is a reason to be honest about the real economics. A compliant AI tool is not merely a subscription. It is a controlled process, a training burden, a validation exercise, a vendor-management problem and an inspection topic. The savings are real only if the governance work is counted too.

Medical-device AI offers a preview

FDA has been wrestling with AI longer in medical devices, where the agency maintains a public list of AI-enabled devices authorized for marketing in the United States. That list is dominated by radiology and imaging tools but reaches across multiple clinical areas. The device side has produced guidance and principles on predetermined change control plans, good machine-learning practice and transparency for machine-learning-enabled devices. Those documents repeatedly return to lifecycle management, representative data, human-AI performance, monitoring, retraining risk and clear information for users.

Drug manufacturing is different from clinical AI software, but the themes travel. A device algorithm may change performance as data shifts. A manufacturing AI assistant may change behavior when a vendor updates a model, when its retrieval index is refreshed or when users start asking it for tasks outside its intended scope. A device needs labeling that tells clinicians what the output means and when not to rely on it. A quality AI tool needs procedures that tell staff what the output means and when not to use it. A device sponsor must show safety and effectiveness for an intended use. A manufacturer using AI in cGMP should be able to define the intended use of the AI system inside the quality process.

FDA’s January 2025 draft guidance on AI to support regulatory decision-making for drugs and biological products also points in this direction. It proposed a risk-based credibility framework for AI model outputs used to support decisions about safety, effectiveness or quality. The idea is not that every spreadsheet, model or algorithm receives the same scrutiny. It is that the level of evidence should match the consequence of being wrong. If an AI model is helping organize background literature, the controls may differ from a model used to support a critical quality attribute, a clinical endpoint or a release decision. The same risk logic should apply to AI agents in manufacturing documentation.

International regulators are moving through the same terrain. The European Medicines Agency has adopted a reflection paper on AI in the medicines lifecycle, issued principles for large-language-model use by regulatory-network staff and, with FDA, identified good AI practice principles for medicine development. EMA’s public materials emphasize a human-centric approach, legal compliance, cross-checking outputs, safe data handling and early regulatory engagement when AI may affect benefit-risk assessment. The direction is not a ban. It is accountability by design.

The counterargument: AI can make quality safer

The strongest case for AI in pharmaceutical quality is not hype. It is that the current system is already strained. Quality records are voluminous. Regulations, guidance documents and inspection observations are hard to track. Deviations can hide in unstructured text. Signal detection in adverse-event systems is labor-intensive. Human reviewers can miss patterns, suffer fatigue, copy old language into new procedures or spend too much time on formatting instead of investigation. A well-designed AI system could help find anomalies, compare records, flag missing evidence, summarize large files, identify recurring deviations and make expert staff more effective.

That argument deserves weight. A ban on AI-assisted quality work would be unrealistic and probably counterproductive. FDA itself clearly believes AI can help with review, surveillance and compliance functions. Researchers and industry groups are already exploring domain-specific AI agents and professional training for pharmaceutical operations. The question is not whether AI belongs anywhere in the system. It is whether organizations can keep the tool from becoming a credentialed shortcut around expertise.

The right comparison is not AI versus a perfect human process. It is controlled AI plus accountable human review versus overstretched humans using informal tools in the shadows. If companies prohibit all AI, employees may still paste text into consumer chatbots or use unapproved tools to meet deadlines. If companies embrace AI without controls, they may convert informal risk into official process. The middle path is hard but necessary: approved tools, narrow use cases, training, audit trails, traceable outputs, periodic validation, data protections, escalation rules and a culture that treats AI uncertainty as a reason to ask more questions, not fewer.

That middle path also requires regulators to avoid sending mixed signals. If agencies praise AI as a way to move reviews from days to minutes, companies will hear the productivity message. If agencies enforce only after failures, smaller firms may learn the governance lesson too late. FDA has an opportunity now to publish more practical examples: acceptable and unacceptable uses of generative AI in cGMP documentation, expectations for validation of AI-assisted quality systems, treatment of prompts and outputs as records, and model-change controls for tools used in regulated processes. Guidance should not freeze the technology. It should clarify the accountability architecture.

The batch-release lesson

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, release is a moment of trust. A batch record says, in effect, that the process happened as required, the evidence was reviewed and the product can enter the world. Most consumers will never see the batch record. They cannot smell whether a product was made under control. They cannot inspect the supplier qualification file, the microbial test, the deviation investigation or the cleaning log. They rely on a system of people, rules and records.

AI now wants a place in that system. It can be useful. It can be fast. It can make the first draft cleaner and the search less painful. But it cannot be allowed to become the unseen approver. The public-health promise of cGMP is not that every document exists. It is that the document reflects a real controlled process and that someone with authority has checked the evidence.

The Purolea warning letter is memorable because it gives the AI era a plain regulatory sentence: the agent did not tell us. That sentence is not a defense. It is an admission of a broken quality system. The next phase of AI in regulated work will be decided not by whether models can produce better prose, but by whether institutions can preserve responsibility when prose becomes cheap. In a drug plant, the machine may draft the record. The quality unit still has to own it.
