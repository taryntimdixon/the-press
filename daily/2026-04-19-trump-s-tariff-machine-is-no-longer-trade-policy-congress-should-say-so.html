Trump’s tariff machine is no longer trade policy. Congress should say so.

The president’s 2026 tariff escalation has turned a once-disputed import tax into a standing power to reprice the economy. The right answer is not another court fight by proxy. It is a statute that restores Congress’s tariff authority, limits emergency levies, and forces a real industrial policy debate into the open.

Written and Researched by AI • April 19, 2026 • 4:00 p.m.

The strongest argument for President Donald Trump’s tariff program is also the most unsettling thing about it: it works politically because it has become something closer to a fiscal and industrial strategy than a conventional trade policy. In the White House’s April 2 actions, the administration escalated tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, and patented pharmaceuticals, presenting them not as narrow bargaining chips but as national-security tools, supply-chain corrections, and leverage for rebuilding American capacity. At the same time, after the Supreme Court struck down the earlier emergency tariff program on February 20, the administration moved quickly to preserve tariff authority through other statutory routes. That combination has pushed tariffs toward something more durable than a temporary negotiating weapon. It is becoming a standing instrument of state power, which is precisely why it should alarm anyone who believes tariffs ought to be legislated, bounded, and openly debated rather than expanded by executive improvisation. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-strengthens-tariffs-on-steel-aluminum-and-copper-imports/?utm_source=openai))

Congress should answer that alarm with legislation. Not because tariffs can never be justified. They can. Not because every factory-revival promise is nonsense. Some sectors do merit strategic protection. But because the present drift is converting emergency authority into an all-purpose economic weapon, and that is a constitutional and economic problem at once. A tariff set by statute, debated in public, bounded in time, and paired with compensation or industrial investment is one thing. A tariff regime that can expand by proclamation, reclassify itself by sector, evade judicial defeat by statute-shopping, and shift from trade to health care to geopolitics is another. The latter is not a coherent economic doctrine. It is executive improvisation with a customs seal on it. The legal aftermath since February underscores the point: importers have been pursuing refunds, courts have been managing the consequences, and states have already sued the new tariff structure as well. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

The facts of the moment matter. As of early April, the administration had already used tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, and some pharmaceuticals to project a more durable version of the same strategy that helped trigger the post-IEEPA legal fight. The White House has also issued actions that set future effective dates, carve out special categories, and condition tariff relief on negotiations or national-security determinations. Those are the hallmarks of a tariff system meant not simply to protect industries but to manage the economy from the Oval Office. And because the Supreme Court invalidated the prior global emergency tariffs in February, the new actions are also a constitutional stress test: can the president keep doing via adjacent statutes what the Court said he could not do via emergency powers? The answer for policymakers should be no. If Congress agrees that the nation needs tariffs on pharmaceuticals, steel, or some other sector, then Congress should say so in statutes with sunsets, thresholds, and oversight. If it does not, the president should not be permitted to build a permanent customs architecture out of emergency rhetoric and administrative improvisation. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-strengthens-tariffs-on-steel-aluminum-and-copper-imports/?utm_source=openai))

The case for tariffs is stronger than the old free-trade caricature admits

The best defense of Trump’s tariffs begins with a real failure of the pre-2020 trade consensus. It is now impossible to deny that the United States became overly dependent on fragile, foreign-centered supply chains for everything from generic drugs to critical mineral inputs, and that some hollowing-out of domestic industrial capacity left the country exposed to shocks. FDA materials say more than half of pharmaceuticals distributed in the U.S. are manufactured overseas, and the agency has also said the country remains reliant on overseas sources for active pharmaceutical ingredients. The same agency has documented a globalized pharmaceutical supply base and has recently launched programs to spur U.S. manufacturing and testing. In that context, some tariff pressure on strategically important goods is not crazy. It is a crude tool, but not an irrational one. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-alerts-and-statements/fda-announces-new-anda-prioritization-pilot-support-us-generic-drug-manufacturing-and-testing?utm_source=openai))

There is also a fiscal argument that tariffs have become too easy to dismiss. At a time when the federal deficit remains large, customs revenue is no small matter. Tax Foundation estimates have shown that broad tariff scenarios can raise substantial revenue and that tariffs can function like a large hidden tax on households. Even critics must admit that a tariff is one of the few taxes politicians can raise without naming it a tax. That makes it tempting, especially when Congress is unwilling to reform the rest of the tax code. For lawmakers who want revenue without voting for revenue, tariffs can look like the only politically survivable instrument left. ([taxfoundation.org](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/trump-tariffs-revenue-estimates/?utm_source=openai))

And then there is the strategic case. The White House’s pharmaceutical action explicitly treated drug imports as a national-security and public-health vulnerability. The administration is not inventing that risk from thin air. FDA’s own globalization materials say the agency is dealing with a product universe in which many facilities are overseas and many active ingredients come from abroad. A country that cannot reliably make basic medicines, critical metals, or essential components at home is not maximally free; it is maximally exposed. The most serious tariff defenders are not defending old-fashioned protectionism. They are arguing for resilience, for industrial depth, and for a state that can use trade barriers as a bridge to domestic capacity rather than as a permanent crutch. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-bolsters-national-security-and-strengthens-u-s-supply-chains-by-imposing-tariffs-on-patented-pharmaceutical-products/?utm_source=openai))

That is the steelman. It deserves respect because it is partly right. The old model of indiscriminate tariff skepticism often treated all consumer price increases as self-evidently worse than all industrial dependence. That was too simple. The pandemic, supply-chain squeezes, and geopolitical rivalry have vindicated a more serious view: not every low-price global sourcing arrangement is efficient once risk is priced honestly. For some goods, the country should be willing to pay for redundancy, domestic production, and supply assurance. The question is not whether those goals are legitimate. The question is whether Trump’s tariff machinery is the right way to pursue them. It is not. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/news-events-human-drugs/fda-public-meeting-onshoring-manufacturing-drugs-and-biological-products-09302025?utm_source=openai))

Tariffs are a tax on Americans, just spread out in a way politicians like

The economic case against the administration is now less abstract than it was a year ago. Inflation has not disappeared; it has shifted, and tariffs have been one of the forces pushing goods prices higher. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the Consumer Price Index rose 3.3 percent over the year in March 2026 and 0.9 percent over the month. The IMF, in its April 2026 Article IV consultation for the United States, said inflation moved sideways during 2025 as tariffs boosted goods prices while services inflation moderated. That is exactly the kind of pass-through economists warned about: the import tax shows up first at the border and later on the shelf, often after firms try to absorb some of the shock. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/consumer-prices-up-3-3-percent-over-the-year-0-9-percent-over-the-month-in-march-2026.htm?utm_source=openai))

The research consensus is now uncomfortable for tariff enthusiasts. NBER’s 2026 paper by Gopinath and Neiman found that tariff pass-through to U.S. import prices is almost 100 percent, meaning the United States bears a large share of the cost. Another NBER paper on the 2025 tariff wave estimated that about 90 percent of the tariffs were passed through to tariff-inclusive prices paid by U.S. importers. Those findings do not prove every tariff is disastrous. But they do blow up the comforting fantasy that foreign exporters pay the bill. In the real world, tariffs are mainly a domestic tax collected in an uglier way. They are paid by importers, then by consumers, workers, and downstream firms. If a policy raises prices at the border and then calls itself industrial renewal, it should at least be honest that it is taxing households to subsidize selected sectors. ([nber.org](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34620?utm_source=openai))

That matters because the administration has chosen not a narrow, temporary correction but a large, shifting, uncertainty-heavy tariff stack. After the Supreme Court knocked down the broader emergency tariff regime, the White House pivoted quickly to new duties and new legal authorities, including a 15 percent tariff approach under Section 122 and an array of sectoral proclamations. That churn creates planning costs even when the nominal tariff rate looks manageable. Firms do not make multiyear capital decisions on the basis of a slogan. They do it on the basis of rules, and the present system has turned the rulebook into a moving target. The uncertainty itself is a tax: on investment, on sourcing, on hiring, and on long-term contracts. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/2247451a7cbc9b8283c4574e3ee54537?utm_source=openai))

The damage is not evenly distributed. That is what makes tariffs politically durable and economically deceptive. A household that buys one imported good may notice a few dollars more here and there. A manufacturer that uses imported components notices the cost base rising every week. A physician’s office or pharmacy may not think of itself as part of trade policy, but a tariff on patented pharmaceuticals can flow into treatment costs, formulary pressures, and delayed access. The administration is simultaneously trying to lower drug costs, widen access, and impose tariff pressure on a sector already struggling with supply fragility. It can do all three only if it ignores the interaction among them. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-bolsters-national-security-and-strengthens-u-s-supply-chains-by-imposing-tariffs-on-patented-pharmaceutical-products/?utm_source=openai))

That contradiction extends to housing and construction. Brookings estimated that current tariffs, including those just announced, will add roughly $30 billion to the cost of investment in residential structures, with about 90 percent of the costs falling on new-home construction. In a country still short millions of homes, adding tariff pressure to materials used in construction is not industrial policy in the virtuous sense. It is a tax on one of the few sectors the nation desperately needs to expand. Tariff defenders often talk about rebuilding the middle class. But if the policy makes homes more expensive to build and buy, the burden lands on the very households the rhetoric claims to help. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/recent-tariffs-threaten-residential-construction/?utm_source=openai))

That is why the administration’s revenue argument is so dangerous. Revenue that arrives through imported goods is politically easy because the tax line is hidden from the voter. But hidden taxes are not free taxes. Tariff revenue can be large on paper, but large revenue does not equal public value. It may simply mean Americans are paying more for the same goods and services while the government uses the proceeds to finance other priorities. In other words, tariffs can function as a regressive consumption tax with a nationalist accent. If the country wants more revenue, it should debate taxes honestly rather than pretending customs duties are something else. ([taxfoundation.org](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/trump-tariffs-revenue-estimates/?utm_source=openai))

The strongest counterargument: strategic autonomy may be worth the price

The most persuasive counterargument is not libertarian absolutism. It is national resilience. The United States may indeed need to accept more expensive inputs in exchange for greater domestic capacity, especially for medicines, critical materials, and defense-adjacent supply chains. The federal government has already signaled that logic in multiple places. FDA materials on shortages, the agency’s global manufacturing data, and the onshoring initiatives all show that fragile foreign dependence is a policy problem, not merely a market outcome. The White House’s pharmaceutical proclamation likewise says plainly that the point is supply-chain security. From this angle, tariffs are not a punishment; they are a steering mechanism. They tell firms that if they want to sell in the American market, they should invest in American production or partner with allies in more secure jurisdictions. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-alerts-and-statements/fda-announces-new-anda-prioritization-pilot-support-us-generic-drug-manufacturing-and-testing?utm_source=openai))

There is also a geopolitical case. Trade dependence can become coercive dependence. If the United States cannot source critical pharmaceutical ingredients or industrial metals without inviting strategic vulnerability, then cheap imports may be a mirage. In a world of conflict, sanctions, and weaponized interdependence, a government that refuses to use tariffs at all risks confining itself to a passive posture while rivals plan more aggressively. Tariffs can be a blunt signal that the market is not the only sovereign force in the room. Sometimes the government must raise costs in order to reduce exposure. That argument is not absurd; it is a sober reaction to a more dangerous world. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/international-programs/fda-globalization?utm_source=openai))

And for all the warnings about price effects, the welfare literature is more mixed than partisans on either side often admit. The April 2026 NBER paper by Fajgelbaum and Khandelwal concluded that the overall welfare effect of the 2025 tariff increases ranges from a loss of 0.13 percent of GDP to a gain of 0.10 percent, depending on terms-of-trade effects and other assumptions. That is not a trivial finding. It means the net macro cost may be smaller than some headline-grabbing predictions, even if the distributional harms are real and the pass-through high. Tariff defenders can argue, with some academic support, that a country’s strategic gains and revenue gains may offset part of the consumer cost, especially if the policy is narrow and designed to change sourcing rather than to maximize protection forever. ([nber.org](https://www.nber.org/papers/w35064?utm_source=openai))

That is the best case for the president: maybe the old free-trade model was too cheap, too complacent, and too blind to resilience. Maybe the country should pay more for reliability. Maybe tariffs, if aimed carefully and paired with domestic investment, can help re-shore some capacity and accelerate a healthier industrial structure. Maybe they are not just taxes but insurance premiums. Those arguments deserve a hearing. They are not vibes; they are strategic claims about national security, industrial depth, and the limits of market dependence. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-alerts-and-statements/fda-announces-new-anda-prioritization-pilot-support-us-generic-drug-manufacturing-and-testing?utm_source=openai))

Why the current approach still fails

The problem is not that the administration is trying to solve the wrong problem. The problem is that it is solving a real problem with a constitutional shortcut and then compounding that shortcut with chaos. The Supreme Court’s February ruling made clear that the broad emergency tariff program exceeded the authority the president claimed. Yet instead of treating that as a prompt for legislation, the White House has moved to recast the same basic project through alternative statutes and sectoral proclamations. That may be clever lawyering. It is not healthy governance. If Congress agrees that the nation needs tariffs on pharmaceuticals, steel, or some other sector, then Congress should say so in statutes with sunsets, thresholds, and oversight. If it does not, the president should not be permitted to build a permanent customs architecture out of emergency rhetoric and administrative improvisation. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

This is where the opposition’s moral confidence often outruns its policy discipline. Strategic industrial policy requires specificity. It requires deciding which sectors matter, which risks matter, which allies count, and what the exit ramps are. The Trump approach has repeatedly blurred those distinctions. One week tariffs are supposed to curb deficits. Another week they are supposed to raise revenue. Another week they are about fentanyl, then about national security, then about reciprocity, then about geopolitics, then about domestic manufacturing, then about punishing a trading partner. That flexibility is politically useful because it lets the policy survive every critique. But a justification that can mean anything usually means too much. If everything is a national-security issue, then nothing is genuinely prioritized. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/872c8f04112a8991d8aa6ae5005767b6?utm_source=openai))

There is also a practical reason to distrust tariff maximalism: it invites retaliation and unpredictability. Even when other countries do not match the U.S. rate-for-rate, they can respond through procurement, regulatory delays, sourcing shifts, or bargaining leverage. Brookings’s recent work on North American auto manufacturing noted that higher U.S. tariffs create incentives for manufacturers to relocate or avoid the tariff, but those incentives do not magically produce integrated domestic capacity overnight. They also raise costs and complicate the USMCA ecosystem that still binds North American production. The more tariffs are used as a constant threat, the more firms are pushed to hedge, delay, or diversify away from the United States as a final destination market. That is not resilience. It is friction for its own sake. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/recent-tariffs-threaten-residential-construction/?utm_source=openai))

The geopolitical story is similarly double-edged. Yes, dependence is a vulnerability. But indiscriminate tariffs can also weaken alliances and undercut the coalition-building needed to actually reduce dependence on adversarial suppliers. The European Parliament and European Commission have both been dealing with the consequences of U.S. tariff escalation, including retaliation and the suspension or redesign of trade arrangements. That reality should matter to American policymakers. If the goal is to build a resilient bloc, then the better tool is a coordinated industrial strategy with shared standards, targeted incentives, and negotiated rules of origin. Tariffs may be part of that package, but they cannot be the whole package. ([eesc.europa.eu](https://www.eesc.europa.eu/en/news-media/news/eu-us-trade-deal-eu-parliament-set-conditions-lowering-tariffs-us-products?utm_source=openai))

Even the evidence base for broad tariff success is weaker than the administration suggests. The NBER papers indicate high pass-through and, at best, ambiguous aggregate welfare effects. The IMF says tariffs have already boosted goods prices in the United States. The BLS data show inflation remains above the Fed’s target. Brookings estimates the housing burden is large. The Tax Foundation shows that tariffs can raise substantial revenue but at significant household cost. Put together, the empirical picture is not that tariffs are costless but strategically useful. It is that tariffs are costly, their benefits are uncertain, and their distributional winners are highly concentrated. That is exactly the profile of a policy that should be debated by lawmakers, not managed as if it were an executive utility function. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/consumer-prices-up-3-3-percent-over-the-year-0-9-percent-over-the-month-in-march-2026.htm?utm_source=openai))

The right fix is legislative, not performative

The case for Congress is not anti-tariff. It is anti-shadow-government. A serious legislative response would do four things. First, it would reclaim tariff authority from emergency powers and specify exactly which statutes permit which kinds of duties. Second, it would require automatic expiration dates and periodic reauthorization so that tariffs cannot become permanent by inertia. Third, it would require transparent impact assessments on consumer prices, upstream input costs, and retaliation risks before major tariff changes take effect. Fourth, it would pair any strategic tariffs with explicit industrial investment, such as domestic manufacturing credits, procurement commitments, or infrastructure support, so the policy is a bridge rather than a dead end. None of that requires a fantasy return to the pre-Trump trade consensus. It requires adult supervision. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

That would also clarify the national debate the administration has blurred. If the country truly wants to pay more to secure drug manufacturing, then let elected lawmakers vote for it. If Congress believes the trade deficit is the central macroeconomic problem, let it own the tax and spending tradeoffs openly. If the nation wants to rebuild steel, shipbuilding, or advanced manufacturing, then align tariffs with procurement, training, permitting reform, and capital formation. But if the real agenda is simply to keep a flexible tariff tool available for whatever political purpose seems useful this month, then the public should be honest that this is about executive discretion, not durable strategy. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-strengthens-tariffs-on-steel-aluminum-and-copper-imports/?utm_source=openai))

The administration will say Congress is too divided to act, and that may be true. But legislative failure is not a license for executive enlargement. If anything, it is the reason to force a vote. The longer tariffs are run as a presidential weapon, the harder it becomes to tell where policy ends and coercion begins. Markets adapt to that uncertainty by charging more. Firms adapt by delaying investment. Families adapt by paying more for staples, medicines, and homes. The burden is diffuse enough that no single vote can capture it, which is exactly why Congress should. Democratic accountability exists for the things that are easiest to hide. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/is-us-trade-policy-on-a-new-path/?utm_source=openai))

The verdict

Trump’s tariff project is built on a legitimate insight and a dangerous method. The insight is that resilience matters, that some supply chains are too brittle, and that national power is not measured by the cheapest import invoice alone. The method is to turn tariffs into a semi-permanent executive instrument, detached from meaningful congressional control and elastic enough to justify nearly any policy goal after the fact. That method will almost certainly keep producing price pressure, uncertainty, retaliation risk, and legal instability. It may also produce a few real factories and a few strategic gains. But those gains do not justify letting the presidency become a customs house with its own foreign policy and tax code. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-bolsters-national-security-and-strengthens-u-s-supply-chains-by-imposing-tariffs-on-patented-pharmaceutical-products/?utm_source=openai))

So the right position is not to defend free trade as an article of faith, nor to cheer tariffs as a patriotic instinct. It is to insist that if the United States wants a new industrial policy, it should build one in the open, through Congress, with limits and tradeoffs visible to voters. Tariffs may belong in that package. A tariff machine governed by presidential improvisation does not. Congress should take the keys before the country gets locked into a permanent regime of expensive uncertainty. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/2247451a7cbc9b8283c4574e3ee54537?utm_source=openai))
