America’s AI Buildout Hits the Grid, the Classroom and the Law

A new phase of the AI boom is forcing Washington to choose between speed, safety and control as power demand rises, federal standards sharpen and lawmakers push back over state rules, children and jobs.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 21, 2026 • 10:43 a.m. • 4234 words

Washington’s AI debate has shifted from a contest over model rankings to a fight over the scaffolding that makes the technology usable at scale: electricity, procurement standards, schools, child safety, labor rules and who gets to regulate first. Over the past year, the federal government has sharpened its focus on measurement and evaluation, agencies have begun wiring AI into procurement and public services, lawmakers have moved to overturn a White House push against state AI laws, and the power demands of the buildout have moved from abstract warnings into federal planning. The result is a more concrete political economy of AI — less about what the systems can do in principle, more about what the country can actually absorb, govern and afford. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

The most visible part of that shift is infrastructure. The Department of Energy says data centers consumed about 4.4% of U.S. electricity in 2023 and could reach roughly 6.7% to 12% by 2028, a sharp rise for a sector that was once treated as a niche slice of the grid. DOE has also moved to pair AI with energy planning, announcing in March a public-private partnership to redevelop federal land at the Portsmouth site in Ohio for a large data center and related power generation, including 10 gigawatts of new generation intended to connect to the local grid. Those steps do not settle whether the boom is sustainable; they do show that the federal government is now actively trying to make room for it. That, in turn, raises a harder question: whether the grid, local communities and ratepayers can be protected while the AI industry expands at a pace more accustomed to software than to physical systems. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the government is building a second layer of infrastructure: the standards and testing apparatus that will decide which systems are trusted. NIST’s Center for AI Standards and Innovation has spent the year issuing draft guidance on automated benchmark evaluations, launching an AI Agent Standards Initiative, and signing a memorandum with the General Services Administration to support AI evaluation inside USAi, a secure generative-AI procurement toolbox for federal agencies. Those moves matter because they mark a transition from broad principles to technical gatekeeping. If federal agencies are going to buy and deploy AI, the government needs a way to compare models, test agents, document failures and decide what “good enough” means in public-sector use. That work is not glamorous, but it is likely to shape the market far more than most press releases about new chatbots. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/03/caisi-signs-mou-gsa-boost-ai-evaluation-science-federal-procurement-through?utm_source=openai))

In parallel, the politics of AI have become more openly adversarial. The Trump administration’s March 2026 national AI legislative framework called for a consistent national policy and stronger federal direction, arguing that a patchwork of state laws would undermine innovation and competitiveness. Shortly afterward, Senate Democrats Chris Murphy and Brian Schatz introduced the GUARDRAILS Act to reverse the administration’s effort to restrain state-level AI laws, arguing that states should remain free to protect consumers while Congress stalls. That clash is not simply a philosophical argument about federalism. It is also a contest over liability, speed and leverage: whether the AI industry gets a single, uniform national rulebook, or must navigate a patchwork of state protections that may be messy but can move faster than Congress. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

The AI story is also becoming a labor story with less hand-waving and more evidence. Stanford HAI’s 2026 AI Index says the United States dominated private AI investment in 2025, pulling in a large share of global funding, and that AI skill demand is increasingly visible in job postings. But the same research and related summaries also underscore that the labor-market impact is not just about job losses; it is about changing job content, rising demand for generative AI skills and the concentration of high-value activity in a few places and firms. That creates a two-tier economy: companies and workers able to harness AI tools quickly, and everyone else absorbing the churn. The policy question is whether institutions can widen the first group without hollowing out the second. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

Then there is the social question that has become impossible to ignore: children, schools and the everyday use of AI systems that can mimic conversation and companionship. The FTC opened an inquiry into AI chatbots acting as companions and their effects on children and teens, asking seven companies what steps they have taken to evaluate safety, limit use by minors and inform parents of risks. The Education Department has also said AI can be used in schools if it fits existing federal rules and grant requirements. These are not fringe issues. They go to whether AI is treated like a productivity tool, a consumer product, an educational aid or a quasi-social actor that can influence behavior, emotions and development. The answer may be different in each context, which is exactly why the legal system is struggling. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-launches-inquiry-ai-chatbots-acting-companions?utm_source=openai))

This package of decisions — about power, standards, state authority, school use and child safety — is what makes spring 2026 a meaningful moment in the AI story. The headline is no longer merely that AI is advancing. It is that the country is laying down a set of contested rails for how AI will be financed, measured, powered and constrained. Some of those rails are public. Some are private. Some are still missing. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

The grid is now part of the AI story

For two years, the most common critique of AI boom journalism has been that it focuses too much on model launches and not enough on infrastructure. That critique is now obsolete only because the infrastructure has become impossible to miss. Electricity is not a background issue; it is the limiting factor that ties together data center location, permitting, community impact, cooling technology, transmission and the political power of utilities. DOE’s latest reporting makes clear how quickly the sector’s share of electricity use has climbed. The agency says data centers accounted for about 4.4% of total U.S. electricity consumption in 2023 and projects a jump to 6.7% to 12% by 2028. Even if those estimates land near the low end, the change would still be enormous. To put that in perspective, a handful of industries are capable of moving national power demand that fast. AI is now one of them. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

The Portsmouth announcement is especially revealing because it shows how AI development is being paired with energy development. DOE says the project would sit on leased federal land at the former Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant and would be built alongside new power infrastructure, including 10 gigawatts of generation. That is a break from the old assumption that data centers would simply plug into whatever grid already existed. Instead, the federal government is effectively acknowledging that AI at scale may require dedicated generation and transmission planning. Supporters call this pragmatic. Critics worry it is a subsidy of the most capital-intensive part of the AI economy, socializing the risk while privatizing the upside. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/energy-department-announces-partnership-ensure-affordable-energy-and-power-americas-ai?utm_source=openai))

That criticism is not theoretical. Local reporting around data center siting has repeatedly shown that communities worry about water use, noise, land use, industrialization of rural areas and the possibility that promised jobs will not materialize at the scale claimed. Federal enthusiasm can collide with local skepticism when a project is described as transformative but ordinary residents hear only higher bills, traffic and long-term environmental commitments. That tension is part of the reason energy and AI policy can no longer be separated. If the grid needs to be expanded or reconfigured to support AI, the public will ask who pays, who benefits and who decides. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/energy-department-announces-partnership-ensure-affordable-energy-and-power-americas-ai?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, there is a genuine argument on the other side. AI systems can improve grid management, accelerate scientific computing, improve industrial efficiency and help utilities forecast demand. DOE materials on geothermal and grid modernization, as well as its broader energy-efficiency work, show the department sees AI both as a load on the grid and as a tool for improving it. That dual role matters. The right answer is not simply to slow all AI development, but to distinguish between high-value uses and speculative capacity chasing. If the boom is going to be powered by scarce electricity, policymakers should be asking which deployments produce public benefit and which merely inflate model size. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/geothermal/geothermal-and-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

NIST is trying to turn trust into a technical discipline

If energy is the material infrastructure of AI, standards are the institutional infrastructure. NIST’s work this year shows the federal government is trying to move the trust problem out of rhetoric and into measurement science. CAISI has been soliciting public comments on draft best practices for automated benchmark evaluations, launching an AI Agent Standards Initiative and coordinating internationally on evaluation methods. That sounds esoteric, but it is central to the market now forming around frontier models and autonomous agents. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/caisi/guidelines?utm_source=openai))

The reason is simple: AI vendors increasingly sell not just models but systems that act. These systems write code, send emails, browse tools, summarize records and make decisions with partial autonomy. The old way of evaluating AI — asking whether it answered a question correctly in a benchmark — is no longer enough. A federal agency buying an agentic system needs to know whether it can access the right data, follow permissions, avoid unsafe actions, log its behavior and fail safely when it is uncertain. That is why NIST’s initiative emphasizes security, evaluation science and interoperability as much as raw performance. The AI Agent Standards Initiative was explicitly launched to support interoperable and secure innovation, and CAISI’s MOU with GSA ties those ideas to procurement. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/03/caisi-signs-mou-gsa-boost-ai-evaluation-science-federal-procurement-through?utm_source=openai))

What makes this especially important is that the federal government is no longer just a referee. With USAi, GSA is building a centralized procurement and experimentation platform for agencies, and CAISI is helping evaluate the models they may buy. That means the government is creating the practical rules of deployment, not merely advising from the sidelines. In time, those choices can shape the whole commercial market. If an agency can only procure models that meet certain logging, security and benchmarking thresholds, vendors will adapt. In that sense, the government’s procurement standards could become de facto national standards. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/03/caisi-signs-mou-gsa-boost-ai-evaluation-science-federal-procurement-through?utm_source=openai))

There is also a counterargument worth taking seriously: standards can calcify too early. AI systems are improving rapidly, and rules that focus heavily on today’s architectures could become obsolete or even distort innovation. Industry critics of overregulation argue that voluntary standards and flexible evaluation methods are better than hard mandates. They say early federal involvement risks freezing a fast-changing field into compliance rituals that reward paperwork over safety. That concern is not irrational. But the current alternative — a market where every vendor defines its own claims and every customer guesses at the meaning — is worse. The point is not to overpromise certainty. It is to create enough shared measurement to make comparison possible. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/caisi/guidelines?utm_source=openai))

The federal government wants a national AI policy, but states are pushing back

Few AI fights are more consequential than the one over state law. In March, the White House released a national AI legislative framework arguing for uniform policy across the United States and warning that conflicting state laws would burden innovation. Shortly afterward, senators Chris Murphy and Brian Schatz introduced the GUARDRAILS Act to reverse the administration’s effort to prevent states from regulating AI, arguing that states should not be punished for trying to protect families while Congress moves slowly. The White House and its allies argue that a patchwork of state laws would burden innovation and undermine national competitiveness. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

Both claims are partly true, which is why the politics are so hard. A single national rulebook would certainly make compliance simpler for companies. It might also reduce forum-shopping, where firms race to the least restrictive jurisdiction. But a national framework can also become a national loophole if it is too weak. State laws, by contrast, can be more responsive to local harms and public pressure. California’s 2025 action on chatbot disclosure and minors, for example, shows how quickly states can respond to a visible risk. Other states have likewise explored AI rules in areas like hiring, discrimination and transparency. The federal government may dislike that complexity, but complexity is sometimes how democratic systems surface risk early. ([calmatters.org](https://calmatters.org/economy/technology/2025/10/newsom-signs-chatbot-regulations/?utm_source=openai))

What makes the current moment unstable is that AI harms are unevenly distributed. A model failure in a hospital, a hiring system, a school district or a political ad campaign may each require different rules. That makes a single federal law difficult to draft. It also makes a patchwork difficult to live with. The likely result, if Congress does nothing, is a period of legal experimentation in which states act faster than Washington and courts determine how far they can go. That is not ideal, but it may be unavoidable. ([murphy.senate.gov](https://www.murphy.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/murphy-schatz-introduce-legislation-to-reverse-trumps-state-ai-law-moratorium?utm_source=openai))

For the AI industry, the legal uncertainty is costly but not always unwelcome. Firms often prefer broad federal preemption to the prospect of dozens of state regimes. But the industry also benefits from the ability to claim that state rules are fragmented and therefore impractical. In other words, the preemption fight is also a lobbying fight over the pace of accountability. If Congress passes narrow federal rules, the industry wins clarity. If Congress passes broad preemption with weak standards, the industry wins freedom. If Congress does nothing, states become the main source of real constraint. That is why the current battle matters far beyond any single bill. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

Schools and children are where abstract AI risk becomes concrete

If infrastructure and standards are the policy backbone of the AI boom, schools and children are its moral test. Federal education guidance issued in 2025 said AI may be used across key educational functions, with the caveat that such use must align with existing federal requirements. In practice, that means schools are being invited to experiment, but not given a full federal playbook. The result is uneven adoption. Some districts are deploying AI for tutoring, lesson planning and administrative tasks. Others are banning it, or allowing it only in tightly limited forms. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-issues-guidance-artificial-intelligence-use-schools-proposes-additional-supplemental-priority?utm_source=openai))

The reason for caution is obvious. AI can speed up feedback, support differentiation and reduce administrative burdens. But it can also hallucinate, encode bias, create privacy risks and encourage students to outsource thinking. That tension is especially sharp in K-12 settings, where children are not just users but minors with special protections. The FTC’s inquiry into chatbot companions reflects a broader worry that AI systems may blur the line between educational support and emotional dependence. When a product is designed to feel conversational and responsive, the distinction between tool and companion can collapse. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-launches-inquiry-ai-chatbots-acting-companions?utm_source=openai))

California’s recent legislative and executive actions around chatbots for minors show how quickly child safety has become a policy front. The state has moved toward requiring reminders that users are talking to a chatbot, not a human, while also trying not to impose an overbroad ban on AI products. That split decision captures the central dilemma. A blanket ban may be too blunt. A mere disclosure may be too weak. The law still lacks a stable middle ground for systems that are entertaining, educational, manipulative and therapeutic all at once. ([calmatters.org](https://calmatters.org/economy/technology/2025/10/newsom-signs-chatbot-regulations/?utm_source=openai))

What should be emphasized here is that uncertainty cuts both ways. Advocates of more permissive school AI use argue that students need literacy, not prohibition; that AI is already ubiquitous; and that supervised use can teach critical thinking. Critics reply that schools are not testing grounds for a rapidly evolving commercial product whose incentives are not aligned with child development. Both views have merit. What the current evidence suggests is not that AI should be absent from classrooms, but that deployment without guardrails is a governance failure. If districts adopt AI, they need procurement rules, data-minimization policies, parent disclosure, age-appropriate use limits and clear rules about whether student work can be fed into third-party systems. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-issues-guidance-artificial-intelligence-use-schools-proposes-additional-supplemental-priority?utm_source=openai))

Labor markets are changing, but the effects are more uneven than the hype suggests

The labor story around AI is no longer just the old “jobs lost versus jobs created” argument. It is a more complicated process of task redesign, skill upgrading and uneven bargaining power. Stanford’s 2026 AI Index reports that AI investment remains highly concentrated in the United States and that generative AI skill demand is visible in job postings. That is evidence of diffusion, but not equal diffusion. The biggest gains accrue to firms with capital, data and talent to absorb the technology quickly. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

That matters because AI can be a force multiplier for highly skilled workers while making entry-level pathways thinner. In law, consulting, finance, marketing and software, AI is increasingly used to draft, summarize, search and automate routine tasks. Managers may see productivity gains. Workers may see fewer apprentice tasks that once trained them up the ladder. The challenge for labor policy is that the most severe impacts may not show up as mass unemployment. They may show up as slower hiring, weaker wage growth for junior roles, greater contract labor, and pressure to produce more with less supervision. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

Some executives insist the effect will be net positive. Business surveys in 2026 suggest many CEOs expect AI to change their workforces, but not necessarily through layoffs alone. That aligns with a widespread business argument: AI is a tool for augmentation, not replacement. There is truth in that claim. Yet it is also true that firms often use augmentation to justify headcount restraint. If one worker can do the work of one and a half, the company may still choose to keep the payroll flat and capture the efficiency gain. The burden of adjustment then falls on labor markets, not firms. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/newsletter/the-briefing/the-briefing-2026-03-05/?utm_source=openai))

There is a useful comparison here with previous waves of enterprise software and automation. The first-order effect is usually not instant elimination of whole occupations. It is the reshaping of roles, the compression of teams and the reallocation of value toward the owners of complementary assets. In AI, those assets are data, compute, distribution and regulatory influence. That is why concentration matters. Stanford’s report points to a system in which the United States overwhelmingly leads private investment, but within the U.S. activity is still clustered in a few states and firms. The question is whether AI will become a generalized productivity layer or another engine of concentration. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

What complicates the pessimistic narrative is that AI can also lower barriers for small firms and solo workers. A small business can now produce marketing copy, customer support drafts, code snippets and analytic summaries at very low cost. That does not erase the power imbalance, but it does create a genuine democratizing channel. The policy challenge is to widen access to that upside. That means skills training, broadband, affordable compute access, and public-sector deployment that doesn’t just favor incumbent vendors. ([murphy.senate.gov](https://www.murphy.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/murphy-schatz-introduce-legislation-to-reverse-trumps-state-ai-law-moratorium?utm_source=openai))

Public opinion is cautious, not panicked

One reason the political debate feels more brittle than public opinion is that Americans are not uniformly afraid of AI. Pew’s March 2026 findings show the public remains cautious but open to some benefits, while experts are much more enthusiastic. Americans are more comfortable with AI in some functional domains than in intimate ones. They are more willing to let it help with logistics, weather, or some health-care tasks than with relationships or religious or creative judgment. That pattern matters because it suggests the public is drawing a line between assistance and substitution. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

This is where policymaking can get ahead of itself. Industry rhetoric often treats AI skepticism as ignorance to be overcome by exposure. That is too simple. The public may be correctly detecting that AI is more acceptable in bounded, low-stakes contexts than in areas where trust, identity and vulnerability are at issue. A system that helps schedule appointments is one thing. A system that counsels a teenager through loneliness is another. A system that drafts a school lesson plan is one thing. A system that evaluates a student’s ability or emotional state is another. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

That said, public caution is not the same as public rejection. The same survey data show many Americans already encounter AI in everyday life and are willing to see some uses expand. This leaves policymakers with a delicate job: avoid moral panic, but do not confuse convenience with consent. Good AI governance should reflect the public’s differentiated instincts. The market often wants a single answer — yes or no. Society is already giving a more nuanced one. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

The world outside Washington is not waiting

One of the less noticed but most important facts in the current policy environment is that AI governance is being internationalized even as national politics harden. NIST’s international measurement network includes partners from multiple countries and is working on common approaches to evaluation. The OECD has been publishing briefings on AI in health and public administration, and the World Bank has set AI-adjacent development questions within its 2026 agenda. This means the U.S. debate is not happening in isolation. Other governments are building their own frameworks for AI adoption, procurement and safety, and the U.S. is trying to influence those norms while also shaping its own market. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/02/international-network-advanced-ai-measurement-evaluation-and-science?utm_source=openai))

That matters because AI policy is increasingly a competition over standards, not just chips or models. Whoever sets the measurement language often sets the market language. If U.S. agencies and allied institutions align on evaluation methods, they can make it easier for firms to sell systems across borders. If they diverge, vendors will face fragmented compliance burdens and governments will have weaker leverage over safety claims. That is one reason the NIST work on evaluation and the White House’s emphasis on global leadership are strategically linked, even if they look like separate bureaucratic tracks. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a geopolitical dimension that is easy to overstate. AI competition with China is real, but the frontier is not a simple winner-take-all race. The Stanford AI Index suggests the U.S. dominates private investment; NIST’s evaluations suggest some foreign frontier models remain behind leading U.S. models in certain tests; but the global picture is far more mixed, with open-weight releases, model diffusion and cost declines complicating any clean lead. The important point is not to turn AI policy into a sports scoreboard. It is to recognize that technological advantage may come from institutional trust as much as from raw capability. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/12/key-findings-about-how-americans-view-artificial-intelligence/?utm_source=openai))

The hard question is not whether to regulate AI, but where to draw the line

The current moment invites two opposing mistakes. One is to treat AI as so transformative that normal rules no longer apply. The other is to treat it as just another software category with some publicity problems. Both are wrong. AI is already entangled with education, procurement, labor, utilities, federal lands, consumer protection and even public safety. That means the right regulatory question is not whether to govern, but which risks warrant hard limits, which require transparency, and which can be managed through standards and procurement. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-launches-inquiry-ai-chatbots-acting-companions?utm_source=openai))

A sensible approach would separate the AI stack into layers. At the bottom, energy and compute require permits, grid planning and environmental review. At the next layer, model evaluation and agent security require standardized testing, logging and procurement rules. At the application layer, schools, hospitals, workplaces and platforms need sector-specific rules for privacy, notice, human oversight and liability. At the social layer, children, employment and political speech demand especially strong protections because the harm can be irreversible or diffuse. This kind of layered governance is not elegant, but it reflects how the technology is actually being deployed. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

The strongest argument against this approach is that it may slow innovation and cede the field to foreign competitors. But the strongest argument for it is even simpler: if AI is going to be woven into essential public and private systems, then trust is not optional. A system that cannot be measured, powered, explained or constrained will eventually hit a wall — political, infrastructural or social. The federal government seems to understand that, at least in fragments. The challenge now is whether it can act before those fragments harden into a patchwork of conflict. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

That is what makes this spring a real turning point. AI is no longer just the future arriving. It is a set of present-day choices about land, electricity, classrooms, children, labor and law. The boom can keep going. The question is under what terms, and for whose benefit. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

In the coming months, the most important developments will likely come from three places. First, any further DOE decisions on AI and data center siting will show whether federal land becomes a core part of the industry’s expansion strategy. Second, NIST’s evaluation and agent standards work will reveal whether Washington can turn AI measurement into a real market discipline. Third, the state-federal clash over AI rules will show whether Congress intends to write a national framework or leave the issue to the courts and the states. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/energy-department-announces-partnership-ensure-affordable-energy-and-power-americas-ai?utm_source=openai))

There are still major uncertainties. Power demand projections can change if model efficiency improves faster than expected. Public sentiment can shift if there is a high-profile chatbot incident or a labor-market shock. And the policy debate could be overtaken by elections or by a major court ruling. But the broad direction is already clear: AI is moving from a speculative boom to a regulated infrastructure project. That is a more serious story, and a more demanding one, than the industry’s original pitch. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))
