A Spending-Cap Table Waits for the Justices

The 2026 campaign-finance fight is not only about super PACs or billionaires. A small FEC chart — $65,300 for most House races, $4,071,800 for a California Senate race — could decide how closely party committees and candidates may work together before the midterms.

By The Press • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most important campaign document of the spring may not look like campaign literature at all.

It is not glossy. It does not carry a slogan. It does not show a candidate in a rolled-up shirt sleeve pointing at a factory floor. It is a table on the Federal Election Commission website, the sort of government chart that appears to have been made for treasurers, lawyers and people who already know where to find the footnotes.

But the table is now sitting at the edge of a Supreme Court decision. For 2026, the FEC says a political party may spend $65,300 in coordination with a House nominee in states with more than one representative. For House races in states with only one representative, the limit is $130,600. For Senate races, the ceiling runs from $130,600 in the smallest states to $4,071,800 in California. The agency posted the updated limits on March 3, 2026, along with a warning that turns a routine compliance page into a live political artifact: the Supreme Court is considering whether the limits are constitutional, and unless the Court says otherwise, they remain in force.

That pending case, National Republican Senatorial Committee v. Federal Election Commission, is easy to misread as another general argument over big money in politics. It is narrower and more operational than that. The question is whether party committees can spend unlimited money with their candidates — planning the ad, shaping the message, sharing campaign needs, timing the buy — or whether Congress may keep a dollar cap on that cooperation.

The distinction matters because federal campaign law is full of near-twins. A party committee can make independent expenditures to help a candidate, so long as it does not coordinate the spending with the campaign. A super PAC can raise unlimited sums and spend unlimited sums independently, too. But once spending is coordinated with a candidate, federal law has long treated it more like a contribution than like outside speech. Contributions are easier for Congress to limit because they carry a more direct risk that money will buy favors, or appear to buy them.

The Supreme Court heard argument on December 9, 2025. As of the early morning of April 26, 2026, the official docket did not list a decision. It did, however, show continued post-argument movement: on April 20, the Court granted a motion by the Democratic committee intervenors to file a supplemental brief after oral argument. That means the 2026 midterm campaign is operating with a live question tucked beneath it: are the parties still playing under the chart, or are they waiting for the chart to disappear?

The cap is not a donation limit. It is a cooperation limit.

To understand the case, start with the household version of campaign-finance law.

If a person gives money directly to a federal candidate, that is a contribution. Federal law caps it. In the 2025-2026 cycle, the individual limit to a candidate committee is $3,500 per election. The individual limit to a national party committee’s regular federal account is $44,300 per year. Other limits apply to PACs, state and local party committees, candidate committees and the national party committees’ special accounts. The exact numbers change with inflation, but the basic premise is familiar: direct political giving has ceilings.

Spending is different. If a person, corporation, union, super PAC or party committee spends money on its own message without coordinating with a candidate, Supreme Court doctrine generally treats that as independent political speech. Since the Court’s 2010 Citizens United decision and related rulings, independent political spending has been much harder for Congress to cap.

The category at issue in NRSC v. FEC sits between those two worlds. A coordinated party expenditure is money a party spends for the benefit of its nominee after consulting with, cooperating with, or acting at the request or suggestion of the candidate or campaign. The party does not simply hand the money to the candidate. It pays for the thing itself — an ad, mail, polling, campaign services or other campaign expense. But because the party and candidate are working together, federal law treats the expenditure as a special regulated form of party support.

The reporting trail is the physical clue. The FEC says coordinated party expenditures are reported by the party committee on Form 3X, line 25, and are always itemized on Schedule F, regardless of amount. If this case were a detective story, Schedule F would be the scrap under the door. It is where the law tries to distinguish between a party speaking as an outside chorus and a party becoming part of the candidate’s own campaign machine.

The current statutory system traces to the Federal Election Campaign Act framework Congress built in the post-Watergate era. The law says national and state party committees may make certain coordinated expenditures for federal candidates, but only up to limits. Those limits are based on statutory formulas and inflation adjustments. For Senate candidates, they rise with voting-age population and the cost-of-living adjustment. For most House candidates, the national figure is a much smaller flat amount.

The FEC’s 2026 table gives the problem its practical shape. A party may coordinate $65,300 for a House race in Ohio, Arizona, New York, North Carolina or any other state with more than one House seat. That sounds like substantial money until one imagines buying serious television time, producing a late digital campaign, printing districtwide mail or funding field operations in a race where total candidate, party and outside spending can run into many millions. In statewide Senate races, the cap grows with population: $1,369,700 in Pennsylvania, $2,483,800 in Florida, $3,148,400 in Texas and $4,071,800 in California. Those are larger numbers, but modern statewide campaigns can exhaust them quickly.

That is the Republican committees’ practical complaint. They argue that political parties exist to elect their candidates and that the law penalizes them precisely when they do the most natural party thing: talk to the nominee before spending money to help. The federal government, through the Solicitor General’s office, is no longer defending the limits and has sided with the challengers. The Democratic National Committee, Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee and Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee intervened to defend the limits. The Supreme Court also appointed lawyer Roman Martinez to argue in support of the Sixth Circuit judgment after the federal government changed sides.

Both sides say they are defending parties

The case is not a tidy morality play in which one side loves parties and the other hates them. Both camps claim they are trying to rescue political parties from a distorted money system.

The challengers, led by the National Republican Senatorial Committee and the National Republican Congressional Committee, say the limits weaken parties relative to super PACs and other outside groups. A party committee, they argue, is accountable in ways a single-cause outside group is not. Parties have names, platforms, candidates up and down the ballot, donors whose identities are disclosed through federal reports, and institutional reasons to think beyond one television spot. If campaign-finance law drives money away from parties and toward independent outside groups, the challengers say, it strengthens less accountable actors and makes politics less coherent.

That argument has force beyond partisan convenience. For years, scholars, litigants and reformers have debated whether the post-Citizens United system has weakened formal parties while super PACs, politically active nonprofits and donor-driven operations became more important. Party committees must disclose donors and operate under contribution limits for their regular federal accounts. Independent-expenditure-only committees may accept unlimited money for independent spending. A donor who wants maximum firepower in one race can already find legal vehicles outside the party structure.

The counterargument is that the challengers are using a real problem to remove one of the remaining walls against direct donor influence. Defenders of the limits say parties are not helpless outsiders. They are deeply connected to candidates, officeholders and congressional leaders. If a donor who has already given the legal maximum to a candidate can give far more to a party knowing that the party may spend in full coordination with that candidate, then the party can become a side door around candidate contribution limits.

That concern is not abstract. Campaign money often follows relationships, not textbook diagrams. A donor does not need to write an instruction memo saying the funds are for Candidate X if the political need is obvious to everyone involved. The defenders argue that removing the coordinated-spending cap would increase the value of large donations to party committees because party officials could translate those dollars into candidate-approved campaign activity.

That is why the case keeps returning to one legal phrase: quid pro quo corruption. The term means something for something — money or value given in exchange for official action. The modern Supreme Court has narrowed campaign-finance anticorruption law around that concept. General influence, access or gratitude usually is not enough. The fight in this case is whether unlimited coordinated party spending creates a serious enough risk of donor-to-candidate quid pro quo corruption, or the appearance of it, to justify a cap.

A 2001 precedent is the old hinge

The Supreme Court has seen this question before, though the campaign system around it has changed.

In 2001, in a case commonly called Colorado II, the Court upheld federal limits on coordinated party expenditures. The majority treated coordinated party spending as functionally close to a contribution and concluded that limits could prevent donors from using party committees to evade candidate contribution caps. Justice Clarence Thomas dissented, joined by three other justices, arguing that the limits burdened core political speech and association.

The Sixth Circuit, sitting en banc, upheld the limits in September 2024 because Colorado II was still binding Supreme Court precedent. But the lower court did not describe the 2001 ruling as a perfect fit for the modern campaign-finance world. The Supreme Court’s question-presented document quotes the Sixth Circuit’s acknowledgment that later cases, 2014 statutory changes and the rise of unlimited PAC spending had created tension with the old precedent. The appeals court’s point was institutional: lower courts must follow Supreme Court precedent until the Supreme Court itself changes it.

The later cases are the challengers’ runway. In Citizens United, the Court struck down restrictions on independent expenditures by corporations and unions. In McCutcheon v. FEC, it invalidated aggregate limits on how much a donor could give across all candidates and committees combined, while leaving base contribution limits in place. In FEC v. Ted Cruz for Senate, it struck down a limit on how much post-election money a campaign could use to repay a candidate’s personal loans.

The challengers say these decisions narrowed the government’s power to restrict political money and undermined the logic of Colorado II. The defenders say Colorado II remains different because the spending there is coordinated. In plain English: an outside group shouting from across the street is not the same as a party sitting in the campaign war room.

There are also jurisdictional and vehicle questions. The court-appointed amicus raised arguments about whether the case remained properly before the Court and whether the right parties were present under the special campaign-finance review statute. During argument, Justice Thomas pressed the petitioners on why the Republican committees in the case fit the relevant statutory framework when the Republican National Committee was not itself a petitioner. The challengers responded that Vice President JD Vance, who entered the case when he was an Ohio Senate candidate, and the committees still supplied a live controversy. The official docket and transcript show those issues remained part of the case, even as the merits question stayed central.

The 2014 accounts are the loose thread

One of the most important details in the case is not a constitutional phrase. It is a budget category from 2014.

That year, Congress allowed national party committees to create special segregated accounts for presidential nominating conventions, headquarters buildings, and election recounts, contests and other legal proceedings. Donors can give much larger sums to those accounts than to ordinary national party accounts. In the 2025-2026 cycle, the individual limit is $132,900 per account, per year for those additional national party accounts. A party’s national committee, Senate campaign committee and House campaign committee are each considered separate national party committees with separate limits for those accounts, though only a national party committee may have a presidential nominating convention account.

The challengers say those exceptions make the old anticorruption rationale incoherent. If donors may legally give much larger amounts to party accounts for recounts, legal fights, headquarters and conventions, they ask, why is coordinated campaign advertising uniquely dangerous? The argument is not merely mathematical. It is structural: the challengers say Congress has already accepted large party-money channels while singling out the most politically useful form of party support.

The defenders answer that these accounts are not the same as direct campaign support. A recount account or headquarters account does not necessarily pay for a candidate-approved persuasive message to voters. Congress, they argue, can draw practical lines, even imperfect ones, between party infrastructure or litigation expenses and the direct campaign activity that a candidate can help design.

The FEC’s own 2025-2026 data show that the special accounts are not small side jars. In the first 12 months of the cycle, national party segregated accounts reported $143.5 million in receipts. Recount and other legal-proceeding accounts brought in $67.9 million, headquarters accounts $64.6 million and convention accounts $11 million. For the full 2023-2024 cycle, the same broad category of national party segregated accounts reported $446 million in receipts.

Those figures complicate both sides. They support the challengers’ point that federal law already permits large party-money channels. They also support the defenders’ warning that parties are sophisticated fundraising machines, not fragile civic clubs passing a hat.

What changes if the caps fall?

If the Supreme Court strikes down the coordinated party expenditure limits, campaigns will not wake up in a moneyless republic. They will wake up in a system already dense with money, disclosure reports and legal classifications.

The FEC reported that in the 2023-2024 cycle, presidential candidates raised about $2 billion and spent about $1.8 billion; congressional candidates raised about $3.8 billion and spent about $3.7 billion; party committees raised about $2.7 billion and spent about $2.6 billion; and PACs raised about $15.7 billion and spent about $15.5 billion. Independent expenditures reported to the FEC totaled about $4.4 billion for that cycle. For the first 12 months of the 2025-2026 cycle, congressional candidates had already raised about $1.5 billion, parties had raised $834.1 million and PACs had raised $4.6 billion, while reported independent expenditures in connection with congressional elections totaled $56.4 million through December 31, 2025.

Those totals are so large that the mind tends to slide off them. So think instead of one contested House district. Under current rules, a party may coordinate $65,300 in most House races. If the limit disappears, the party could sit with the candidate and plan much more: message testing, ad timing, canvassing, digital targeting, mail, phone banks, opposition research deployment and late attacks. The candidate’s campaign would gain a more efficient partner. The party would no longer have to pretend it does not know what its own nominee needs.

That could make parties more central. A donor choosing between a super PAC and a party might prefer the party if the party can coordinate. Why fund a legally independent outside group that must guess what a campaign wants if a party committee can spend with the campaign’s full cooperation?

But the same change could make high-dollar party giving more valuable. A donor who wants to help a particular candidate could give to a party committee with greater confidence that the party can translate its resources into direct campaign assistance. Disclosure would still matter. Base contribution limits to ordinary party accounts would still matter unless the Court went further in a future case. Earmarking rules would still matter. Bribery laws would still exist. But the practical value of party money could rise.

The defenders fear that this would invite a familiar Washington sentence: everyone knows what is being asked, but nobody says it out loud. The challengers call that suspicion too vague to justify restricting political speech. The defenders call it the reason campaign-finance law exists.

The case is also about who defends a statute

There is a quieter institutional story inside the case: what happens when the federal government declines to defend a federal law that remains on the books.

The FEC is the named respondent, but the federal government told the Supreme Court that the coordinated party expenditure limits should be struck down. The government’s brief framed the case as a rare exception to the usual practice of defending federal statutes. That left the Democratic committees and the court-appointed amicus to defend the law’s constitutionality.

This kind of posture is not unheard of, but it is notable. Congress writes statutes. Agencies enforce them. Administrations sometimes change constitutional positions. When that happens, courts may appoint someone to argue the side that would otherwise be missing, especially when the Court wants the judgment below fully tested. A court-appointed amicus is not a substitute judge. It is a lawyer asked to present arguments that the ordinary parties are no longer making.

In this case, the courtroom lineup was unusual. Noel Francisco argued for the Republican committee challengers. Sarah Harris, then Principal Deputy Solicitor General, argued for the federal respondents in support of the challengers. Roman Martinez argued as court-appointed amicus in support of the judgment below. Marc Elias argued for the Democratic committee intervenors. Campaign-finance law often claims to regulate the system above party. In the courtroom, the system arrived with party committees on both sides and the federal enforcement agency’s name in the caption.

That matters because the case will be read not only as a campaign-finance decision, but as an institutional signal. If the Court strikes down the limits, it will not simply change a compliance table. It will also validate a path in which a new administration can decline to defend a law and leave other institutional actors to carry the defense. If the Court upholds the limits, it will preserve a rule the federal government itself declined to support before the justices.

What the chart cannot answer

The FEC’s 2026 table can tell a party treasurer how much coordinated authority exists in Nevada, Wisconsin, Georgia or California. It cannot answer the deeper democratic question: should parties be treated as ordinary speakers, special corruption risks or both?

Political parties are strange institutions. They are not government, but they help decide who gets to run government. They are private associations, but they organize public power. They can discipline extremists or amplify them. They can aggregate many interests or become shells for a few donors. A healthy democracy probably needs parties strong enough to organize choices and weak enough not to become toll booths.

That is why the case resists a bumper-sticker answer. Striking the limits could strengthen parties at the expense of super PACs. It could also make parties more attractive vehicles for donors seeking influence. Upholding the limits could preserve a barrier against circumvention. It could also keep pushing money into less accountable outside channels. Those possibilities are not mutually exclusive. They are the tradeoff at the heart of the case.

The Court could issue a broad First Amendment ruling, a narrower ruling focused on party coordinated communications, a procedural decision, or some combination. The broadest ruling would overrule or sharply limit Colorado II and erase the coordinated party expenditure caps. A narrower ruling might focus on coordinated advertising, the current statutory exemptions or whether later facts have made the old rule untenable. A procedural ruling could avoid the constitutional question, though the case has already received full briefing, argument and supplemental filings.

Until the decision comes, the chart remains live. Treasurers still count. Lawyers still advise. Campaigns still decide whether a conversation is worth the cap it may trigger. Somewhere, a party operative can look at a battleground district and know that $65,300 is either the rule of the road or a number waiting to be crossed out.

That is what makes the artifact so revealing. The table is dull because law often looks dull right before it changes behavior. A voter may never see Schedule F. A candidate may never mention line 25. But if the Supreme Court rewrites the rule, the next ads, texts, mailers and door knocks may carry the imprint of that little government chart — or of the blank space where it used to be.
