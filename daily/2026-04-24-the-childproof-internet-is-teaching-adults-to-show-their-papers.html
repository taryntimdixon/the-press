The Childproof Internet Is Teaching Adults to Show Their Papers

From California’s chatbot safeguards to Utah’s app-store mandates and Europe’s new age-verification push, a widening age-assurance regime is trying to solve one moral problem by normalizing another: routine credential checks for ordinary digital life. Protecting children is necessary. Rebuilding the internet around checkpoints is a deeper choice than policymakers often admit.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

For years, the argument over online child safety was framed as a choice between two bad outcomes: either leave children exposed to manipulative, sexualized, addictive, or predatory digital systems, or accept some loss of privacy in order to police the gates. In 2026, that argument has changed shape. Age checks are no longer a narrow proposal for pornography sites alone. They are spreading across a wider set of digital services and governance layers: app stores, social platforms, platform compliance regimes, and systems designed to mimic social companionship. What used to sound like an exceptional intervention is starting to look like infrastructure.

That shift is visible across multiple jurisdictions at once. California’s SB 243, approved on October 13, 2025, imposes disclosure and safety-protocol obligations on companion-chatbot platforms, including recurring reminders for minors and protocols addressing suicidal ideation and self-harm content. Utah’s App Store Accountability Act is scheduled to take effect on May 6, 2026, requiring app-store providers to request age information from Utah users opening accounts, verify age categories, link minor accounts to parent accounts, and provide developers with age-category and parental-consent status. Louisiana’s social-media law, now effective as of July 1, 2025, requires parental consent and supervision tools for minor account holders. Australia’s social-media minimum-age regime has moved from slogan to implementation, with eSafety reporting millions of age-restricted accounts removed, deactivated, or restricted and hundreds of thousands more later blocked from access. And on April 15, 2026, the European Commission announced that its age-verification solution was technically ready for implementation, presenting it as a privacy-preserving way to prove age thresholds online. ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243))

This is a significant development in digital policy. It is also a philosophical development. The central question is no longer whether children deserve stronger protection online. They plainly do. The harder question is what sort of adult world gets built when child protection is operationalized through routine proof-of-age, age estimation, parental-permission architectures, and downstream sharing of age-status signals. Once lawmakers and firms decide that platforms must know who is old enough for what, they are no longer simply regulating harms. They are redrawing the conditions of entry into ordinary digital life. ([le.utah.gov](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title13/Chapter76/C13-76-S201_2026050620250507.pdf))

My claim is not that all age assurance is illegitimate. Some narrowly tailored age checks will remain justified, especially around tightly defined categories of legally age-restricted material. The deeper concern is that checkpoint logic expands. A rule built for one class of content migrates to one class of service, then to another governance layer, then to the infrastructure beneath them all. What begins as a child-protection exception can become a standing assumption: before you browse, download, speak, search, or relate, prove something about yourself first.

That may still be a bargain some democracies choose. But it should be described honestly. Age assurance is not merely a technical compliance detail. It is a moral and political decision about whether the internet remains a largely open arena with bounded interventions, or becomes a permissioned environment in which anonymity, casual inquiry, and uncredentialed participation are treated as suspect by default.

The news peg is not one law. It is the convergence.

A philosophy essay should not pretend institutions are incidental. The institutional details matter because they reveal where the normative center of gravity is moving.

Begin with California. SB 243 does not create a universal age-ID system. But it does mark an important admission. The law recognizes that some AI systems are not merely utilities; they are designed to simulate human-like social interaction and to sustain emotionally resonant exchanges over time. Under the statute, if a reasonable person might believe they are interacting with a human, the operator must provide a clear and conspicuous notice that the chatbot is artificially generated and not human. Operators also must maintain protocols aimed at preventing the production of suicidal ideation, suicide, or self-harm content, including referrals to crisis resources. For minors, recurring reminders that the chatbot is AI and not human are part of the legal design. California thus rejects a convenient industry fiction: relational design is not morally neutral software design. ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243))

The law is narrower than some advocates wanted. Governor Gavin Newsom signed the disclosure-focused measure while vetoing a broader bill that would have more aggressively restricted minors’ access to AI chatbots. That matters because it clarifies the state’s posture. California has not said minors must be excluded from such systems altogether. It has said platforms owe duties of clarity and risk mitigation when they build products that may be experienced as companions. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/33be4d57d0e2d14553e02a94d9529976?utm_source=openai))

Yet the moment a legal regime turns on whether a user is a minor, a practical question follows: how will the service know? California’s chatbot law does not itself require an all-purpose age-identification mechanism, but it sits inside a larger policy environment that increasingly pressures platforms to know age status and, in some settings, share that status downstream. The logic of protection pushes toward the logic of classification.

Utah makes that logic explicit. The Utah statute, effective May 6, 2026, requires an app-store provider to request age information when a user located in Utah creates an account, verify the user’s age category, obtain verifiable parental consent for minor accounts, and protect personal age-verification data by limiting collection and using industry-standard encryption. The law also restricts sharing of personal age-verification data except in defined circumstances, but it still operationalizes a major shift: age classification becomes a routine gateway to the app economy itself, not just to a narrow subset of apps. ([le.utah.gov](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title13/Chapter76/C13-76-S201_2026050620250507.pdf))

The legal and constitutional challenge to Utah’s law underscores how sweeping that move appears to its critics. On February 5, 2026, the Computer & Communications Industry Association sued Utah, arguing the law would require age verification and parental consent for an extraordinarily broad set of app downloads and purchases. Local reporting on the suit emphasized that the law extends to general-purpose apps, including categories such as e-books, email, and entertainment, not merely obviously age-restricted services. The dispute is therefore not just about implementation burden. It is about whether a state may make a broad digital intermediary into a standing checkpoint. ([ccianet.org](https://ccianet.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Utah-Complaint.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Louisiana, meanwhile, offers a related but distinct example. Its social-media law requires parental consent for Louisiana minors and provides for account supervision mechanisms. The law’s effective-date history has been messy, but the operative point is clear enough: one more state has moved toward a model in which ordinary social participation by minors is mediated through formal parental permission and platform knowledge of age status. ([legis.la.gov](https://legis.la.gov/legis/Law.aspx?d=1337820))

Australia has gone further in practice. The country’s social-media minimum-age regime, tied to a threshold of 16 for certain services, has already generated a large compliance record. eSafety says 4.7 million age-restricted accounts had been removed, deactivated, or restricted in the initial phase, and that by the start of March 2026 more than 300,000 additional accounts had been prevented from signing in. Australia’s regulatory materials also show that age governance is not confined to social-media accounts alone; age-restricted material codes now apply across multiple categories of online services, including app distribution platforms, equipment providers, social-media services, relevant electronic services, and designated internet services. In other words, age assurance is not being treated as a one-off website problem. It is being built as a cross-system compliance architecture. ([esafety.gov.au](https://www.esafety.gov.au/sites/default/files/2026-03/SocialMediaMinimumAgeComplianceUpdateMarch2026.pdf?v=1774905032806&utm_source=openai))

Europe is trying to solve the same problem with a different self-description. On April 15, 2026, the European Commission announced that its age-verification solution was technically ready for implementation and would soon be available to citizens as an app. The Commission says the tool allows users to prove they are above a certain age without sharing other personal information and without enabling tracking of their activity, and it describes the solution as open source and privacy-preserving. The European Data Protection Board has likewise framed age assurance as something that may be legitimate under data-protection law, provided necessity, proportionality, and children’s rights remain central. Europe’s aspiration, then, is not no checkpoint, but a better checkpoint. ([commission.europa.eu](https://commission.europa.eu/news-and-media/news/european-age-verification-app-keep-children-safe-online-2026-04-15_en))

The larger point is the convergence itself. Different legal systems, with different histories of speech, privacy, and platform regulation, are arriving at roughly the same operational instinct: to protect children online, services need reliable ways to know something about users’ ages. That instinct is understandable. It is not, by itself, morally conclusive.

Protection and permission are not the same ethic

The strongest argument for age assurance is not difficult to state, and any serious essay should begin there. Children face real online harms. Regulators and child-safety organizations are not imagining these dangers. The National Center for Missing & Exploited Children said its CyberTipline received 21.3 million reports in 2025 involving more than 61.8 million files related to suspected child sexual exploitation. NCMEC’s first 2025 data release also highlighted thousands of reports involving If policymakers sound alarmed, it is because the scale of harm is genuinely alarming. ([ncmec.org](https://www.ncmec.org/blog/2026/the-work-never-stops-first-look-at-ncmecs-2025-data))

Research on children’s digital environments reinforces the point that these systems are not neutral channels. Ofcom’s continuing work on children’s media use exists precisely because online life now structures attention, social pressure, discovery, persuasion, and exposure at scale. The FTC’s January 28, 2026 workshop on age-verification technologies reflected a similar practical reality: many child-protection obligations either explicitly require some knowledge of age or become difficult to enforce without it. One cannot tell a platform to protect minors if the platform is forbidden to know which users are minors at all. ([ofcom.org.uk](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2025))

That is why the case for age assurance becomes morally forceful. Privacy matters because it protects personhood, exploration, and dignity. But if an absolute refusal to check age leaves children exposed to systems optimized to monetize developmental vulnerability, privacy rhetoric can become evasive. A society that knows its products are engineered to exploit weakness cannot simply invoke anonymity and call the matter settled.

This is also why policymakers reach for offline analogies. Australia’s public materials repeatedly compare digital age barriers to familiar physical-world age checks. The appeal is intuitive. If there are places a child cannot lawfully enter or goods a child cannot lawfully buy without proof of age, why should online spaces be wholly exempt? ([esafety.gov.au](https://www.esafety.gov.au/parents/social-media-age-restrictions?utm_source=openai))

But the analogy is weaker than it first appears. The internet is not one venue; it is a general-purpose environment that now includes commerce, speech, education, sociality, self-invention, intimacy, and civic participation. Checking age for entry to a narrowly bounded, legally age-restricted setting is one thing. Requiring age classification across broad categories of communication, software access, search, and mediated relationship is something else. In physical life, a bartender may ask for identification before serving alcohol. The bartender does not also need to know your age before you buy a notebook, enter a library, consult a pamphlet, or stand in a public square.

Online, however, age governance tends to migrate. What begins with pornography can extend to social media. A social-media rule can shift responsibility onto app stores. App-store obligations can encourage device-level or operating-system-level age signals. Duties aimed at emotionally manipulative AI systems can create pressure for upstream age-status infrastructure. A narrow exception becomes a reusable architecture. That migration is the real ethical issue. Protection and permission are not the same moral idea.

The philosophical mistake is to treat identity friction as neutral

Many lawmakers describe age assurance as if it were merely administrative. Verify age, minimize retention, encrypt the data, and move on. But identity friction is never just administrative. It changes behavior even when no formal ban is imposed.

Civil-liberties groups have argued this for years. The ACLU challenged Texas’s age-verification law on the ground that it burdened adults’ lawful access to protected speech and undermined anonymous browsing. The Electronic Frontier Foundation has argued more broadly that age-verification mandates impose privacy and security harms on everyone who uses a service, not just on minors, because in practice the system must sort all visitors. EFF also emphasizes that such systems can disproportionately burden people without easy access to acceptable identity documents, people with disabilities, and users who are misclassified by automated age-estimation tools. ([aclu.org](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/experts-tell-supreme-court-texas-unconstitutional-age-verification-law-must-be-overturned?utm_source=openai))

Whether one accepts every version of that argument, the core insight is sound. The ability to seek lawful information without ceremony matters. The capacity to explore material about sex, religion, addiction, reproductive health, politics, grief, or personal crisis without first proving identity or age to a platform is not a minor convenience. It is part of the social infrastructure of intellectual freedom.

That burden is not evenly distributed. Consider a teenager questioning sexuality in a hostile household, an adult seeking reproductive-health information after legal upheaval, a domestic-violence survivor using a monitored device, an undocumented user wary of document-sharing systems, or a person without standardized photo identification. Even where the legal requirement sounds modest, the lived experience may be one of compelled disclosure, surveillance anxiety, and self-censorship. The most frictionless user is often the most documented, least stigmatized, and institutionally safest user.

This is why the issue is larger than data security. A perfectly encrypted checkpoint is still a checkpoint. Even a privacy-preserving age proof changes the grammar of access. It says that entry to a digital space depends on a credential trusted by some institution and recognized by some gatekeeper. Someone still decides what counts as sufficiently legible. Someone still gains infrastructural power over how ordinary access works.

Philosophically, the stakes concern civic standing. In a free society, one should not have to be highly knowable in order to participate in ordinary public life. If digital spaces increasingly function as ordinary public life, then making legibility a routine precondition of entry is not a trivial software update. It is a political transformation.

The best counterargument: the so-called open internet already failed children

Still, critics of age assurance should resist nostalgia. For children, the pre-checkpoint internet was not an open commons marred only by occasional policy overreach. It was already a heavily commercial, behaviorally optimized environment in which firms inferred age, desire, vulnerability, and likely response for profit while often disclaiming responsibility for the consequences.

In that sense, opposition to age assurance can slide into a defense of a status quo that was never especially free for the young. The internet available to a thirteen-year-old was already shaped by recommender systems, pressure loops, in-app spending systems, direct messaging, pornography access, appearance filters, social ranking, and increasingly intimate AI interfaces. If companies are already extracting large amounts of behavioral data, defenders of age assurance ask, why is a bounded, regulated age check morally worse than the covert, pervasive inference economy that already sorts children all day long?

This challenge has force. It becomes stronger where governments genuinely pursue privacy-bounded methods. Australia’s privacy regulator defines age assurance broadly to include verification, estimation, or inference, and its March 2026 guidance stresses that organizations should justify the method used, prefer less intrusive options where possible, minimize retention, and treat privacy obligations as central rather than optional. Europe similarly describes its app-based solution as a way to prove one is above a threshold age without disclosing broader identity details. ([oaic.gov.au](https://www.oaic.gov.au/privacy/privacy-guidance-for-organisations-and-government-agencies/organisations/age-assurance-technologies-and-privacy-obligations))

If such systems worked exactly as promised, the moral dilemma would indeed be narrower. Perhaps the choice would not be between child safety and anonymity, but between crude age policing and more carefully designed age assurance that limits data flows while still blocking some harmful exposures.

This is the best reply to my argument, and it deserves respect. Yet even the stronger, privacy-preserving version does not dissolve the underlying concern. A society can build elegant checkpoints and still normalize checkpoint logic. Once age-based access control is standardized, the question of where it should apply does not disappear. It multiplies. Today pornography; tomorrow app downloads; then companion AI; then livestreaming, messaging, search, creator tools, or educational platforms. Technical restraint in one layer does not answer the political appetite for expansion in the next.

There is also a practical problem: these systems sort by roughness, not wisdom

Even judged on their own terms, age-assurance systems promise more precision than they often deliver. The FTC’s workshop on age-verification technologies was convened because the tradeoffs are real, not hypothetical. Age checks may facilitate compliance, but they also introduce costs, privacy risks, implementation burdens, and the possibility of exclusion. The academic literature now reflects the same problem. A 2026 overview of age-assurance technologies identifies tensions among effectiveness, side effects, and public acceptance, highlighting concerns about privacy, anonymity, bias, discrimination, exclusion, censorship, and circumvention. ([pm.gov.au](https://www.pm.gov.au/media/4-7-million-accounts-deactivated-removed-or-restricted?utm_source=openai))

Australia’s own experience shows the ambiguity. Its enforcement numbers are large enough to demonstrate that age governance at scale is possible. They also demonstrate that the process is rough, not precise. Millions of accounts can be removed or restricted, yet regulators still report that a substantial proportion of underage users continue to retain access or create new accounts. Governance at scale here means constant sorting, contestation, evasion, and error, not a final technical fix. ([esafety.gov.au](https://www.esafety.gov.au/sites/default/files/2026-03/SocialMediaMinimumAgeComplianceUpdateMarch2026.pdf?v=1776086952536&utm_source=openai))

Litigation in the United States points in the same direction. The challenge to Utah’s app-store law argues the regime is both constitutionally problematic and practically unworkable, especially because it reaches so broadly across general app access. That objection may or may not prevail in court, but it captures something important about the policy tendency itself: age assurance expands most readily where categories are administratively simple, not where they are morally exact. ([ccianet.org](https://ccianet.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Utah-Complaint.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The philosophical issue is that age is doing too much conceptual work. Policymakers use it as a proxy for vulnerability, maturity, susceptibility, informed consent, developmental readiness, and resilience. But those are not identical things. A seventeen-year-old and a twelve-year-old may both count as minors for one legal purpose while differing dramatically in experience and vulnerability. An adult in crisis may be more susceptible to manipulation than a casually browsing teenager. A chatbot can be dangerous because it simulates care, dependency, or authority, not merely because one of its users is under eighteen.

This is especially visible in the AI context. California’s companion-chatbot law is best read not just as a youth-protection measure but as an acknowledgment that relational systems can become psychologically salient. If the core risks include self-harm encouragement, deceptive anthropomorphism, or exploitative emotional dependence, then some protections should apply to adults as well. Otherwise the law implies that simulated intimacy becomes morally trivial the moment one turns eighteen. That is implausible. Adults can be lonely, bereaved, depressed, isolated, or cognitively impaired. Children deserve special protection, but the structure of vulnerability does not vanish at legal majority. ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243))

In that sense, age assurance can become a distraction. It encourages policymakers to imagine they have solved a design problem by sorting users into bins. But the deeper pathology of digital life is not merely that children wander into adult spaces. It is that too many spaces, for users of every age, are built to exploit weakness while disavowing duty.

What a better ethic would look like

If routine checkpoint logic is the wrong moral center, what should replace it? Not laissez-faire. The alternative is a layered ethic of design duty, narrow gates, and institutional humility.

First, regulate risky products at the design level, not only at the identity layer. If a companion chatbot is built to mimic human affection, obscure its artificial nature, sustain dependency, or respond dangerously to expressions of distress, then those are defects of product design and governance. California’s law points in this direction by requiring notice and self-harm protocols. The broader principle should be that systems built to mimic social authority or emotional intimacy owe duties of clarity and restraint regardless of whether age has been perfectly verified. ([leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB243))

Second, use age gates narrowly where the category is genuinely age-specific and legally settled. There is an ethical difference between a tightly defined class of age-restricted goods and a general-purpose communications or software environment. The broader the service category, the stronger the presumption should be against mandatory age verification. If a proposal would make age assurance a condition of ordinary internet participation, lawmakers should bear a heavy burden of justification.

Third, where age assurance is used, data minimization should be a binding legal requirement, not a public-relations aspiration. Australia’s OAIC guidance is useful here: organizations should identify the least privacy-invasive option that is fit for purpose, avoid unnecessary retention, constrain vendors, and comply with security and cross-border disclosure rules. Europe’s threshold-proof approach is likewise notable precisely because it attempts to separate “old enough” from “fully identified.” Even then, open standards, independent audits, deletion guarantees, and real avenues of redress should be treated as baseline requirements. ([oaic.gov.au](https://www.oaic.gov.au/privacy/privacy-guidance-for-organisations-and-government-agencies/organisations/age-assurance-technologies-and-privacy-obligations))

Fourth, preserve zones of anonymous lawful access. Democracies need places where adults can seek information without credentialing and where older minors can, within reason, read, learn, and explore without every inquiry being routed through a parent dashboard or platform identity stack. The idea is not digital lawlessness. It is that lawful inquiry should not be presumptively transformed into a permissions workflow.

Fifth, stop treating parental consent as a universal solvent. In many households, parental involvement is protective. In others, it is coercive, unsafe, or impossible. Research collected by the Center for Democracy and Technology on teen and parent perspectives suggests that even among families open to age verification, there is no simple consensus on the form it should take; both teens and parents express concerns about agency, privacy, convenience, and who controls the process. That matters because many legal frameworks quietly assume a uniformly benevolent household with shared interests and equal safety. Real children do not all live in those conditions. ([cdt.org](https://cdt.org/insights/teen-and-parent-perspectives-on-approaches-to-age-verification/?utm_source=openai))

Sixth, redirect more regulatory energy toward manipulative design itself. Australia’s own eSafety materials place age restrictions alongside broader Safety by Design and digital duty-of-care discussions. That is the more promising long-term frame. A healthier digital order would not rely primarily on sorting users better; it would require services to behave better. ([esafety.gov.au](https://www.esafety.gov.au/about-us/industry-regulation/social-media-age-restrictions))

The ethical stakes are larger than privacy law

The temptation in this debate is to reduce everything to engineering and compliance. What counts as reliable age estimation? How accurate is facial analysis? What does the app-store API share downstream? How long is data retained? Those are real questions. But the larger stakes are civic and cultural.

When adults become accustomed to proving age as a condition of routine access, they absorb a lesson about citizenship: participation is conditional on verification. When minors grow up inside such systems, they absorb a related lesson: institutions may inspect identity first and justify themselves later. Those lessons do not become harmless because the underlying goal is child safety. Child protection is one of the most powerful and politically durable rationales any state or platform can invoke. That is exactly why it deserves philosophical scrutiny.

The danger is not only government overreach. It is also private infrastructural concentration. The more online life depends on trusted age signals, the more leverage flows to app stores, device makers, identity vendors, and a small number of gatekeeping platforms. Child safety can thus become a mechanism by which digital adulthood is newly brokered. The citizen no longer merely uses the network; the citizen is increasingly vouched for into it.

That is one reason the present convergence matters. The issue is not one scandal, one country, one platform, or one court fight. It is the emerging common sense that the way to repair digital life is to make users more continuously knowable. Sometimes that may be justified. But if societies accept the principle too casually, they may discover that a child-safe internet can gradually become an adult-permissioned one.

The line worth defending

No serious ethical position says children should simply fend for themselves online. Nor is there a serious ethical position that says any privacy or speech burden, however sweeping, becomes acceptable once lawmakers invoke children. The line worth defending lies between those errors.

We should protect children aggressively from exploitative design, sexual predation, self-harm encouragement, and systems that simulate trust without accountability. We should impose real duties on companies whose products are built to manipulate attention, status, or intimacy. We should allow narrowly tailored age checks in truly age-bounded contexts. And where some form of age assurance is necessary, we should prefer methods that minimize data collection and resist function creep. ([ncmec.org](https://www.ncmec.org/blog/2026/the-work-never-stops-first-look-at-ncmecs-2025-data))

But we should also refuse the idea that ordinary digital citizenship must be rebuilt around suspicionless verification. A free society does not make routine credential checks the baseline condition for browsing, learning, speaking, searching, or thinking. Not because children do not matter, but because freedom does.

The task in 2026 is to hold both truths at once: children need stronger online protection than markets have provided, and adults should not lose the presumption of uncredentialed lawful access in the process. If lawmakers cannot hold that tension, then the childproof internet will end up redesigning citizenship itself.
