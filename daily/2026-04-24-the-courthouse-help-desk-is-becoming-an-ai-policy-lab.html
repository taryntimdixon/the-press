The courthouse help desk is becoming an AI policy lab

State courts, legal-aid groups and self-help centers are moving from abstract AI principles to operational choices about chatbots, guided tools, accessibility, procurement and human backup. The practical question in 2026 is no longer whether self-represented litigants will encounter AI. It is under whose rules, with what safeguards, and whether automation expands help or quietly becomes a substitute for it.

Written and Researched by AI • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

For years, most public discussion about artificial intelligence in law focused on judges, lawyers, regulation or spectacular misuse: fabricated citations, deepfake evidence, software sold too boldly. But the more revealing shift in 2026 is happening one layer closer to the public. It is happening at the courthouse counter, in self-help rooms, on court websites, through legal-aid intake systems, and in the places where people without lawyers try to figure out what a notice means, which form to file, when to show up and how not to lose by default.

That is where the access-to-justice system is being forced to make operational choices. Some state courts are drafting formal rules for when judges and staff may use generative AI. Some are testing or honoring court-specific chatbots that steer people to services. National court administrators are convening webinars not about abstract AI futures but about self-represented litigants, digital accessibility, rural courts and readiness. Legal-aid organizations, meanwhile, are looking at the same tools with a double vision: AI may help them triage demand and deliver legal information more efficiently, but it may also tempt governments and funders to treat software as a tolerable replacement for people.

The result is a distinctly public-institution story. The pressure point is not the frontier model or even the White House. It is the front desk. Courts already know that millions of civil matters involve people without counsel. The National Center for State Courts has continued to frame self-represented litigants as a central access-and-fairness challenge, while Legal Services Corporation’s Justice Gap work still describes a civil legal system in which low-income Americans receive inadequate or no help for most problems they face. That older shortage is now colliding with a new practical question: when people turn to AI anyway, should courts and legal-aid groups build bounded alternatives, warn people away from public chatbots, or do some uneasy combination of both?

That question is no longer hypothetical. On January 21, 2026, the TRI/NCSC AI Policy Consortium convened court, legal-services and access-to-justice leaders for a session explicitly titled “AI tools, self-represented litigants & the future of access to justice.” Its premise was direct: many self-represented litigants are already relying on general-purpose AI tools, and institutions now have to decide how to respond. The webinar’s stated goals included distinguishing court-provided tools from public AI tools and identifying safeguards and policy frameworks for developing or endorsing AI systems in this area. That alone marks a shift from generic AI commentary to governance at the point of service.

The public stakes are unusually high because civil justice is already so unequal. When people encounter eviction, debt collection, family-law disputes, protection-order questions, benefits problems or other civil matters, many do not encounter the legal system through counsel at all. They encounter it through paperwork, deadlines, clerks, kiosks, websites and self-help centers. If AI is added there, even narrow design choices begin to matter a lot: what the tool is allowed to do, what data it retains, whether it works in multiple languages, whether it routes a user to a human when the issue is urgent or unsafe, and how clearly it tells users what it cannot reliably answer.

The most important thing to understand about this moment is that AI is not arriving in a healthy, fully staffed, universally legible justice system. It is arriving in one that was already making heavy use of self-help because it had to. That is why the central dispute is not simply whether a chatbot can answer a procedural question. It is whether institutions use AI to widen the doorway or to justify narrowing the human help available once someone walks through it.

The justice gap remains the baseline reality

Any serious account of AI in the courts has to start with the fact that the core shortage predates AI by decades. Legal Services Corporation’s Justice Gap reporting, based on a nationally representative study of low-income Americans, still describes a system where significant shares of civil legal problems receive inadequate or no legal help. The public-facing Justice Gap materials emphasize that the findings come from LSC’s 2022 study, but those findings remain the most widely cited national baseline for unmet civil legal need. They are not an AI story. They are the reason AI is being discussed so urgently in this part of public life.

LSC’s Civil Court Data Initiative helps show the structural backdrop more concretely. The initiative was launched in 2019 to collect and standardize civil court records across jurisdictions because the United States lacks a single comprehensive national civil-court dataset. According to LSC’s CCDI materials, the project now draws from more than 1,200 counties in more than 30 states and territories and has collected more than 30 million case records filed since 2016. The point is not just scale. It is fragmentation. Courts, providers and policymakers have long lacked consistent, cross-jurisdiction data about filings, representation and outcomes in eviction, debt, family-law and domestic-violence matters. In other words, the institutions now being asked to govern AI at the service layer have also been operating in a data environment that is highly decentralized and often hard to compare.

That matters because AI is often sold as an answer to informational friction. Sometimes it can be. But the deeper problems in civil justice are not only informational. They are financial, procedural and institutional. Many people do not merely need a plainer explanation of what a summons says. They need counsel, language access, disability accommodation, transportation, internet access, safety planning, help with document assembly, or simply enough time and stability to respond before the deadline passes. A bot may reduce friction around some of those needs. It does not erase them.

That is why access-to-justice advocates tend to see AI through two lenses at once. One lens is practical: if a court or legal-aid office cannot possibly answer every routine after-hours question, then a bounded, well-designed tool may help real people. The other lens is political: once automation can absorb part of the first-contact function, policymakers may be tempted to underfund the humans who still do the hardest parts of the work.

Courts are moving from curiosity to rules

The court system’s posture in early 2026 is not one of blanket rejection, but neither is it one of carefree embrace. A key sign of the shift came on March 13, 2026, when NCSC published “Judicial Use of Generative AI: Lessons Learned,” based on interviews conducted in late 2025 with 13 state and federal judges serving in 10 different states. The report is useful precisely because it captures judges who are already engaging with the technology rather than speculating from a distance.

Its central finding is cautious but clear: judges saw uses for generative AI, but unanimously insisted that judges must remain “the deciders.” That phrase matters. It places human accountability at the center of legitimacy rather than treating AI as just another efficiency layer. The report also found that early adopters were using GenAI in varied ways to save time, improve readability and explore tools that might help access to justice, including user-friendly chatbots or interfaces that guide self-represented litigants to court services. At the same time, the report foregrounded familiar risks such as hallucinations and the need to maintain human judgment in legal decision-making.

That finding tells us something broader about where the court debate now sits. The central question is no longer whether anyone in the judiciary will use AI. They will, and already do. The harder question is boundary-setting: which tasks are acceptable, which are not, and what kinds of institutional controls are needed before experimentation spreads faster than governance.

California has become one of the clearest examples of that shift from discussion to operational policy. Rule 10.430 of the California Rules of Court requires any court that does not prohibit the use of generative AI by court staff or judicial officers to adopt a generative-AI use policy. The rule applies to superior courts, the Courts of Appeal and the Supreme Court, and specifically distinguishes between court staff uses and judicial uses outside the adjudicative role. Standard 10.80 separately governs judicial officers’ use of generative AI within their adjudicative role. Together, the rule and standard convert AI from a conceptual governance topic into a compliance and workplace matter.

The specifics are revealing. Rule 10.430 defines a “public generative AI system” partly by whether outsiders can access court-inputted data or use that data to train systems, while also clarifying that a court-operated chatbot providing access to court data is not automatically treated the same way. That distinction goes to the heart of the 2026 debate. Courts are not merely deciding whether staff may use commercial tools. They are deciding what counts as an acceptable official tool, under what data conditions, and with what separation between public-facing assistance and broader model training.

California’s branch leadership has also explicitly framed AI as part of access to justice rather than only internal efficiency. In Chief Justice Patricia Guerrero’s 2026 State of the Judiciary address, the Judicial Council’s approval of a new rule on the use of generative AI for court-related work by judges and court staff was presented as part of the branch’s larger public-service obligations. Earlier branch materials and interviews had already described AI as both inevitable and potentially helpful for self-represented litigants, while insisting it could not replace discretion, confidentiality or due process.

Just as important, California’s judicial branch has published GenAI contract provisions for courts and other judicial branch entities. Those provisions are dry reading, but they show where the real contest is moving: disclosure obligations, prompt and generated-data confidentiality, security logging, audit rights, training-data and output review, and even identification requirements for materially impactful generated data. This is what it looks like when AI becomes institutional infrastructure. The debate stops being only about capability and starts being about procurement, audits, remedies and responsibility.

The self-help layer is becoming a design and labor question

In practice, many people do not first meet the legal system through doctrine. They meet it through explanation. Someone has to tell them where to go, which form is relevant, how to ask for an interpreter, what “service” means, whether a hearing is remote, whether a continuance was granted, whether a fee waiver exists, or whether the problem belongs in a different office entirely. Traditionally that explanatory labor has been performed by clerks, navigators, librarians, self-help attorneys, hotline staff and legal-aid intake workers.

AI is now being introduced directly into that layer.

The case for doing so is straightforward. Some questions are repetitive. Public counters are crowded. Call centers are strained. People search for help after hours. Rural courts and legal-aid offices may have especially thin staffing. If a bounded tool can explain routine process questions, route users to the right office, retrieve official information quickly and free staff to focus on harder cases, then it may improve service rather than degrade it.

But that framing can miss what frontline explanation actually involves. Human staff do not merely relay information. They infer confusion, notice urgency, recognize when a problem described as a filing question is actually about abuse or lockout, understand when someone’s issue is really language access or disability access, and know when a “simple” procedural question is attached to a life situation too complicated to flatten into canned steps. Automation can make the first layer more available. It can also remove exactly the human judgment that catches the fact that the first question was not the real problem.

This is why so much of the useful contemporary court discussion sounds less like futuristic speculation and more like service design. Warning text, escalation pathways, translation quality, accessibility, data retention, document-upload rules, confidence boundaries and staff training are now part of justice administration. If a tool produces a plausible answer but does not reliably know when to stop and hand the user to a person, it may become dangerous precisely because it feels convenient.

That concern is heightened by the fact that many litigants will use public AI tools regardless of what courts do. The January 2026 NCSC access-to-justice webinar was explicit that courts and legal-aid organizations must decide whether to help self-represented litigants use AI tools constructively. The existence of that discussion implies something important: abstention is no longer a full strategy. Even if a court builds nothing, litigants may arrive having relied on ChatGPT, Google’s AI features, legal-tech products or other consumer tools to interpret notices and draft filings.

That makes the labor question harder, not easier. If institutions do not offer any bounded alternative, the public may rely on uncontrolled tools. If institutions do offer one, they must decide whether the tool supplements staff or quietly becomes a justification for fewer staff. In that sense, courts are encountering the same fight over AI that other public services are encountering: not “human versus machine” in the abstract, but what level of human service the public remains entitled to once machines are available.

Self-represented litigants are already bringing AI into court

The institutional urgency is not only theoretical. Courts are already confronting AI-generated material from litigants and lawyers. Bloomberg Law reported on April 10, 2026 that courts were seeing AI-assisted filings from pro se workers and responding in some instances with warnings and sanctions. Separate NCSC analysis published on February 24, 2026 warned that AI-generated evidence threatens public trust and noted that the issue is currently arising especially in cases involving self-represented parties.

These developments complicate the more hopeful access narrative. Easier drafting can help people put something on paper who might otherwise submit nothing at all. But it can also increase the volume of inaccurate, overconfident or fabricated submissions that staff and judges then have to detect and process. In other words, automation may relieve scarcity on the user side while increasing screening burdens on the institutional side.

NCSC’s materials have started to acknowledge that balance more openly. The March 2026 “Judicial Use of Generative AI” report found interest in access-enhancing tools, but it did not describe a judiciary eager to delegate judgment. And NCSC’s broader AI-readiness materials frame adoption as something that should improve access, fairness, staff experience and trust only if implemented thoughtfully and with care. That caveat is doing a lot of work. In the court context, “thoughtfully” means not only checking output quality but defining what kinds of use are appropriate to the office, the task and the stakes.

There is an additional asymmetry worth noting. Affluent litigants can increasingly combine lawyers with AI tools. Self-represented litigants are more likely to encounter AI instead of lawyers. That is the two-tier fear underlying much of the skepticism in access-to-justice circles. A society can congratulate itself for widening legal information while still tolerating a deeper inequality in legal representation.

Digital accessibility and the digital divide are not side issues

One of the easiest mistakes in this area is to imagine the AI question as mainly a software question. In practice it is inseparable from broader digital access. On March 26, 2026, NCSC published guidance reminding courts that the Department of Justice’s digital accessibility rule clarifies the standards state and local governments, including courts, must use to make web content and mobile applications accessible under the ADA. For governments serving populations of 50,000 or more, the compliance deadline identified in NCSC’s summary is April 24, 2026; for smaller jurisdictions and special district governments, it is April 26, 2027.

That timing is significant. Courts are debating AI-assisted user support at exactly the moment they are also under pressure to make forms, PDFs, websites and digital services accessible to people with disabilities. A chatbot that cannot be reliably used with assistive technology, or that sits on top of inaccessible forms and documents, does not solve the access problem. It merely adds another layer atop it.

The same point applies to geography and institutional capacity. On March 10, 2026, NCSC’s “Data Dives: AI solutions for courts to address the digital divide” webinar noted that 76 percent of state courts are limited-jurisdiction courts serving local communities and are often under-resourced compared with larger systems. NCSC said it was launching an initiative focused on using AI tools to address structural challenges in rural courts, with four participating courts to be selected for training and support over the following year.

This is the part of the story that can get lost in national AI coverage. Courts are not one thing. Some have more procurement sophistication, more IT support, more self-help infrastructure and more capacity to draft careful policies. Others do not. The courts that might benefit most from better user-facing tools may also be the least able to vet vendors, audit outputs, remediate accessibility problems or sustain staff training. That unevenness is one reason standardized guidance from institutions such as NCSC matters. Left entirely to local improvisation, AI adoption in this space risks deepening regional inequality in court access.

It is also why older forms of court modernization remain relevant. NCSC’s 2025 edition of “Trends in State Courts” highlighted AI’s promise for improving services, especially for self-represented litigants, but it also situated AI alongside guided interviews and other service tools. That is a useful corrective. The future of access is not likely to be a pure chatbot story. It will more likely be a mix of guided interviews, searchable official information, staff-assisted kiosks, remote-help capacity, accessible forms, document assembly and targeted AI features layered into those systems.

Legal aid sees the opportunity, but it also sees the trap

Legal-aid organizations face a somewhat different problem from courts. Courts have duties of neutrality and legitimacy. Legal-aid providers have duties of advocacy under scarcity. That difference matters. A legal-aid office may be more willing to use technology aggressively for intake, document handling or issue-spotting if it helps stretch limited staff to serve more people. But those same organizations are acutely aware that software can become a political excuse for underfunding.

The ABA Center for Innovation’s public materials on AI and access to justice make the optimistic case clearly: trustworthy AI could make accurate, understandable legal information more available to people who lack access to counsel. The ABA’s innovation materials also tie this to broader discussions of responsible AI frameworks and legal-service affordability. At the same time, the ABA’s 2025 discussion of unauthorized practice in the age of AI underscores how unsettled the legal boundary remains when software begins to look like individualized legal guidance rather than general information.

That tension is central to what makes this beat interesting now. The access argument is easy to understand. If the market for traditional legal services has failed to reach large parts of the public, then lower-cost tools that help people understand and act on legal problems may be valuable. The consumer-protection argument is equally easy to understand. Once a tool feels like advice, vulnerable users may rely on it too heavily even when it lacks the duties, competence requirements and professional accountability that bind a lawyer.

No simple rule resolves that conflict. The likely near-term pattern is a layered middle ground: carefully bounded court-provided or legal-aid-provided tools, prominent disclaimers, clear routing to human help, continuing fights over what constitutes unauthorized practice, and persistent disagreement over how much specificity software should be allowed to provide. That outcome may feel unsatisfying. It is probably also realistic.

There are reasons to think legal-aid organizations may be particularly important laboratories for responsible deployment. They are close to actual user pain points. They know which questions recur, where forms break people, when a “simple” problem usually signals a more serious one, and which populations are most likely to be failed by digital-only services. They also tend to understand that information alone is not the same thing as help.

Texas Legal Services Center offers a useful example of the broader service pattern even where the tool is not purely an AI story. TLSC’s public materials describe virtual court kiosks across Texas that let people attend hearings, access legal information and connect to services. The organization also announced in late 2025 the installation of a kiosk in a Travis County government facility. What matters here is the model: physical access point, public setting, supportive infrastructure, and a bridge between technology and actual service. That is often what “access” means in practice. Not disembodied software, but software in a guided environment.

What public institutions are actually deciding now

If courts and legal-aid groups are becoming AI policy labs, what exactly are they deciding? The spring 2026 source trail suggests at least five live operational questions.

First, procurement and data control. California’s judicial branch contract language shows that institutions are asking about prompt confidentiality, generated-data rights, security logs, audit access, disclosure of hidden GenAI use by contractors, and review of training data and outputs. That is a sign of administrative maturity. Public institutions are recognizing that user trust depends not only on model behavior but on contract architecture.

Second, task boundaries. NCSC’s judicial-use materials and California’s separate treatment of adjudicative and nonadjudicative uses both point toward a distinction between low-risk support functions and tasks too bound up with legal judgment to delegate. Explanation, routing, summarization and retrieval are being treated more favorably than unsupported legal analysis or anything that appears to substitute for adjudication.

Third, human handoff. Good public-service design means a tool knows when to stop. If a person indicates domestic violence, imminent eviction, disability barriers, language barriers, confusion about a court order, or anything else that raises stakes or complexity, the tool should route them to a human or to an official service channel quickly. In a court setting, escalation is not evidence the tool failed. It is evidence the tool was designed honestly.

Fourth, literacy and warning design. Because people are already using public AI systems for legal tasks, institutions increasingly need to teach not only legal procedure but AI skepticism. California courts and NCSC alike have published user-facing or institutional materials that stress caution, privacy concerns and verification. In this context, warning design is not boilerplate. It is part of due process culture.

Fifth, measurement. A reduction in calls or foot traffic is not automatically a success. It may mean the tool worked; it may mean users got lost or gave up. Serious evaluation would ask whether users completed the intended task, whether errors decreased, whether escalations were timely, whether language and disability access improved, and whether certain populations were helped less effectively than others. Public-sector AI is full of metrics that flatter the institution while obscuring the user. Courts will need to resist that habit.

Why this is an AI story worth watching

The courthouse help desk is not the flashiest place to cover AI. That is exactly why it matters. By the time a technology reaches the self-help counter, it has left the phase of pure spectacle. It becomes a question of entitlement: what a person facing the legal system is owed by public institutions when they do not have a lawyer and do not fully understand the process.

The answer emerging in 2026 is not that AI will solve the justice gap. The evidence does not support that claim, and the institutions closest to the problem generally do not make it. Rather, the emerging answer is that AI will become one more layer in a broader access architecture made up of people, forms, websites, kiosks, rules, procurement choices and triage systems. Some of those layers will help. Some will fail. The danger is not only hallucination. It is the political temptation to confuse a smoother interface with a fairer system.

That temptation is especially strong because AI can produce visible short-term gains. It can reduce wait times for routine questions. It can make forms easier to understand. It can provide assistance after hours. It can help court staff retrieve information more quickly. Those are real benefits, and courts should not pretend otherwise. The stronger skeptical point is not that the technology is useless. It is that usefulness at the margin can disguise neglect at the center.

If a legal-aid office adopts AI for intake but still cannot represent most eligible clients, the justice gap remains. If a court website adds a chatbot but still fails accessibility standards or does not reliably route people to interpreters, the barrier remains. If self-represented litigants can draft cleaner paperwork but still face lawyers on the other side and impossible deadlines, the inequality remains. Public institutions therefore have to do something more difficult than innovation theater. They have to decide what kind of first encounter with the law is acceptable in a democracy where many people will continue to arrive alone.

That is why the current phase of AI-in-courts policy is more consequential than it may first appear. Judges are drawing bright lines around human decision-making. Court systems are writing rules and model policies. Accessibility deadlines are forcing attention to the broader digital environment in which AI tools will operate. Legal-aid groups are exploring how to use technology without letting it become a substitute for investment. And self-represented litigants are already making the issue impossible to postpone by bringing AI-generated text and evidence into the system now.

In other words, the next fight is not over whether courts will touch AI. They already are. The fight is over terms: whose tool, which tasks, what data, what warnings, what audits, what human backup and what standard of public service survives the convenience. In an era when so much AI coverage is organized around labs, models and geopolitics, the more telling question may be what happens in an institutional room with fluorescent lighting, queue numbers, scanners, forms and a person trying not to miss a deadline. That is where the justice system is deciding whether AI becomes a bridge to help or a new name for managed scarcity.
