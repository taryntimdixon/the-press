Sudan enters fourth year of war with aid workers warning the world has moved on

At a Berlin donor conference, officials warned that Sudan’s war is deepening while global attention shifts elsewhere, leaving millions displaced, hungry and at risk.

Written and Researched by AI • April 16, 2026 • 1:22 p.m.

Sudan marked three years of war this week with a blunt warning from aid officials: the conflict has become a catastrophe the world has learned to live with, even as civilians cannot. The latest appeals from the United Nations and humanitarian groups describe a country where homes, hospitals, markets and schools have been shattered, where displacement continues to climb, and where hunger is spreading faster than relief can reach it. On Wednesday, as diplomats and aid agencies gathered in Berlin to try to mobilize support, the message from the ground was that the emergency is not fading. It is hardening into a long war of attrition.

The numbers remain staggering. The UN says Sudan’s war has driven millions from their homes and left tens of millions in need of assistance. AP reported this week that at least 13 million people have fled their homes, while nearly two out of three Sudanese now require aid. In Port Sudan, where many displaced families have sought refuge, health workers are treating malnourished infants in overcrowded stabilization centers. Aid officials warn that the health system is buckling under the combined pressure of war injuries, disease outbreaks and shortages of fuel, medicines and supplies. The crisis is not just large; it is compounding.

What makes this moment especially dangerous is the mismatch between the scale of suffering and the scale of attention. Sudan’s war began in April 2023 as a power struggle between the army and the Rapid Support Forces, but in the months since, the conflict has become a proxy battleground with regional implications and repeated accusations of outside backing. At the same time, the war has struggled for space in a crowded geopolitical news cycle. Aid agencies and UN officials say that the result is predictable: fewer headlines, slower funding and an even smaller chance of creating pressure for a ceasefire.

A war that has become a catastrophe in plain sight

The UN’s Sudan office said this week that the country continues to face a humanitarian crisis of immense scale and suffering, describing homes, markets, hospitals and schools as destroyed and the response as critically underfunded. The UN relief chief has also warned that the world is failing Sudan at the very moment the war is entering its fourth year. Human Rights Watch urged leaders meeting in Berlin to commit to time-bound measures to protect civilians and increase pressure on the warring parties. The underlying concern is that Sudan is no longer merely underreported. It is being normalized.

That normalization is visible in the war’s geography. The army has consolidated control over much of the north, east and central regions, while the RSF and its allies hold parts of Darfur and Kordofan. The country is effectively partitioned, even if neither side can claim decisive victory. Meanwhile, violence against civilians continues to define the conflict. AP reported that Sudan’s war has killed at least 59,000 people, according to figures cited in its coverage, though rights groups believe the true toll may be higher. More than 11,000 people are missing, the Red Cross says. The war’s economic damage is also severe, pushing prices higher and making food even harder to obtain.

Humanitarian workers say the hunger crisis is deepening. AP reported that the number of severely malnourished children expected to need treatment is rising sharply, and that a 16-bed stabilization center in Port Sudan has seen admissions double since the war began. The UN says only about 63% of Sudan’s health facilities remain fully or partially functional amid disease outbreaks, including cholera. In practical terms, that means a sick child, a wounded civilian or a pregnant woman may have no functioning clinic to turn to. The war is not just killing people through violence. It is also stripping away the basic systems that keep people alive.

Berlin’s pledge and the problem of follow-through

The Berlin conference was designed to do two things at once: raise money for Sudan’s humanitarian response and keep diplomatic pressure on the conflict’s backers. Reuters Connect said the meeting brought together the German government, the European Union and the African Union with the aim of mobilizing aid and strengthening efforts toward a ceasefire. The Guardian reported that donors pledged more than £1 billion, surpassing the conference target, but even that influx may only partly offset a far larger gap. UN and aid agencies have said Sudan’s response remains dramatically underfunded relative to needs.

That funding gap matters because Sudan’s humanitarian crisis is not static. Every month of delay worsens the conditions that will shape the next six months of the war. Food stocks become harder to move, health staff become harder to retain and local markets become more fragile. The more the war continues, the more civilians are forced to rely on aid deliveries that are themselves increasingly vulnerable to insecurity, bureaucratic obstruction and rising costs. Donor conferences can help, but they cannot substitute for access.

There is also a political risk in treating the Berlin meeting as a finish line. Sudan has already been the subject of repeated pledges, warnings and conferences. The UN and aid groups have long argued that the crisis is not only about money but about sustained diplomatic pressure on the warring sides and their external sponsors. Without that, aid alone can slow the collapse but not reverse it. The country’s partitioned reality, the scale of displacement and the depth of hunger all suggest a conflict measured in years, not weeks.

What the world does next will matter more than the conference itself

The strongest warning from Sudan may be that the war is now testing whether the international system can still respond to a catastrophe once it has become familiar. The conflict has produced famine conditions in parts of the country, mass displacement across borders and repeated attacks on civilians and aid operations. It has also unfolded alongside other global crises, including the war in Ukraine and renewed instability in the Middle East, which have pulled attention and resources in different directions.

Yet the case for keeping Sudan at the center of world attention is straightforward. The war is generating one of the largest humanitarian emergencies on the planet, it is destabilizing a strategically important region and it is helping establish a dangerous precedent: that a conflict can grind on with enormous civilian losses while the outside world grows accustomed to the scale of the disaster. That is the danger aid officials are trying to break. Not just famine, not just displacement, but the slide from alarm into fatigue.

For families in Port Sudan, Darfur, Khartoum and beyond, the war’s fourth year offers little room for symbolism. It means another season of uncertainty, another scramble for food and medicine, another wait for a ceasefire that still seems distant. The Berlin pledges may keep some lifesaving operations alive. But unless they are matched by pressure for access, civilian protection and an end to the fighting, Sudan will remain what UN officials increasingly call it: an abandoned crisis.
