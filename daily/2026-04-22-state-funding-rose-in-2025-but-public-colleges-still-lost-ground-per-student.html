State Funding Rose in 2025 — But Public Colleges Still Lost Ground Per Student

A national finance report shows states put a record amount into higher education in fiscal 2025, yet enrollment rose even faster. That left public colleges with less support per student and renewed pressure on tuition, staffing and access.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 1:39 p.m.

State lawmakers spent more on public higher education in fiscal 2025 than ever before. But the headline number hides the more important one: when enrollment is taken into account, public colleges and universities received less support per student for the first time in more than a decade. That reversal, documented in the 2025 State Higher Education Finance report released by the State Higher Education Executive Officers Association this month, is a warning sign for campuses already juggling affordability concerns, staffing strain and uncertainty around federal aid and oversight. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

The report says state and local appropriations for public higher education reached $130.7 billion in fiscal 2025 after inflation adjustment, a 2.6 percent increase from the year before. Yet full-time equivalent enrollment rose 3.6 percent, to 10.8 million students, which meant per-student public funding fell 1.0 percent, to $12,082. SHEEO says that was the first annual decline in per-student state support since 2012. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

For education leaders, the shift is more than a spreadsheet curiosity. It marks the point where the post-pandemic rebound in college attendance has begun to outrun the fiscal recovery in state support. That mismatch matters because public colleges rely heavily on state appropriations to keep tuition manageable, fund student services and sustain the labor force that teaches, advises and supports students. When the per-student slice shrinks, colleges can try to make up the difference with tuition increases, cuts to programs, larger classes, less advising or more aggressive recruitment of full-pay students. Every one of those choices has consequences for families. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

The change also complicates a common narrative in higher education: that states have fully repaired the damage of the Great Recession and years of austerity. In one sense, they have. The system is still sitting on the highest total public funding in the SHEF dataset, which dates back to 1980, and per-student support remains above the 2008 level after inflation adjustment. But in another sense, the recovery remains incomplete. SHEEO says 24 states still fund higher education below pre-Great Recession levels, which means the national average can obscure sharp differences from one state to another. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

A broad source map points to the same tension

The new SHEF numbers are the strongest anchor for this story because they synthesize state and local public finance data across the country and compare it with enrollment trends. SHEEO’s own release says fiscal 2026 state support rose another 1.0 percent to $133.1 billion, but that slower pace suggests the post-pandemic rebound in public funding is cooling. The organization’s Grapevine update also says national state support per capita rose only 0.5 percent from 2025 to 2026, a sign that growth is no longer surging the way it did just after the pandemic. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/new-grapevine-data-shows-softening-state-support-for-higher-education-in-fy-2026/?utm_source=openai))

That does not mean campuses are headed into crisis automatically. It does mean the funding environment is becoming less forgiving at the same time student demand remains elevated. SHEEO’s FY25 release says public FTE enrollment increased 3.6 percent over 2024, while still remaining 1.3 percent below the pre-pandemic 2019 level and 7.2 percent below the 2011 peak. In other words, enrollment has rebounded, but not in a straight line, and the rebound itself is helping push per-student support down. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Higher education finance is not the only place where demand is outstripping supply. The U.S. Department of Education says chronic absenteeism remains a serious post-pandemic problem in K-12 schools, with the national rate reaching about 31 percent in 2021-22 and falling only to 28 percent in 2022-23. That is an improvement, but still a long way from normal. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Those K-12 figures matter to the college story because the pipeline into higher education begins long before a student applies to a university. Attendance, engagement, family income, transportation, mental health and school climate all shape whether students finish high school with the preparation and confidence to enroll in college. Brookings has argued that attendance problems and disengagement reinforce one another, while The 74 has pressed for more transparent attendance data so parents and communities can respond faster. The evidence supports the broader point even if the policy prescriptions differ. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Inside higher education, meanwhile, the financial pressures extend beyond appropriations. Reporting this year has shown that colleges continue to cut programs and jobs when enrollment or budgets weaken, and that some institutions have shifted more of the burden onto students and families through borrowing. Those examples do not prove that every campus is in distress, but they do show that the same macro trend can feel very different depending on institution type, state policy and student income level. ([insidehighered.com](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/business/cost-cutting/2026/03/03/february-brought-flurry-program-and-personnel-cuts?utm_source=openai))

Federal policy adds another layer. The Education Department says the 2026-27 FAFSA launched on time last fall and that submissions for that cycle are running well ahead of the same point last year. That is good news for students and colleges, because predictable aid access matters. At the same time, the Government Accountability Office warned in March 2026 that staffing reductions and oversight gaps have weakened the department’s ability to monitor student-loan servicers, and Urban Institute analysts have warned that new federal loan limits will change borrowing patterns for graduate and parent borrowers. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-secretary-of-education-confirms-time-launch-of-2026-27-fafsa-form?utm_source=openai))

Why the per-student decline matters more than the top-line increase

The public conversation around college finance often stops at the top-line state appropriation. That is understandable: a higher number is easy to market. But the per-student figure is the more revealing one because it shows whether states are actually keeping pace with the population colleges serve. If public college enrollment grows faster than appropriations, each student is effectively supported by a smaller share of state resources. SHEEO’s FY25 report says that is exactly what happened in 2025. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

That can show up in several ways. Colleges may postpone hiring, leave vacancies open longer or rely more heavily on contingent faculty. They may reduce course offerings, making it harder for students to graduate on time. They may raise mandatory fees or housing charges. They may increase the number of students assigned to each adviser, counselor or financial-aid officer. They may cut services that low-income, first-generation and working students depend on most: emergency grants, childcare, transportation help, food pantries, wraparound advising and mental health support. Those are operational choices, but they quickly become access questions. ([insidehighered.com](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/business/cost-cutting/2026/03/03/february-brought-flurry-program-and-personnel-cuts?utm_source=openai))

None of those responses is guaranteed. Some schools will use reserves or philanthropic support to blunt the impact. Some states are deliberately shifting money toward student aid rather than institutional operating support. SHEEO says state public financial aid per FTE reached an all-time high of $1,271 in fiscal 2025, up 5.1 percent from the prior year. That is real progress for affordability, especially if the aid is well targeted. But it does not cancel out the fact that operating support per student fell at the same time. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

That split is the key policy tension. Financial aid can help students pay the bill; operating support helps keep the bill from rising as quickly. States can do one or the other, or both, but the tradeoffs get harder when budgets tighten. SHEEO’s Grapevine update suggests support growth is slowing, which could make that balance even harder to sustain in fiscal 2026 and beyond. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/new-grapevine-data-shows-softening-state-support-for-higher-education-in-fy-2026/?utm_source=openai))

Enrollment rebound or demographic cliff?

The enrollment increase is central to understanding why per-student funding dipped. SHEEO says public FTE enrollment rose 3.6 percent in 2025, to 10.8 million. That is notable because higher education spent much of the past decade bracing for a long-feared demographic decline as the population of traditional-age college students leveled off in many parts of the country. The rebound has not erased those concerns, but it has delayed the cliff in some places and forced states to reckon with higher demand than their budgets anticipated. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Not all enrollment growth looks the same. In some states it is coming from community colleges, where students are often older, lower-income and more likely to need flexible schedules. In other places it is driven by public four-year institutions, especially those that have recovered from pandemic losses more slowly. SHEEO’s report does not settle the debate over whether growth is structural or temporary, but it does show that states cannot assume post-pandemic student numbers will settle neatly back into pre-2020 patterns. ([shef.sheeo.org](https://shef.sheeo.org/report-2/?utm_source=openai))

That uncertainty is echoed in national higher-ed reporting. Inside Higher Ed and Higher Ed Dive both reported on the first per-student decline since 2012, and GAO’s March 2026 oversight report underscores a broader public-finance reality: the education system depends on staffing and administrative capacity as much as on raw dollars. When institutions are asked to do more with less, the strain shows up in people, schedules and services, not just balance sheets. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

The labor question: who absorbs the squeeze?

Education budgets are labor budgets. The easiest way to absorb flat or slower-growing revenue is usually to limit hiring, freeze positions or ask existing employees to do more. That is why the per-student decline matters to teachers, faculty members, advisers, custodians, librarians and campus workers as much as to presidents and trustees. When public systems get tighter, labor tends to absorb the shock. ([insidehighered.com](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/business/cost-cutting/2026/03/03/february-brought-flurry-program-and-personnel-cuts?utm_source=openai))

At the K-12 level, the effects show up in teacher shortages, classroom staffing, substitute coverage and attendance support. AP recently reported on Anchorage School District’s proposal to cut more than 500 staff positions and increase class sizes to close a projected budget deficit. The district attributed the strain partly to years of flat state funding. That is not the same system as higher education, but it is the same logic: when the money lags behind the need, staffing is where the pressure lands first. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/5d0c179df7a778b1e259da52bb470d9a?utm_source=openai))

At the college level, the labor picture is similar but less visible to the public. When institutions lean on adjuncts and overload courses, students may have fewer office hours, less mentoring and more scheduling bottlenecks. When counseling and aid offices are understaffed, low-income students can wait longer for answers about grants, loans and registration. When maintenance and student-services jobs are cut, campus life becomes less accessible, especially for working students and those who commute. Those are not abstract harms; they are the mechanisms by which a funding shortfall becomes an access problem. ([insidehighered.com](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/business/cost-cutting/2026/03/03/february-brought-flurry-program-and-personnel-cuts?utm_source=openai))

Recent federal warnings add to that pressure. GAO said in March 2026 that the Education Department’s Office of Federal Student Aid had reduced staff and stopped assessing loan servicers on accuracy and call quality because of capacity limits, while overseeing a portfolio of more than $1.6 trillion in student loans. That is a reminder that the labor required to run a modern education system includes not only classroom staff but also administrators who can process aid, monitor compliance and keep systems functioning. ([gao.gov](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-108534?utm_source=openai))

Affordability is improving in some places and worsening in others

One reason the SHEF report is so important is that it captures only one side of affordability. Public funding is part of the price equation, but so are tuition discounting, institutional pricing, state aid and federal loan policy. The combined effect can move in different directions at once. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

For some students, aid is getting better. SHEEO says state public financial aid per FTE hit a record in fiscal 2025, and the Education Department says the 2026-27 FAFSA is launching and processing more smoothly than in recent years. Those are meaningful developments for families trying to pay for college, especially after the turmoil of the earlier FAFSA rollout. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

For others, the picture is less encouraging. Urban Institute researchers say new federal loan limits are likely to affect a modest share of all students but a much larger share of those who rely on Parent PLUS loans, and GAO says oversight weaknesses remain in the loan-servicing system. Inside Higher Ed has also reported on colleges steering some Pell Grant recipients toward Parent PLUS borrowing and on institutions cutting jobs or programs as budgets tighten. Together, those sources suggest that affordability gains in one part of the system can coexist with more expensive borrowing in another. ([urban.org](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/how-new-federal-student-loan-limits-could-affect-borrowers?utm_source=openai))

The burden is not evenly distributed. Low-income students, students of color, first-generation students and students with caregiving responsibilities are more likely to be harmed when colleges reduce advising, shrink course schedules or raise mandatory fees. They are also more likely to depend on public transportation, food assistance and flexible scheduling. That means a seemingly modest per-student funding decline can have outsized effects on those least able to absorb disruption. Urban’s work on non-tuition expenses reinforces that point. ([urban.org](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/how-new-federal-student-loan-limits-could-affect-borrowers?utm_source=openai))

What complicates the main thesis

The simplest version of this story is: states spent a record amount, but it still was not enough. That is true, but incomplete. There are at least four reasons to be cautious about reading the report as evidence of a sweeping funding crisis. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

First, the top-line increase in public support was real. After inflation, states and local governments put more money into public higher education in 2025 than in 2024. That means the system is not in a uniform retreat. In some states, campuses did get more support, and in some sectors that may have outweighed enrollment growth. SHEEO’s figures show the national pattern, but they do not erase the state-by-state variation underneath it. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Second, per-student funding remains above 2008 levels after inflation adjustment. That does not make current pressures imaginary. It does mean the sector is still better off than during the post-Great-Recession trough, even if momentum has turned less favorable. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Third, higher aid can offset some of the strain. If states choose to shift dollars toward grants and need-based aid, students may still be better protected even if institutional support softens. The danger is not simply less public spending, but public spending that is misaligned with the cost pressures colleges actually face. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Fourth, higher education is not the only public system under stress. K-12 schools are still dealing with chronic absenteeism, staffing shortages, unresolved pandemic learning gaps and the end of federal relief money. A state that redirects money from schools to colleges, or from colleges to K-12, may simply be moving strain around rather than solving it. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Those complications matter because they change what policy response makes sense. If the problem were simply too little money, the answer would be more money. But the broader pattern suggests something more specific: states are struggling to keep pace with student demand while dividing scarce dollars across a public education system that now includes everything from early literacy to dual enrollment to graduate school debt. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

Why this is news now

The release of the 2025 SHEF report is newsworthy because it captures a turning point. After a period of unusually strong state support, public higher education has entered a phase where demand is once again outrunning funding growth. The decline in per-student support is not catastrophic, but it is meaningful because it ended a long stretch of recovery and did so at the exact moment campuses were hoping enrollment gains would translate into more stability. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

This comes at a time when other education systems are also being asked to do more with less. K-12 districts are still trying to recover attendance and learning loss. Colleges are handling federal policy shifts on loans, grants and oversight. States are confronting budget volatility. Families are navigating tuition, aid, transportation and housing costs. The education system is not one thing; it is a chain, and pressure at one link travels quickly to the others. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

The deeper question is whether states want to treat higher education as a discretionary expense that can rise or fall with the budget cycle, or as infrastructure that must be funded in step with enrollment and workforce needs. The SHEF report does not answer that question for them. It does, however, show the cost of postponing it. A record appropriation is easy to celebrate. Keeping up per student is harder, and it is the harder measure that determines whether public colleges can remain accessible, adequately staffed and financially sustainable for the students who depend on them. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

For now, the most accurate headline is not that states have abandoned public colleges. It is that they have not yet figured out how to fund them at the pace students are arriving. And until they do, the pressure will keep landing where public systems can least afford it: on classrooms, advising desks, labs, libraries, dorms, buses and the families trying to make college work. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/shef_fy25_release/?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Three indicators will show whether this year’s per-student decline is a one-year blip or the beginning of a longer squeeze. First, whether enrollment continues to rise in 2026, especially at public two-year colleges. Second, whether state appropriations in fiscal 2026 keep pace with inflation and student growth or flatten under budget pressure. Third, whether campuses respond by raising prices, cutting jobs or expanding aid in ways that preserve access without hollowing out support. The answer will help determine whether public higher education is entering a period of managed adjustment or renewed austerity. ([sheeo.org](https://sheeo.org/new-grapevine-data-shows-softening-state-support-for-higher-education-in-fy-2026/?utm_source=openai))
