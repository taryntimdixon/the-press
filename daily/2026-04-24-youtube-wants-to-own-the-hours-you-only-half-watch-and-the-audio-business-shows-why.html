YouTube Wants to Own the Hours You Only Half Watch, and the Audio Business Shows Why

SiriusXM’s new role selling YouTube’s U.S. audio inventory is not just an ad-market footnote. It clarifies a larger pop-culture shift: the most valuable media in 2026 is increasingly built to travel between watching and listening, between TV and earbuds, and between active fandom and background habit.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 10:09 a.m.

On April 22, 2026, SiriusXM announced that SiriusXM Media had become the exclusive U.S. advertising representative for YouTube’s audio inventory. The companies framed the deal in business language: advertisers would be able to buy YouTube audio through SiriusXM’s sales operation, with access to inventory spanning podcasts, music and talk content. But the announcement deserves attention beyond the ad trades, because it reveals something larger about where pop-culture consumption now lives. The decisive platform contest is no longer only about what people sit down to watch. It is also about what they keep on while they drive, cook, work, clean, scroll, or drift between tasks.

That distinction matters because pop culture spent years talking as though the central power shift was purely visual. The debate was about streaming television, short-form video, creator video, video podcasts, and whether YouTube had finally become a television company. Those debates were real, and they are not over. But they missed a parallel change underneath them: major platforms have been racing to control the hours when audiences are not fully looking at a screen but are still inside a platform’s ecosystem. The SiriusXM-YouTube arrangement makes that strategy unusually legible. It says, in effect, that YouTube’s listening behavior has become valuable enough to package and sell as a distinct media business. ([investor.siriusxm.com](https://investor.siriusxm.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/2261/siriusxm-media-becomes-exclusive-audio-advertising?utm_source=openai))

The cultural significance comes from what kind of behavior is being formalized. A person may open YouTube for a filmed podcast, minimize the app, keep the audio running through dinner prep, let the next episode autoplay, send clips to friends later, and encounter sponsor messages all along the way. That sequence is not neatly “video” or neatly “audio.” It is platform time. It is companion media: content that can be watched when attention is available, heard when it is not, and monetized in both conditions. For pop culture, that hybrid form increasingly matters more than old category lines.

The timing of the deal reinforces the point. It arrived after a year in which YouTube kept emphasizing its scale on TV screens, its podcast growth, and its status as a creator-centered entertainment system rather than a mere upload site. In February 2026, Nielsen said YouTube led all media distributors in U.S. television viewing share for January, at 12.5%, ahead of Disney and Netflix. In March, YouTube used NewFronts to pitch creator partnerships more aggressively to brands. And YouTube’s own podcast materials have continued to treat podcasts as a native product category connected to YouTube Music and optimized for both watching and background listening. The SiriusXM announcement extends that message into the ad market: if YouTube already competes for the living room, it now wants to compete more directly for the audio budget too. ([nielsen.com](https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2026/disney-scores-best-performance-in-a-year-in-nielsens-january-2026-media-distributor-gauge/?utm_source=openai))

Audio is no longer the leftover format

The easiest way to underrate this shift is to think of audio as what remains when viewers stop paying attention. That idea fit an older hierarchy in which prestige attached to the image and audio looked secondary, cheaper or residual. Current audience data make that framing harder to sustain. Edison Research’s Infinite Dial 2026 found that 81% of Americans age 12 and older listened to online audio in the last month, 58% had consumed a podcast in the last month, and 45% had consumed a podcast in the last week, all record highs. Edison also reported that 57% of Americans 12 and older had both listened to and watched a podcast. The important point is not that audio is beating video. It is that audiences increasingly move across both modes, often within the same title, the same creator relationship, and the same day. ([edisonresearch.com](https://www.edisonresearch.com/the-infinite-dial-2026/?utm_source=openai))

That fluidity has practical consequences. Once audiences stop treating listening and watching as separate cultural universes, platforms can build products and sales systems around the transition between them. YouTube’s help documentation for creators makes this explicit. On YouTube, a podcast is a playlist of videos; those videos can be surfaced with podcast badges, distributed into YouTube Music, discovered through podcast-specific pages, and in many cases made available for audio-only playback. In other words, YouTube is not hiding the hybridity. It has productized it. ([support.google.com](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/12751636?hl=en&utm_source=openai))

That matters because hybrid formats solve one of the central problems in contemporary media economics: how to remain present when users are busy. Video is lucrative, but it is situational. It demands a certain posture. Audio expands the day. It reaches commuters, people at the gym, office workers toggling among windows, shoppers with earbuds in, and households using smart speakers or phones as ambient media devices. SiriusXM’s announcement explicitly described YouTube audio ads as reaching users in podcasts, talk shows, music, and audio-first behaviors. SiriusXM Media separately said the inventory includes both listening-first content and video content consumed in audio-forward ways, such as background listening or smart-speaker playback. That is a telling formulation. The sale is not limited to conventional audio files. It is about identifying audio behavior inside a broader platform and converting that behavior into inventory. ([investor.siriusxm.com](https://investor.siriusxm.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/2261/siriusxm-media-becomes-exclusive-audio-advertising?utm_source=openai))

The result is that audio is better understood not as a fallback but as a durability layer. A prestige show may generate headlines, but a recurring talk format, music commentary series, comedy podcast, sports recap or fandom roundup may occupy more total hours of a user’s week. Those formats produce routine. They structure errands, showers, commutes and chores. They also create a more intimate kind of loyalty, because the relationship is built on recurring voice contact rather than occasional visual event status. In pop culture, that can matter as much as spectacle.

The deal’s real subject is the routine economy

SiriusXM is a revealing intermediary because it sits between older and newer listening systems. It is a satellite-radio company, a streaming owner through Pandora, an ad-tech operator through AdsWizz, and a seller of podcast inventory. That makes it a natural broker for a market in which “radio,” “podcast,” “streaming audio,” and “audio behavior on a video platform” increasingly blur together on the buy side. Reuters’ account of the April 22 announcement described the deal as a move to monetize YouTube’s growing podcast and music-listening base beyond traditional video ads. Digiday went further, describing the package as YouTube’s “audio-first” inventory and quoting SiriusXM’s top ad-revenue executive saying the companies could now aggregate that listening behavior and monetize it more directly. ([investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/siriusxm-to-sell-youtube-audio-ads-exclusively-in-us-4629073?utm_source=openai))

That language may sound technical, but it points to a larger cultural economy: the market for routine time. For decades, radio held a privileged position in that market because it lived in the car, the store, the workshop, and the kitchen. Podcasts then captured part of that same territory by offering host intimacy and on-demand control. Music streaming deepened the habit by making every mood searchable. What YouTube is now asserting is that it too belongs in that everyday listening map, and not just incidentally. The platform wants advertisers to think of it as a regular stop in audio planning, not only as a video giant that happens to contain podcasts and songs.

That has become easier because YouTube increasingly spans several media postures at once. It is a TV app in the living room. It is a search engine for clips and music. It is a podcast destination. It is a background tab. It is a smart-speaker source. It is a music service through YouTube Music. And because the account, recommendation system and ad stack connect these settings, the company can turn scattered daily encounters into one continuous ecosystem. The value of that ecosystem rises when the platform reaches not only prime-time attention but also the unglamorous hours around it.

Put differently: the commute is now a pop-culture battleground. So is the sink, the treadmill, the checkout line and the laundry basket. Media companies used to distinguish sharply between the premium screen and the secondary format that trailed behind it. In 2026, the strategically valuable property is often the one that can survive the transition from screen to speaker without losing the user.

YouTube’s podcast push helps explain the sales move

The SiriusXM deal did not appear out of nowhere. Over the last two years, YouTube has made podcasts a more explicit part of its business. In February 2025, the company said it had surpassed 1 billion monthly podcast users. At the time, it also said viewers had watched more than 400 million hours of podcasts monthly on living-room devices in the prior year. Then, in January 2026, YouTube said podcast viewing on TVs had climbed further, with more than 700 million hours watched on living-room devices in October 2025, and noted that it had launched a U.S. podcast chart to improve discovery. These are company-provided numbers and should be read with that in mind, but they still indicate clear product investment and a desire to reframe podcasting as a mainstream, cross-screen category within YouTube. ([blog.youtube](https://blog.youtube/news-and-events/1-billion-monthly-podcast-users/?utm_source=openai))

The YouTube help materials are just as revealing as the press releases. They show that podcasts on YouTube are not treated as an awkward import from the RSS era. They are organized in Studio, connected to analytics and revenue tools, and made eligible for discovery systems designed to surface them both as watchable and as listenable media. The mechanics are not glamorous, but they matter. Platforms become cultural institutions partly by changing what creators can expect from the infrastructure: what gets discovered, what gets measured, what gets backgrounded, what gets bundled into music, and what qualifies for monetization. ([support.google.com](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/12751636?hl=en&utm_source=openai))

This is one reason old arguments about whether podcasts are “really” audio or “really” video have become less useful than they once seemed. For a platform, the ambiguity is an advantage. A single show can be watched on a TV, listened to on a phone, clipped for social feeds, embedded in recommendation loops, and sold to advertisers as part of a multi-context plan. The same intellectual property can behave like television, radio, social media and merchandise bait at different moments. That flexibility makes it more durable than a format tied to a single posture of attention.

It also helps explain why YouTube has become so central to an area once thought to belong more naturally to dedicated podcast apps. Edison has repeatedly shown YouTube at the top of U.S. podcast consumption by service, and the 2026 findings underscore how normal it now is for Americans to both watch and listen to podcasts. The important development is not simply that YouTube hosts podcasts. It is that it has made them native to its recommendation and monetization logic. ([edisonresearch.com](https://www.edisonresearch.com/the-infinite-dial-2026/?utm_source=openai))

The ad market is catching up to audience behavior

The SiriusXM arrangement suggests that the ad market has decided this hybrid behavior is worth treating as a specific product. The press release says advertisers will gain access to guaranteed audio impressions at scale in the United States. That phrase matters. “Guaranteed” implies a more standardized, budgetable offer than the looser opportunism that often accompanies emergent media categories. It signals an attempt to make YouTube listening legible to major buyers in the way radio, streaming audio and podcast networks already are. ([investor.siriusxm.com](https://investor.siriusxm.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/2261/siriusxm-media-becomes-exclusive-audio-advertising?utm_source=openai))

There is broader economic context for why this would happen now. IAB and PwC have been projecting continued growth in podcast advertising, and recent 2025 industry-revenue reporting described digital audio as an $8.4 billion U.S. category with podcast revenue nearing $3 billion. Even allowing for the usual caution around category definitions and trade extrapolation, the direction is clear: digital audio is large enough that platforms want a bigger piece of it, and buyers are used to treating audio as a serious line item rather than a niche experiment. YouTube, whose ad identity has historically been dominated by video, now has reason to carve out and professionalize its audio behavior instead of leaving it implicit inside the larger platform. ([iab.com](https://www.iab.com/insights/us-podcast-advertising-revenue-study-2024/?utm_source=openai))

The move also fits a wider reorganization of ad buying around creators. At NewFronts in March, YouTube emphasized creator partnerships and described its creators as trusted guides for audiences and brands. The company’s sales language increasingly places YouTube in direct competition not only with TikTok or Instagram but with linear television, premium streamers and the brand-planning systems long associated with them. Audio strengthens that case because it lets YouTube argue that it can serve as both a screen-based and an ears-based environment. It is no longer selling only the impression that appears before or during a video. It is selling access to recurring, audio-forward attention that may occur when the image is secondary. ([blog.youtube](https://blog.youtube/news-and-events/youtube-creator-partnerships-newfronts-2026/?utm_source=openai))

That shift is subtle but important. In a mature ad market, what counts as a category affects what gets funded. Once a behavior becomes measurable, guaranteed and easy to brief into a media plan, it gains institutional weight. That does not automatically make it culturally important, but it helps stabilize the economics around it. The SiriusXM deal is therefore not merely an inventory-management update. It is a step in defining YouTube listening as something buyers should seek intentionally.

Spotify’s strategy shows the same pressure from another angle

YouTube is not alone in trying to turn podcasts and audio-adjacent creator formats into broader franchises. Spotify has spent the last year pushing hard on video podcasts, creator monetization and podcast prestige. In January 2026, Spotify expanded its Partner Program and said it was making it easier for more video podcasters to earn, including creators hosted on outside platforms. The company described the program as a way to help creators build sustainable businesses through a mix of Premium video revenue and ad revenue. In a separate January post, Spotify said its investments across its podcast ecosystem had contributed more than $10 billion to the podcast industry over five years. In March, it launched a live-event format in Germany called Legends Club for hit podcasts that record video episodes in front of fans. ([newsroom.spotify.com](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2026-01-07/spotify-partner-program-updates/?utm_source=openai))

Each of those claims comes from Spotify and should be read as self-interested corporate messaging. But together they still illuminate the market logic. Podcasts are being treated less as humble audio files and more as expandable entertainment properties that can move across apps, video, live events, sponsorships and subscription tiers. The modern winner in audio is not simply the company with the best listening interface. It is the company that can turn voice-driven formats into multi-surface businesses.

That is where YouTube’s structure gives it unusual leverage. It begins with discovery at scale, already dominates a great deal of casual viewing, and can route users from clips to full episodes to TV apps to background listening within one giant recommendation environment. Spotify, by contrast, has had to add video more deliberately and explain to users why a historically audio-first service should also become a screen destination. YouTube’s advantage is that it starts from abundance and ambiguity. Nearly any entertainment object can plausibly live there.

Still, Spotify’s moves are useful because they confirm that the pressure is industry-wide. Platforms do not think of podcasts as a side pocket anymore. They think of them as a hinge format: cheaper than prestige TV, stickier than many social clips, adaptable to live touring, and well suited to parasocial loyalty. That is exactly why the YouTube-SiriusXM deal matters. It formalizes one lucrative slice of that hinge.

Creators gain opportunity, but the market will not distribute it evenly

There is an upbeat interpretation of all this. In that version, audio-rich platforms create more room for independent creators to build businesses without the capital demands of scripted television or major-label pop. A host with a recurring show can publish regularly, cultivate trust, layer in sponsorships, expand into video if useful, and potentially reach audiences on TVs, phones and speakers at once. The lower production threshold of talk, commentary and interview formats does open doors that older entertainment systems kept closed.

But platform maturation rarely spreads benefits evenly. When inventory becomes more standardized and saleable, the creators who benefit most are often those whose work is already legible to large buyers: consistent publishing schedules, stable audience measurements, brand-safe formats, polished production and enough scale to fit cleanly into managed campaigns. Big platforms and sales intermediaries can absolutely expand creator earnings in aggregate while still increasing the gap between the top tier and everyone else.

That is partly a measurement issue. The ad market tends to favor what it can count with confidence. As podcasts and simulcast shows move deeper into cross-platform ad planning, attribution systems become more consequential. Trade coverage in the past year has highlighted efforts to better model YouTube podcast performance alongside audio-first campaigns. The principle is simple: if agencies believe they can compare or forecast those environments reliably, budgets move more easily. But measurement also nudges creative behavior. It can reward standard ad lengths, predictable audience segments, tidy categories and professionally managed release patterns. Weirdness is culturally valuable; standardization is commercially valuable. Those incentives do not always align. ([digiday.com](https://digiday.com/marketing/youtube-is-turning-audio-into-an-ad-product-siriusxm-is-selling-it/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a labor question hidden beneath the language of frictionless creator entrepreneurship. Hybrid shows do not magically make themselves. Even ostensibly simple formats involve booking, prep, editing, clipping, moderation, rights management, platform optimization, sponsor delivery, comment management and constant audience maintenance. When a show is expected to function simultaneously as a podcast, a video series, a live-event springboard and a social clip source, the workload expands. The creators most likely to thrive are often those who can hire staff, sign with networks, or otherwise industrialize what once looked informal.

So yes, the rise of hybrid audio-video culture opens doors. It also industrializes them. The microphone remains a symbol of accessibility; the business around it increasingly looks like a system.

Music, talk and fandom are collapsing into one pipeline

One of the more important details in the SiriusXM announcement is that the deal covers podcasts, music and talk rather than isolating one of those genres from the others. That reflects how audiences actually use YouTube. They do not visit the platform only to hear songs or only to watch interviews. They move across tracks, reactions, commentary, gossip, live sets, rankings, recaps, fan edits, explainers and conversation formats in one continuous environment. What older media structures treated as separate verticals now bleed into one another as a single recommendation stream. ([investor.siriusxm.com](https://investor.siriusxm.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/2261/siriusxm-media-becomes-exclusive-audio-advertising?utm_source=openai))

That convergence matters for pop culture because taste now often forms in companion formats rather than in the primary artwork alone. A song gets context through a reaction channel, a festival vlog, a chart debate, a beauty tutorial tied to an event, an interview segment, a fan-history explainer, or a weekly culture podcast. The “soundtrack” is no longer just the music itself. It includes the voice-led ecosystem that interprets, ranks, jokes about and recirculates it. Platforms with strong audio and video crossover are well positioned to capture that surrounding chatter, which can be as sticky as the original release.

In that sense, the deal says something about where cultural authority is moving. Legacy gatekeepers still matter, especially for financing and distribution at scale. But everyday taste formation increasingly happens through repeated contact with hosts, creators and recommendation loops whose authority comes from familiarity rather than institutional prestige. Audio is crucial to that familiarity because it enters domestic and mobile routines so easily. It can sound like company. That affective texture is commercially powerful.

It also changes what counts as “entertainment infrastructure.” Once companion talk, personality-driven commentary and fandom maintenance become central to how people experience music and celebrity, the relevant business is no longer just labels, studios and streamers. It is the broader system that delivers ongoing voice contact. That is why an audio-ad sales deal belongs on the pop-culture desk. It is really about how the supporting layers of culture become primary.

The skeptical view: maybe this is just ad plumbing

A fair objection is that all of this risks reading too much culture into a sales agreement. Perhaps the deal is simply what it appears to be: ad-market plumbing. Media companies slice behavior into finer categories all the time, not because the culture has transformed, but because finance, targeting and inventory management demand new packaging. On that view, “YouTube audio” is just a monetization label applied to background listening that already existed, and the surrounding rhetoric about hybridity mainly flatters buyers.

There is truth in that caution. Corporate messaging routinely overstates coherence. Most users do not narrate their day in omnichannel language. They still distinguish, experientially, between opening Spotify for music, a podcast app for subscriptions, car radio for traffic, and YouTube for clips or shows. A sales category can become precise on paper while remaining fuzzy in actual life. Guaranteed impressions do not prove emotional centrality.

But the skeptical view also has limits. This is not a single press release standing alone. It fits a pattern: record U.S. podcast and online-audio usage in Edison’s 2026 data; YouTube’s established push into podcasts and TV screens; Nielsen’s evidence that YouTube is already a dominant TV distributor; Spotify’s emphasis on video podcasts and cross-format monetization; and the ad market’s continued interest in digital audio growth. When multiple institutions with different incentives describe overlapping changes, the safer conclusion is not that the shift is imaginary. It is that a once-messy behavior is being formalized quickly enough to change how money moves around it. ([edisonresearch.com](https://www.edisonresearch.com/the-infinite-dial-2026/?utm_source=openai))

That is often how cultural infrastructure changes: first as behavior, then as product, then as budget line, then as common sense. The SiriusXM deal sits in the product-and-budget phase. It does not create hybrid listening culture. It acknowledges that the behavior is big enough to professionalize.

The larger lesson for pop culture in 2026

The deeper story is that entertainment power is increasingly tied to portability across attention states. The strongest formats are not necessarily the most visually elaborate or the most critically sanctified. They are the ones that can move among the couch, the dashboard, the phone speaker, the earbuds and the background tab without losing economic value. A filmed conversation can be TV in one room, radio in another, social fodder later, and a merchandising engine all week. That portability is extraordinarily useful in an environment where audience attention is fragmented and expensive to reacquire.

It also changes the meaning of influence. Prestige television can still dominate awards and weekend discourse, but voice-led media often wins the battle for recurrence. The host you hear three times a week may shape your tastes, your sense of celebrity, your understanding of a scene, or your buying habits more durably than a lavish series you binge once and forget. Audio scales intimacy, and intimacy scales habit. Platforms know this. Advertisers know this. The deal announced on April 22 is one sign that the market now knows it in a more organized way.

None of this means older audio institutions disappear. Radio still matters, especially in the car and for local information. Podcast apps remain meaningful for many heavy users. Spotify and Apple are not fading from the picture. Rather, the market is becoming more overlapped and more contested. The largest platforms want to be present in every posture at once: watchable when you can watch, listenable when you cannot, measurable in both cases, and saleable as one continuous relationship.

That is why the most revealing phrase in the story may not be “podcast” or even “audio.” It may be “background.” For years, background media sounded secondary, like a diminished form of attention. In 2026 it looks more like a strategic frontier. Background does not mean worthless. It often means habitual, domestic, mobile and daily. Those are exactly the conditions under which platforms become hard to dislodge.

So the YouTube-SiriusXM deal is useful not because it settles the future of audio, but because it clarifies the current direction of travel. Pop culture is not just moving from television to creators or from audio to video or from old media to new. It is moving toward formats and platforms that can accompany ordinary life without demanding total attention. The winning companies are trying to own not only the spectacle you make time for, but the routine you barely notice making room for them.

For a long time, the shorthand was that everything was becoming video. A better description of this moment is that everything is becoming ambient media: sometimes watched, often heard, endlessly repackaged, and valuable precisely because it can follow people across the day. SiriusXM’s new sales role does not invent that reality. It gives it a rate card.
