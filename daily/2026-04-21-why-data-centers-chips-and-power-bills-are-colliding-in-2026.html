Why data centers, chips, and power bills are colliding in 2026

A U.S. technology boom is now running into export controls, utility limits, and a fast-moving fight over who pays for the infrastructure behind AI.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 21, 2026 • 10:43 a.m. • 2839 words

In the spring of 2026, the biggest story in technology is not a single product launch or a new chatbot feature. It is the collision of three slower, heavier systems: semiconductor supply chains, electric grids, and government controls over where advanced chips can be sold. The result is a new kind of infrastructure politics. Artificial intelligence may be framed as software, but its immediate bottlenecks are physical: transformers, interconnects, cooling systems, fabs, export licenses, and monthly power bills. The fight now playing out in Washington, in state utility commissions, and in regional grid markets is over who gets access to that hardware first, who pays to build it, and how much of the global market U.S. firms can still serve under tightening rules. Federal energy officials have also been explicit that large computing centers are now a material driver of U.S. power demand. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

That convergence is making 2026 feel different from the last two years of AI exuberance. In 2024 and 2025, the dominant question was whether demand for AI would justify vast new spending on chips and data centers. In 2026, the question has become whether the grid, the permitting system, and export policy can absorb that demand without pushing up household electricity rates, slowing chipmakers’ revenue growth, or forcing utilities and regulators to choose between reliability and speed. The Energy Information Administration’s January forecast said U.S. electricity use will grow for four consecutive years, with large computing centers, including data centers, driving much of the increase. Its April outlook still expects total U.S. electricity demand to rise 1.2% in 2026 and 3.3% in 2027. Commerce, meanwhile, has continued to adjust semiconductor export policy, rather than treating it as a fixed switch. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

The broad arc is easy to state and harder to manage: the AI buildout is driving a power buildout, and the power buildout is forcing a political reckoning. In some places, utilities are racing to meet demand with new generation and transmission. In others, regulators and activists are pushing back, worried that data-center load growth will crowd out other customers, raise standby costs, or undermine climate targets. At the same time, the U.S. government has continued to use export controls to shape who gets the most advanced chips, while loosening and tightening selected rules in ways that show the policy is still evolving. The result is a policy stack thick enough that companies are effectively operating under a moving target. ([pjm.com](https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/committees-groups/committees/mrc/2026/20260219/20260219-item-03---1-connect-and-manage---problem-statement.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The load-growth problem is no longer theoretical

The cleanest evidence that this story has become urgent comes from the energy side. The Energy Information Administration’s April 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook expects total U.S. electricity demand to rise this year and next, and its long-range outlook now treats data-center server load as a major driver of future growth. DOE has separately said data centers consumed about 4.4% of U.S. electricity in 2023 and could account for 6.7% to 12% by 2028, depending on the scenario. EIA’s decision to launch a pilot survey on data-center energy use in 2026 is itself a signal: the federal statistical apparatus is trying to catch up to a load category that used to be too small and dispersed to merit dedicated field studies. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))

That matters because electric grids are not just large pipes that can be widened on demand. They are capital-intensive systems with lead times measured in years, not quarters. A request for more load can require new substations, upgraded transmission, more generation, and a different reserve-margin calculation. The delay between a data center announcing a site and a utility actually delivering firm power is now one of the central frictions in the AI economy. EIA’s updated outlook and DOE’s report both show that demand growth is not a rumor; it is a planning assumption. The question is whether it stays a forecast or becomes a constraint. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))

Regional grid operators are already acting as if the answer may be “constraint.” PJM, which serves the Mid-Atlantic and parts of the Midwest, said in January that it is facing an unprecedented surge in large-load interconnection requests, particularly from data centers, and in March its board outlined plans to integrate large loads more reliably while preserving affordability and reliability. PJM’s own materials say data-center growth could add around 30 gigawatts by 2030, a scale that helps explain why reserve margins, queue reform, and large-load interconnection are now board-level issues rather than niche planning topics. ([pjm.com](https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/committees-groups/committees/mrc/2026/20260219/20260219-item-03---1-connect-and-manage---problem-statement.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That regional stress is not uniform. Some utilities and states see data centers as a growth engine and are courting them aggressively. Georgia is the most prominent example. In February, federal officials announced a $26.5 billion loan to Georgia and Alabama utilities, saying the money would help finance power-plant and transmission expansion tied in part to demand from computer data centers. Supporters say this is what industrial policy looks like when it is real: if the U.S. wants the value created by digital infrastructure, it has to build the physical backbone that powers it. Critics say the cost burden may still leak onto ordinary ratepayers, no matter the promises. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3aa78ffb7e67ae8e5ce3c42b7d4d3134?utm_source=openai))

That critique has been reinforced by a wave of local and state-level opposition. AP reported in April that Maine lawmakers approved a yearlong freeze on new energy-hungry data centers, citing blackouts, electricity bills and water use. The same month, AP also reported that states struggling with rising energy costs are revisiting ambitious climate targets, a sign that data-center growth is now colliding with broader electricity affordability politics. In Georgia, the issue has been playing out through taxes and development policy rather than only through utility planning. A 2024 AP report showed how difficult it is to unwind incentives once the industry is established. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/aa63ba087d5ad53ab0735893646e7357?utm_source=openai))

What complicates the narrative is that not every demand forecast is equally reliable. PJM’s latest long-term forecast remains very large, but the operator has also said it improved its vetting of data-center requests. That does not negate the growth story; it warns against linear extrapolation. Some announced loads never fully materialize, some are delayed, and some are revised after financing or permitting changes. The right takeaway is not that the boom is fake; it is that the system is now so overloaded with speculative and real projects that planners are being forced to distinguish between press-release load and bankable load. ([insidelines.pjm.com](https://insidelines.pjm.com/pjms-updated-20-year-forecast-continues-to-see-significant-long-term-load-growth/?utm_source=openai))

Export controls are reshaping the chip business while the market is still hot

If the grid story is about where power goes, the export-control story is about where chips go. The U.S. has spent years building a policy regime that tries to slow China’s access to the most advanced AI hardware and chipmaking equipment while preserving as much legitimate trade as possible. In January 2026, the Commerce Department said it would review licenses on a case-by-case basis for Nvidia H200, AMD MI325X, and similar chips destined for China, under conditions meant to ensure that the sales would not reduce capacity available to U.S. customers and that the Chinese buyer had adopted compliance procedures and U.S. testing. That was not a repeal of export controls; it was a recalibration. ([bis.gov](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-revises-license-review-policy-semiconductors-exported-china?utm_source=openai))

The shift reflects a hard fact: the original logic of broad restrictions collides with the commercial reality that U.S. chip companies still want access to enormous foreign markets. Nvidia’s filings show how punitive and unstable the China market has become. In its most recent annual reporting, the company said China remains highly competitive and that export controls have hindered its ability to address that market. Earlier SEC filings also said the company believed the China AI market would be significant and that export controls would greatly hinder its ability to compete there. That is not just a quarterly earnings footnote; it is evidence that export-control policy has become a structural variable in semiconductor revenue forecasting. ([sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1045810/000104581025000098/finalforfiling-2025xannual.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The China market story is more complicated than a simple loss column, though. There is evidence that some U.S. restrictions have accelerated Chinese domestic substitution and overseas design-outs, while at the same time preserving pockets of sales through approved or adjusted products. The January 2026 rule itself is a sign of that complexity: Washington is not trying to eliminate all chip sales to China, but to gate them with compliance checks, end-customer screening and security testing. That points to a familiar problem: policy may be written at the level of strategy, but it is implemented at the level of paperwork, personnel, and throughput. ([bis.gov](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-revises-license-review-policy-semiconductors-exported-china?utm_source=openai))

There is also a strategic contradiction. The U.S. wants to reduce China’s access to frontier AI hardware, yet a blanket denial regime can encourage foreign customers to redesign around U.S. components altogether. Nvidia explicitly warns that export controls can push customers toward non-U.S. alternatives and reduce its ability to serve the China AI market. That warning matters because market share lost to a substitution ecosystem is harder to recover than a few quarters of foregone revenue. Once software developers, system integrators, and procurement teams build around non-U.S. alternatives, the lock-in can travel in the opposite direction. ([sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1045810/000104581025000098/finalforfiling-2025xannual.pdf?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, supporters of tighter controls argue that the policy is doing exactly what it is intended to do: slow the diffusion of frontier capability into military and surveillance systems, force higher compliance standards, and preserve U.S. leverage over critical chokepoints. BIS’s August 2025 move to close the foreign-owned fab loophole in China and its January 2026 licensing policy both fit that logic. The thesis is not to sever all ties; it is to keep the most sensitive nodes under political control. ([media.bis.gov](https://media.bis.gov/sites/default/files/documents/Commerce%20Closes%20Export%20Controls%20Loophole%20for%20Foreign-Owned%20Semiconductor%20Fabs%20in%20China%20%281%29.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The problem is that the policy has to work through a world of substitutes, loopholes and third-country routing. When controls are too porous, they fail. When they are too rigid, they may push trade and innovation into less visible channels or into competitors’ ecosystems. The U.S. and Taiwan’s 2026 trade and investment framework also shows that the same government can simultaneously tighten controls on one part of the chip chain and incentivize relocation in another. ([uscc.gov](https://www.uscc.gov/trade-bulletins/china-bulletin-march-4-2026?utm_source=openai))

Taiwan is now a bargaining chip and a supply-chain insurance policy

Taiwan sits at the center of the global semiconductor system not because of one company but because of density: fabrication, packaging, equipment, logistics, talent and customer relationships are concentrated there in a way that no other place fully matches. That is why the January 2026 U.S.-Taiwan trade framework was so consequential. U.S. and Taiwan trade materials, as well as the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission, describe the arrangement as linking tariff treatment for semiconductors to new Taiwanese investment in the United States and to broader commitments in semiconductors, AI and energy. Whatever the precise diplomatic framing, the deal showed that chips are now being treated as a bilateral strategic asset, not merely a traded good. ([uscc.gov](https://www.uscc.gov/trade-bulletins/china-bulletin-march-4-2026?utm_source=openai))

That bargain has both upside and risk. The upside is resilience: more fabrication and supply-chain capacity on U.S. soil, fewer single-point failures, and more political alignment between Washington and Taipei. The risk is that the United States may come to believe that every strategic problem can be solved by domestic reshoring, when the ecosystem still depends on a global web of lithography tools, materials, specialty chemicals and assembly capacity. Commerce and trade officials can steer incentives, but they cannot instantly recreate the world’s most concentrated semiconductor cluster. The likely near-term reality is a hybrid: more U.S. capacity, more allied capacity, and a continued need to manage dependence rather than eliminate it. ([trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/taiwan-semiconductors-including-chip-design-ai?utm_source=openai))

That is why the debate over semiconductor policy cannot be separated from the debate over industrial electricity. Taiwan’s official trade materials describe ambitions in digital economy, silicon photonics and AI computing. The U.S. wants those capabilities to land in trusted jurisdictions and to support supply-chain security. But new fabs, new packaging lines and new AI infrastructure are all power-hungry. The semiconductor story therefore feeds the grid story, not only because data centers consume electricity but because advanced manufacturing does too. The politics of chips is becoming the politics of where to site extremely large, extremely expensive and extremely energy-intensive physical assets. ([trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/taiwan-digital-economy?utm_source=openai))

China’s counter-moves are changing the economics of scarcity

Export controls did not happen in a vacuum. China has responded with its own leverage, especially in critical minerals and rare earths. The USTR’s 2026 National Trade Estimate says China has used export controls on several critical minerals, including rare earths, and expanded those controls in 2025. Even where the details vary by month, the broader pattern is clear: both governments now see supply-chain choke points as strategic weapons. The technology contest is no longer only about the highest-end chips; it is also about the materials and manufacturing inputs that make chips, motors, magnets, batteries and cooling systems possible. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20NTE%20Report%20_%20Final.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That creates a second-order risk for the U.S. technology sector. If controls on advanced chips are paired with material shortages or retaliatory restrictions, the cost structure of the whole AI stack rises. Companies may need more inventory, more duplicate sourcing and more compliance staff. The result is not just geopolitics; it is margin pressure. Chip firms, cloud operators and data-center builders all face a more brittle operating environment when every major input is politically sensitive. And because demand for AI infrastructure is still strong, the burden can be hidden for a while in capital expenditure budgets before it shows up in prices, delays or lower returns. Nvidia’s SEC filings underscore that export controls now shape business planning, not just policy debate. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/Press/Releases/2026/2026%20NTE%20Report%20_%20Final.pdf?utm_source=openai))

There is a reason the phrase “digital infrastructure” sounds increasingly literal. The system now depends on power plants, substations, transformers, transmission corridors, cooling water, packaged semiconductors, networking gear and a licensing regime that can either accelerate or impede trade. A search for the latest AI model or cloud service may begin in software, but the decisive constraints are often elsewhere. That is the core real-world machinery behind digital life in 2026. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))

The policy fight is shifting from “should AI grow?” to “who pays and who waits?”

That phrase captures the most important shift in the debate. Until recently, AI policy questions were often framed around innovation, safety and competition. Those remain important, but the infrastructure bottlenecks have moved the argument into a more immediate arena: ratepayers, interconnection queues, curtailment rules, utility upgrades and permitting. The question is no longer whether data centers matter enough to regulate. They do. The question is how to allocate scarce grid capacity while preserving industrial growth and household affordability. PJM’s 2026 planning work makes that tradeoff explicit. ([pjm.com](https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/about-pjm/newsroom/2026-releases/20260116-pjm-board-outlines-plans-to-integrate-large-loads-reliably.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That is why proposals now range from tougher load studies to data-center moratoria. In March, AP reported that Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez were pushing a bill to pause new data centers until national safeguards are in place. In April, AP reported Maine lawmakers advanced a yearlong freeze. Both developments show that the backlash has moved from local zoning disputes into mainstream state and federal politics. On the other side, business groups and some energy analysts argue that the U.S. should not strangle a once-in-a-generation investment wave by over-regulating large loads or by making export controls so broad that domestic firms lose global scale. Both camps are reacting to the same facts; they just value the tradeoffs differently. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/65651bd28c3d911d18eeb46cd54f4c75?utm_source=openai))

A sober reading suggests that both sides have part of the truth. The critics are right that load growth can raise system costs, and that utilities and regulators may be tempted to socialize infrastructure upgrades. The advocates are right that if the U.S. wants to host the next generation of AI systems, it needs enough power, enough transmission and enough manufacturing capacity to do so. The policy challenge is to design tariffs, export rules, interconnection standards and cost-allocation rules that make the winners pay for the system they consume without freezing investment. That is hard, but the alternative is worse: opaque subsidy, political backlash and a boom that collides with the grid all at once. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1780f09228246dee569c3b63d70bd014?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Three developments will tell us whether this is a temporary scramble or the beginning of a durable new regime. First, watch the federal export-control pipeline. If licensing remains slow and selective, companies will continue to reprice China exposure and accelerate supply-chain diversification. If the rules loosen further, investors may regain some confidence, but the strategic rationale for controls will face sharper criticism. Second, watch regional grid operators and utilities. If “connect-and-manage” and similar tools spread, it will mean the system has accepted that some large loads must be rationed dynamically. Third, watch local politics. The more ratepayers, water districts and city councils rebel against data-center expansion, the more the industry will have to prove that it is not simply private demand wrapped in public infrastructure. ([bis.gov](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-revises-license-review-policy-semiconductors-exported-china?utm_source=openai))

The deeper lesson is that the AI boom has entered its infrastructure phase. That does not make it less important; it makes it more concrete. The question for technology policy in 2026 is not whether the digital world will keep expanding. It will. The question is whether the political system can build, price and govern the machinery underneath it fast enough to keep the boom useful, secure and broadly bearable. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))
