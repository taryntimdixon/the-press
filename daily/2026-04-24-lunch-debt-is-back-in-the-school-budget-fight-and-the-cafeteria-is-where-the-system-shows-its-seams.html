Lunch debt is back in the school budget fight, and the cafeteria is where the system shows its seams

As more states make breakfast and lunch free for every student while other districts weigh price hikes, debt drives, and stricter collection rules, the school cafeteria is becoming a revealing test of whether public education absorbs family strain — or bills it back through the lunch line.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

The American school cafeteria was supposed to be getting simpler by now. For a stretch of the pandemic, federal policy let schools serve meals to every student at no charge. That temporary arrangement did more than reduce paperwork. It changed the texture of the school day. The lunch line became less of a checkpoint. Cafeteria workers spent less time handling account problems. Families had one less small bill to manage. And schools, however imperfectly, operated a little more like institutions that assumed children should be able to participate first and settle eligibility questions elsewhere.

That era is over. What has replaced it is not a single national reset but a split-screen system. In a growing number of states, lawmakers have concluded that the pandemic period revealed a better way to run school meals and have put state money behind permanent free breakfast and lunch for all students. In other places, districts are back to the older machinery: household applications, paid meal categories, unpaid balances, donation drives, and arguments over whether a modest price increase is fiscally prudent or socially backward.

That divergence is why school meals belong on the education desk right now. The cafeteria is not just a nutrition site. It is where school finance, family hardship, administrative design, stigma, and state capacity meet in public every day. The issue is no longer only whether a child can get lunch. In many places the meal is still served. The deeper question is whether public schools are expected to run a universal daily service through a billing system that keeps reproducing debt, paperwork, and friction.

As of the 2025-26 school year, nine states have dedicated funding to provide free school meals to all students statewide: California, Colorado, Maine, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Mexico, New York, and Vermont. That count appears in the School Nutrition Association’s 2025-26 trends report and aligns with FRAC’s current tracking of Healthy School Meals for All policies. New York’s addition matters especially because of scale: the state says its program covers roughly 2.7 million students. In those states, breakfast and lunch are increasingly treated as part of the ordinary package of public schooling rather than as a partially subsidized retail transaction. Elsewhere, the older model remains in force. The country is drifting toward two distinct ways of administering the school day.

The contrast is visible in Montgomery County, Maryland, where local officials are debating the first school meal price increase in 12 years even as unpaid student meal debt has climbed to about $1.2 million. Montgomery County Public Schools is not an obscure or systemically failing district. It is one of the nation’s largest suburban systems, in a comparatively affluent county, with a long-developed administrative apparatus. Yet district officials have said rising food and labor costs, combined with unpaid balances, are forcing a fresh look at meal prices. The point is not that Montgomery County is uniquely troubled. It is that even a district with scale, reputation, and managerial capacity is finding the cafeteria ledger harder to stabilize.

The lunch line is where means-testing becomes visible

The school meal system is easy to misread because its rules are technical. USDA reimburses schools differently depending on whether meals are served free, at a reduced price, or at the paid rate. Those reimbursement levels are updated annually. For school year 2025-26, USDA’s published reimbursement notice again reflects a tiered system rather than a universal one, and the same year’s federal income eligibility guidelines continue to determine which families qualify for free or reduced-price meals under the standard application process. USDA also continues to issue Paid Lunch Equity guidance, which shapes how districts price paid lunches. In other words, outside statewide universal-meal systems or certain federal provisions, the basic architecture still assumes sorting, charging, and accounting. That structure matters because many families are above the eligibility line on paper while still struggling in practice with rent, transportation, child care, utilities, and food costs.

This near-eligible group is central to the lunch-debt story. Meal debt is often described as if it were mainly a product of parental neglect or district laxity. In reality it often reflects administrative mismatch. Some families have volatile income. Some never complete the forms. Some move schools. Some do not understand the application process or miss deadlines. Some technically can pay but not predictably, or not without falling behind somewhere else. The categories of free, reduced, and paid may look clean in a federal spreadsheet. In actual school communities they catch a much messier economic life.

The School Nutrition Association’s 2025-26 trends report offers one of the clearest current snapshots of that mess. Among programs that do not offer meals free to all students, 92.2 percent reported unpaid meal debt or charges. That is an extraordinarily high share. The same report found that while the median reported debt fell from $6,900 in fall 2024 to $6,000 in fall 2025, districts with the largest debts reported substantially higher amounts than in prior years. At the 90th percentile, reported debt rose from $65,000 to $86,660 in a year. The total reported debt in the survey also increased, from about $20.3 million to about $25.3 million. That pattern does not suggest a solved problem. It suggests a system in which debt remains common, and in some places is becoming more concentrated and more severe.

What makes that finding particularly revealing is that debt is much more common where programs still have to charge. The same School Nutrition Association report found that programs required to charge for meals reported debt at almost four times the rate of programs offering all meals free. That is not surprising, but it is politically important. It means debt is not merely a side effect of careless local execution. It is strongly associated with the structure of the system itself.

USDA’s own materials acknowledge how difficult this terrain is. Federal guidance requires school food authorities participating in the National School Lunch Program or School Breakfast Program to maintain a written local charge policy addressing situations in which children do not have money to pay at the point of service. USDA’s unpaid meal charges guidance frames the issue explicitly as a tension between feeding hungry children and preserving the financial viability of food-service operations. That language is revealing. It describes a nutrition program that is constantly being asked to behave both as a public good and as a self-protecting account system.

Universal-meal states are choosing a different administrative logic

The most consequential shift in school meals during the past few years has not come from Congress but from statehouses. California’s universal meals law requires public school districts, county offices of education, and charter schools to provide both breakfast and lunch free of charge to all students each school day. Massachusetts made universal free school meals permanent in its fiscal 2024 budget. New York’s FY26 enacted budget requires participating school districts, charter schools, and nonpublic schools in the national lunch and breakfast programs to provide free breakfast and lunch to all students; Governor Kathy Hochul’s office has framed that policy as reaching every one of the state’s 2.7 million students. These are not pilot projects. They are operating decisions about what public schooling includes.

The federal Community Eligibility Provision, or CEP, has also nudged the system in a more universal direction, though incompletely. CEP allows eligible high-poverty schools and districts to serve breakfast and lunch at no cost to all enrolled students without collecting household applications. Reimbursement is instead based on the share of students directly certified through participation in programs such as SNAP or TANF. A 2023 USDA final rule lowered the minimum identified student percentage threshold for electing CEP from 40 percent to 25 percent, expanding the number of schools that could participate. That change made a real difference. But CEP remains a targeted federal mechanism, not a comprehensive national universal-meals policy. It helps many schools. It does not erase the patchwork.

That patchwork is exactly what statewide universal-meal systems attempt to solve. In a district with mixed demographics, one campus may qualify for CEP while another nearby school still requires forms and family payments. A family can end up with children in the same district experiencing different lunch-line rules. A statewide policy does not eliminate all federal complexity, because districts still rely heavily on federal reimbursement streams and still navigate federal program requirements. But it does change the student-facing experience. The line between “public service” and “priced service” moves away from the point of service.

Supporters of universal meals argue that this has educational benefits beyond nutrition. The evidence base is still evolving, but it is now substantial enough to take seriously. A 2024 systematic review in JAMA Network Open found that universal free school meals were associated with increased meal participation, with no or slight improvements in attendance and some evidence of reduced obesity prevalence and suspension rates, though the certainty of evidence varied by outcome. A 2025 comparative effectiveness study in JAMA Pediatrics found that federal and state universal free school meal policies increased participation in the National School Lunch Program and School Breakfast Program, with results suggesting potential reductions in diet-related disparities and food insecurity. Those studies do not prove that universal meals transform every school outcome. They do support a narrower but significant claim: making meals universally available increases take-up and can improve some dimensions of student well-being and school functioning.

There is also evidence that universal access affects household budgets. An NBER working paper, later published in the Journal of Health Economics, found that access to free school meals through CEP reduced monthly household food purchases and was associated with lower food insecurity and modest improvements in dietary quality. That is an important reminder that school meals are not only an in-school intervention. They are a household-budget policy delivered through schools.

That budget effect matters especially now because family stress does not neatly track formal poverty lines. In states without universal meals, families above the free and reduced-price cutoffs can still be highly exposed to cost shocks. A system that requires those families to keep meal accounts funded may be imposing friction on households that are not officially poor but are financially brittle. Universal-meal advocates sometimes talk about dignity and stigma, and those arguments are real. But the administrative case may be even stronger: a universal service generates fewer points of failure.

The fiscal counterargument is real, but it is not the whole story

There is, of course, a serious argument against statewide universal meals. They cost money, and they compete with other education priorities. States are not operating in a vacuum. Districts face staffing pressure, special education costs, transportation expenses, deferred maintenance, and academic recovery demands. A state that chooses to subsidize meals for all students, including middle- and upper-income families, is choosing not to spend some of that money elsewhere. That is not a frivolous objection. It is how budgeting works.

School nutrition administrators themselves consistently warn about financial sustainability. In the School Nutrition Association’s 2025-26 trends report, 94.9 percent of responding directors reported serious or moderate concern about the financial sustainability of their school nutrition program three years from now. USDA’s Economic Research Service has similarly documented the fragility of school food-service finances. In a June 2025 ERS chart and related analysis, fewer school food authorities experienced deficits during the pandemic than before or after, in part because of additional federal support and administrative flexibilities. As that support expired and costs rose, the share of authorities breaking even or running a surplus declined. By the end of 2022-23, 28 percent reported revenues insufficient to cover costs. Those numbers make clear that “feed everyone for free” is not self-executing. It requires either stronger reimbursements, state supplementation, or local budget capacity.

But the anti-universal argument often understates the cost of means-testing. Application processing, verification, accounting, software, communication with families, debt notices, staff time, and donation drives are not free. Nor is the political and administrative cost of managing unpaid balances. When districts maintain a charged-meal system, some portion of the program’s instability is simply shifted into other forms: hidden labor, family arrears, ad hoc philanthropy, and periodic public controversy. A targeted system can look cheaper on a spreadsheet precisely because some of its costs are distributed across institutions and households rather than appearing as a single state appropriation.

That is one reason the universal-meals debate can become oddly distorted. Critics ask why states should subsidize lunch for children from comfortable households. Supporters answer with hunger, stigma, and family strain. Both points are valid, but the underlying administrative question is different: what is the least dysfunctional way to operate a meal program inside a universal public institution? When almost all non-universal programs report debt, and when districts are repeatedly forced into arguments over pennies, price hikes, or collections, it becomes harder to argue that the old model is especially elegant or efficient.

Montgomery County shows how the strain reaches mainstream districts

The reason Montgomery County is such a useful case is that it strips away an easy explanation. This is not a tiny district with no administrative capacity. It is not a system that fits neatly into a stereotype of collapse. Yet local reporting from WTOP and NBC Washington in April 2026 shows the district considering its first meal price increase in 12 years while carrying about $1.2 million in unpaid student meal debt. District officials have said rising food and labor costs are part of the pressure. Some board members and parents have pushed back, arguing that raising meal prices sends the wrong signal at a time when many families remain under economic stress.

Montgomery County Public Schools’ own meal-payments materials show why a district like this can still end up in a fragmented system. Some schools qualify for universal access through CEP, while others rely on the district’s Free and Reduced-price Meals System, or FARM, application process and paid meal accounts. That means students in the same district do not all encounter the same cafeteria rules. Some move through a no-charge line. Others move through a line connected to account balances and household eligibility status. This is exactly the kind of internal patchwork that statewide universal policies are meant to eliminate.

It would be easy to read Montgomery County’s situation as merely local housekeeping. Why not improve outreach? Why not enroll more eligible families? Why not collect more diligently? Why not raise prices modestly and close the gap? Those are practical questions, and districts have to ask them. But they do not answer the larger one. If a wealthy, administratively sophisticated suburban system still ends up with seven figures in debt and a politically sensitive price debate, then the problem is not just poor execution. It is a sign that the underlying model produces recurring strain even where local capacity is comparatively strong.

This is where lunch debt becomes a more revealing metric than it first appears. In budget terms, meal debt may look small compared with a district’s overall operating budget. In institutional terms, it is a signal. It shows where the school system is being used to absorb a mismatch between household finances and public-program design. It also shows which staff end up carrying that burden. Cafeteria workers and school administrators are not just serving meals. In non-universal systems they often become the human interface of means-testing, arrears management, and family contact.

There is a social meaning to that arrangement. To adults looking at budget books, a few dollars on lunch may appear minor. To children, the lunch line is one of the most routine and public encounters they have with the state. Whether that encounter feels unconditional or contingent matters. Many states and districts now restrict punitive practices associated with lunch shaming. Virginia law, for example, bars school boards from requiring students to discard meals after being served, from making them do work to pay for food, from suing students or parents over meal debt, and from denying extracurricular participation because of unpaid meal charges. Virginia law also requires schools participating in the federal lunch and breakfast programs to make meals available to any student who requests one, regardless of whether the student has money to pay that day or owes prior meal debt, unless a parent has expressly authorized withholding meals. Those protections matter. But they also underscore the point: even when children still receive food, the system remains organized around debt and collection unless a state chooses a different model.

Virginia’s new reporting law shows the politics moving from shame to systems

One of the more telling recent developments came in Virginia, where a new law is set to expand public information about school meal debt. WVTF reported in early April that supporters see the measure as a way to gather statewide data on who is carrying debt, who is collecting it, and which students and families are affected. That may sound bureaucratic, but it represents an important change in the politics of the issue.

Older lunch-debt controversies tended to erupt around a single upsetting episode: a child denied a hot meal, marked in front of peers, or otherwise publicly singled out. Those incidents were politically potent because they were emotionally legible. The emerging argument is more structural. It asks whether a state can honestly claim to run a rational public meal system if it cannot say how much debt districts hold, how unevenly they manage it, or how often districts depend on donations and informal workarounds. The focus is shifting from scandal to governance.

That shift matters because administrative opacity has often masked the true scale of the problem. Some divisions quietly absorb debt in their budgets. Some rely on philanthropy. Some pursue parents more aggressively. Some do little of any of these but muddle through. Without statewide data, those differences remain hard to compare and easy to ignore. Virginia’s reporting move suggests that lunch debt is being treated less as a sporadic embarrassment and more as a public-systems question.

It also points to a broader national reality: the meaning of lunch debt is changing. It is no longer only evidence that some families cannot pay. It is evidence that the state has chosen not to simplify the system. The more universal-meal states normalize a different approach, the less natural the debt model looks elsewhere.

Research supports participation gains; it does not promise miracles

The evidence for universal meals is meaningful but should not be oversold. Universal access does not automatically fix chronic absenteeism, raise test scores, or eliminate food insecurity. Effects vary across settings, and implementation quality matters. Food waste remains a live concern in some contexts. Reimbursement formulas can still leave districts or states carrying gaps. And universal availability is not the same thing as universal participation; families and students still make choices about school food.

Still, the evidence base has moved well beyond anecdote. The 2024 JAMA Network Open review found the strongest evidence for increased lunch participation, with lower certainty around some other outcomes. The 2025 JAMA Pediatrics study found participation gains during both the federal universal-free period of the pandemic and in states that continued universal policies afterward. USDA’s ERS has documented the central role of school meals in nutrition and food security, as well as the changing financial position of school food authorities during and after the pandemic support period. Taken together, the research supports a modest but important conclusion: universal meals make it easier for students to use the program, and they remove enough friction to change both school participation patterns and family spending behavior.

That last point is easy to undervalue. Public systems often fail not because the nominal benefit is too small but because the path to claiming it is too burdensome. Forms go unfinished. Families churn in and out of eligibility. Staff have to interpret exceptions. Children experience the consequences in real time. Universal provision does not remove all administration, but it shifts the burden away from repeated household sorting. In a school setting, that is not a small technical improvement. It changes the logic of service delivery.

For that reason, debates over school meals should not be framed only as anti-hunger policy. They are also debates about institutional design. Schools already provide many things universally that are more expensive than lunch: classrooms, heating, attendance systems, counseling infrastructure, and often transportation. Nobody pretends those services are free. The question is whether meals belong in that category of basic school-day infrastructure, or whether they remain an exception handled through a quasi-retail model nested inside a public system.

The next fights will be quiet, local, and revealing

The next phase of this story will probably not arrive through a single dramatic national headline. It will show up in state appropriations, district fee schedules, CEP election decisions before the June 30 federal deadline, and the accumulating size of debt ledgers. One track to watch is whether more states join the nine-state list for the 2026-27 school year. Another is whether states already funding universal meals can sustain that commitment as budget pressure rises elsewhere.

Colorado’s experience is a reminder that even popular meal programs depend on financing choices that can become politically contentious. California’s implementation shows that statewide commitment still requires coordination between state reimbursement, federal provisions, and local operations. New York’s rollout shows how quickly the issue can move from advocacy demand to statewide affordability policy once a governor and legislature decide to fund it. None of that means the universal model is effortless. It means the policy question has moved from theory to maintenance.

In non-universal states, the key indicators will often be more local and less glamorous: whether school boards approve meal price hikes, how many campuses adopt CEP after the lowered threshold, whether districts build donation campaigns into routine operations, and whether state lawmakers seek better debt reporting or tighter guardrails on collection practices. These may sound like management details. They are not. They determine whether the lunch line functions as a public service or as a thinly buffered household billing point.

There is also a federal angle that could matter more in the next round than in the last. Urban Institute analysis published in 2025 warned that changes to SNAP participation could reduce direct certification for free school meals and increase the number of students who would have to return to household applications. Because CEP reimbursement and eligibility are tied in part to direct certification counts, reductions in SNAP participation could also raise costs for some schools using CEP or threaten participation at the margin. If that happens, school meal access will become even more entangled with broader safety-net retrenchment. The school cafeteria would again become the place where cuts made elsewhere show up in daily life.

Why this is an education story, not a side issue

School meals can be mistakenly filed under nutrition, poverty, or local features. But the stronger frame is education governance. The lunch line is one of the clearest places where the public school system reveals what it means by universality. If schools tell families that attendance matters every day, that belonging matters, that students should focus on learning, and that schools are civic institutions open to all, then the cafeteria becomes a practical test of those claims. Is lunch included as part of ordinary participation, or only for those who clear the right administrative thresholds and keep the right balances current?

That is why lunch debt is not merely a small-bore bookkeeping matter. It shows where public education tries to target aid so narrowly that it reintroduces friction into a routine part of the day. It also shows where districts are asked to manage social hardship with tools that were built for a more stable economic environment. When meal debt rises, schools can try to raise prices, chase applications, court donations, or absorb losses. None of those options is especially clean. All are signs that the system is using the cafeteria to process a larger mismatch between need and design.

The strongest case for universal school meals is therefore not sentimental, even if the issue understandably invites moral language. It is administrative and civic. Universal provision simplifies service, increases participation, reduces opportunities for stigma, and removes a recurring source of low-level institutional friction. The strongest case against it is also real: money is finite, and states have many competing obligations. But policymakers who reject universality should be precise about what they are choosing instead. They are not choosing a perfectly targeted, low-waste, no-harm system. They are choosing forms, thresholds, uneven take-up, unpaid balances, staff time spent on debt, and periodic local conflicts over whether children’s lunches should be one more line item to collect.

That is the fork in front of the country now. The pandemic’s temporary federal universal-meals era has ended. What remains is a widening divide between states that have decided the lesson was to simplify and include, and places drifting back toward a system in which the cafeteria doubles as a billing office. As Montgomery County’s debate shows, that older model does not only strain poor districts or crisis systems. It strains mainstream ones too. And as Virginia’s reporting law suggests, even states that have not embraced universal meals are starting to admit that debt in the lunch line is not just an unfortunate anecdote. It is a measurable feature of how the school system works.

The cafeteria may look like a small arena compared with state aid formulas, teacher contracts, or federal loan policy. But it is where the theory of public education becomes tactile. A tray moves forward. A register lights up. A balance is either irrelevant or suddenly decisive. In that moment, the system is telling students and families something about what school includes, what it charges for, and where the public promise stops.
