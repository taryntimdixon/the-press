America’s Data-Center Boom Needs Rules Before It Needs More Praise

The U.S. should stop treating AI infrastructure as an unalloyed good and start regulating it like the power-hungry public utility it has become.

Written and Researched by AI • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m.

America has spent the last two years talking about artificial intelligence as if it were an abstract software revolution. In 2026, that illusion is gone. AI is now a physical build-out: giant buildings, transformers, substations, gas turbines, water systems, transmission corridors, financing vehicles, and political fights over who pays. The central fact of the moment is not that AI is powerful. It is that AI has become an infrastructure problem. And infrastructure, unlike hype, can be regulated.

The strongest case for the current rush is easy to make. The United States has a genuine strategic interest in building the computing capacity needed to remain competitive. The White House has embraced that argument, saying American leadership in AI requires domestic data-center infrastructure and a policy approach that keeps ratepayers from bearing the full burden of new electricity demand. The administration’s AI Action Plan also calls for faster permitting of data centers and related facilities. At the same time, the Energy Information Administration says U.S. electricity use is rising, with large computing centers a major driver, and the International Energy Agency says U.S. data centers consumed about 180 TWh of electricity in 2024 and are expected to keep climbing through 2030. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge-proclamation/?utm_source=openai))

That case deserves respect. It is not paranoid to worry that overregulating AI infrastructure could slow productivity growth, weaken the U.S. against China, and push investment elsewhere. It is also true that electricity demand is broadening for multiple reasons, not only data centers: electrification, industrial growth, and weather-driven load are all part of the picture. The EIA’s April 2026 outlook projects total U.S. electricity demand will rise in 2026, and its Annual Energy Outlook says electricity consumption will continue growing through 2050, with data-center server energy use a major factor. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))

But the proper response to a strategic build-out is not blank-check enthusiasm. It is rules. The emerging evidence says the country is not merely racing to build AI capacity; it is racing to socialize the risks while privatizing the gains. The public is being asked to shoulder grid upgrades, rate uncertainty, water stress, local land-use disruption, and the possibility that some of the bet-on demand never fully materializes. That is a bad bargain. America should keep building AI infrastructure, but it should do so under a regime of mandatory transparency, ratepayer protection, grid interconnection discipline, water accounting, and community consent. If the industry is serious that this is a national project, then it should accept national responsibilities. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/?utm_source=openai))

The build-out is real, and so are the bottlenecks

There is no serious dispute that the data-center boom has moved from forecast to concrete. Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory’s 2024 data-center energy report estimated that data centers consumed about 4.4% of total U.S. electricity in 2023, and that share could rise to between 6.7% and 12% by 2028 depending on broader economic growth. Berkeley Lab also estimated data-center electricity use would rise from 176 TWh in 2023 to somewhere between 325 and 580 TWh by 2028. A later Berkeley Lab research summary said the data-center energy-use estimate nearly tripled between 2016 and 2023, largely because of AI servers. ([newscenter.lbl.gov](https://newscenter.lbl.gov/2025/01/15/berkeley-lab-report-evaluates-increase-in-electricity-demand-from-data-centers/?utm_source=openai))

That matters because electricity systems do not expand on venture-capital time. Utilities, grid operators, transmission planners, and permitting agencies work on multiyear cycles. Brookings has argued that planning, permitting, and constructing major generation and transmission typically takes eight to 10 years, while data-center developers often want power much sooner. Whether or not every project fits that timing, the mismatch itself is the story: a software industry accustomed to near-instant scaling is colliding with an energy system built around long lead times and physical constraints. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/boom-or-bust-how-to-protect-ratepayers-from-the-ai-bubble/?utm_source=openai))

That mismatch helps explain why the current boom is already provoking backlash. Maine lawmakers approved a yearlong freeze on some energy-hungry data-center development, reflecting local alarm about blackouts, higher electricity bills, and water use. In Arizona, Tucson has moved to regulate large water users after a secretive data-center proposal helped spur public backlash. And on the federal side, the Environmental Protection Agency has created a Clean Air Act resources page for data centers, a sign that regulators are already treating the sector as a distinct permitting and emissions challenge. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/aa63ba087d5ad53ab0735893646e7357?utm_source=openai))

These are not isolated anecdotes. They are signals. When an industry grows fast enough to alter the politics of power planning, the old assumption that markets will self-correct is too simple. AI data centers are not just another commercial landlord. They are unusually large, unusually concentrated, and unusually dependent on scarce public goods: reliable electricity, water, land, and transmission. That makes them more like an industrial-policy issue than a normal real-estate one. ([newscenter.lbl.gov](https://newscenter.lbl.gov/2025/01/15/berkeley-lab-report-evaluates-increase-in-electricity-demand-from-data-centers/?utm_source=openai))

The public is being asked to backstop private ambition

The most revealing part of the debate is not the rhetoric from tech executives; it is the emerging structure of risk-sharing. In March, the White House announced the Ratepayer Protection Pledge, saying hyperscalers and AI companies must pay the full cost of the energy and infrastructure needed for data centers and should not pass those costs to the public. Days later, the administration highlighted the pledge again as part of its effort to keep electricity costs down amid the data-center boom. At the same time, federal officials have moved to accelerate energy projects on federal lands to support AI infrastructure. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/?utm_source=openai))

That may be prudent. It is not, by itself, a public-interest guarantee. When hyperscalers secure their own supply, the rest of the system can still be left with the costs of interconnection, backup capacity, transmission congestion, and socialized infrastructure upgrades. Brookings has warned that if AI demand turns out to be overbuilt or overhyped, utilities and ratepayers could be left exposed. The OECD’s March 2026 outlook likewise warns that lower-than-expected returns from net AI investment could trigger broader risk repricing in financial markets. In other words, there is a real risk that the country is overbuilding one of the most expensive forms of infrastructure on the assumption that demand will keep compounding forever. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/boom-or-bust-how-to-protect-ratepayers-from-the-ai-bubble/?utm_source=openai))

That risk is not theoretical. The very scale of spending tells us the market is trying to outrun uncertainty. SEC filings and company disclosures in recent months show businesses positioning themselves around AI data-center financing, GPU deployment, and power expansion. But private balance sheets do not tell us who pays if demand slows, if interconnection queues jam, if transformers are scarce, or if local governments are left with infrastructure burdens after a project fails to materialize. The public often gets the environmental and financial residue long after the ribbon-cutting press release is forgotten. ([sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001878848/000187884826000014/a991irenreportsq2fy26res.htm?utm_source=openai))

This is why the proper policy question is not “Should America build AI?” It is “Under what terms should America allow a handful of firms to consume unprecedented amounts of public utility capacity?” If the answer is “whatever gets built fastest,” then the public is not governing a strategic industry. It is subsidizing it. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/?utm_source=openai))

Why the pro-build argument is still compelling

The steelman case for rapid build-out begins with competition. AI systems are now foundational to products, scientific research, defense, coding, media, and enterprise software. The OECD has documented the broader rise in AI use and investment, and the White House has framed AI as a strategic national competition that requires innovation, infrastructure, and security policy moving together. If the U.S. slows too much while others accelerate, the risk is not only that American firms lose money. It is that the country cedes the industrial base of the next computing era. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/economic-outlook/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a climate argument on the other side. Some of the new capacity is being paired with nuclear, renewable, storage, and grid modernization. The IEA says clean generation continues to expand, while the EIA expects renewable additions and broader supply growth to help meet rising load. From that perspective, a bigger data-center economy does not automatically mean dirtier power. It could, if managed well, accelerate investment in low-carbon generation and grid resilience. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-mid-year-update-2025/demand-global-electricity-use-to-grow-strongly-in-2025-and-2026?utm_source=openai))

Then there is the productivity case. If AI genuinely boosts coding, drug discovery, logistics, design, and manufacturing, a dense domestic computing ecosystem could raise output and wages. That is why it is too glib to say data centers are just warehouses for the cloud. They are, in part, the physical substrate of future software productivity. And if the United States wants those gains to accrue here rather than abroad, it cannot afford to treat every new campus as a nuisance. It must be capable of saying yes. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/research/2026/01/artificial-intelligence-and-the-great-divergence/?utm_source=openai))

Finally, the best pro-build argument is democratic: if Americans want the benefits of AI, they should accept some of the costs of the infrastructure that makes it possible. That argument has real force. Every major technological advance has required land, wire, steel, and power. A society that wants abundant intelligence cannot pretend intelligence comes free. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/sites/default/files/2024-08/Powering%20AI%20and%20Data%20Center%20Infrastructure%20Recommendations%20July%202024_1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

But this argument only works if the costs are distributed fairly and the benefits are real. Right now, neither is assured.

The case for restraint is stronger than the industry admits

The clearest reason to impose constraints is that data-center growth is not just another industrial expansion. It is a concentration of demand that can distort local markets and socialize risk. The IEA warns that U.S. demand growth is being driven materially by data centers. Berkeley Lab says the share of national electricity they consume could nearly triple from 2023 to 2028. EIA’s new pilot survey initiative exists because the government still lacks enough detailed data on where these facilities are, how much they consume, and how they cool. That is a striking fact: the country is allowing a major transformation of the power system while still building the measurement apparatus. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-mid-year-update-2025/demand-global-electricity-use-to-grow-strongly-in-2025-and-2026?utm_source=openai))

That measurement gap matters because without it, public officials cannot easily determine whether ratepayers are subsidizing private loads, whether transmission upgrades are being allocated fairly, or whether load forecasts reflect real demand or speculative land banking. In the meantime, the political pressure is moving toward secrecy and speed. The safer position is the opposite: a big, power-intensive industry should be required to disclose its actual and anticipated load, its water use, its interconnection status, its backup arrangements, and the share of infrastructure costs it is bearing. A project that cannot stand up to that sunlight is not ready for prime time. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press585.php?utm_source=openai))

There is also a land-use and water-use argument. Data centers are not just boxes of servers; they are heat engines that need cooling. The World Bank has noted that data centers use water evaporation to keep servers from overheating and that precise reporting remains patchy. AP has also reported community concerns in Tucson about the water demands of a proposed data center and the city’s response in tightening rules for large water users. In a country already confronting climate volatility, drought risk, and regional generation shortages, inviting thousands of megawatts of new steady load without rigorous siting rules is a recipe for public resentment. ([blogs.worldbank.org](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/water/when-the-cloud-meets-a-thirsty-world?utm_source=openai))

And then there is the bubble risk. The more the economy reorganizes itself around the assumption of endless AI demand, the more dangerous a demand disappointment becomes. If companies are overcommitting capital to a capacity curve that flattens, the fallout can hit utilities, municipalities, workers, and investors. The OECD’s warning about a possible reassessment of AI returns is therefore not a side note. It is a reminder that “build more” is not a strategy unless the demand is durable, the pricing is sound, and the costs are internalized. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-economic-outlook-interim-report-march-2026_d4623013-en/full-report/component-4.html?utm_source=openai))

What the counterargument gets right about public policy

The strongest objection to a tougher regulatory approach is that America often confuses friction with virtue. Permitting delays can be anti-competitive. Local moratoria can become parochial vetoes. Environmental review can be weaponized by communities that want digital services but not the facilities that support them. If policymakers make data centers too hard to build, they could freeze in place an aging grid, force more computation overseas, and leave local economies without jobs or tax revenue. That is not a frivolous concern. It is a real institutional failure mode in the United States, where projects of all kinds can get lost in overlapping jurisdiction and inconsistent rules. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/boom-or-bust-how-to-protect-ratepayers-from-the-ai-bubble/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a fairness point. Many communities have spent decades hosting the back office of the digital economy without complaint. A sudden turn against data centers can feel opportunistic, especially when the same communities rely on the taxes, construction jobs, and utility investment those projects generate. Pro-build advocates are right to ask why the burden of national ambition should fall so heavily on a few counties and utility districts. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4ce76b73c102727d71edbbb56abe1388?utm_source=openai))

And yet even this counterargument points toward regulation, not deregulation. The problem is not that communities have too much power. It is that they often have too little information and too few national standards. If a county is going to host an AI campus, it should know the project’s water intensity, grid implications, noise impact, backup fuel plans, and shutdown contingencies. If a utility is going to approve interconnection, it should be able to require cost-sharing and load-flexibility commitments. If taxpayers are going to subsidize development, they should be able to see the payoff in jobs, wages, and long-term revenue. That is not anti-growth. It is adult supervision. ([epa.gov](https://www.epa.gov/stationary-sources-air-pollution/clean-air-act-resources-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

A sensible policy package would do four things

First, it would force load transparency. EIA’s new pilot surveys are a start, but Congress and states should require large data-center operators to report actual electricity use, water use, and anticipated expansion in standardized form. This should not be treated as confidential forever. The public cannot manage what it cannot measure. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press585.php?utm_source=openai))

Second, it would make ratepayer protection explicit. If a project triggers transmission or generation upgrades, the operator should bear a meaningful share of the cost, and those charges should not be hidden in generalized utility rate increases. The point is not to punish data centers. The point is to prevent everyday households from subsidizing luxury-grade compute for trillion-dollar firms. Brookings and the White House have both warned that ratepayers should not be left holding the bag. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/boom-or-bust-how-to-protect-ratepayers-from-the-ai-bubble/?utm_source=openai))

Third, it would require flexible load planning. Data centers should be built with demand-response capability, on-site storage, and contractual curtailment provisions where appropriate. If the industry wants priority access to scarce power, it should help the grid manage peaks instead of worsening them. The EIA’s outlook and Berkeley Lab’s large-load research both underscore that planning for future supply is not enough; demand flexibility and interconnection management matter too. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/elec_coal_renew.php?utm_source=openai))

Fourth, it would tie incentives to public returns. If state or local governments grant tax breaks, those incentives should be conditional on real job creation, local procurement, workforce training, and transparent community-benefit agreements. Washington’s long-running tax-break debates show why this matters: the state has seen large cumulative tax savings for data centers, but reporters also found that some promised local benefits were limited and that the industry often generated few permanent jobs after construction. ([propublica.org](https://www.propublica.org/article/washington-data-centers-tech-jobs-tax-break?utm_source=openai))

None of these measures would stop the AI race. They would simply make the race legible.

Why the moment is larger than AI

The data-center boom is also exposing a broader truth about the American political economy: we are increasingly willing to celebrate private technological ambition while underinvesting in the public institutions that make it possible. The grid is not a byproduct of AI. It is a precondition. So is water infrastructure. So are permitting agencies, environmental oversight, and local planning departments. Yet the country often treats those systems as obstacles rather than as the civic machinery of growth. ([epa.gov](https://www.epa.gov/stationary-sources-air-pollution/clean-air-act-resources-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

That is why the fight over AI data centers is really a fight over whether the United States still believes in coordinated capacity-building. A nation that wants abundant AI must also want abundant transmission, abundant substation capacity, abundant clean generation, and abundant public data. If the only thing we scale with enthusiasm is the private campus, then we are not modernizing the economy. We are offloading its costs onto everyone else. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/aeo/pdf/AEO_Narrative.pdf?utm_source=openai))

There is a tempting libertarian fantasy here: let the market build, let the market price, let the market sort it out. But electricity is not an app store. A hidden grid externality can hit a grandmother’s utility bill long before any correction arrives. A water shortage cannot be resolved by a product update. A transmission line cannot be conjured by investor sentiment. The physical world has veto power. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/?utm_source=openai))

The verdict

The right answer is not to stop building AI data centers. It is to stop pretending that building them is costless. The United States should treat the AI infrastructure boom as a strategic public-utility problem and govern it accordingly. That means faster permitting in exchange for stricter disclosure, stronger interconnection rules, mandatory cost allocation, and real community bargaining. It means welcoming the capacity the country needs while refusing to let a handful of companies convert scarce public infrastructure into a private subsidy. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2025/07/white-house-unveils-americas-ai-action-plan/?utm_source=openai))

Supporters of the current rush are right about one thing: the nation cannot afford to sit still. But that does not mean it must sprint blindfolded. If AI is going to reshape the country’s energy system, then energy policy must reshape AI back. The companies building the future should not be allowed to write off the bill as an externality. America should build the intelligence economy. It should just stop pretending the bill is someone else’s problem. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/04/2026-economic-report-of-the-president-released/?utm_source=openai))
