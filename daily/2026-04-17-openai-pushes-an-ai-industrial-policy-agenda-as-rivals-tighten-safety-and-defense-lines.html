OpenAI pushes an AI-industrial policy agenda as rivals tighten safety and defense lines

A wave of new policy papers from OpenAI and Anthropic shows how the AI industry is trying to shape the rules that will govern its next phase — from workforce disruption and compute access to military use and frontier safety.

By The Press • April 17, 2026 • 1:18 a.m.

Two of the world’s most influential AI companies are using April to make a broader argument about where artificial intelligence should go next — and who should help set the rules. OpenAI on April 6 published an “industrial policy for the Intelligence Age,” a policy blueprint that calls for new fellowships, research grants, a Washington workshop and a suite of proposals meant to keep advanced AI development “people first.” Anthropic, meanwhile, updated its Responsible Scaling Policy on April 2, clarifying how it will govern future frontier models and when it may pause development or tighten safeguards.

Put together, the documents capture a new phase in the AI debate. The competition is no longer just about model quality, product launches or market share. It is also about regulation, labor, defense, data retention and the social bargain around how much power AI companies should hold as their systems become more capable. The timing matters: both firms are trying to influence policy before governments settle on rules that could shape the next several years of AI deployment. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

OpenAI’s policy pitch: growth, grants and guardrails

OpenAI’s April 6 memo frames AI as a national economic transformation that will require more than incremental policy changes. The company says the coming era will demand public and private action around skills, infrastructure and institutional resilience, and it is inviting feedback on its ideas through a dedicated email address, along with a fellowship and grant program aimed at outside researchers and policy thinkers. OpenAI also says it plans to open a Washington workshop in May. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

The document is not a formal policy recommendation in the legislative sense. It reads more like an attempt to establish an agenda. But its proposals are revealing. OpenAI is arguing that the central challenge is not whether AI will affect the labor market, education and public institutions; it is how quickly those changes will arrive, and whether governments can respond with enough speed and scale. By describing its ideas as “early and exploratory,” the company signals that it wants to shape the conversation without appearing to dictate it. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

That matters because OpenAI is not just a vendor of consumer chatbots. It is one of the most politically visible firms in AI, with product ambitions, infrastructure demands and public-policy interests that now overlap. Its latest memo places it squarely in the role of industrial strategist, not just software maker. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

Anthropic’s message: frontier AI needs measurable limits

Anthropic’s update to its Responsible Scaling Policy takes a different tack. Rather than making a broad economic argument, the company is tightening the technical and governance language around how it will manage future model risk. The April 2 update to version 3.1 says Anthropic clarified the meaning of its AI R&D capability threshold and explicitly states that, even when not required by the policy, it remains free to pause model development whenever it deems appropriate. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy?utm_source=openai))

Anthropic says the policy is a living document and that the changes are not meant to alter its substance. Even so, the revisions are notable because they show the company trying to translate safety claims into operational rules. That is especially important for a lab whose public identity has long rested on the idea that frontier AI needs hard boundaries before systems become too powerful to supervise responsibly. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy?utm_source=openai))

The roadmap accompanying the policy adds more detail. Anthropic says it completed an internal report on data retention risks on March 29, and it sets future dates for additional safeguards and alignment work. The company also notes that it offers many customers zero data retention, while acknowledging that more restrictive retention rules can make it harder to detect misuse and learn from real-world usage. That tension — privacy versus oversight — is now one of the central governance problems in AI. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy/roadmap?utm_source=openai))

Military use, worker pressure and the politics of red lines

The policy turn comes amid intensifying conflict over military uses of AI. In late February, Reuters and AP reported friction between Anthropic and the Pentagon over what kinds of uses the company would permit. AP also reported that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth warned Anthropic to let the military use its technology as it sees fit. Around the same time, Axios reported that more than 200 employees from Google and OpenAI signed a letter supporting limits on AI use for domestic surveillance and fully autonomous warfare. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3d86c9296fe953ec0591fcde6a613aba?utm_source=openai))

Anthropic has also emerged as the company most willing to publicly defend hard limits. Its updated policy and prior public statements reinforce the message that some applications are off-limits or require extraordinary scrutiny. That position has helped make Anthropic a reference point for critics of AI militarization, but it has also placed the company at the center of a larger debate about whether private labs should be allowed to set de facto safety standards for national-security uses. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy?utm_source=openai))

The labor implications are equally striking. OpenAI’s industrial-policy memo argues that AI should expand opportunity and share prosperity, but that message lands in a moment when workers inside the tech industry are increasingly being asked to adopt AI tools faster than they may want. The politics of “AI for everyone” are becoming harder to separate from the practical realities of workplace automation, cost cutting and productivity mandates. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

Why this matters now

The significance of the April policy wave is not that either company has solved AI governance. It is that the leading firms are now acting as policy actors in their own right. OpenAI is trying to define the economic and institutional case for rapid AI deployment. Anthropic is trying to define the safety architecture that should constrain it. Those goals are not mutually exclusive, but they are not the same thing.

That divergence could shape the next round of legislation and regulatory debate. If policymakers adopt OpenAI’s framing, they may prioritize competitiveness, infrastructure and workforce transition. If Anthropic’s approach gains traction, the emphasis may shift toward capability thresholds, auditability, retention controls and explicit red lines around the most dangerous uses. In practice, the final policy environment is likely to blend both, but the balance will matter. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

For now, the most telling detail is that both companies are moving early. OpenAI is offering a policy agenda before laws harden. Anthropic is refining safeguards before the next jump in capability. In a sector where technical change usually outruns regulation, that is the clearest sign yet that the AI industry knows the rules are coming — and wants a hand in writing them. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))
