CinemaCon 2026 puts theatrical marketing back in the spotlight as studios race to reassure exhibitors

From Nolan’s IMAX showcase to Disney’s sequel-heavy 2026 slate, this year’s CinemaCon is doubling as a referendum on whether movie theaters can still command culture.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 16, 2026 • 4:57 p.m. • 1128 words

LAS VEGAS — For four days in April, the movie business has been trying to answer a question that has hovered over Hollywood for years: what exactly still gets people into theaters? At CinemaCon 2026, the annual gathering of theater owners and studio marketers, the answer has been presented in the language Hollywood knows best — spectacle, star power and the promise that some films are built for a room full of strangers.

The timing matters. CinemaCon is taking place as the industry continues to manage a volatile post-pandemic box office, a stretched-out release calendar and a consumer base that has more viewing choices than at any point in cinema history. Yet the mood in Las Vegas has not been one of retreat. Studios have used the convention to argue that theatrical moviegoing still has a distinct value proposition, especially when a film is framed as an event rather than just another title on a crowded content menu.

That pitch has been especially visible in Universal’s presentation, where Christopher Nolan offered theater owners a first extended look at The Odyssey, his next large-format epic and one of the year’s most anticipated releases. Nolan said the project had been filmed entirely on IMAX cameras, turning the movie into both a technological showcase and a reminder that certain directors still treat theatrical exhibition as a creative medium, not just a distribution window. Universal has the film dated for July 17, 2026. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))

Disney, meanwhile, is closing out CinemaCon by leaning into the scale of its franchise pipeline. The studio enters the second quarter after already getting a strong boost from Pixar’s Hoppers, and it is preparing to send several tentpoles into the marketplace, including The Mandalorian and Grogu, Toy Story 5, a live-action Moana and Avengers: Doomsday. For exhibitors, the message is straightforward: if the biggest films in the world still arrive in cinemas first, theaters still matter. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b))

Why CinemaCon still matters

CinemaCon has always been a trade show, not a fan convention, but in a fractured media landscape it has become one of Hollywood’s clearest pressure points. Studios are not just selling their upcoming slates to exhibitors; they are selling the idea that theatrical is a premium product worth preserving. That explains why the presentations tend to emphasize scale, exclusivity and the communal experience of seeing a movie with other people in a dark room.

This year’s edition has made that argument in especially stark terms. The convention comes as the theatrical business continues to recover unevenly from the industry shocks of the past several years, while studios face their own strategic tensions over streaming, franchise fatigue and the cost of mounting giant productions. The result is a mini arms race in presentation: clips, sizzle reels, surprise appearances and carefully managed teases designed to make theater owners believe the next tentpole can still become a cultural event. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

That effort is not merely promotional. It is also defensive. Theater chains need evidence that studios will keep delivering films that justify premium formats, concession sales and opening-weekend urgency. Studios, for their part, want exhibitors to continue making room for a release strategy that still depends on a burst of theatrical attention before titles move downstream to home entertainment and streaming. CinemaCon remains one of the few places where those interests are negotiated in public. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/654f2c37aa97031320ac26b6dc89881b))

The event movie is back at the center

If one theme has united the week’s biggest presentations, it is the return of the “event movie” as Hollywood’s favorite selling point. Universal has framed The Odyssey not just as a literary adaptation but as a technical achievement and a once-in-a-generation production. Disney is positioning its 2026 slate around familiar IP with built-in awareness. Amazon MGM, in a more comic register, used its Spaceballs sequel reveal to show that even legacy parody can be turned into a theatrical draw if handled with enough nostalgia and self-awareness. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))

That last example mattered more than its punchline. Mel Brooks’ video message unveiling the title Spaceballs: The New One was funny, but it also served a practical purpose: it showed exhibitors a studio willing to market a film as a shared experience, with a release date and recognizable brand attached. Amazon MGM has slated the sequel for April 23, 2027, and the project’s CinemaCon unveiling underscored how far the studio sees theatrical comedy as still being able to travel when packaged as an event rather than a modest release. ([aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/entertainment/spaceballs-2-sequel-amazon-mgm-studios/?utm_source=openai))

That is the broader lesson of the week. Hollywood is not abandoning theaters. It is narrowing the kinds of films it believes can reliably make theatergoing feel urgent. Big-format spectacle, franchise continuation, star-driven nostalgia and technically ambitious filmmaking are the categories receiving the loudest push. Mid-budget dramas and comedies may still exist, but at CinemaCon they are no longer the story; the story is the movie that justifies leaving home. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))

A crowded calendar raises the stakes

One reason the rhetoric feels so forceful is that the 2026 and 2027 calendars are unusually competitive. Universal, Disney, Paramount and Amazon MGM are all promoting projects meant to claim specific weekends, IMAX screens and audience mindshare months in advance. The strategy is partly about certainty: the farther out the release date, the more confidence studios can project. But it is also about dominance. Each announcement is effectively a land grab for the future of theatrical attendance. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))

Paramount’s presentation arrives with its own subtext. The studio is taking the stage while its pending $111 billion deal to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery hangs over the convention, a reminder that the business of making and distributing films is still being reshaped by consolidation. In that context, even a routine slate presentation becomes a statement about scale and survival. Exhibitors are being asked not only to believe in specific movies, but to believe that the companies behind them will remain stable enough to deliver them. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13))

That uncertainty may be exactly why the mood in Las Vegas has felt so determined. The industry knows that attention is scarce and audience habits are unpredictable. It also knows that the movies that break through tend to do so by looking and feeling unlike everything else available at home. CinemaCon 2026 has therefore become less a showcase of next season’s titles than a renewed argument that theatrical cinema still offers something singular: scale, anticipation and the promise that some stories are still bigger when they are seen together. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))

Whether that argument translates into sustained ticket sales remains to be seen. But for one week in April, Hollywood has rolled out its most convincing version of the case — not through nostalgia alone, but through the careful, expensive and unmistakably current effort to make the movies feel like an occasion again. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569))
