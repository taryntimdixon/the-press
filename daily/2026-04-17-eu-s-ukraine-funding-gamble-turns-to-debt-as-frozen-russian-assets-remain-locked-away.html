EU’s Ukraine funding gamble turns to debt as frozen Russian assets remain locked away

Brussels has committed to keep Ukraine financed through 2027, but legal risk, Belgian resistance and Moscow’s retaliation show how hard it is for Europe to turn sanctions into strategy.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 17, 2026 • 1:18 a.m. • 937 words

Europe’s biggest geopolitical test is no longer only how long it can keep sanctioning Russia. It is whether it can still finance Ukraine at the scale required to keep the country functioning while Washington’s support has become less predictable and Moscow has made clear it will punish any move that turns frozen state assets into wartime cash.

That dilemma has sharpened over the past several months as the European Union settled on a large loan package for Ukraine, after a more ambitious plan to use frozen Russian central bank assets ran into legal and political resistance. The EU’s leaders agreed in December to provide Ukraine with a 90 billion-euro loan for 2026 and 2027, after Belgium objected to a scheme that would have used the assets themselves as collateral. The bloc then moved to keep the Russian funds immobilized indefinitely, limiting the ability of sanctions skeptics inside the EU to force an early release. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/abc7b025112dba1f074755e454c29681?utm_source=openai))

The result is a distinctly European compromise: maintain pressure on Moscow, keep Ukraine afloat, but avoid crossing the line into outright confiscation of sovereign assets. That line matters. European officials have repeatedly warned that seizing the assets could damage confidence in the euro and expose states such as Belgium, where most of the money is held, to retaliation and legal challenges. Moscow has already responded by pursuing claims against Euroclear, the Brussels-based clearing house that holds the bulk of the immobilized funds. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cc6062353d6ef8dd91a1fff87ce208ee?utm_source=openai))

Why the asset fight matters

The frozen-asset dispute is about more than accounting. It is a test of whether sanctions can evolve from a punitive tool into a long-term war-financing instrument. Since Russia’s full-scale invasion in 2022, the EU has steadily tightened its sanctions regime, renewing economic restrictions and building out measures that target energy, finance, transport and sanctions evasion. By late 2025, EU leaders had also agreed to keep Russia’s assets immobilized until Moscow ends its war and pays reparations. ([consilium.europa.eu](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/sanctions/restrictive-measures-against-russia-over-ukraine/history-restrictive-measures-against-russia-over-ukraine/))

For Ukraine, the stakes are immediate. Kyiv needs outside financing to cover military expenses, public salaries, pensions, basic government functions and reconstruction. The International Monetary Fund has estimated Ukraine’s needs for 2026 and 2027 at roughly 137 billion euros, a figure that illustrates why European leaders moved to guarantee support well before the money runs out. AP reported that the EU’s loan package was designed to bridge two years of needs and that other partners, including Britain, Canada, Japan and Norway, may be asked to contribute as well. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cc6062353d6ef8dd91a1fff87ce208ee?utm_source=openai))

But the political logic is fragile. The more the war drags on, the harder it becomes for European governments to convince voters that open-ended support for Ukraine is sustainable. At the same time, any hint that the West is wavering would be read in Moscow as proof that endurance, not battlefield gains, is enough to divide the coalition backing Kyiv. That is why the frozen-assets debate now sits at the center of Europe’s broader strategy: it is both a financial question and a signal to Russia about how long the West is prepared to hold the line. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/abc7b025112dba1f074755e454c29681?utm_source=openai))

Belgium’s veto power and Europe’s caution

Belgium has emerged as the pivotal holdout because Euroclear is headquartered there and because Brussels would be exposed first if Russia retaliates. Belgian officials have argued that the reparations-loan model carries legal and financial risks that are hard to quantify and difficult to insure away. That resistance forced the EU to abandon its initial plan to use the assets directly and instead lean on joint borrowing backed by the bloc’s budget machinery. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cc6062353d6ef8dd91a1fff87ce208ee?utm_source=openai))

In practical terms, that decision reveals a deep feature of European geopolitics: the EU can be forceful in a crisis, but it is rarely fast. Every major step still has to survive legal scrutiny, national caution and the fear of precedent. European leaders are willing to keep sanctions in place for months or years, but they remain wary of actions that could be framed by adversaries as theft or by their own courts as overreach. The compromise leaves Russia’s funds locked away and Ukraine funded for now, yet it also shows that Europe’s most powerful economic weapon still operates inside strict political limits. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a65c92625cab565da7b8e07013dead05?utm_source=openai))

Moscow is betting on patience

Russia has tried to turn that caution into leverage. The Kremlin has denounced the use of frozen sovereign assets to support Ukraine and has filed claims in Russian courts against Euroclear, seeking to punish the European institution that stands at the center of the sanctions regime. That legal pressure is only one part of Moscow’s response. Russia also benefits whenever uncertainty grows inside the EU, because delay complicates Ukraine’s planning and raises the cost of maintaining a united front. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d1e86f0b24f8ec3c759c8950d372a2a0?utm_source=openai))

That is why the current arrangement, though substantial, is still only a partial answer. The 90 billion-euro package buys time, not closure. It gives Kyiv some financial certainty through 2027, but it does not resolve the larger question of how Europe will sustain support if the war remains frozen, intensified or politically contested. Nor does it remove the possibility that future negotiations, whether with Moscow or Washington, will reopen the question of what should happen to Russia’s immobilized reserves. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/abc7b025112dba1f074755e454c29681?utm_source=openai))

For now, the European Union has chosen endurance over escalation. It is keeping the assets locked, borrowing against its own balance sheet, and signaling that the sanctions regime will last as long as the war does. That may sound cautious, but in the geopolitics of 2026 it is a meaningful assertion of will: Europe is no longer only punishing Russia. It is trying to build a financial architecture for a protracted confrontation, one loan and one sanctions renewal at a time. ([consilium.europa.eu](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/sanctions/restrictive-measures-against-russia-over-ukraine/history-restrictive-measures-against-russia-over-ukraine/))
