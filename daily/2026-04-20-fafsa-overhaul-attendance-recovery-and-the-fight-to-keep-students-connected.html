Education

FAFSA overhaul, attendance recovery, and the fight to keep students connected

As states and districts enter the final stretch of the 2025-26 school year, education leaders are being forced to tackle three intertwined crises at once: unstable federal aid rules, stubborn chronic absenteeism, and deep staffing shortages that keep schools from offering the consistency families need.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 20, 2026 • 4:20 p.m. • 2613 words

Education systems in the United States are entering a late-spring pressure test. At the K-12 level, schools are still trying to reduce chronic absenteeism that surged after the pandemic and remains far above pre-2020 norms. At the college and workforce-training level, students and families are navigating a federal aid overhaul that is changing how the FAFSA is processed, when new Pell Grant rules take effect, and which short-term workforce programs may become eligible for federal aid. Across both sectors, staffing shortages and budget strain are limiting how much help schools and colleges can actually deliver. The common thread is capacity: who shows up, who can be served, and whether public systems can still do the work they promise. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

This is not a single-policy story so much as a system story. Attendance problems do not just lower daily head counts; they destabilize funding formulas, undermine learning recovery, and increase pressure on already thin school staffs. Likewise, federal aid changes do not just affect a financial aid office; they shape enrollment decisions, training pipelines, and whether low-income students can afford to persist. Federal agencies, research organizations, and local reporting all point to the same basic conclusion: public education is still working through the aftershocks of the pandemic, while also absorbing new political and fiscal pressures. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Why this moment matters now

The immediate news hook is the Education Department’s spring 2026 FAFSA processing update tied to the new federal law governing aid changes. Federal Student Aid says system changes will go live on April 26, 2026, with eligibility changes and modified loan limits taking effect on July 1, 2026 or later. The department says the update will affect its processing systems, the NSLDS, COD, and the 2026-27 ISIR format, and that it does not anticipate a large-scale reprocessing effort. For institutions, vendors, and state agencies, this is a transition point that matters for planning, training, and data systems. ([fsapartners.ed.gov](https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/electronic-announcements/2026-03-09/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-fafsa-processing-updates?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the federal government said in December 2025 that more than 5 million 2026-27 FAFSA forms had been successfully submitted, which it described as a major milestone for the cycle. The department also said the 2026-27 form had launched earlier than any prior FAFSA cycle. Those are encouraging signs of a more stable rollout after the well-documented problems of the 2024-25 cycle. But they do not settle the larger question families face now: completing the form is only one step, and the aid rules themselves are still changing. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-reaches-historic-milestone-fafsa-completions?utm_source=openai))

Meanwhile, the K-12 attendance story is not fading. The Education Department says chronic absenteeism is typically defined as missing at least 10% of school days, or about 18 days in a year, and notes that the national rate reached about 31% in 2021-22 before easing to 28% in 2022-23. Even with that improvement, the rate remains extraordinarily high by historical standards. NCES and IES continue to frame absenteeism as an academic and engagement crisis, not merely an attendance-office problem. The burden falls hardest on students with disabilities, English learners, low-income students, and students in districts with weak transportation or unreliable staffing. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

The federal aid reset: more targeted, more complicated

The biggest higher-ed policy change now moving through the system is the creation of Workforce Pell Grants for certain short-term programs. Federal Student Aid’s March 2026 announcement says the FAFSA processing systems will be updated on April 26 to support those changes, and the department’s proposed rules say students could begin using Pell Grant aid for eligible workforce programs starting in July 2026. The department’s technical guidance also shows that schools and vendors need to prepare for new processing fields, warning edits, and updates to the FAFSA specifications. That is not the kind of change families see directly, but it matters enormously to the administrators who determine whether a student can actually use aid to enroll. ([fsapartners.ed.gov](https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/electronic-announcements/2026-03-09/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-fafsa-processing-updates?utm_source=openai))

Supporters of Workforce Pell argue that it could give working adults a faster, more affordable path into jobs where employers are struggling to hire. The department’s own materials describe the new programs as short-term, high-quality, and aligned to workforce needs, and related FSA training materials emphasize that the eligible programs must lead to recognized credentials. That rationale has obvious appeal in sectors such as health care, manufacturing, transportation, and early childhood education, where employers often say the problem is not simply vacancies but a shortage of people with the right credentials. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-issues-proposed-rules-implement-working-families-tax-cuts-acts-workforce-pell-grants?utm_source=openai))

But the counterargument is serious. GAO’s March 2026 testimony says Education still has major accountability gaps in higher education oversight, including concerns about the department’s ability to monitor loan servicers and hold colleges accountable for financial health. If the federal system is still struggling to police program quality, the argument goes, then expanding aid into new categories without strong guardrails could increase risk for students and taxpayers. That does not mean Workforce Pell is a bad idea. It does mean its success will depend on how accurately the department, states, accreditors, and institutions define eligible programs and monitor outcomes after students enroll. ([gao.gov](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-109069?utm_source=openai))

There is also political uncertainty hanging over federal aid. The department’s fiscal 2027 congressional justification says workforce Pell will begin with the 2026-27 award year for short-term programs that meet the new criteria, and it projects a large Pell footprint overall. At the same time, Congress and the department are still sorting out implementation details, which means the financial aid landscape is not simply becoming more generous or more restrictive; it is becoming more contingent. Schools are preparing for one set of rules while policymakers continue fighting over the next one. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/media/document/fy-2027-congressional-justification-student-financial-assistance-113540.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Why FAFSA still matters after the better rollout

Even after the federal government’s improved form rollout, FAFSA completion is only the front door. Students still need colleges to interpret the data, calculate awards, and bridge the gap between sticker price and what a family can pay. The department’s own FAFSA page says the redesign was meant to improve completion, satisfaction, and support. But the national application process still feeds a highly fragmented pricing structure, and each institution decides how to package aid and what the student will actually owe. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/better-FAFSA?utm_source=openai))

For many families, the larger issue is not whether the form exists but whether aid keeps pace with the actual cost of attendance. As college prices, housing costs, and local living expenses remain high, FAFSA completion alone does not make school affordable. The department’s own milestone announcement is evidence of restored momentum, not proof that the affordability problem has been solved. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-reaches-historic-milestone-fafsa-completions?utm_source=openai))

The shift toward workforce-oriented aid may help some adults who were previously shut out of Pell eligibility. But it may also sharpen debate about what counts as a legitimate educational investment. A short-term credential can be transformative if it leads to stable wages, licensure, and advancement. It can also be a dead end if the program lacks quality, employer value, or recognized credentials. That is why the new federal rules should be read not only as access policy but as quality-control policy. The key question is whether the government can expand opportunity without repeating the low-value program problem that has plagued parts of postsecondary education for years. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-issues-proposed-rules-implement-working-families-tax-cuts-acts-workforce-pell-grants?utm_source=openai))

Attendance is still the hidden crisis in K-12

Chronic absenteeism remains one of the clearest signs that schools have not fully regained pre-pandemic stability. The Education Department says missing at least 10% of the school year can have serious academic consequences and notes that chronic absenteeism was about 31% nationally in 2021-22 and 28% in 2022-23. NCES and IES resources continue to urge educators to track absence patterns by grade, severity, and subgroup rather than relying only on broad averages. Those numbers are not abstract. They mean millions of students are missing enough school to disrupt reading growth, math growth, credit accumulation, and the social ties that keep students engaged. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Research from Brookings complicates the common assumption that attendance recovery is just a matter of pushing rates back toward a single target. One Brookings analysis found that attendance problems persist below the chronic-absence threshold and that many students continue to show partial disengagement even if they are no longer flagged as chronically absent. Another Brookings study found that parents often underestimate how much their children miss school. That matters because attendance improvement requires family trust and shared definitions of what counts as too much absence. If a student misses six or seven days in a semester, a school may see an early warning; a family may see something manageable. The mismatch can delay intervention. ([ies.ed.gov](https://ies.ed.gov/sites/default/files/migrated/rel/regions/appalachia/pdf/RELAP_3-3-1-6_03_Handout1-Understanding_Chronic_Absenteeism_approved_Acc-1.pdf?utm_source=openai))

What seems to work best is not punishment alone. IES guidance says educators should use evidence-based continuous improvement and targeted communication. Its texting toolkit says parent messaging can reduce chronic absence, with the study showing reductions in chronic absence rates and in the number of days missed. That is a more practical approach than a generic truancy letter. It recognizes that absenteeism often reflects transportation problems, health challenges, caregiving responsibilities, bullying, housing instability, or a weak sense of belonging. ([ies.ed.gov](https://ies.ed.gov/use-work/supporting-recovery-with-evidence-based-practices/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

The attendance problem is also a labor problem

Attendance recovery is harder when schools themselves are short-staffed. Teacher and principal shortages, substitute shortages, bus driver gaps, special education vacancies, and counselor overload all make attendance work less effective. State policy organizations continue to treat educator retention as central to school quality, and AP reporting in March 2026 described how rural districts have historically relied on international teachers to fill gaps. The report said immigration policy is now disrupting that pipeline in some places. That is a reminder that the staffing crisis is not confined to large urban systems or a single policy domain. It touches transportation, instruction, and the ability to maintain routines that make school a predictable place to be. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08080546339ac7aadefce42fc73e4281?utm_source=openai))

Recent AP coverage of San Francisco teachers striking over wages and benefits in February 2026 illustrated the same point from another angle. When schools cannot resolve staffing and compensation disputes, the result can be closures, lost instructional days, and more family frustration about the reliability of public education. In other words, the attendance crisis and the labor crisis feed each other. Poor staffing reduces the quality of school experience, which can worsen absenteeism; absenteeism then complicates staffing and funding.

There is evidence that targeted interventions can help. IES and NCES say text messaging and other communication strategies can improve attendance, especially when paired with supportive outreach rather than threats. Afterschool organizations also argue that well-designed out-of-school-time programs can reduce absenteeism by building relationships and making school feel more relevant. Those claims deserve scrutiny, especially because effects vary by age and program quality. But they point toward a broader conclusion: attendance improves when schools address the reasons students disengage, not just the symptoms that show up in the attendance office. ([ies.ed.gov](https://ies.ed.gov/use-work/supporting-recovery-with-evidence-based-practices/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

Funding is tighter, and the room for error is smaller

Education budgets are under strain at nearly every level. The expiration of federal pandemic relief removed a major cushion for districts that used the money for tutoring, staffing, and enrichment. NSBA’s education funding materials and state budget reporting show that districts are still contending with declining enrollment, inflation, and labor shortages even as they lose the temporary relief funds that helped bridge earlier gaps. That combination leaves schools with fewer ways to respond to chronic absenteeism, student mental health needs, and special education service gaps. ([edsource.org](https://edsource.org/2025/budget-education-funding-california/735265?utm_source=openai))

At the state level, budgets are still trying to preserve core school operations while also funding early literacy, tutoring, and mental health support. California’s 2025-26 budget debate, for example, showed lawmakers protecting K-12 funding while deferring some payments to later in the year. Illinois’ FY 2026 budget left little room for new school spending while keeping major K-12 formulas intact. New Jersey’s FY 2027 proposal, meanwhile, paired larger education spending with additional tutoring and preschool investments. Those are not isolated state stories. They are evidence that the fiscal pressure is national, even when the political choices differ. ([edsource.org](https://edsource.org/2025/budget-education-funding-california/735265?utm_source=openai))

For higher education, the strain is different but related. The GAO testimony says the department needs stronger accountability tools, while federal aid officials are revising the FAFSA processing system for the new Workforce Pell rules. Colleges are being asked to enroll more students, serve more diverse populations, and train more workers, all while facing uncertainty about revenue, regulation, and student demand. In short, the higher-ed finance system is trying to do more with less certainty. ([gao.gov](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-109069?utm_source=openai))

Complicating the main thesis: not every attendance problem is the same

A broad narrative about absenteeism can flatten important differences. The 74’s analysis of chronic absenteeism trends in 27 states found that attendance has improved since the pandemic but that statewide averages mask large gaps by race, income, and English-learner status. That is a crucial caveat. A district can celebrate an improving average and still leave behind the students who were already most vulnerable. In practice, that means a state headline can look stable while a neighborhood school is still dealing with families who are juggling work shifts, unstable housing, or health issues.

There is also a counterargument to the growing emphasis on absenteeism as a leading indicator of school health: schools should not overinterpret every missed day as proof of disengagement. Students miss school for real reasons, including illness, caregiving, transportation breakdowns, and mental health concerns. Heavy-handed truancy enforcement can backfire, especially in communities that already distrust institutions. That is one reason researchers and practitioners increasingly prefer supportive, data-driven strategies over punitive ones. The goal is not to police families. It is to identify barriers early enough that schools can help remove them. ([ies.ed.gov](https://ies.ed.gov/use-work/supporting-recovery-with-evidence-based-practices/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

The same caution applies to federal aid reform. Workforce Pell may be a win for some adults, but it will not help if low-value programs get a quality seal they have not earned. And FAFSA improvements are only meaningful if students can afford to attend and persist after the aid award arrives. The policy lesson across both K-12 and higher ed is the same: administrative reforms matter, but capacity and trust matter more. Systems can change forms and formulas quickly; rebuilding reliable attendance, staffing, and affordability takes longer. ([fsapartners.ed.gov](https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/electronic-announcements/2026-03-09/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-fafsa-processing-updates?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Over the next several months, the most important developments will not be singular headlines but whether the system can absorb the changes already underway. One question is whether the April 26 FAFSA processing update goes smoothly for institutions, especially those that serve large numbers of adult learners and workforce students. Another is whether colleges can explain the new Pell rules clearly enough that students understand which programs qualify and what their aid will cover. A third is whether states and districts can turn attendance work into a durable operational habit instead of a short-lived campaign. ([fsapartners.ed.gov](https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/electronic-announcements/2026-03-09/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-fafsa-processing-updates?utm_source=openai))

Watch for whether states publish more granular attendance data by grade, school, and student subgroup; whether more districts adopt family outreach, transportation fixes, and mentorship-based interventions; whether federal accountability efforts in higher education gain traction; and whether labor shortages in schools improve or worsen as districts head into summer hiring season. Also watch for signs that the current aid policy debate expands beyond access and cost to the harder issue of value: what kinds of programs should receive public support, and how can the public tell whether those programs are worth the investment? ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))

The education story of this spring is not just that schools and colleges are changing. It is that the institutions meant to make opportunity real are still trying to recover the basic capacity to do so. Attendance, funding, and aid administration may sound like separate bureaucratic categories. In practice, they are the infrastructure of learning. When any one of them falters, students feel it first. When all three are under stress at once, the public system itself is what comes into question. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism?utm_source=openai))
