The Hold List Is a Labor Story. Illinois Is Trying to Rewrite the Terms of Digital Reading.

A live bill in Springfield has turned library e-books back into a legislative fight. But the deeper culture story is not just price. It is the hidden work of buying, timing, replacing, comparing, and protecting digital books that libraries do not truly own, even as readers increasingly treat the app as a branch.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 10:09 a.m.

The most revealing library fight in Illinois this spring is not about a challenged title, a building renovation, or a gala speech about literacy. It is about contract terms. House Bill 5236, titled the Digital Library Protection Act, has pushed a familiar library grievance back into live legislative space: whether publishers and aggregators can dictate the terms on which libraries lend e-books and digital audiobooks, and whether those private terms can override basic public-library norms about lending, privacy, and cooperation.

That can sound narrow until you translate it into ordinary library life. Readers open Libby or another app, tap Place Hold, and see a wait estimate. The interface suggests a simple digital shelf. But behind that shelf sits a procurement system in which libraries often pay far above consumer prices for temporary access, buy multiple licenses to reduce wait times, monitor expirations, and work inside contracts that may limit what can be disclosed to peer institutions. A print copy wears out physically. A digital copy, in the library market, can expire by license.

Illinois lawmakers are now trying to draw a line around some of those terms. HB5236 was introduced on February 10, 2026, heard in the House Consumer Protection Committee on April 14, and as of April 24, 2026, was listed as held on the calendar order of second reading in the House. The bill’s introduced text would bar publishers from entering into contracts or license agreements for electronic literary materials that restrict a library’s customary operational functions, restrict customary lending functions, prevent disclosure of contract terms to another Illinois library, or require a library to violate the state’s Library Records Confidentiality Act. It would also make violations unlawful practices under Illinois consumer-fraud law and render prohibited provisions unenforceable and void. An amendment filed on April 13 revised definitions and expanded the bill’s framing to include aggregators as well as publishers. The legislation is real, active, and unusually direct about what libraries say has gone wrong in digital lending. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/BillStatus?DocNum=5236&DocTypeID=HB&GAID=18&LegId=0&SessionID=114&utm_source=openai))

What makes this a Culture story rather than a technical Statehouse brief is that the bill is really about the infrastructure of reading: who controls access once books move from owned objects to licensed files, what counts as normal borrowing in a digital environment, and how much invisible labor public institutions must perform to keep digital reading available at all. Libraries have always done back-office work. What has changed is that digital lending turns contract management itself into a central cultural task.

The copy became a license

In the print world, the library buys a copy, catalogs it, circulates it, repairs it, weeds it, and maybe keeps it for years. In the digital world, libraries usually do not acquire that same durable kind of ownership. The American Library Association’s Digital Public Library Ecosystem report lays out the structure bluntly: digital library service now runs through a complex web of publishers, distributors, platforms, apps, library systems, and policy constraints. The report was released in December 2023, but its diagnosis remains useful in 2026 because it explains why patrons experience one seamless transaction while staff manage a multilayered market underneath. It also notes that digital book borrowing rose 34 percent since 2019, increasing pressure on budgets and making transparency more important as libraries depend more heavily on licensed content. ([ala.org](https://www.ala.org/news/2019/12/new-ala-report-maps-increasingly-complex-digital-public-library-ecosystem?utm_source=openai))

That dependence is no longer marginal. OverDrive said in January 2026 that libraries and schools using its platforms recorded 820.5 million digital checkouts in 2025, up 10 percent year over year, and that more than 200 library systems, consortia, and schools surpassed one million digital checkouts. American Libraries highlighted the same milestone, treating it as evidence that digital borrowing has become routine institutional scale, not a pandemic-era anomaly. OverDrive is a company and its figures are promotional, so they should be read with that in mind. But even with that caveat, the totals matter because they show how central digital circulation now is to library service. ([company.overdrive.com](https://company.overdrive.com/2026/01/21/libraries-break-digital-lending-records-in-2025-with-over-820-million-checkouts-and-over-1-billion-minutes-streamed/?utm_source=openai))

Once digital borrowing becomes core service, licensing terms stop being niche industry details. They become cultural policy in practice. A patron sees a six-week wait for a popular e-book. A selector sees a choice between paying for more metered copies now, preserving money for other formats later, or accepting longer queues. A director sees a budget line increasingly shaped by private terms that the library did not meaningfully set. The hold list is not just a consumer inconvenience. It is a symptom of how public access is rationed in a licensed environment.

That rationing can look bizarre from the outside because digital files feel infinitely reproducible. Libraries spend much of their time explaining why technological abundance does not translate into legal abundance. GovTech summarized the contradiction well in 2023: libraries may pay roughly $55 for one popular e-book license lasting two years while a consumer might pay around $15 for continuing personal access to the same title, because print copies are governed by copyright’s first-sale logic while digital books are licensed on publisher terms. The precise prices vary by title and model, but the structural point remains accurate. ([govtech.com](https://www.govtech.com/biz/data/behind-e-books-libraries-find-restrictions-and-high-costs?utm_source=openai))

Illinois is targeting power, secrecy, and privacy more than price alone

The Illinois bill is notable because it does not simply try to impose a universal price ceiling or require a particular lending model. Instead, it goes after four pressure points libraries have complained about for years: interference with normal operations, interference with lending, nondisclosure of contract terms among Illinois libraries, and contractual provisions that would force libraries into conflict with reader-privacy law. That makes the bill less of a crude price-control proposal and more of an attempt to establish nonwaivable public boundaries around library service. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/documents/legislation/104/HB/PDF/10400HB5236.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The anti-secrecy provision may be the most quietly consequential. Library markets are shaped not just by what publishers charge, but by what individual systems know. If libraries cannot compare terms with one another, richer systems may absorb unfavorable deals while smaller systems remain in the dark about whether they are paying unusually high rates or accepting unusually restrictive language. HB5236 would bar contracts that prevent an Illinois library from disclosing terms to another Illinois library, an unusually explicit attempt to weaken information asymmetry inside a public-service market. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/documents/legislation/104/HB/PDF/10400HB5236.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That matters even more in a state that already treats libraries as a coordinated public system rather than a scatter of isolated branches. Illinois library law emphasizes resource sharing through the state’s library system structure, and a recent public act on nonresident cards again tied access questions back to statewide administration and confidentiality rules. The legal backdrop does not predetermine the outcome of HB5236, but it does show that Illinois already has a statutory habit of thinking about libraries as interconnected infrastructure. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/publicacts/view/103-0267?utm_source=openai))

Support filings in the legislative record also suggest the fight extends beyond Chicago. Witness slips on the bill include support from the Illinois Heartland Library System, indicating that the issue reaches consortia and regional systems whose bargaining position may be weaker and whose dependence on cooperation may be greater. For those libraries, restrictions on sharing terms can translate directly into diminished leverage. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/BillStatus?DocNum=5236&DocTypeID=HB&GAID=18&LegId=0&SessionID=114&utm_source=openai))

The queue is where the economics become visible

One reason the digital-lending fight stays obscure is that its consequences are usually experienced as patron-policy tweaks rather than headline events. The Seattle Public Library offered one of the clearest public explanations of this reality when it announced in March 2024 that it would reduce the maximum number of digital holds a patron could place, from 25 to 10, to manage spending amid surging demand. Seattle said annual digital checkouts had almost tripled from 2015 to 2022, as had costs, and that libraries often pay up to three times or more than print prices for digital books. It also said 62 percent of its 2023 digital-book spending went to additional copies purchased to fulfill holds on popular titles. That is the kind of plain-language institutional accounting libraries do not always surface, and it makes clear that holds are not just a service feature; they are cost drivers. ([blog.spl.org](https://blog.spl.org/2024/03/13/the-seattle-public-library-is-reducing-our-maximum-digital-holds-heres-why/?utm_source=openai))

Chicago Public Library’s public-facing digital rules are less rhetorical but point in the same direction. Its current FAQ says cardholders in good standing who live in Chicago can borrow e-books, audiobooks, and magazines; it lists platform-specific limits that include 10 OverDrive checkouts, 5 OverDrive holds, and 5 Hoopla checkouts per month; and it notes that some publishers do not offer e-books to libraries, do not offer new titles promptly, or delay e-book availability. Those statements tell readers, in a stripped-down way, that the digital collection is governed by constraints external to the library’s own desires. ([chipublib.org](https://www.chipublib.org/faq/ebooks/?utm_source=openai))

Seen from a patron account, those limits may feel arbitrary. Seen from an acquisitions desk, they are management tools. Digital borrowing policies translate budgets into queue behavior. A library can widen access in one place only by absorbing cost, delaying access elsewhere, or reducing variety. Popular demand does not simply reveal what readers want; it forces a public institution to decide how much of its collection strategy will be devoted to buying down waits for bestsellers versus sustaining depth, format diversity, multilingual collections, children’s materials, and local priorities.

That tradeoff belongs squarely on the culture desk because libraries are not only distributors of civically approved classics. Their digital platforms carry romance, thrillers, celebrity memoir, comics, language learning, practical nonfiction, children’s series, and commuter audiobooks. They are public infrastructure for ordinary taste. When the queue gets longer or a title never appears, the effect is not abstract market friction. It changes what kinds of reading remain easily public.

The labor of digital collections is the real picture

This is why the right visual for the story is not a glowing tablet in a generic hand. The labor is elsewhere. Someone in a library office studies hold ratios, waits, price models, and expiration clocks. Someone decides whether a heavily used digital audiobook should be repurchased on a new term or allowed to lapse. Someone explains to a patron why a file that never physically degrades can still disappear from the catalog. Someone tracks which platform has which title, whether accessibility features differ, whether patron data handling raises concerns, and whether a confidentiality clause limits what peers can compare.

The ALA’s broader ecosystem work and Public Library Association data reinforce this hidden-work picture. The PLA’s technology-survey summary released in 2024 emphasizes that public libraries’ digital roles are not just about equipment; they involve staffing, training, technology capacity, and ongoing support obligations. In other words, digital service is labor-intensive even before content licensing enters the frame. Once you add procurement and privacy obligations, the frictionless app experience depends on a lot of nonfrictionless institutional work. ([ala.org](https://www.ala.org/news/2024/07/new-public-library-technology-survey-report-details-digital-equity-roles?utm_source=openai))

That labor is not just clerical. It is also interpretive. Staff must decide what counts as a good use of public money in a market that keeps mutating. Library Journal’s sponsored reporting on perpetual-access models, while aimed more at academic contexts, captures a broader institutional desire for predictable long-term availability rather than endlessly fluctuating subscriptions. Public libraries face their own version of that desire: if a title is important, heavily used, or central to a local collection strategy, repeated temporary payments can feel less like circulation management than like permanent instability. ([libraryjournal.com](https://www.libraryjournal.com/story/security-cost-efficiency-and-long-term-planning-the-varied-benefits-of-perpetual-access-subscriptions?utm_source=openai))

Privacy adds another layer. Illinois’s bill specifically references the Library Records Confidentiality Act, which protects records identifying library users with specific materials. In a print setting, privacy practices may feel settled. In a platform-heavy digital environment, they require active management across vendors, authentication systems, and app ecosystems. The bill’s privacy clause is therefore not decorative. It acknowledges that digital lending is also a data-governance question. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/ILCS/Articles?ActID=1004&ChapterID=16&Print=True&utm_source=openai))

The opposition case is more serious than a caricature suggests

It would be easy to cast this as libraries versus greedy publishers, but that is not the strongest or fairest account. Authors, publishers, and some trade groups argue that state e-book legislation can distort a copyright market that supports not just corporate margins but author income, editorial work, and investment in new books. Their concern is that forced or constrained licensing terms could reduce earnings, especially for digital titles that might otherwise sell directly to readers, and could lead some rightsholders to narrow what they offer to libraries.

The Authors Guild’s 2025 annual report, published in March 2026, includes ebook licensing bills among its policy concerns and argues that mandatory licensing measures can harm author interests even when framed as pro-library. The guild also operates Library ATM, a tool that helps authors see where their books are available in libraries, which underscores a different truth: libraries are both a public good and a meaningful discovery and revenue channel for writers. That dual role is why the politics get messy. Libraries matter to authors, but so do the terms under which digital use is monetized. ([authorsguild.org](https://authorsguild.org/app/uploads/2026/03/2025-Authors-Guild-Annual-Report.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The legal climate also favors caution. The Second Circuit’s 2024 opinion in the Internet Archive case did not address ordinary licensed library lending, but it did affirm a skeptical judicial view of unlicensed digital copying and highlighted the existence of licensed e-book markets. That does not settle what Illinois may regulate in contracts, yet it strengthens the background argument that digital lending is not simply a frictionless extension of print-era ownership rules. Publishers have court language they can cite when they say digital access depends on negotiated markets, not automatic public entitlement. ([publishersweekly.com](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/libraries/article/88403-illinois-rhode-island-introduce-new-library-e-book-bills.html?utm_source=openai))

History also suggests that states entering this arena can trigger difficult fights. Publishers Weekly’s coverage of earlier state e-book bills in Illinois, Rhode Island, Tennessee, and Missouri shows that similar measures have circulated for years and have repeatedly raised questions about copyright preemption, market interference, and whether legislators are trying to legislate what is essentially a licensing business. The Illinois bill now on the table is not identical to those earlier efforts, but it arrives inside a long-running national dispute rather than on clean ground. ([publishersweekly.com](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/libraries/article/88403-illinois-rhode-island-introduce-new-library-e-book-bills.html?utm_source=openai))

All of which is to say: the counterargument is not frivolous. Books are copyrighted works, not water pipes. But the seriousness of the publisher-side case is exactly why the Illinois bill’s structure matters. It is not demanding free books. It is trying to say that some contractual terms are incompatible with the public character of a library, even in a licensed market.

Public access increasingly depends on private architecture

Another reason the story belongs on the culture desk is that digital reading now depends heavily on private platform architecture. OverDrive remains the dominant name in public-library e-lending, but it is not alone. OCLC’s CloudLibrary continues to expand, and OCLC said in January 2026 that the service offers millions of e-books and audiobooks plus magazines, newspapers, comics, and streaming video. OCLC also emphasizes features such as CloudLink, which lets libraries share content across participating institutions to broaden collections and reduce holds. That detail is revealing: vendors now market not just access to titles but systems for managing scarcity, discoverability, and interlibrary cooperation inside digital environments. ([oclc.org](https://www.oclc.org/en/news/releases/2026/20260115-cloudlibrary-gold-2026-mla.html?utm_source=openai))

The point is not that vendors are parasitic or unnecessary. They supply authentication, interfaces, content feeds, accessibility features, and administrative tools that libraries rely on. But as their platforms become more central, the basic civic act of borrowing a book increasingly runs through a commercial stack that libraries do not fully control. A patron may think they are interacting with the library. In one sense they are. In another, they are moving through a negotiated service environment shaped by publishers, aggregators, platform design, and vendor contracts.

The ALA’s ecosystem report made this visible in 2023, and it is even truer now. The report describes a marketplace in which competition and innovation can improve access, but also one in which different actors often poorly understand one another’s constraints. That mismatch helps explain why the public debate stays thin. Readers think scarcity is fake. Publishers think libraries understate substitution risk. Libraries think publishers and vendors import retail logic into public service. Everyone is partly describing a different layer of the same system. ([ala.org](https://www.ala.org/news/2019/12/new-ala-report-maps-increasingly-complex-digital-public-library-ecosystem?utm_source=openai))

The equity case is not incidental

When digital-lending debates are reduced to procurement disputes, they lose their social meaning. Public libraries do not merely provide a nice alternative to bookstore purchases. They offset inequality in who can afford contemporary reading, audiobook subscriptions, device ecosystems, and the endless small frictions of platform culture. Chicago Public Library’s annual-report materials and broader service presentation frame the institution around access across neighborhoods, ages, and needs. Digital borrowing is not a side perk in that framework. It is part of how the library remains legible as a citywide service rather than a building people visit occasionally. ([chipublib.org](https://www.chipublib.org/?utm_source=openai))

Large systems outside Illinois show similar pressures. The New York City Council’s budget materials for fiscal 2027, responding to the fiscal 2026 preliminary mayor’s management report, describe persistent capital and operating pressures across the city’s three library systems. The New York Public Library’s 2025 annual report presents the institution as both branch service and digital public platform. Those materials are not about Illinois and not specifically about e-book law, but they do show that major urban library systems are being asked to expand access and stay technologically current amid continuing fiscal strain. That is the environment in which digital licensing costs land. ([council.nyc.gov](https://council.nyc.gov/budget/wp-content/uploads/sites/54/2026/03/Libraries.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Children’s reading programs offer a useful comparison. Dolly Parton’s Imagination Library is not a public-library e-book system, but its state-by-state expansion shows how much logistical and financial effort now goes into maintaining broad access to books. The program’s Illinois page says that, as of April 2026, it had expanded to 93 of 102 counties in the state with state-partner support. Oregon’s materials describe statewide coverage and a sharp acceleration after state backing. These are different mechanisms from library licensing, but they illuminate the same cultural reality: universal reading access increasingly depends on braided public, philanthropic, and administrative systems rather than on a simple, self-sustaining book market. ([imaginationlibrary.com](https://imaginationlibrary.com/illinois/?utm_source=openai))

That patchwork makes the library app more important, not less. For households that cannot buy every bestseller, pay for every subscription, or rebuild around each new device ecosystem, public-library digital access is often the practical way to stay connected to mainstream reading culture. A longer hold queue therefore has an equity meaning. It is time added to the public route.

Why secrecy matters so much in this market

There is a temptation to focus on the most vivid licensing oddities: the expiring copy, the high price, the vanishing title. But the bill’s ban on nondisclosure terms may be the place where Illinois is most clearly trying to restructure power. Markets work differently when public buyers can compare notes. If one Illinois library learns that another paid different rates, received different conditions, or negotiated more favorable renewal terms, that knowledge does not instantly change the market. But it does erode the isolation on which opaque contracting thrives.

Libraries are peculiarly vulnerable to this problem because they are public institutions buying into markets with private contracts while serving patrons who often assume access should be uniform. Two neighboring systems can look similar to readers and still face very different licensing realities. Confidentiality preserves that fragmentation. HB5236’s anti-secrecy rule therefore addresses not just transparency as a moral good, but transparency as bargaining infrastructure.

That approach also fits the broad trend in library advocacy away from describing digital-lending complaints as one-off annoyances and toward describing them as ecosystem failures. The ALA’s recent ecosystem initiative and field guide use that exact vocabulary, stressing that library service depends on interconnected actors and that library workers need tools for understanding structural relationships, not just local symptoms. While those materials are not about this specific bill, they help explain why legislators are now seeing digital terms as governance issues rather than vendor minutiae. ([ala.org](https://www.ala.org/advocacy/ala-ecosystem-initiative?utm_source=openai))

What happens if Illinois succeeds, and what happens if it does not

HB5236 may advance, stall, be narrowed, or provoke legal challenge. As of Friday, April 24, 2026, the reliable thing to say is that it remains live and has already forced the issue into plain legislative language. That alone matters. Culture-policy fights often become visible only when lawmakers decide to name the underlying mechanics. Here, the named mechanics are lending functions, operations, confidentiality, and privacy. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/BillStatus?DocNum=5236&DocTypeID=HB&GAID=18&LegId=0&SessionID=114&utm_source=openai))

If the bill passes, Illinois could become a test case for whether states can invalidate especially restrictive digital-library contract terms without pushing rightsholders to retreat, sue, or reorganize their offerings. If the bill fails, it may still have value as a forcing device. It has already made it harder to pretend that digital-library friction is just a matter of apps not keeping up with modern expectations. The friction is contractual, institutional, and political.

And if the bill is rewritten, that too will be revealing. Amendment 1 already broadened definitions and refined the bill’s structure. The live legislative process suggests lawmakers are trying to specify the actors and materials at issue rather than leave the law at a level of abstraction that would be easy to dismiss. Whether that makes the measure sturdier or more litigable remains to be seen. ([ilga.gov](https://www.ilga.gov/documents/legislation/104/HB/PDF/10400HB5236ham001.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The broader cultural question will remain either way. Public libraries are increasingly expected to behave like modern digital platforms while lacking platform power. Readers want instant access, synced devices, predictive recommendation, and broad selection. Libraries are asked to provide that under public budgets, privacy obligations, accessibility commitments, and licensing terms they do not set. That mismatch is not temporary. It is becoming one of the defining administrative facts of public reading.

So the queue deserves to be read differently. It is not just a line. It is evidence of a bargain: the public gets digital access, but only through recurring negotiation with private sellers whose incentives are not the same as a library’s. Each hold, renewal, and expired license records that bargain in miniature.

That is why the right image for this story is a staffer at a desk, not a reader with a glowing screen. The drama is in the work: the spreadsheet, the annotations, the vendor dashboard, the privacy caution, the budget cap, the decision to buy one more copy or not. Illinois has not solved the digital-library problem. What it has done is make the labor behind the hold list easier to see.

And that may be the most important cultural move of all. Once the app stops looking frictionless, the public can finally ask the sharper question: when books become licenses, what exactly is a library being allowed to do?
