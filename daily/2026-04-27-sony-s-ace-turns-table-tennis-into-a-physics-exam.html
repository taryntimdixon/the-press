Sony’s Ace Turns Table Tennis Into a Physics Exam

In Tokyo, Sony AI’s paddle-wielding robot has beaten elite players in ITTF-style matches and, Sony says, a later professional opponent. The science story is not a robot uprising. It is a careful, weirdly athletic experiment in speed, sensors, uncertainty and what it takes to make artificial intelligence move in the real world.

By The Press • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

The ball is only 40 millimeters wide, light enough to seem unserious and fast enough to embarrass almost anything with gears.

In elite table tennis, it arrives with a private weather system: topspin that yanks it down, backspin that makes it float and die, sidespin that kicks sideways after the bounce. A human player reads all of this in a blink, from wrist angle, sound, arc and years of memory stored in knees, shoulders and fingertips.

Sony AI’s Ace does not have knees. It does not squint across the table or feel the little jolt that tells a player the rubber has brushed the ball just right. Its eyes are distributed around the court. Its body is an eight-degree-of-freedom robotic arm on a movable base. Its judgment comes from software trained in simulation, then forced to survive the rude physics of a real rally.

That is why the new paper in Nature, published April 22, is more interesting than the easiest version of the headline. Ace has not solved robotics. It has not made human athletes obsolete. It did not beat the best table-tennis players on Earth. In the peer-reviewed evaluation, based on April 2025 matches, it won three of five matches against elite players, defined by the authors as athletes with more than 10 years of intensive training, and it played competitively in the rest. It lost the two matches it played against professional players in that evaluation, winning one game out of seven. Sony says later work produced further improvement, including a professional-level win, but the cleanest scientific claim is narrower: a real robot has now crossed from clever rally machine into credible, rule-following opponent for expert humans.

That is still a big deal. For decades, robots have been brilliant at repeating fixed motions in factories and awkward at dealing with the live mess of the world. Table tennis is the live mess compressed into a few meters: a tiny object, high speed, spin, an adversary and almost no time to recover from being wrong. If a robot can play that game well, the result matters less because the world needs a champion ping-pong machine than because the same ingredients — fast sensing, safe motion near people, quick adaptation, better simulation — are needed in factories, clinics, warehouses, disaster sites and assistive devices.

But the point is not that a sport has been conquered. The point is that a lab in Tokyo has turned a white plastic ball into a measuring instrument for physical artificial intelligence.

What Ace actually did

The peer-reviewed experiment was staged in Sony’s Tokyo table-tennis setup, with licensed umpires and human players across the net. The authors say the matches followed International Table Tennis Federation rules with one important exception: they used a Japanese T.League-style golden-point rule, under which the first player to 11 points wins the game rather than needing a two-point lead after 10-10. That detail does not erase the achievement, but it keeps the claim properly sized.

The setup used regulation equipment where it mattered for the game: an approved table, net, flooring, rackets and official competition balls. The elite players played best-of-three matches. The two professionals, Minami Ando and Kakeru Sone, both active in Japan’s T.League, played best-of-five matches. All players faced Ace for the first time, and the paper says no player-specific data were used to prepare the robot before the evaluation.

The result was not a clean sweep. That matters. Ace’s three wins over five elite players show a threshold crossed, not a final ranking. Against the two professionals in the paper’s core evaluation, Ace lost. One way to read the study is that the robot has reached expert competition; another is that humans at the next level quickly reveal weaknesses.

The weaknesses are scientifically useful. The Guardian reported that elite player Rui Takenaka found Ace difficult when he used complex spin, because the robot could answer with complex spin. But simpler, low-spin serves could draw weaker returns and open the third shot for attack. That is a lovely detail because it punctures the comic-book version of AI progress. The robot was not merely vulnerable to the impossible. It could also be bothered by the plain.

Ace’s strength, by contrast, showed up when the ball behaved in ways that humans find nasty. The paper highlights a ball that clipped the net and changed trajectory; Ace adjusted fast enough to return it. The authors also describe a rapid backspin shot that surprised former Olympic table-tennis player Kinjiro Nakamura. The fun part is that this did not just make the machine look clever. It suggested a loop in the other direction: machines may sometimes invent or rediscover physical tactics that humans can study.

That possibility is easy to overstate. A machine doing one strange shot on a custom platform does not rewrite a sport. But it does make a modest, charming claim: when an artificial player is built differently from a human, it may explore a slightly different corner of the same physical landscape.

Not a brain in a box, a whole machine

The word artificial intelligence can make Ace sound as if software is the whole story. It is not. Ace is a system, and the system is the story.

First comes perception, which in robotics means turning the world into usable measurements. Ace uses nine conventional active-pixel cameras to locate the ball in three-dimensional space. In the Nature paper, the authors report ball tracking at 200 hertz, meaning 200 updates per second, with about 3 millimeters of error and an average latency of 10.2 milliseconds. A millisecond is one thousandth of a second. In ordinary life, 10 milliseconds is nothing. In table tennis, it is precious real estate.

Then comes spin. A table-tennis ball’s path is not governed by position alone. A spinning ball drags air differently and rebounds differently. Ace estimates spin partly by watching the logo printed on the official ball. To do that quickly, it uses event-based vision sensors, a kind of camera that does not record full frames like a movie camera. Instead, it reports changes in brightness at individual pixels. That makes it useful for fast motion because it can notice what changed without waiting for an entire picture.

Sony’s project materials describe 12 high-speed sensors in all: nine frame-based cameras and three event-based vision systems. The paper identifies the event cameras as Sony IMX636 sensors mounted inside gaze-control systems with pan-and-tilt mirrors and a tunable telephoto lens. Sony Semiconductor’s public description of event-based vision explains the basic idea: the sensor asynchronously detects luminance changes and outputs only the changing data, with coordinate and time information. In plain English: Ace watches the ball with a crowd of specialized eyes, then tries to infer not just where the ball is, but what it is about to do.

The next layer is control: deciding how to move the robot so the paddle meets the ball at the right place, time and angle. Ace’s rally policy was trained with deep reinforcement learning. Reinforcement learning is a method in which software learns by trying actions and receiving rewards or penalties. If a policy returns the ball well, it gets a better score in training. If it misses, hits the net or violates a constraint, it gets a worse one. Deep means the policy uses neural networks, mathematical systems inspired loosely by brains but better understood here as flexible pattern-fitting tools.

The important trick is that Ace did much of its learning in simulation. That is safer and faster than letting a physical robot flail for thousands of hours. But simulation creates its own trap. A simulated table-tennis ball is a mathematical guess. The real ball has seams, logos, air drag, rubber contact, tiny vibrations and all the small insults of matter. A policy that wins in a virtual world can fail instantly when asked to move steel and plastic in a room.

Sony’s team leaned into that problem. The paper describes physics models, noise models, simulated ball states and a learning setup in which a training component could see cleaner information than the final policy would get in a real match. Sony calls this idea a privileged critic. Think of it as a coach who sees the whole replay during practice but trains the player to compete later with only ordinary senses.

That division matters because it keeps the final system honest. During play, Ace must act from measured, imperfect signals. It cannot pause the match and ask the simulator where the ball truly is.

Why table tennis keeps seducing roboticists

Robot ping-pong has been a research target since at least the early 1980s. It sounds whimsical, but it is a brutal benchmark.

A good benchmark is a task that is simple to describe and hard to fake. Table tennis fits. The court is bounded. The rules are public. The scoring is obvious. A point either happened or did not. Yet the underlying task pulls together almost every headache in robotics: sensing, prediction, high-speed control, contact physics, safety and opponent modeling.

Earlier systems made real progress. Academic groups built table-tennis robots that could rally, aim and learn strokes. Google DeepMind reported a learned robot agent in 2024 that reached amateur human-level play and won 13 of 29 matches against people of varying skill. Other papers studied model-based control, muscular robots, ball tracking and simulation-to-real transfer. Those efforts are the road Ace drove on.

Sony’s claim is different because of the level of opponent and the completeness of the setup. The company did not merely return cooperative balls from a launcher. Ace served, received, rallied and scored against trained human players under close-to-competition conditions. It also handled the annoying parts of a match, including spin and net deflections, not just tidy laboratory trajectories.

Still, Ace enjoys nonhuman advantages. It has cameras around the playing area rather than two eyes in a face. It does not need to balance on legs. It does not get nervous at 10-10. It does not have body language to read, which may itself be an advantage over human opponents who are used to studying a rival’s shoulders and gaze.

That makes the achievement both stronger and weaker than a casual viewer might think. Stronger, because the machine really did close a hard physical loop fast enough to compete. Weaker, because the setup is a custom laboratory organism, not a general humanoid walking into a club with a paddle.

The robot won because engineers did unglamorous things

The most newspaper-friendly part of Ace is the rally footage: ball, blur, paddle, point. The more important part is the workbench.

Someone had to place cameras so the ball could be seen without flooding the system with useless data. Someone had to design a gaze-control system — a mirror-and-lens setup for tracking the ball closely enough to estimate spin. Someone had to decide how quickly commands should reach the arm, how to avoid collision with the table, how to reset the robot between strokes and how to keep a human player safe across the net.

The Nature paper describes a system that chooses among different learned policies during rallies. It maps policy outputs to short joint trajectories, checks for collision risks and executes reset trajectories when necessary. That is not the glamorous language of intelligence. It is the language of engineering: clocks, constraints, latency budgets, calibration, repeatability.

Sony’s own blog is unusually explicit about the iterative nature of the work. The team says Ace began with modest tasks, such as juggling the ball and cooperative rallies, before moving toward stronger opponents. The company also says the project uncovered errors in its own physics models, including a case in which the model overestimated aerodynamic drag on very fast shots. That is a crucial scientific point. The robot did not merely apply a finished theory. Its failures exposed where the theory was not good enough.

This is where the story becomes local-to-global. In a Tokyo room, a ball flew farther than the simulation expected. That sounds tiny. But the same gap between model and world haunts autonomous driving, warehouse robotics, surgical robotics and drones. If the model is just a little wrong at high speed, the machine can do the wrong thing with great confidence.

Ace’s lesson is not that reinforcement learning magically escaped that gap. It is that the gap can be narrowed by measuring better, modeling better, building more predictable hardware and treating losses as data.

What this does not prove

There are several reasons to keep the champagne cork halfway in the bottle.

First, the sample is small. Seven players in the core paper is a serious demonstration, not a world ranking system. Different professionals, especially world-class players with time to study Ace, might exploit weaknesses quickly. The robot’s record could change with opponent style, serve variety, pressure, lighting, camera placement or the way warm-up is handled.

Second, the lab setup is specialized. Ace is not perceiving the world like a household robot. It depends on an engineered environment with multiple external sensors. That is fair for a research benchmark, but it limits the immediate conclusion. A robot that plays brilliantly when the room is instrumented may not be close to a robot that can walk into any gym, identify a table, ask for a paddle and join a match.

Third, table tennis is not manipulation in the ordinary sense. Jan Peters, a robotics researcher at the Technical University of Darmstadt who has worked on table-tennis robots, told The Guardian the project was impressive but would not solve major robotics challenges such as manipulating objects. That distinction matters. Returning a ball requires extreme speed, but the contact is brief and the object is simple. Folding laundry, washing dishes, helping a person out of bed or sorting irregular parts requires slower but deeper forms of dexterity.

Fourth, safety is not solved by being good at a sport. Sony says the project used a safety process, including early protective gear and later validation. That is reassuring, but fast human-robot interaction remains a serious engineering and standards problem. ISO’s collaborative-robot technical specification and NIST’s work on collaborative robotics both emphasize context, risk assessment, force, speed, test methods and the work environment. A table-tennis robot across a net is one controlled version of coexistence. A robot sharing a crowded factory aisle is another.

Fifth, this is corporate research. That does not make it wrong. Sony released a paper, project materials, videos, a dataset of post-event ball states and pseudocode for key parts of the training process. Those are useful transparency steps. But the paper also notes patent applications covering aspects of the hardware and gaze-control system. Independent replication would strengthen the claim, especially if another lab could reproduce similar performance with different hardware or publish a fuller accounting of later professional matches.

Why it still matters

If the caveats are real, why care?

Because many of the hardest practical robots will need to act at human speed around human unpredictability. Manufacturing robots are already fast when fenced off and repetitive. The future promise is different: machines that can adjust when parts arrive crooked, when a person reaches into the workspace, when a tool slips, when a patient moves unexpectedly, when a warehouse item is crushed, shiny, dangling or half-hidden.

Ace does not give us that future. It gives researchers a sharp example of how to approach one slice of it.

The system suggests that high-speed robotics may advance less through one grand algorithm than through integration. Better sensors alone are not enough. Better reinforcement learning alone is not enough. Better motors alone are not enough. The achievement comes when perception, control, mechanics and safety are designed together.

That has an old-fashioned moral. In a season when artificial intelligence is often discussed as if it lives in clouds, chat windows and data centers, Ace is a reminder that intelligence in the physical world has screws. It has cables. It has vibrations. It has a paddle face that must meet a ball at a place smaller than a cookie and a time window shorter than a sneeze.

It also has people. The project depended on athletes willing to play the machine, engineers willing to chase tiny errors and researchers willing to call losses useful. Sony’s public materials name professional players including Minami Ando, Kakeru Sone and Mayuka Taira, along with elite players, coaches, umpires and a large technical team. That matters because physical AI is not only software confronting the world. It is a social arrangement: who trains the system, who stands near it, who benefits from it and who decides it is safe enough.

What would change the conclusion

The conclusion today is cautious: Ace is a milestone in high-speed physical AI, not a general robot and not the end of human advantage in sport.

Several things could make that conclusion stronger. Independent teams could reproduce expert-level table-tennis play using the released data and their own platforms. Sony or others could publish larger match datasets against a wider range of professional players, including players given time to study and train against the robot. Future systems could work with fewer room-mounted sensors, more humanlike sensing or more portable hardware. Safety studies could show how high-speed learned motion behaves near people across many plausible failure cases.

Several things could weaken the conclusion. If specialists find that Ace’s wins depend heavily on a narrow group of opponents or on features of the lab setup, the result would look more like a brilliant demonstration than a broad robotics threshold. If later professional claims are not documented with the same clarity as the Nature evaluation, they should remain secondary. If the system proves brittle to simple tactical changes, low-spin serves or unfamiliar equipment, then the scientific lesson shifts from expert play to the boundaries of simulation-trained control.

The most likely future is mixed, which is how real science usually behaves. Ace will inspire better systems. Some will fail in funny ways. Some will move from sport to training tools, rehabilitation devices or industrial sensing. Some techniques may matter more than the robot itself. A sensor trick invented for spin might help inspect fast-moving parts. A control method tuned for a paddle might help a robot catch, place or avoid objects. A safety procedure developed for athletes may inform other high-speed human-machine settings.

For now, the cleanest image is not a robot crushing humanity. It is a lab bench beside a table, a paddle being tightened, a ball placed in a palm, a camera calibrated to see a logo spinning faster than a person can follow. Then the serve rises, the rally begins, and the question becomes wonderfully concrete.

Can the machine get there in time?
