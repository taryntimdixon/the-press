A Toothy Keychain Walks Onto the Studio Lot

Labubu’s Sony project is not just a cute-toy headline. It is a live experiment in how a blind-box collectible becomes fashion, platform commerce, investor risk, counterfeit bait and, maybe, a character sturdy enough to survive the jump from handbag to screen.

By The Press • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most useful way to look at Labubu is not as a doll. That makes the story too small.

Start with the object itself: a furry little creature with pointed ears, a grin full of small teeth and the air of something that escaped from an illustrated forest and learned modern retail on the way out. It hangs from handbags, backpacks and car mirrors. It appears in unboxing videos, resale listings, counterfeit warnings, earnings notes, customs seizures and now movie-development stories. It is cute, but not quite cuddly. It is mass-produced, but often sold with the suspense of a trick. It is small enough to fit in a palm, yet large enough to move a public company’s share price.

In March 2026, Pop Mart and Sony Pictures made the next stage official: a Labubu feature film, described in trade coverage as an early-development live-action and computer-generated project, with Paul King attached to direct and produce and Steven Levenson joining him on the script. That is not the same as a release date. Development announcements can become blockbusters, change creative teams, linger for years or vanish into studio files. But the announcement still matters because it clarifies what the Labubu business is trying to become: not merely a toy run, but a character system that can move from shelf to screen.

Less than a week later, Pop Mart reported 2025 revenue of 37.12 billion yuan, up 184.7 percent from 2024. Profit attributable to the company’s owners rose to 12.78 billion yuan. Those are not old-fashioned toy-aisle numbers. They are numbers from a company trying to prove that sealed boxes, character universes, mall stores, vending machines, mobile apps and social commerce can behave like one global pop-culture engine.

Yet the earnings day also showed the tension inside the craze. Pop Mart’s Hong Kong-listed shares fell by more than 20 percent after the results, with market reports and analysts pointing to concern that the company had become too dependent on The Monsters, the Pop Mart family that includes Labubu. That is the odd burden of a hit in 2026: success creates proof, then immediately creates suspicion. If one creature works this well, investors ask whether it can last. If it lasts, they ask whether anything else can match it.

That is the pop-culture story. The question is not simply why people like a funny-looking plush. The better question is how a small character becomes a circuit connecting TikTok, malls, adult collectors, Chinese consumer brands, Hollywood studios, safety regulators, counterfeit workshops, platform storefronts and public markets. Labubu is a keychain. It is also a stress test for the modern culture business.

The surprise is the product

A blind box is a package that hides the exact item inside until after purchase. The buyer may know the series, the character family and the possible designs. The buyer does not know which specific figure is in that box. A series may also include a rare hidden variant, often called a chase figure. That makes the purchase part toy, part suspense ritual, part social event and part probability exercise.

Blind boxes are not new. Japan has had capsule-toy machines for decades. Trading cards have long sold suspense in sealed wrappers. Fast-food toys, cereal prizes and mystery bags trained earlier generations to enjoy the little jolt of not knowing. Pop Mart’s achievement was to make that suspense feel less like a children’s promotion and more like a design economy for adults. The box is not just packaging. It is the countdown before the reveal.

That matters because the reveal is now shareable. A person can buy one box, film the opening, post the result, trade duplicates, complain about odds, compare authenticity markers and return for another try. The suspense that once ended at the cash register now continues on platforms. The object is small, but the ceremony around it is large.

Pop Mart’s own 2025 results show how deliberately that ceremony has been built. In China, the company sells through retail stores, online channels, roboshops and Pop Draw, a digital draw channel. Overseas, its mix includes stores, official websites, apps, e-commerce platforms, TikTok and other channels. In the Americas, Pop Mart said 2025 revenue rose 748.4 percent to 6.81 billion yuan. Online channels made up 64 percent of the region’s revenue, and TikTok alone accounted for just over 1.04 billion yuan.

That is a remarkable detail. TikTok is not only where a trend is noticed. For Pop Mart’s American business, it is also a storefront. The scroll, the drop, the checkout and the unboxing can collapse into one loop. Culture becomes commerce without asking the audience to leave the mood.

That is why Labubu should not be treated as a silly exception. It is a visible example of a broader retail pattern: products designed for display, suspense, social proof and repeat purchase. The buyer is not only acquiring a thing. The buyer is acquiring a moment: the unopened box, the possible rare pull, the post, the reaction, the shelf, the bag charm, the proof of having been in the stream while it was still moving.

A monster with a backstory, but not too much backstory

Labubu did not arrive fully formed from an algorithm. The character was created by Hong Kong-born artist and illustrator Kasing Lung and appeared in picture books in 2015, in a world associated with The Monsters and Nordic-inspired mythology. Lung struck a deal with Pop Mart in 2019 to sell Labubu figurines. Pop Mart began selling Labubu plush toys on key rings in 2023, and that format later helped turn the creature into a fashion-adjacent object rather than only a shelf collectible.

That history matters because Labubu’s appeal depends on a useful imbalance. It has enough backstory to feel authored, but not so much story that the audience is trapped inside a fixed plot. It can be a monster, a fashion charm, a desk companion, a gift, a collectible, a joke, a status object or a small mascot for a mood the owner cannot quite name.

Many dominant entertainment characters travel from story to merchandise: film to toy, comic to lunchbox, television show to plush. Labubu’s route is more complicated. It became famous to many global consumers as an object before they knew much about its fictional universe. That makes the Sony project interesting. The film is not simply adapting a story that the mass audience already knows. It is trying to build screen life around a character whose present power has partly come from not explaining itself too much.

That is harder than it sounds. A bag charm can thrive on ambiguity. A film usually needs stakes, movement, jokes, feelings and a world. The risk is that too much explanation drains the strangeness. The opportunity is that a successful film could give Labubu what Hello Kitty, Barbie, Lego and Pokémon have all had in different ways: a bridge from merchandise to durable cultural property.

Here the business term intellectual property needs plain translation. It means owned creative material: characters, names, designs, stories and images that can be licensed, sold and extended. In the entertainment business, a strong IP is not only a popular thing. It is a thing that can keep generating new products without being invented from scratch each time.

Pop Mart’s financial report shows why that matters. In 2025, the company said 17 artist IPs each generated more than 100 million yuan in revenue. The Monsters generated 14.16 billion yuan, or about 38 percent of total company revenue. Pop Mart also reported large revenue from Skullpanda, Crybaby, Molly, Dimoo, Twinkle Twinkle and Hirono. This is the company’s central promise to investors: Labubu is meant to be proof of system, not the only trick.

The market is not yet fully convinced. A hit character can look like genius on the way up and concentration risk on the way down. That is why the same annual report could contain extraordinary growth and still be followed by a stock selloff. Investors were not asking whether Labubu was famous. They were asking whether fame can be repeated, diversified and protected.

The adult toy aisle is not a joke

One reason Labubu became powerful is that the buyer is often not a child. The industry term kidult, a blend of kid and adult, can sound faintly patronizing, as if grown people are being caught with crayons. But the behavior it describes is real and commercially important. Adults buy Lego sets, plush toys, trading cards, miniatures, action figures, nostalgia objects and game-related collectibles for themselves. Sometimes the purchase is fandom. Sometimes it is stress relief. Sometimes it is display. Sometimes the object is simply pleasing.

Circana reported that in the first quarter of 2025, toy sales for adults aged 18 and over grew 12 percent from the prior-year period in the United States, making adults the fastest-growing age demographic in that market. Adults accounted for 1.8 billion dollars in toy spending during the quarter, the highest spending among age groups. The Toy Association’s 2025 trend report also pointed to collectibles, personalization, nostalgia and social media as forces shaping the toy business.

That context makes Labubu less strange. It belongs to a world in which adults want small, affordable or semi-affordable luxuries that can be displayed, collected and discussed. A plush charm can function like a lipstick, a sneaker drop, a vinyl record, a phone case or a coffee-table object. It says something, but not too loudly. It offers a piece of identity that can be clipped to a bag and changed later.

Labubu’s ugliness helps. Many modern cute objects are too smooth, too babyish or too obviously optimized. Labubu’s grin is slightly wrong. Its teeth complicate the sweetness. That makes it easier for adults to carry without feeling they are only performing childhood. The creature looks like it knows the world is odd.

This is where the fashion piece enters. A charm attached to a luxury handbag does not have to be expensive to change the meaning of the outfit. In fact, part of the appeal is the collision: polished leather plus fuzzy goblin. The accessory says the wearer is not merely buying status; they are editing it. That edit photographs well. It also travels well across language, which helps explain the global spread.

Scarcity is useful until it starts a fire

Scarcity is one of the engines of collectible culture. If everyone can get everything at any time, the chase weakens. But scarcity has side effects. It creates lines, resellers, counterfeiters, frustrated buyers and speculative behavior. It can turn a charming hobby into a miniature commodities market.

Pop Mart has been expanding distribution quickly. Its 2025 report said the company had 445 retail stores and 2,396 roboshops in the PRC at the end of 2025. In the Americas, the store count grew from 22 in 2024 to 64 in 2025. In Asia Pacific, stores grew from 54 to 85. In Europe and other regions, stores grew from 14 to 36. The company is not hiding the product. Still, demand can outrun orderly distribution when the item is tied to drops, rarity and platform attention.

That is where the blind-box model becomes both elegant and controversial. Research on doll blind boxes has examined how uncertainty, instant gratification, perceived scarcity and the gambler’s fallacy can affect consumer behavior. The gambler’s fallacy is the mistaken belief that a desired outcome becomes more likely after a run of undesired outcomes, such as thinking the next box must contain the rare figure because the last several did not. Physical blind boxes are not identical to casino gambling. The buyer receives a product. But the emotional mechanics can rhyme with chance-based systems.

China’s market regulator moved in 2023 to set clearer rules for blind-box businesses. Official coverage of the trial guideline said certain products, including medicine, medical equipment, live animals and flammable or explosive objects, could not be sold through blind boxes. It also said sales to minors under age eight were prohibited and that merchants should publicize values, drawing rules and outcome probabilities. The regulatory interest is unsurprising. When a business model depends on uncertainty, governments eventually ask how much uncertainty is fair.

The strongest defense of blind boxes is that collecting has always involved chance and trade. A child opening a pack of baseball cards, an adult buying a mystery art print, a gamer receiving a random cosmetic item and a shopper pulling a capsule toy are all participating in surprise. Surprise is not automatically exploitation. It can be fun, social and affordable.

The strongest criticism is that companies can tune surprise into compulsion. If the rare item is heavily advertised, if odds are unclear, if resale prices are hyped, if minors are targeted, or if the product is pushed through endless livestreams and countdowns, the line between play and pressure gets harder to see. Labubu lives on that line: charming enough to invite affection, scarce enough to invite pursuit, visible enough to invite comparison.

The fake monster is part of the real story

Every hot collectible eventually attracts fakes. Labubu’s counterfeits even acquired their own nickname: Lafufu. The joke is funny until safety agencies get involved.

In August 2025, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission warned consumers about fake Labubu plush dolls, sometimes called Lafufu, saying they posed a serious choking and death risk to young children. The agency said some fakes sold as plush figures and keychains were small enough for a child to put in the mouth and block the airway. It also said it had reports of fake dolls breaking apart easily and releasing small pieces. The CPSC said investigators had identified multiple shipments attempting to enter the United States from China and had requested seizure of thousands of units after confirming violations of federal small-parts rules.

The United Kingdom’s product-safety system also published a report on a counterfeit Popmart Labubu Have a Seat doll, identifying chemical and choking risks. The report said hands and feet contained certain phthalates and that parts detached below the specified minimum force, creating small parts capable of fitting inside a small-parts cylinder. The goods were seized from a local retailer and were to be destroyed.

Chinese customs authorities also reported seizures of counterfeit Labubu-related goods in 2025. China Daily reported that customs intercepted more than 40,000 fake Labubu toys in June, including suspected counterfeit products at the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge checkpoint, plus additional seizures in Ningbo and at Shanghai Pudong International Airport. People’s Daily Online, citing Global Times and customs posts, reported earlier June cases involving thousands of unlicensed blind boxes, plush toys and keychains.

These seizures are not only an intellectual-property story. They show how quickly a cute object can become a supply-chain problem. A viral toy does not stay inside the official store. It spills into third-party sellers, marketplaces, street stalls, forwarding services, resale groups and lookalike factories. For families, that can turn a trend into a safety question. For Pop Mart, it turns popularity into an enforcement burden.

The counterfeit problem has also moved into digital fabrication. In March 2026, Tom’s Hardware reported that Bambu Lab had settled an intellectual-property dispute with Pop Mart over Labubu-related fan-art files on MakerWorld, Bambu’s model-sharing platform. Bambu published an apology on Weibo, said it had reached a settlement and said the problematic content had been removed from the platform. That is a very 2026 kind of toy story: not only fake plushes in boxes, but digital files that can be downloaded, modified and printed into knockoff objects.

For fans, authenticity becomes part of the culture. Is the QR code real? Does the hologram look right? Are there nine teeth? Is the foot stamp correct? The collector becomes a detective. That can deepen engagement, but it also raises the cost of trust. The official product gains aura because the fake exists. The fake gains a market because the official product is scarce or expensive. Each feeds the other.

From soft power to soft toy

Labubu has also become a cultural-export story. That phrase can sound stiff, but the idea is simple: a country’s image changes not only through leaders, armies or trade agreements, but through things people voluntarily enjoy. K-pop, anime, manga, French fashion, Hollywood films, Italian food, British fantasy novels and Japanese game characters all do this kind of work. They make a place feel emotionally familiar.

Labubu is an unusual example because it is not an official state project. It was created by a Hong Kong artist, scaled by a Beijing-based company and spread through celebrity styling, social platforms, stores and collectors. Commentators have described it as a form of Chinese soft power, but that label needs care. A toy does not erase geopolitical tensions. It does not mean consumers are endorsing a government. It does show that Chinese consumer brands and Chinese-linked creative properties can travel globally with humor, taste and emotional fluency.

This is one reason the movie project matters beyond box office. If Sony and Pop Mart can turn Labubu into a film that works outside the existing collector base, it would mark another step in the globalization of Chinese-origin pop IP. Not the export of a finished Chinese film to the world, but the insertion of a Chinese-linked character into Hollywood’s development system.

That path is not guaranteed. Hollywood has a long history of assuming that a famous product equals a workable film. Sometimes that is true. Barbie became a blockbuster by understanding the contradictions inside the brand. Lego became a movie by making its commercial nature part of the joke. Other toy and brand adaptations have stumbled because awareness is not the same as affection, and affection is not the same as story.

Labubu’s screen challenge is especially delicate. The character’s current power is partly tactile. It has fur, size, rarity and portability. A film must translate that into movement, voice, setting and emotion. Give the monster too much personality and it may stop being a mirror for the fan. Give it too little and it may not carry a movie. What works clipped to a handbag may not automatically work across 100 minutes.

The Funko warning sits on the shelf

Collectibles have a graveyard of lessons. Beanie Babies remain the classic cautionary tale. More recently, Funko Pop offered another reminder that a booming collectible business can run into inventory, fatigue and value problems. In 2023, Funko said it expected to eliminate 30 million to 36 million dollars worth of products as it tried to manage overstock and fulfillment costs. Funko did not disappear; its 2024 results showed a company still operating, improving some financial measures and talking about disciplined diversification. That is the point. The warning is not that every collectible boom ends in collapse. The warning is that collectible magic depends on a difficult balance between availability and scarcity.

If there are too few products, fans get angry and fakes flourish. If there are too many, rarity collapses and shelves start to look tired. If every character gets endless variants, collectors may feel hunted rather than delighted. If prices rise too quickly, the hobby becomes a test of spending power. If resale speculation dominates, ordinary fans can feel pushed out by people treating plush toys like day trades.

Pop Mart appears aware of the need for diversification. Its annual report emphasizes multiple IPs, exhibitions, accessories, content, theme-park-style experiences and offline events. The company wants to be a platform for characters, not only a manufacturer of one famous monster. It also says The Monsters joined its ten-billion-yuan IP tier, while several other lines generated billions. The business case is clear: build a portfolio so one character’s cooling does not chill the whole company.

The supply side is changing too. Reuters reported in January 2026 that Pop Mart had added manufacturing partners in Mexico, Cambodia and Indonesia, after previously having production bases in China and Vietnam. The company said the partner-led network was meant to improve resilience, efficiency and customer service. That expansion makes sense for a global craze. It also shows how far a plush charm now reaches: from an artist’s character to factories, logistics, customs, platform shops and earnings calls.

Still, culture is not a spreadsheet. Fans do not automatically migrate from Labubu to Hirono or Crybaby because a revenue table asks them to. Each character must earn its own affection. The danger of a system built around repeatable IP is that the audience can smell when repeatability becomes the point. People like discovering a strange little creature. They are less charmed by being told the next strange little creature has been optimized for global conversion.

The box is sealed, the result is not

So what is Labubu now?

It is a hit toy, certainly. It is also a fashion accessory, a collector ritual, a platform-commerce machine and a Chinese consumer-brand success. It is a source of investor anxiety, because the hotter a hit becomes, the more everyone asks how long heat can last. It is a safety story when counterfeits enter homes. It is a regulation story when surprise becomes a business model. It is a supply-chain story when official manufacturing expands and fake production follows demand. It is a film story now, though still at the early stage of becoming one.

The best reason to take Labubu seriously is not that every Labubu will matter five years from now. Many may not. The point is that Labubu reveals how modern pop culture is increasingly built from small objects surrounded by large systems. The object has to be cute enough, strange enough or emotionally sticky enough to start the loop. Then the loop does the rest: platform visibility, scarcity, drops, resales, celebrity styling, counterfeit detection, investor scrutiny, licensing, safety enforcement and finally the studio pitch.

A century ago, a character might have traveled from comic strip to toy shelf. Late in the twentieth century, it might have traveled from Saturday-morning television to lunchbox. Labubu’s path is different: artist book to designer toy, blind box to bag charm, TikTok object to public-company growth engine, counterfeit target to movie development.

That path may not produce a classic screen character. The Sony project could become a clever family film, a strange misfire or a development footnote. Pop Mart could keep expanding its character universe, or it could discover that the world only had room for one grinning monster at this scale. Regulators could leave blind boxes mostly alone, or they could tighten rules if consumer harms grow. Fans could stay loyal, or they could move on to the next small creature with a better face for the feed.

For now, Labubu sits at an interesting crossing. It is not old enough to be nostalgia, not new enough to be underground, not cheap enough to be casual for everyone, not expensive enough to be luxury, not fully a toy, not fully a fashion item, not yet a movie star. That in-between status is exactly why it works. It gives people room to project.

The sealed box is still the right metaphor. The culture has bought in. The package is in hand. Everyone is waiting to see which version comes out.
