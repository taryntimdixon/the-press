The new minerals bloc is being built in customs law, not in mines

Across April’s rare-earth diplomacy, the most consequential shift is not another headline-grabbing pit or a single export curb. It is an allied attempt to build a survivable market outside concentrated supply chains through trade rules, coordinated demand, stockpiling, processing hubs, traceability and financing that can endure coercion and price pressure.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

The freshest geopolitical development in critical minerals is not a spectacular mine opening. It is not a single sanction, a newly discovered ore body, or a triumphant promise of mineral independence. The more consequential move is quieter: allied governments are trying to write a market into existence.

That is the sharper 2026 story. The policy center of gravity has moved away from the romance of extraction and toward the harder administrative work of making non-Chinese supply chains commercially durable. The real contest now runs through customs classifications, joint purchasing mechanisms, offtake agreements, stockpiling design, emergency planning, processing capacity, traceability, and the legal architecture that determines whether a refinery or magnet plant outside the incumbent system can survive long enough to matter.

Several live developments support that reading. On February 4, 2026, the United States, the European Union and Japan issued a joint statement after a Critical Minerals Ministerial in Washington that pledged deeper cooperation on supply-chain resilience, including support for mining, processing, recycling, information-sharing, and resilience against disruptions. On February 26, the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative opened a public process on the design of a plurilateral Agreement on Trade in Critical Minerals and explicitly floated “border mechanisms, such as price floors and tariffs,” to create what it called a “resilient and non-distorted marketplace among aligned trading partners.” On March 19, Washington and Tokyo released a U.S.-Japan Critical Minerals Action Plan that pushed further into crisis response, stockpiling, project support and joint work on trade and investment issues. And on April 8, the International Energy Agency published a special report on rare earth elements intended to inform discussions under France’s 2026 G7 presidency. Those are not isolated events. They look like pieces of the same strategy.

The strategic shift is subtle but important. For several years, Western and allied governments mostly described mineral security as a supply problem: find more deposits, approve more mines, subsidize more output. That framing was never wrong, but it was incomplete. In 2026, the diagnosis is becoming more precise. The hardest problem is not only finding ore. It is whether a coalition of like-minded states can build enough trusted capacity in the middle of the chain, and enough policy support around that capacity, that alternative supply can survive concentrated processing power, export controls, opaque pricing and downstream customer inertia.

That is why customs law matters more than heroic extraction stories. A mine can be announced in one news cycle and still leave the strategic picture largely unchanged. A refinery, separations plant, alloy line, magnet plant or recycling hub lives or dies on a more prosaic set of conditions: who buys its output; under what pricing formula; with what working capital; under which origin rules; against which incumbent imports; and with what confidence that a sudden wave of cheaper material or a licensing shock will not wipe out the business case before the facility reaches scale.

In other words, the emerging minerals bloc is being built in filings, contracts, and border policy as much as in geology.

Why the strategic problem sits in the middle of the chain

Governments keep talking about critical minerals because they sit inside too many systems to remain an industrial niche. Rare earth elements, lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite, gallium and other materials now cut across clean energy, advanced manufacturing, electronics, transport, defense and aerospace. But not all mineral dependencies are equally dangerous. The deeper vulnerability often sits not at the mine mouth but in the stages that turn material into usable industrial input.

The White House underscored that distinction on January 14, 2026, when President Donald Trump issued an action on imports of processed critical minerals and derivative products. The order said that, as of 2024, the United States was 100 percent net-import reliant for 12 critical minerals and 50 percent or greater net-import reliant for 29 more. It also made the more revealing point: even where the United States has domestic mining capacity, including for cobalt, nickel and rare earth elements, it lacks enough domestic processing capacity to avoid downstream import dependence. That is a concise statement of the problem the mine-centric story tends to miss.

The IEA’s recent work arrives at a similar conclusion from a market-security angle. Its Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025 warned that supply concentration remains especially acute at refining and processing stages, and the agency’s March 2026 commentary on copper smelting stated that China is the top refiner for 19 of the 20 strategic minerals it tracks, with an average market share of around 70 percent. The IEA’s April 2026 report on rare earths drives the point deeper: concentration is high all along the chain, but it is especially consequential in separations, metals, alloys and magnets, where the gap between announced diversification and commercially ready output remains large.

That matters because rare earths are not “rare” in the everyday sense. Deposits exist in multiple countries. The strategic challenge is that rare-earth supply chains are chemically complex, capital intensive, environmentally sensitive and commercially fragile. The chokepoint is not merely where ore is dug up. It is where ores and recycled feedstock become separated oxides, then metals, then alloys, then magnets that manufacturers are actually willing to qualify into motors, turbines, electronics and defense systems.

Once the problem is framed that way, geopolitics begins to look less like a scramble for deposits and more like a struggle over who can keep the midstream open.

From diversification rhetoric to alliance market design

The February 4 U.S.-EU-Japan joint statement is important not because it unveiled a complete new regime, but because it showed how far the language has shifted. It did not read like a standard free-trade communiqué. It emphasized resilience, disruption-prevention, project support and cooperation across mining, refining, processing and recycling. That is the vocabulary of strategic supply management.

The February 26 USTR notice made the shift more explicit. Washington did not merely ask for ideas on cooperation. It sought comment on a plurilateral critical-minerals trade agreement and on “strategic trade policy and border mechanisms,” including price floors and tariffs. That phrasing is unusually revealing. It suggests that U.S. policymakers no longer assume a neutral global commodity market will, by itself, produce secure diversification. They are exploring whether aligned countries need a managed framework that can protect new supply chains from commercial attrition long enough to become viable.

The March 19 U.S.-Japan Critical Minerals Action Plan reinforces the same logic. The document builds on an October 27, 2025 framework and includes cooperation on stockpiles, geological information, supply-chain monitoring, investment support, and responses to coercive or disruptive conduct. It pairs the language of resilience with the machinery of implementation. That combination matters. A stockpile policy without trade coordination can become a warehouse. Project finance without downstream commitments can become stranded capital. Investment promotion without screening rules can create new dependencies while trying to reduce old ones. The emerging allied effort is notable because it tries to connect those pieces rather than treat each as a separate portfolio item.

Europe is moving in a related direction. The EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act, in force since 2024, remains the legal backbone. But the more current story is how Brussels is trying to operationalize it. The Commission’s RESourceEU package, announced on December 3, 2025, proposed a more interventionist supply-security toolkit: a European Critical Raw Materials Centre, stronger use of the Raw Materials Platform to aggregate demand and secure offtake, coordinated stockpiling, more support for strategic projects, and wider measures to protect industry from geopolitical and price shocks. On March 4, 2026, the Council adopted its position on amending the CRMA, emphasizing risk management, recycling of permanent magnets, and stronger transparency around large companies’ vulnerabilities to supply disruption.

This is not yet a formal bloc in the treaty sense. But it is becoming a bloc in function. What ties the U.S., EU and Japan approaches together is not identical policy design. It is a shared conclusion that concentrated mineral supply chains are not just an economic irritant. They are a standing geostrategic vulnerability, and the response requires institutions that can shape markets rather than simply observe them.

April’s rare-earth peg: why this spring matters

The reason April 2026 is a legitimate news peg is not that the rare-earth problem suddenly appeared this month. It is that several lines of policy and analysis are visibly converging now.

First, the IEA’s April 8 report elevated rare earths inside allied policy discussions. The agency said demand for magnet rare earths such as neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium and terbium has doubled since 2015 and is projected to rise by more than 30 percent by 2030. It framed the report as analytical support for G7 discussions under France’s 2026 presidency. That is significant because it treats rare earths not as a technical side topic of the energy transition, but as a cross-sector security concern.

Second, the IEA published a new report on April 22, 2026 on critical-mineral traceability for energy and economic security. Its premise is revealing: when supply chains are highly concentrated and geopolitical risks are rising, the ability to track origin, custody and transformation becomes strategically important in its own right. Traceability is not just an ESG exercise. It is becoming part of the statecraft of diversified supply. Once governments start caring about where material came from, who processed it, what custody chain it followed and whether it can be trusted in procurement or stockpiling systems, customs procedures and data architecture become part of the geopolitical terrain.

Third, actual projects are beginning to give the strategy a physical map. The Caremag heavy rare-earth project in Lacq, southwestern France, is a good example. In March 2025, Japan’s Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry said the Japanese and French governments supported Iwatani Corporation and JOGMEC’s participation in the project, alongside a long-term supply contract for stable supply to Japan. METI said the plant is planned to separate rare earths from recycled magnets and raw ore and produce individual rare-earth oxides. The strategic value of Lacq is not that France suddenly became a mining superpower. It is that an allied midstream node is being built in a politically stable, industrially legible, logistically connected jurisdiction.

That is the real April story: allied policy is becoming less abstract. It is no longer just about the aspiration to diversify. It is about identifying actual corridors and nodes: mines in one place, separation in another, magnet fabrication somewhere else, with financing, offtake, traceability and emergency planning wrapped around the chain.

The corridor, not the pit, is the new unit of strategy

Once governments accept that mineral security is a corridor problem, the map changes. The old energy-security imagination centered on oilfields, pipelines, tankers and straits. The new mineral-security map includes industrial parks, chemical-processing sites, customs procedures, bonded storage, port access, recycling facilities, qualifying labs, and procurement systems that can connect miners, processors, magnet makers and final manufacturers.

A secure rare-earth corridor might include upstream supply from Australia, the United States or Brazil; processing or recycling capacity in France, Malaysia, Estonia or Japan; financing or demand guarantees from the United States, the EU or Japan; and trade rules that reduce the odds a new entrant will be commercially crushed by incumbent overcapacity or sudden export measures. Each node may be in a different jurisdiction. The system only works if the corridor is governable end to end.

This is why the EU Energy and Raw Materials Platform deserves more attention than it usually gets. The Commission says the Raw Materials Mechanism supports strategic projects by aggregating demand, connecting offtakers and suppliers, enabling private coordinated procurement, and linking firms with storage, financing and investment providers. That sounds bureaucratic, and it is. But bureaucracy is exactly the point. The system is trying to make a fragmented market more legible, bankable and resilient. If allied diversification depends on dozens of firms in several jurisdictions discovering each other, matching technical specifications, arranging offtake, and financing storage during a supply shock, then the platform itself becomes part of strategic infrastructure.

That same logic explains why stockpiling has returned to the agenda. The IEA’s February 2026 commentary on strategic stockpiling argues that stockpiles can cushion supply shocks while diversified supply is still being built, but also warns that design matters: what to stockpile, who holds it, how it is released, and how it interacts with private markets. A badly designed stockpile can immobilize material without solving the vulnerability. A well-designed one can buy time during a licensing shock, transport disruption or export-control event and provide a measure of demand certainty to emerging suppliers.

Seen this way, customs law becomes infrastructure. Rules of origin, tariff schedules, licensing categories, traceability systems, price-support mechanisms and coordinated procurement rules do not merely regulate trade after the fact. They shape whether trade can sustain an alternative supply chain at all.

Why governments are flirting with managed trade

The most striking policy signal in the current round of diplomacy is not simply that governments want cooperation. It is that they are considering policies that look increasingly like managed trade.

The USTR consultation is the clearest example. The mention of price floors and tariffs suggests policymakers are asking a difficult question out loud: if concentrated supply chains can use pricing power, export controls or industrial policy to keep rivals from reaching scale, should allied governments create a protected commercial space in which new entrants can survive? That is a more interventionist question than the term “supply-chain resilience” often implies.

The rationale is easy to see. Private capital has ample reason to hesitate. Rare-earth and other mineral projects face long lead times, permitting risk, chemistry risk, customer qualification risk, environmental liabilities, uncertain demand and price volatility. A refinery or magnet plant outside the dominant system can be built with subsidies, but it still may not be financeable unless lenders believe it can remain solvent through the early years when scale is limited and incumbents can undercut prices.

Hence the search for what officials increasingly describe as “non-distorted” markets among aligned partners. The phrase is not neutral. It carries the implication that existing prices may not be reliable guides for strategic investment because they reflect concentrated capacity, policy support and asymmetric leverage rather than frictionless competition alone. A price floor in this context is not simply protectionism for its own sake. It is an attempt to create investable conditions for new capacity. A tariff is not merely a border tax. It can function as breathing room for a politically desired supply chain that cannot otherwise clear the financing hurdle.

Europe’s newer measures point in the same direction, though in a less blunt register. RESourceEU ties together joint purchasing, offtake support, stockpiling and project backing. The Council’s March 2026 position on CRMA amendments gives the Commission a larger role in identifying large vulnerable companies and in proposing risk-mitigation measures. Those are the administrative skeleton of an interventionist market.

None of this guarantees a neat allied commodity club. But it does show that the center of the debate has shifted. The question is no longer whether governments should intervene. They already are. The question is how far they are prepared to go in socializing risk and shaping cross-border commerce to keep alternative mineral supply chains alive.

What the emerging bloc is trying to deter

The coalition now taking shape is trying to deter several different threats at once, and each one points to a different policy tool.

The first threat is coercive export leverage. The OECD said in 2025 that export restrictions on industrial raw materials had increased more than fivefold since 2009. It also found that the impact was especially pronounced for cobalt and rare earth elements, with 67 percent and 46 percent of their respective trade affected. UNCTAD’s 2025 SDG Pulse analysis, drawing on OECD data, likewise showed a rising pattern of export restrictions across critical minerals. The lesson for importing states is direct: dependence on concentrated supply chains is no longer just a matter of market efficiency. It can become an administrative weapon.

The second threat is commercial attrition. A dominant player does not need a formal embargo to preserve its position. It can use scale, pricing, process know-how, customer familiarity and policy uncertainty to make alternative projects uneconomic. That is why allied officials are now talking about price floors, demand aggregation, offtake guarantees and market transparency. They are trying to deter not only sudden shocks, but the slower erosion of competing supply chains before they mature.

The third threat is internal fragmentation among allies. This is less discussed and just as important. The United States may emphasize domestic processing and national-security screening. Europe may emphasize strategic autonomy, circularity and regulatory accountability. Japan may prioritize secure contracted imports and stable midstream nodes abroad. Producer countries may want more local value-added rather than merely exporting concentrate. Those preferences overlap, but they do not line up automatically. A bloc can fail not only because of external pressure, but because its own members cannot agree on burden-sharing, specialization, local-content rules or acceptable foreign investment.

The fourth threat is information failure. In thin, opaque markets, governments and firms often do not know enough about who controls capacity, how fast material can move, what technical substitutions are possible, or where chain-of-custody vulnerabilities sit. The IEA’s new traceability report matters precisely because it addresses that problem. A diversified market that cannot reliably identify origin, processing pathway and ownership structure is not yet a secure market.

The logistics and chemistry problem hiding behind the diplomacy

Even if the policy architecture improves, the physical system remains difficult. Rare-earths and other strategic mineral chains are not simple pipelines into which governments can inject money and expect predictable output. Ore grades vary. Feedstocks differ. Separation chemistry is demanding. Waste management is costly. Community opposition can delay projects. Skilled labor takes time to train. Customers, especially in automotive, aerospace and defense markets, do not casually switch material or component suppliers without qualification cycles that can take months or years.

This is why the workshop is the more honest image of the story than the mine. The strategic contest is about intermediate capability: can an allied network move material through awkward middle stages with enough purity, reliability, and price discipline that downstream manufacturers trust the system not to fail?

The IEA’s rare-earth report offers a useful warning here. Even with planned diversification, downstream capacity for metals, alloys and finished magnets lags diversified mining plans. In its executive summary, the agency notes that cumulative planned production of those downstream stages from projects announced as of early 2026 amounts to only about one third of diversified mining capacity on a rare-earth-content basis. That means governments can succeed in encouraging more upstream supply and still discover that the more difficult bottlenecks have merely shifted down the chain.

That is also why project nodes like Lacq matter so much. A processing plant or recycling hub inside allied space can anchor a chain in ways a mine alone cannot. It can attract technical workers, testing capacity, logistics relationships and downstream customers. It can also fail if those complements do not arrive. The corridor logic is unforgiving: a weak link in one jurisdiction can undermine the strategic value of stronger links elsewhere.

CSIS has repeatedly made versions of this point in its work on critical minerals, arguing that the real challenge is not announcing initiatives but sequencing upstream, midstream and downstream investment into coherent systems. That assessment remains sound. Fragmented national programs can create headlines. They do not automatically create a functioning chain.

France’s Lacq and the politics of allied processing hubs

The Caremag project in Lacq illustrates why the geopolitically interesting story is increasingly about processing hubs and not just deposits. According to METI, the project is designed to separate rare earths from both recycled magnets and raw ore in southwestern France and to produce individual oxides. Japan’s involvement, through Iwatani and JOGMEC, adds financing and a long-term demand channel. France contributes an industrial site and policy support. The result is not mineral autarky for anyone. It is a cross-border node in an allied chain.

This kind of site has several strategic advantages. It can operate in a jurisdiction with rule-of-law credibility, strong logistics and established chemical-industry capabilities. It can serve multiple allied customers. It can help turn recycling into something more than a slogan. And it can reduce the risk that every consuming country tries to build every stage at home regardless of economic sense.

But it also exposes a core political challenge. The bloc will only work if countries accept asymmetry. Not every state can host every stage of the value chain. Some will mine. Some will separate. Some will refine metals and alloys. Some will produce magnets or integrate them into motors and systems. Economically, specialization is rational. Politically, it is hard. Every government wants jobs, strategic relevance and a domestic narrative of control. That tension is likely to shape the next phase of allied mineral policy as much as any external threat.

The case against the bloc

The strongest criticism of the emerging minerals bloc is not that the problem is unreal. It is that the cure could become expensive, clumsy and self-defeating.

One version of the critique is economic. Governments may overcorrect for concentration risk and subsidize high-cost or duplicative projects that never become competitive. If several allied jurisdictions all build stockpiles, all guarantee offtake, all shield local projects and all add trade barriers, the result could be a permanently more expensive market for downstream industry. That cost would not vanish. It would show up in manufacturing margins, procurement bills and public budgets.

Another critique is geopolitical. Producer countries in Africa, Latin America and Southeast Asia do not necessarily want to become upstream appendages to a U.S.-EU-Japan buyers’ architecture. UNCTAD has emphasized that critical-mineral trade shapes industrial development, not just resource extraction. Many producing states want processing, fiscal returns, policy space and infrastructure, not merely export volumes. If the bloc looks too much like a gated demand club, producers may hedge, bargain harder or prefer commercial ties they see as less restrictive.

A third critique is legal and political at home. Democracies do not build mines, refineries, waste facilities and recycling plants by decree alone. They move through permits, courts, consultation, environmental review, labor rules and local politics. The World Resources Institute has highlighted the community impacts of critical-mineral expansion in the United States, including water, land and equity concerns. Security arguments can accelerate decision-making, but they do not erase legitimacy constraints. If governments treat those constraints as obstacles to be bulldozed rather than conditions to be governed, backlash may delay the very projects they say are urgent.

A fourth critique concerns the language of “non-distorted” markets itself. It can obscure how much discretion governments are claiming for themselves. If officials decide which prices are acceptable, which suppliers are trusted, which origin chains qualify, and which firms deserve support, the result is not simply resilience. It is a more political market. Some of that may be necessary. But it also creates risks of favoritism, diplomatic friction and industrial policy by lobbying.

These objections are serious. They do not invalidate the strategy. They do define its limits.

What 2026 changes, exactly

The significance of 2026 is not merely that governments care more about critical minerals. They have for years. The change is that three different lines are now converging.

The first is strategic diagnosis. Official documents from the White House, IEA, OECD and the EU all point to the same underlying reality: dependence is not only about import volumes, but about concentrated processing, trade restrictions, opaque markets and downstream vulnerability.

The second is institutionalization. The U.S.-EU-Japan ministerial statement, the USTR consultation on a plurilateral Agreement on Trade in Critical Minerals, the U.S.-Japan action plan, the IEA’s G7-linked rare-earth work, the EU Raw Materials Platform and the CRMA amendment process all indicate that mineral security is moving from broad aspiration into administrative form.

The third is project formation. Strategic projects under the EU framework, the Caremag site in France, and other allied efforts to back processing, recycling and midstream nodes show that this is no longer a story of white papers alone. The map is beginning to populate with real facilities, counterparties and commercial arrangements.

Together those developments produce a more interesting geopolitical thesis than the usual “race for resources” narrative. The story is not simply that the West wants more minerals. It is that allied governments are trying to convert scattered projects into a governed market system able to withstand coercion, price pressure and internal fragmentation.

The likely next fights

If the strategy advances through the rest of 2026, four fights are likely to dominate.

The first is over trade design. Price floors, tariffs, origin rules and procurement standards may sound technical, but they decide who pays for resilience. Upstream and midstream developers want certainty strong enough to unlock capital. Downstream manufacturers want secure inputs without a crippling premium. Governments want anti-coercion tools that do not trigger broad retaliation or violate other commitments. The distributive fight is not whether resilience is desirable. It is who absorbs the cost.

The second is over stockpiling and emergency governance. What gets stored? Who owns it? When is it released? Does the stockpile support private market formation or crowd it out? The IEA is right that design matters more than stockpile rhetoric. A badly governed stockpile can become a symbol of preparedness rather than preparedness itself.

The third is over processing geography. The bloc will need enough trust to accept specialization. If every country insists on hosting every stage, the result may be a patchwork of politically satisfying but commercially weak mini-chains. If countries do specialize, governments will have to explain why dependence on trusted partners differs from the dependencies they are trying to escape.

The fourth is over legitimacy and traceability. As the IEA’s April 22 report suggests, the next phase of mineral politics is not just about tonnage. It is also about proving where material came from, how it moved, who transformed it and whether it meets the standards required for public procurement, strategic investment or emergency use. That makes filing cabinets, data systems and customs records part of the security architecture.

A bloc with a workshop and a filing cabinet

Geopolitics likes the rhetoric of resolve. This story is more administrative than dramatic. The new minerals bloc, if it takes coherent shape, will succeed or fail in agencies, not only at summits; in contract structures, not only in speeches; in warehouses, procurement platforms, customs schedules, traceability systems and environmental files, not only in declarations of strategic autonomy.

That is exactly why it deserves attention now. The sharp turn in early 2026 is that allied governments have begun to accept the institutional reality of deterrence in mineral markets. If concentrated supply can be weaponized, then resilience has to be built through governed interdependence: coordinated stockpiles, emergency plans, project support, border tools, demand aggregation, trusted processing hubs and better visibility across the chain.

There is no guarantee this will work. China’s incumbency remains formidable. New projects can be delayed, underpriced or litigated. Producer countries may resist being slotted into someone else’s strategic design. Allied governments may quarrel over subsidies, screening rules and local content. And if prices weaken, political support for expensive intervention may fade.

But the shift itself is real. The central geopolitical question in critical minerals is no longer simply who can dig. It is who can organize a market resilient enough to survive coercion, price war, opacity and political fragmentation. In 2026, that answer is being written less in mine plans than in the paperwork of an alliance trying to turn vulnerability into governed trade.
