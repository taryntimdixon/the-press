A Caldera Near Naples Is Being Read One Millimeter at a Time

Campi Flegrei is not sending a simple countdown. Its latest official bulletins show small weekly quakes, continued ground rise, hot fumaroles and a yellow alert — a science story built from instruments, models and the limits of prediction.

By The Press • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The most important number at Campi Flegrei this week is not a cinematic one.

It is not a plume height. It is not a lava temperature. It is not a countdown.

It is about 10 millimeters a month, plus or minus 3.

That is the average speed at which the ground has been rising since early February 2026 in the most deformed part of the caldera west of Naples, according to the April 21 weekly bulletin from Italy’s National Institute of Geophysics and Volcanology, known by its Italian initials, INGV. The bulletin, covering April 13 through April 19 and still the latest official weekly Campi Flegrei bulletin available early Sunday, April 26, reported 14 located earthquakes with duration magnitude at or above zero. The largest was Md 1.8, plus or minus 0.3. It also said the latest two days of geodetic data hinted at a possible increase in the uplift rate — too preliminary, the observatory warned, to treat as a confirmed change.

That combination is the story: a volcano that is being watched closely, a city-scale risk that is real, and a signal that refuses to turn itself into a simple alarm bell.

Campi Flegrei, the Phlegraean Fields, is a broad volcanic caldera rather than a neat cone. A caldera is a large volcanic depression associated with past eruptions and collapse of the ground. This one sits under towns, roads, schools, ports, apartments, fumaroles, buried faults and part of the western Naples metropolitan area. Its last confirmed eruption began on September 29, 1538, and ended on October 6, building Monte Nuovo, or New Mountain. Today it is famous for bradyseism, the slow rising and sinking of land caused by pressure and mass changes underground.

To live there is to inhabit a place where geology does not stay politely beneath the floor. Harbors grow shallower. Walls crack. Schools close after tremors. A sulfurous field steams. A monitoring station can be mounted on an ordinary public building, until the building is scheduled for demolition and the instrument has to be moved. The March INGV monthly bulletin noted exactly that kind of practical detail: the SOLO GNSS station at Solfatara had been decommissioned because the school building that hosted it was to be demolished.

The hard science question is not whether Campi Flegrei is dangerous. It is. The harder question is what kind of danger the current unrest represents: mostly shallow earthquake hazard, mostly a pressurized hydrothermal system, a deeper magmatic process feeding heat and gas into the system, or some moving mixture of all three.

The answer matters because different hazards move at different speeds. A shallow magnitude 4 or 5 earthquake can injure people and damage buildings without any eruption. A phreatic explosion — a steam-driven blast caused when water flashes into steam — can be sudden and local. A magmatic eruption would require a much larger civil-protection response, including evacuation from the red zone before the eruption begins.

As of the latest official bulletins available for this issue, the official message is cautious: Campi Flegrei remains at yellow alert, and the April 21 bulletin says the monitoring picture does not show elements suggesting significant short-term evolution. But that does not mean nothing is happening. It means the instruments are describing unrest, not yet an imminent eruption.

The volcano is not one instrument

If a schoolchild were asked to draw volcano monitoring, the result might be a scientist staring at one needle. Campi Flegrei is the opposite. It is a many-instrument argument.

There are seismometers listening for earthquakes, including tiny ones that residents do not feel. There are GNSS stations — Global Navigation Satellite System receivers, cousins of GPS — measuring whether the land moves by millimeters. There are tide gauges at the harbor, tiltmeters that sense tiny changes in ground angle, gravimeters that detect slight changes in Earth’s pull, gas sensors, thermal cameras, drones and field crews sampling fumaroles.

The March 2026 INGV monthly bulletin reads almost like the inventory of a very nervous workshop. The permanent GNSS network includes 39 land and marine stations. The MEDUSA seafloor system in the Gulf of Pozzuoli uses buoy and underwater modules to measure deformation offshore, though the monthly bulletin also notes the operating status of individual components. Thermal surveys use handheld infrared cameras, thermocouples and drones. Geochemical stations track carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulfide, sulfur dioxide, water vapor and fumarole temperatures. At the end of the April 21 weekly bulletin, the observatory even lists how many permanent monitoring stations in each network had sufficient data acquisition during the week.

Each instrument is useful. None is sovereign.

A seismometer can say that rock broke. It cannot, by itself, say whether magma is about to erupt. A gas sensor can say that carbon dioxide is high. It cannot, by itself, separate every gram of gas from deep magma, hot hydrothermal reactions or carbonate rocks heated underground. A satellite or GNSS receiver can show uplift. It cannot, by itself, prove the exact shape and depth of the pressure source.

That is why Campi Flegrei is best understood as a translation problem. The Earth speaks in shakes, heat, gases and moving stone. Institutions have to turn those observations into probabilities, alert levels and public instructions.

The latest bulletin is boring only if you ignore the basement

The April 21 weekly bulletin was not dramatic on the earthquake count. Fourteen located earthquakes over the week is quiet compared with periods of intense swarming. The maximum reported magnitude, Md 1.8, is not the kind of event that makes international news.

But the same bulletin kept three other facts on the table.

First, the ground is still rising. The April 21 bulletin put the recent average uplift rate at about 10 millimeters a month since early February 2026 in the area of maximum deformation. The March monthly bulletin put the longer rise at about 163.5 centimeters at the RITE GNSS station in Pozzuoli’s Rione Terra area since November 2005, the start of the current unrest phase in that accounting, and about 25.5 centimeters since January 2025.

Second, the hydrothermal system is hot and active. The weekly bulletin put the average temperature of the Bocca Grande fumarole in the Solfatara crater at about 173 degrees Celsius during the reporting period. A fumarole is an opening that releases volcanic gases and steam. That temperature does not mean lava is at the surface. It means the shallow system of hot fluids and gases remains energetic enough to demand attention.

Third, INGV noted that the most recent two days of ground-movement data suggested a possible increase in uplift speed, while warning that the data needed reprocessing with more precise orbital products and more days of observation. That caveat is not bureaucratic hedging. It is how geodesy works. A few days of millimeter-scale motion can be blurred by satellite geometry, processing choices, atmospheric effects and instrument noise. In a place where decisions affect hundreds of thousands of people, a wiggle is not yet a trend.

This is the scientific mood of Campi Flegrei in late April 2026: not calm, not panicked, not settled.

A city under a shallow earthquake problem

The short-term danger many researchers emphasize is not necessarily an eruption. It is shaking.

On March 13, 2025, a strong local earthquake shook the Naples area. AP, using the commonly reported magnitude 4.4, described minor damage, 11 people seeking hospital treatment, residents running into the streets, some schools closing as a precaution and several buildings declared off-limits. INGV’s own overview of Campi Flegrei lists the same date as Md 4.6, plus or minus 0.3, a reminder that different magnitude scales and reporting practices can produce slightly different headline numbers. The important point for residents is not the decimal. It is that shallow earthquakes in a densely built caldera can feel abrupt, loud and intimate.

For a subduction-zone country or a deep tectonic earthquake zone, the mid-4 range may sound moderate. In an urban caldera, shallow depth changes the experience. A small earthquake close under town can crack plaster, dislodge stone, close a school and change sleeping habits.

A 2025 Science study by Xing Tan and colleagues, summarized by Stanford and INGV, used machine learning to reanalyze continuous seismic data from 2022 into 2025 and expanded the earthquake catalog from about 12,000 events to more than 54,000. That larger catalog sharpened the picture of shallow faults and a ring-fault system around the uplift area. The authors and institutional summaries also reported no seismic evidence for significant upward migration of magma in the analyzed period, reducing short-term concern about a magmatic eruption but not eliminating volcanic risk.

That is a powerful result, but not a magic shield. It says something about what the analyzed earthquakes did and did not show. It does not say an eruption is impossible. It does not say the system cannot change. It does not make buildings stronger.

Machine learning helps here because volcanoes produce many small signals. Traditional catalogs depend on detecting and picking seismic phases — the recognizable arrivals of earthquake waves — in noisy data. A retrained model can find more small events and locate them more precisely. That is like turning up the resolution on a medical scan. A blurry ache becomes a visible cluster. But a sharper image still needs interpretation.

The new picture points to faulting in the shallow crust, much of it likely driven by the same uplift that residents can see indirectly in harbors, roads and damaged structures. It also shows why the problem is engineering as much as volcanology. If the main near-term hazard is repeated shallow shaking, then masonry, schools, evacuation routes, inspections and household preparation are not side issues. They are the frontline science application.

Three stories beneath the same ground

The current unrest has produced several serious explanations. They do not all exclude one another.

One story emphasizes magma and gas at depth. Campi Flegrei has a deep magmatic system. Geochemical studies have found changes in fumarole gases that can be consistent with increased magmatic input into the hydrothermal system. INGV’s March bulletin noted that the carbon dioxide-to-water ratio at the Bocca Grande and Bocca Nuova fumaroles remained high even after a slight decrease, generally indicating a large magmatic-gas fraction in the fumarolic fluids. It also described long-term warming and pressurization of the hydrothermal system.

A second story emphasizes a shallow hydrothermal reservoir — hot water, steam and gas trapped beneath a caprock. Stanford researchers, using subsurface imaging and laboratory rock-physics experiments, argued in 2025 that pressure in a geothermal reservoir beneath Pozzuoli can help explain repeated uplift and earthquake swarms. Their provocative implication is that managing runoff, groundwater or reservoir pressure could one day reduce unrest. That is not an operating manual for drilling into a restless caldera tomorrow. It is a hypothesis about mechanism and possible intervention, one that would require difficult safety, legal, engineering and public-trust work before any field action.

A third story emphasizes how faults respond to uplift. A February 2026 Communications Earth & Environment paper by Sebastian Hainzl, Torsten Dahm and Anna Tramelli combined a physics-based model of stress loading with a statistical aftershock model. In plain language: the ground rises, stress changes on faults, earthquakes cluster, and some earthquakes trigger more earthquakes. Their hybrid model reproduced observed patterns and offered probabilistic short-term forecasts of earthquake rates and maximum magnitudes over weekly to monthly horizons.

Other papers complicate the picture rather than simplify it. A 2025 Nature Communications paper described burst-like seismic swarms that became increasingly frequent from 2021 to 2024 and are concentrated around the Solfatara-Pisciarelli hydrothermal area and a geodetic anomaly. A 2025 Communications Earth & Environment paper argued that seismicity since 2023 has clustered along a plane near the center of the caldera, possibly marking the birth or reactivation of a volcanotectonic fault. A January 2026 scenario paper modeled a magma-injection end member and concluded that, under that assumption, potentially eruptible magma could be present at about 4 kilometers depth. That paper is useful not because it settles the mechanism but because it makes clear how strongly conclusions depend on the starting scenario.

These stories can sound like competing campfire tales: magma, water, faults. In reality, Campi Flegrei may be all three in conversation. Deep magma can release gases and heat. Those can pressurize hydrothermal fluids. Pressurized fluids and uplift can weaken or load faults. Faults can fracture rock, changing pathways for fluids. A steam-rich system can produce earthquakes that are not magmatic intrusions in the simple sense.

That is why confident one-sentence explanations are usually a warning sign. A restless caldera is not a kettle with one burner. It is a plumbing system in an old city, except the pipes are fractured rock, the pressure source is partly hidden, and the repair crew is forbidden to turn off gravity.

The gas is a clue, not a verdict

Gas data are among the most fascinating and most easily misread parts of the Campi Flegrei story.

Carbon dioxide matters because magma releases it, and because it can move through rocks and hydrothermal systems. But carbon dioxide can also be affected by reactions in heated carbonate rocks and by changes in water and steam conditions. INGV’s March bulletin explicitly warns that interpreting fumarole ratios is complex. Processes such as steam condensation in the hydrothermal system, changes in pressure and temperature, and reactions involving hydrothermal calcite can influence measured gases.

The sulfur story is similarly nuanced. A 2025 Nature Geoscience paper argued that increasing emissions of isotopically light sulfur at Campi Flegrei can indicate escalation of caldera unrest and may reflect decompression-driven degassing of mafic magma at depth, along with sulfur remobilized from hydrothermal minerals. INGV’s March bulletin discussed sulfur ratios at the Bocca Grande fumarole and linked earlier divergence after 2018 to interpretations compatible with degassing of rising magma between roughly 9 and 6 kilometers depth. But the bulletin also said recent sulfur concentrations showed a return toward hydrothermal equilibrium conditions.

That sentence matters. It is not a movie-script reversal. It is the difference between a real warning system and a rumor mill. Gas can support concern without proving the next stage. A parameter can rise, peak, wobble, then partly relax. The job is to watch combinations: gas, ground movement, earthquake locations, earthquake types, temperature, deformation geometry and whether changes are sustained.

Carbon dioxide also has a daily-life side. Italy’s civil-protection guidance warns that volcanic gases in the caldera can be dangerous to people and animals, and that some gases cannot be smelled, tasted or seen. It tells residents to avoid using basements and semi-basements for living, sleeping, work or recreation, and to ventilate closed or underground spaces before entering. That is not glamorous volcano advice. It is the practical edge of geochemistry.

The plan is built around uncertainty

Italian civil protection divides volcanic risk planning around red and yellow zones. The red zone is the area exposed to possible pyroclastic flows — fast, hot avalanches of gas, ash and volcanic fragments — and the official plan says preventive evacuation is the only protective measure there in the alarm phase. The red zone includes Pozzuoli, Bacoli, Monte di Procida and Quarto, parts of Giugliano in Campania and Marano di Napoli, and several Naples neighborhoods, with about 500,000 residents. The yellow zone is mainly exposed to significant ash fallout and includes more than 800,000 residents.

The plan’s headline number is 72 hours. Once the alarm operational phase is declared, evacuation from the red zone is planned over three days: 12 hours for preparation and traffic-control setup, 48 hours for steady departure and 12 hours as a safety margin.

That is a logistical statement, not a scientific guarantee. A plan can specify buses, waiting areas, meeting areas, regional twinning and road gates. It cannot guarantee that a volcano will wait politely. Nor can it guarantee that every family, hospital patient, pet, tourist, school and commuter will move according to the diagram.

That is why the 2024 and 2025 Exe Flegrei exercises matter. They did not test lava. They tested institutions. The 2024 exercise simulated a shift from the current yellow alert to orange and then red, with monitoring centers, the Major Risks Commission and civil-protection command structures moving through the decision chain. The 2025 exercise schedule included activation of local operational centers, a simulated command-and-control directorate outside the red zone, setup at the Port of Naples and assisted evacuation of students and teachers from a Naples school to waiting areas and then to a meeting point.

This is the part of volcano science that looks least like science and most like a clipboard. Yet it is central. A forecast that cannot be translated into transport, shelters, accessible care, communications and trust will fail at the human end, even if the instruments were excellent.

What would change the conclusion?

A good monitoring story should say what would make it wrong.

The current cautious conclusion — unrest, yellow alert, no evidence in the latest weekly bulletin of significant short-term evolution — would weaken if several independent signals moved together in the wrong direction.

One change would be a sustained, confirmed acceleration of ground uplift, especially if it were not limited to one noisy station or a two-day processing artifact. A step from 10 millimeters a month to a much higher rate, confirmed across GNSS, tide gauges, tilt and satellite radar, would demand attention.

A second change would be a new earthquake pattern: larger events, repeated shallow damaging quakes, seismicity migrating upward or inward in a way that suggested new pathways opening, or the appearance of volcanic tremor and long-period signals consistent with moving fluids or magma rather than ordinary brittle faulting.

A third change would be gas chemistry shifting in a sustained and coherent way: stronger magmatic signatures across multiple fumaroles, rising fluxes, hotter emissions, new vents or a clear departure from hydrothermal equilibrium that could not be explained by weather, sampling, condensation or shallow reactions.

A fourth would be visible hydrothermal escalation: new boiling pools, sudden steam blasts, strong changes at Solfatara or Pisciarelli, or dangerous gas accumulation in lived spaces.

A fifth would be model convergence. If independent models — seismic, geodetic, geochemical and petrological — began pointing to the same shallow pressurizing body, at the same depth, with the same timing, confidence would increase. Today, part of the uncertainty is that different methods illuminate different pieces of the system.

Finally, the institutional conclusion would change if Italy’s Major Risks Commission and civil-protection authorities judged that monitored parameters had crossed alert thresholds. The alert level is not declared by a single paper or a single sensor. It is an institutional decision informed by observatories, competence centers and risk authorities.

What the public can misunderstand

Campi Flegrei is often called a supervolcano. The word attracts attention and usually makes understanding worse.

Yes, the system has produced very large eruptions in the deep past. Yes, the consequences of a future eruption could be severe. But civil-protection planning is not based on the largest imaginable ancient catastrophe. Italy’s national plan uses a reference scenario in which there is a 95 percent probability that a reactivation would be smaller than or equal to a medium-size eruption, based on studies of the last 5,000 years of activity. That is still dangerous. It is just not the same as saying a Hollywood supereruption is the working expectation.

The opposite misunderstanding is also common: if there is no imminent eruption warning, then the situation is normal. It is not normal in the everyday sense. The ground has risen more than a meter and a half since 2005 at key stations. Gas emissions are high. Fumaroles are hot. Buildings have been shaken. A yellow alert is not green.

The useful middle position is less exciting and more demanding: Campi Flegrei is a high-risk volcanic system in unrest, with the most immediate confirmed hazards centered on shallow seismicity, deformation, gas and hydrothermal activity, while eruption forecasting remains uncertain.

That sentence does not fit on a panic poster. It does fit the evidence.

The observatory is part of the landscape now

The science at Campi Flegrei is not happening in a remote wilderness. It happens around people’s errands.

A technician checks a sensor near a steaming field. A family decides whether to sleep in a room with a crack. A school administrator reads inspection notices. A civil-protection official thinks about buses. A researcher compares a gas ratio with last month’s sample and then with a 40-year trend. A computer model quietly reclassifies thousands of tiny earthquakes that no one felt.

This is what modern volcano monitoring looks like when the volcano has a postcode.

The latest official week did not bring a decisive signal. It brought a small earthquake count, continued uplift, hot fumaroles, high long-term gas concern and a possible short-lived uplift uptick that still needs confirmation. In a less populated place, those facts might sit in a technical archive. Near Naples, they become public knowledge, public anxiety and public duty.

The right response is neither to shrug nor to shout. It is to keep the instruments working, keep the models honest, keep the plans tested, and keep saying what the data can and cannot show.

At Campi Flegrei, one millimeter is not much. Ten millimeters a month is a sentence the ground keeps repeating. The question is whether, in the coming weeks and months, the rest of the instruments begin to say the same sentence louder — or something different altogether.
