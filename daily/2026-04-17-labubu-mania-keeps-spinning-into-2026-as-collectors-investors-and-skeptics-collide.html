Labubu mania keeps spinning into 2026 as collectors, investors and skeptics collide

The viral plush monster has become more than a toy. It’s now a resale market, a fashion accessory and a test case for how far internet fandom can push a niche collectible into the mainstream.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 17, 2026 • 12:43 p.m. • 971 words

What started as a cult-favorite character in designer toy circles has become one of the most visible collectible crazes of the year. Labubu, the toothy, rabbit-eared plush monster created by Hong Kong artist Kasing Lung and sold by Pop Mart, is now appearing in news about revenue, retail shortages, resale premiums and celebrity-driven fashion. The latest reporting suggests the craze is still growing, even as analysts and longtime collectors debate whether the market can sustain it.

For Niche, Labubu is a useful lens on a broader 2026 story: how a deeply specific object can leap from hobbyist obsession to global consumer phenomenon. The character’s appeal sits at the intersection of art toy culture, blind-box gambling psychology, social media performance and status signaling. In other words, it is weird, emotional and marketable all at once.

From art toy to global status object

Labubu is not new. The character dates back years, but the breakout came as social media accelerated the spread of images showing the dolls clipped to bags, displayed on desks and traded among collectors. AP reported in late March that Pop Mart’s shares fell even as revenue surged, underscoring how dependent the company has become on the character’s viral appeal. The same report noted the dolls had gained huge popularity worldwide since 2024, with demand boosted by celebrity visibility and online trend cycles. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0b0cfc1a1bde2ea5502a0e6479b8702e?utm_source=openai))

That dynamic helps explain why Labubu now functions less like a toy and more like a cultural object. It is affordable enough for casual buyers to join in, but scarce enough to make ownership feel exclusive. The blind-box model adds suspense, while resale markets create a second layer of status for buyers who want rarer versions. When a collectible can be displayed, traded and posted, it becomes both merchandise and identity marker.

Reuters has previously documented the character’s rise through its image and event coverage, including the fashion-world context in which Labubu-like accessories move fluidly between plaything and adornment. That kind of crossover is part of the point: the collectible is designed to be seen. ([reutersconnect.com](https://www.reutersconnect.com/item/fashion-enthusiasts-gather-outside-the-diesel-springsummer-2026-show-venue-in-milan/dGFnOnJldXRlcnMuY29tLDIwMjU6bmV3c21sX1JDMk9YR0FUMkZVWg?utm_source=openai))

Why collectors keep buying

The appeal of Labubu is not just that it is cute, or even that it is ugly-cute in a deliberately offbeat way. It is that the character rewards participation. Collectors chase color variants, limited releases and regional exclusives. Fans form online communities around drops, trade value guides and authenticity checks. In the process, the hobby becomes a game with both emotional and financial stakes.

That pattern fits a wider collectibles market that remains robust in 2026. Research from Grand View Research says the U.S. collectibles market is on a long growth path, with art and antiques still a major segment and limited-edition fashion and sneaker drops increasingly treated like assets. Pop culture toys are part of that same ecosystem, where rarity and narrative can matter as much as utility. ([grandviewresearch.com](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/press-release/us-collectibles-market-analysis?utm_source=openai))

Whatnot, the live-shopping marketplace popular with collectors, said in an April 15 trend forecast that niche collectibles are increasingly tied to categories far beyond traditional toys, including fashion, beauty and sports cards. That is exactly the environment Labubu thrives in: a commerce platform where fandom, scarcity and livestream energy reinforce one another. ([actionfigureinsider.com](https://www.actionfigureinsider.com/whatnot-uncovers-the-top-toys-collectibles-trends-for-2026/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a psychological element. The blind-box format turns purchasing into anticipation, and anticipation is highly shareable online. Fans post unboxings, swaps and failed pulls. Those moments convert private purchasing into public entertainment, which in turn feeds the algorithm and expands demand. The product does not merely benefit from attention; attention is baked into its design.

The risk of a craze becoming a category

The question now is what happens when a niche collectible becomes a category in its own right. The AP report on Pop Mart’s earnings showed the basic tension: strong demand can drive impressive revenue, but it can also create concern that a company is overexposed to a single character. When one mascot carries so much of the brand’s momentum, any slowdown in virality can have an outsized effect. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0b0cfc1a1bde2ea5502a0e6479b8702e?utm_source=openai))

For collectors, the bigger risk may be dilution. Once a niche item is everywhere, part of the allure disappears. The same social platforms that supercharged Labubu can also flatten it into a meme, stripping away the insider feeling that made early buyers excited. A collectible built on exclusivity must constantly negotiate popularity without becoming ordinary.

Still, the character’s crossover seems to be holding. Labubu has become a kind of shorthand for a specific modern consumer logic: people do not just buy products anymore, they buy stories, tribes and visible proof of taste. That is why a small plush monster can become a larger cultural indicator. It tells us how much the marketplace now values personality, scarcity and shareability over traditional usefulness.

In that sense, Labubu is more than a toy trend. It is a symptom of a culture that increasingly rewards objects with built-in narrative velocity. Whether the craze lasts another season or another year, it has already shown how a niche item can become a global signal almost overnight — and how hard it is to turn attention back into quiet fandom once the spotlight arrives.

What comes next

For now, the Labubu story looks likely to continue in parallel lanes: retail demand, resale speculation, fashion adoption and collector skepticism. That mix makes it especially durable as a niche news topic because it is not just a fad story. It is also a business story, a design story and a social behavior story.

If the toy’s rise says anything, it is that the modern niche is rarely small for long. The internet can take an obscure character and make it feel like a shared secret, then a status symbol, then a market. Labubu is simply one of the clearest examples so far in 2026. The fact that a plush monster can now move markets is itself the news.
