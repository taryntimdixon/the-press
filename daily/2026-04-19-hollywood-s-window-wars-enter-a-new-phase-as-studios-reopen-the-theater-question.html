Hollywood’s Window Wars Enter a New Phase as Studios Reopen the Theater Question

At CinemaCon and in earnings calls, the industry is signaling a partial return to theatrical-first strategy — but the new consensus may be narrower, more premium, and more conditional than exhibitors want.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 19, 2026 • 4:00 p.m. • 3016 words

The movie business entered April 2026 with an argument it thought it had already settled: what belongs in theaters, for how long, and on what terms. That argument is back, but it is no longer the simple post-pandemic fight between theatrical and streaming. It has become a broader debate over ownership, consolidation, labor, premium formats, and whether theatrical moviegoing is being rebuilt as a mass market or preserved as a high-margin niche.

The immediate spark is the turbulence around Warner Bros. Discovery’s future and the competing visions from Paramount Skydance and Netflix, which have both made public commitments that are intended to reassure theater owners. At CinemaCon 2026, Paramount Skydance chief David Ellison told exhibitors he would guarantee 30 movie releases a year between Paramount and Warner Bros. and said he was committing to a 45-day exclusive theatrical window, “starting today.” Netflix co-chief executive Ted Sarandos has also said that if Netflix’s Warner Bros. deal had prevailed, Warner Bros. films would still have run in theaters under a 45-day window. Theater owners, meanwhile, worry that the incentives of a more consolidated, debt-heavy ownership structure could still push in the opposite direction. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54fec9042d4e19dfde05348a24323a13?utm_source=openai))

This is not just a corporate chess match. It matters because the current moment is one of the strongest tests yet of the theatrical proposition after the pandemic. Box office recovery is real, but uneven and concentrated. Disney’s Hoppers is one example of a large-scale family release succeeding theatrically in 2026, with more than $158 million domestically and $355 million worldwide by mid-April. Yet Box Office Mojo’s 2026 domestic chart also shows a market still powered by a relatively small number of major titles and several re-releases, which is not the same thing as a broad, healthy middle class of films. The market’s top end is strong enough to support a revival narrative, but the breadth of that revival remains in doubt. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/release/rl284918529/?utm_source=openai))

What makes this year different is that the industry is discussing the theater not as the inevitable first stop for every film, but as a selectively deployed technology for attention. In practice, that means the theatrical run is being reserved for movies that can justify premium pricing, event marketing, and IMAX or Dolby presentation. IMAX says Christopher Nolan’s The Odyssey is the first feature shot entirely with IMAX film cameras, and the company has already put tickets on sale for its IMAX 70mm screenings a year ahead of release. Dolby and AMC have also announced a major expansion of Dolby Cinema at AMC in the United States, with 40 additional auditoriums to be added by the end of 2027, bringing the chain’s U.S. total to more than 200. The message is clear: premium large-format cinema is not a side hustle; it is increasingly the business model theater chains are betting on. ([imax.com](https://www.imax.com/news/the-odyssey-in-imax-70mm-film?utm_source=openai))

The new theatrical consensus is narrower than the old one

For most of Hollywood’s history, theatrical release was the default, even when a film’s ultimate economic life depended on television, cable, home video, or streaming. That hierarchy changed sharply during and after COVID-19, when studios experimented with day-and-date launches, shortened windows, and streaming-first strategies. The present argument is not about whether theatrical will survive. It already has. The real question is which films will count as theatrical films in the first place, and whether exhibitors can still shape the release calendar rather than merely react to it.

Windows have already compressed from traditional multi-month exclusivity to something much shorter for many major titles. Cinema United’s president and CEO, Michael O’Leary, said at CinemaCon that the average theatrical window for the top 100 films in 2025 was 37 days, three days longer than the year before. That is shorter than the standard theater owners once defended, but not as short as the pandemic’s most radical experiments. It also suggests the post-pandemic equilibrium is not a total collapse of exclusivity; it is a re-sorting of power. A film can still enjoy a meaningful opening on the big screen, but the window is now short enough to make the home release part of the launch cycle rather than the epilogue. ([screendaily.com](https://www.screendaily.com/news/film-industry-heads-talk-consolidation-windows-at-cinemacon/5215613.article?utm_source=openai))

That pressure tends to favor movies that can convert audiences quickly: franchise titles, horror, family films, prestige tentpoles, and the occasional breakout. It works less well for adult dramas, mid-budget comedies, and modestly marketed originals. The market structure thus encourages more of what looks like success and less of what looks like variety. The result is not simply fewer theatrical releases, but a market in which the theatrical window is increasingly a marketing tool for the titles most likely to perform under pressure. ([screendaily.com](https://www.screendaily.com/news/film-industry-heads-talk-consolidation-windows-at-cinemacon/5215613.article?utm_source=openai))

Exhibitors are trying to argue that the answer is not just fewer films, but different theaters. AMC’s laser rollout and Dolby’s expansion point to a cinema strategy built around image quality, sound, and luxury. IMAX’s expansion with Regal and other chains goes in the same direction. When premium formats become the product, the logic of the multiplex changes. Instead of being a place where everything arrives, the theater becomes the place where some films arrive first, in their best possible version, and at a higher price. That can stabilize revenues for exhibitors, but it also makes moviegoing more stratified. The danger is that theatrical becomes culturally more prestigious at the same time it becomes less accessible. ([news.dolby.com](https://news.dolby.com/en-WW/248438-dolby-and-amc-entertainment-announce-major-expansion-of-dolby-cinema-at-amc-in-the-us?utm_source=openai))

Consolidation is reshaping the release debate

The biggest complication in the current window fight is that the fight is happening alongside consolidation. Warner Bros. Discovery’s board has been moving through the competing Paramount Skydance and Netflix bids, with WBD publicly setting a special meeting date before later shifting toward discussions with Paramount after the Netflix path faltered. That dynamic matters because any deal large enough to reshape the studio landscape will also reshape how films are financed, scheduled, and distributed. The debate over theatrical windows is therefore not just about audience behavior; it is about who gets to decide the terms of access in the first place. ([wbd.com](https://www.wbd.com/news/warner-bros-discovery-sets-special-meeting-date-march-20-2026-and-unanimously-recommends?utm_source=openai))

Paramount Skydance’s effort to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery, if it succeeds, would combine one of the strongest movie-branded studios with a company that has long functioned as both studio and streaming platform. The company has also said it wants to raise its own annual output to at least 15 theatrical films in 2026 and, in broader merger discussions, to a combined slate of at least 30 theatrical films a year. On paper, that sounds like a pro-theatrical commitment. But exhibitors are skeptical, and for good reason. Output targets are not the same thing as actual wide releases, and they do not guarantee that films will be marketed in a way that supports long runs. They also do not address whether a combined company would be forced to service debt, cut costs, or streamline distribution in ways that reduce the diversity of films it can afford to keep in theaters. ([thewrap.com](https://www.thewrap.com/paramount-skydance-theatrical-releases-2026/?utm_source=openai))

Netflix complicates the conversation further because its theatrical posture has softened without abandoning its core identity. Sarandos has said the company would keep a 45-day theatrical window for Warner Bros. movies if its acquisition had gone through, and the streamer has increasingly treated theaters as one release channel among several. It has also used theatrical runs selectively, often to support awards campaigns, cultivate prestige, or extend the life of specific titles. That makes Netflix less ideologically anti-theatrical than it once appeared, but not necessarily pro-theatrical in the way exhibition wants. A 45-day window can be a concession while still preserving streamer-first economics. The contradiction is the point: the theater is being recognized as valuable precisely because it is no longer assumed to be the center of the system. ([yahoo.com](https://www.yahoo.com/entertainment/movies/articles/ted-sarandos-says-netflix-commit-164128046.html?utm_source=openai))

The fear among theater owners is not just that a studio would shorten windows further. It is that a smaller number of corporate gatekeepers would control more of the terms around release, marketing, and catalog access. Cinema United has said consolidation could give a smaller group of distributors even more power over windows, scheduling, screen placement, and access to libraries. That is why this fight feels larger than a single merger. It is about whether theaters remain partners in a broad distribution ecosystem or become the final presentation layer in a much more centralized media business. ([cinemaunited.org](https://cinemaunited.org/2026/02/27/cinema-united-re-affirms-concerns-around-consolidation/?utm_source=openai))

Awards season still rewards theatrical seriousness

One reason the theatrical debate has not disappeared is that awards culture still confers meaning on theatrical seriousness. The Academy’s 98th Oscars were held on Sunday, March 15, 2026, and the calendar still matters because awards visibility remains tied to theatrical presence in both perception and eligibility. Films aiming for Oscars attention often seek theatrical runs because theatrical visibility still carries prestige value, press attention, and campaign momentum. ([oscars.org](https://www.oscars.org/oscars/98th-oscars-and-academy-key-dates?utm_source=openai))

There is a practical reason for that. In the current awards ecosystem, the films that can dominate conversation often do so because they look and feel like events. Nolan’s The Odyssey is being framed in precisely those terms, but so are a range of prestige titles from major studios and specialty distributors. The films that break through tend to be those that can be discussed as cinematic experiences, not just content units. That helps explain why even streamers are willing to use theaters strategically. Awards voters and critics are still disproportionately influenced by the aura of theatrical presentation, even as audiences are far more fragmented in how they watch movies. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1974009992a3abb6c2d39e30d9480569?utm_source=openai))

Yet awards prestige can also obscure a harder truth: the academy calendar and the box office calendar are increasingly unrelated. A streaming-backed or streamer-distributed film can receive major awards recognition without materially strengthening the theatrical marketplace. Conversely, a solid theatrical release may leave little awards footprint. That gap means the industry often uses prestige films as proof that theaters still matter, when in fact they may mainly prove that prestige still matters. The theater is not disappearing, but it is being asked to do a narrower job: launch prestige, host spectacle, and support the brand value of the films least likely to depend on streaming immediacy. ([oscars.org](https://www.oscars.org/oscars/98th-oscars-and-academy-key-dates?utm_source=openai))

The labor picture is part of the story, not a sidebar

Theatrical release strategy is also a labor issue. The Bureau of Labor Statistics’ industry profile for motion picture and sound recording shows a sector that remains volatile, and the broader data on U.S. entertainment employment underscore how much the business has been shaped by recent production swings and industry restructuring. When studios shorten windows or move more titles toward direct streaming, they do not merely change a marketing strategy; they alter the path through which workers are paid and how often productions are greenlit. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/iag/tgs/iag512.htm?utm_source=openai))

That is one reason unions are watching consolidation so closely. A studio merger that promises more theatrical output can still reduce total leverage for writers, actors, directors, editors, and below-the-line crews if it narrows the number of buying entities and raises cost pressure on each release. Labor’s concern is not only about fewer jobs in the near term, though that is part of it. It is about the long-term structure of bargaining. When a smaller set of buyers controls more of the theatrical and streaming pipeline, workers negotiate with fewer decision-makers who have more power to standardize terms. The BLS data do not answer that policy question by themselves, but they show why it cannot be ignored. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/iag/tgs/iag512.htm?utm_source=openai))

The issue extends to exhibitors too. Theater jobs are different from production jobs, but the same structural trend applies: if the release slate becomes more concentrated, the economics of screens, staffing, and programming become more precarious. A local cinema is not just a venue; it is a small labor market tied to scheduling, concessions, maintenance, and management. As films become more event-driven, the theater’s calendar becomes more dependent on a handful of weeks each year. That can produce spikes in activity without creating durable stability. The result is a business with headline-grabbing openings and thin margins in between. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/year/2026/?area=XSPAI&grossesOption=totalGrosses&utm_source=openai))

Technology is helping theaters, but also raising the bar

Technology is one of the few areas where the theatrical business has a clearly positive narrative. Premium projection, better sound, laser systems, and expanded IMAX partnerships are all real investments. Dolby says its AMC expansion will push the U.S. total to more than 200 Dolby Cinema auditoriums. IMAX, meanwhile, continues to add partner locations and tout IMAX 70mm as a unique draw. These upgrades matter because they give audiences a reason to leave home when streaming convenience is otherwise overwhelming. But they also mean the theater experience has to be notably better to justify the trip. That is a higher standard than the pre-streaming era demanded. ([news.dolby.com](https://news.dolby.com/en-WW/248438-dolby-and-amc-entertainment-announce-major-expansion-of-dolby-cinema-at-amc-in-the-us?utm_source=openai))

There is a subtler technological story here too: data. Theater owners at CinemaCon have described more experimentation with pricing, audience data, and marketing partnerships. That can make exhibition more efficient, but it can also make theaters more legible to studios in ways that shift power upward. If a studio knows exactly which showtimes, format combinations, and audience segments are best performing, it can optimize releases for short-term yield rather than broad cultural circulation. One tradeoff of the data-rich cinema is that it may become more responsive while becoming less forgiving. Smaller films, riskier films, and films that rely on word of mouth may be squeezed out by titles engineered to open hard and move fast. ([thewrap.com](https://www.thewrap.com/creative-content/movies/cinema-united-chief-michael-oleary-interview-warner-bros-paramount/?utm_source=openai))

That is why the technology story cannot be reduced to “better projectors save movies.” Better projectors can help save theaters, but not necessarily the variety of films that once supported them. The more exhibition leans on premium spaces, the more it risks reinforcing a two-tier market: event films in premium rooms and everything else elsewhere. That may still be commercially rational. It may even be the only rational path available to some chains. But it means the industry’s revival story should be read with caution. A healthier premium box office is not the same thing as a healthier film culture. ([news.dolby.com](https://news.dolby.com/en-WW/248438-dolby-and-amc-entertainment-announce-major-expansion-of-dolby-cinema-at-amc-in-the-us?utm_source=openai))

The counterargument: the theater is adapting, not dying

The strongest argument against alarmism is that the theater business has already adapted more than many expected. The box office is not at 2019 levels, but it has regained enough momentum for studios to prioritize theatrical marketing again. Family films, horror, and event franchises continue to work. Some streamers are even experimenting with more visible theatrical strategies. Netflix has not abandoned cinemas entirely; it has instead learned to use them selectively. The result is not the end of theatrical, but a more disciplined, more revenue-conscious version of it. On this reading, the current reshaping of windows is a healthy correction after the pandemic’s emergency experiments. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/year/2026/?area=XSPAI&grossesOption=totalGrosses&utm_source=openai))

There is truth in that. Moviegoing has not vanished, and the market still rewards films that can create urgency. CinemaCon’s exuberance is not entirely marketing theater. Exhibitors are investing in upgrades because audiences do still value the communal event of seeing a movie on the big screen, especially when the presentation offers something home screens cannot match. The theatrical business may be smaller and more selective than it once was, but smaller is not the same as finished. If anything, 2026 may be the year the industry stops pretending that every film needs the same release path and begins admitting that the theater is a premium medium rather than a universal one. ([news.dolby.com](https://news.dolby.com/en-WW/248438-dolby-and-amc-entertainment-announce-major-expansion-of-dolby-cinema-at-amc-in-the-us?utm_source=openai))

Still, the argument for adaptation is strongest when it is also honest about what has been lost. Universal’s old pandemic-era model of a 17-day minimum window has already been replaced by a 31-day minimum for its 2026 slate, and the studio has separately said some 2026 releases will be held even longer. That is a real change, but it is also evidence of fragmentation rather than a clean return to the old order. The business has not converged on a single best model. It has splintered into title-by-title bargaining, premium exceptions, and local arrangements. That is adaptation, but it is also a kind of permanent negotiation. ([thewrap.com](https://www.thewrap.com/creative-content/movies/universal-theatrical-window-changes-explained/?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Over the next few months, three questions will determine whether this moment becomes a real turning point or just another cycle of industry talk. First, what happens to the Warner Bros. transaction? If Paramount Skydance prevails, the company’s promises about theatrical output and 45-day windows will be tested against the realities of debt, labor costs, and integration. If a renewed Netflix path were to re-emerge in any form, theater owners’ worst fears about a streaming-centered studio would intensify even if the company kept some theatrical commitments. Second, do 2026’s biggest films actually translate into broad attendance, or do they mostly reinforce the premium-event model? The answer will be visible in box-office durability, not just opening weekends. Third, will labor negotiations force studios to connect release strategy with employment, residuals, and production volume more openly than they have so far? ([wbd.com](https://www.wbd.com/news/warner-bros-discovery-sets-special-meeting-date-march-20-2026-and-unanimously-recommends?utm_source=openai))

There is a version of the future in which theaters survive by becoming more luxurious, more specialized, and more important for a smaller number of films. There is another in which they become bargaining chips inside larger media conglomerates, with every promising theatrical pledge later diluted by portfolio strategy. The evidence in April 2026 points to both futures at once. The market is strong enough to make theater owners hopeful, and fragile enough to make them nervous. Studios are still talking about moviegoing as an experience, but the experience they are describing is increasingly selective, premium, and conditional. That is not a collapse. It is a redefinition. ([boxofficemojo.com](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/year/2026/?area=XSPAI&grossesOption=totalGrosses&utm_source=openai))

For audiences, the likely outcome is less dramatic than the headlines suggest. The multiplex will not disappear. But the old promise that every big movie would get a generous, reliable theatrical life before being folded into home viewing is already gone. In its place is a more transactional compact: come out for the event, pay extra for the premium room, and accept that most of the rest of film culture will arrive quickly at home. Whether that compact produces a healthier movie business or merely a more unequal one is the question the industry is now asking in public, almost every day, on the exhibit floor, in earnings calls, and in the shadow of the biggest merger fight in years. ([screendaily.com](https://www.screendaily.com/news/film-industry-heads-talk-consolidation-windows-at-cinemacon/5215613.article?utm_source=openai))
