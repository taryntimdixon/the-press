Courts Are Rewriting the Rules of the 2026 Midterms

A wave of rulings and lawsuits over voter data, mail ballots, and congressional maps is forcing states, courts, and the White House into a fight over who really controls the next election.

By The Press • April 19, 2026 • 4:00 p.m.

The 2026 midterms are still months away, but the machinery that will determine them is already being litigated in federal courts, state courts, and in direct confrontation with the White House. In the past two weeks, federal judges have rejected the Justice Department’s attempts to force states to hand over unredacted voter-registration data in Rhode Island and Massachusetts, adding to a growing line of losses for the administration in similar cases across the country. At the same time, Democrats, civil-rights groups, and state officials have sued to block a new White House executive order that seeks to narrow mail voting and create a federal role in determining who may receive ballots by mail. And the partisan fight over congressional maps has continued to move through the courts, with California and Texas both receiving emergency relief that keeps their redrawn maps in place for now. The result is not a single election story but a broader constitutional struggle over the lines of authority that govern American democracy. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

What makes this moment newsworthy is not just the number of cases, but the fact that the disputes sit at the intersection of three durable flashpoints: who runs elections, what data the federal government may demand from states, and whether the Supreme Court will keep allowing aggressive partisanship in redistricting and election rules. Those questions are no longer academic. They are being decided in real time against the calendar of the 2026 cycle, with the Federal Election Commission already posting the 2026 congressional primary dates and warning that the schedule remains subject to change in some states. That matters because litigation over voting rules becomes harder to unwind once ballots start moving, and in many places that point is only weeks away. ([fec.gov](https://www.fec.gov/resources/cms-content/documents/2026pdates.pdf?utm_source=openai))

The voter-data cases are now the clearest sign of an institutional confrontation

The most immediate clash is over the Justice Department’s campaign to collect state voter-registration rolls and other election records. The department’s own public release says it sued six additional states in December 2025 for failure to provide statewide voter registration lists, and AP reported that the campaign has since expanded into a broad, multi-state litigation effort. In the Rhode Island case, AP reported on April 17 that federal officials were seeking detailed voter data including dates of birth, addresses, driver’s license numbers, and partial Social Security numbers, and that the state and civil-rights groups pressed back hard. AP also reported that at least 12 states have either provided or promised to provide the records, while many others have resisted. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-six-additional-states-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The latest Rhode Island ruling is especially significant because it sharpened a line that has already appeared in Massachusetts and elsewhere: the Department of Justice says it needs voter data to enforce election law and ensure accurate rolls, but courts have so far been skeptical that the relevant statutes authorize sweeping requests for full personal datasets. In Massachusetts, AP reported that a judge dismissed the federal lawsuit after finding the department had not taken the required statutory steps before demanding production. In Rhode Island, AP said Judge Mary McElroy concluded that federal law does not permit the kind of “fishing expedition” the government sought. Similar setbacks had already occurred in California, Michigan, and Oregon, according to AP’s reporting. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/b4eefdcac577965913f3e4969bcbb7a6?utm_source=openai))

This line of cases is important for two reasons. First, it tests whether the federal government can use election-integrity language to gain access to the internal data architecture of state election offices. Second, it creates a record that may shape later disputes over whether such data can be shared with other federal agencies. In Rhode Island, AP reported that DOJ attorneys acknowledged the data was being sought so it could be shared with the Department of Homeland Security to check citizenship status. That detail intensified concerns among state officials and privacy advocates and helped turn what might otherwise have been a technical records dispute into a larger fight over the federal government’s reach. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

There is, however, a credible counterargument. Supporters of the department’s effort say election security depends on accurate registration lists, and that states have sometimes been slow to remove ineligible records or to cooperate with federal oversight. The DOJ’s own public statements say “accurate voter rolls” are essential to fair and free elections, and its press releases repeatedly frame the lawsuits as enforcement of the National Voter Registration Act and the Help America Vote Act. That argument is not frivolous. Election offices do need accurate lists, and federal law does give the Justice Department some powers to enforce election statutes. The problem is that the current litigation has turned on whether those powers extend to full personal datasets and whether the department has supplied a lawful, transparent, and narrow justification for obtaining them. The courts so far have answered no, at least on the records currently before them. ([justice.gov](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-sues-six-additional-states-failure-provide-voter-registration-rolls?utm_source=openai))

The broader political backdrop is also shifting. Some states have cooperated with or promised to provide the data, while others have resisted, creating a patchwork that may complicate the federal government’s ability to present the effort as a uniform national audit. That uneven response is part of what makes the litigation politically potent: it allows the administration to say it is enforcing the law, while giving critics a ready-made example of overreach into state election systems. Even if the government eventually prevails in some jurisdictions, the cases are already producing a public record about privacy, list maintenance, and the limits of federal compulsion. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

Mail voting has become the second front

The White House’s new executive order on mail voting has produced a separate wave of lawsuits. On April 2, 2026, Democrats sued to block President Donald Trump’s latest executive order restricting mail voting, arguing that the Constitution gives states and Congress, not the president, authority over election rules. AP reported that the complaint was filed by Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries, the Democratic National Committee, and other party organizations. The challengers said Trump’s order would narrow access to mail ballots and intrude on state election systems. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8d58e1e194c3b85a94a562ef8807a016?utm_source=openai))

A second lawsuit filed by civil-rights and voting-rights organizations followed soon after, and the public messaging around the order has made clear how broad the dispute could become. According to AP’s reporting and subsequent analyses by election-law groups, the order directs the Postal Service to refuse delivery of some ballots unless they meet federal criteria, urges the creation of a federal voter eligibility list, and would condition mail-ballot delivery on data that states do not uniformly collect or verify in the same way. Those details explain why plaintiffs are characterizing the order not as a simple administrative change but as an attempted rewrite of election law. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8d58e1e194c3b85a94a562ef8807a016?utm_source=openai))

The litigation is not occurring in a vacuum. AP has also reported that Trump’s earlier election executive order, issued last year, was blocked in substantial part by federal judges after states challenged it as unconstitutional. In that earlier round, a federal court blocked most of the order against Washington and Oregon, reinforcing the basic proposition that states and Congress, not the president, regulate election mechanics. The National Governors Association also issued a public statement reaffirming state authority over election administration after the latest move, underscoring that the fight has moved beyond partisan litigants to elected officials who view the order as an intrusion on constitutional federalism regardless of their views on mail voting itself. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9331367a061e28a74cdda0e1f25befd0?utm_source=openai))

Yet the mail-voting dispute also exposes a real policy tension. Critics of expansive mail voting argue that election officials face administrative and security burdens, that ballot-chain rules are complex, and that deadlines matter. The Supreme Court has shown continuing interest in those questions. AP reported in 2024 that a conservative federal appeals court said Mississippi could not count mail-in ballots arriving after Election Day, and AP’s later reporting has treated that line of litigation as evidence that the legal status of grace periods remains unsettled. That does not make the White House’s move lawful, but it does show why the administration has chosen a theme—ballot integrity—that already resonates with some judges and voters. If the Court ultimately narrows state counting practices, the executive-order fight could become partly moot as state laws are restricted through the judiciary rather than by presidential decree. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d65b943c27e9e7c5247dc2c14d3dbb15?utm_source=openai))

For now, the bigger immediate question is whether a president can order agencies to alter the mechanics of ballot distribution and return in the first place. That is a core separation-of-powers dispute, not just an argument over the wisdom of mail voting. The plaintiffs say the Constitution’s election clauses place that authority with the states and Congress. The White House, by contrast, is acting as though federal administrative power can be leveraged to standardize ballot rules nationwide. The courts will decide whether that theory has any real constitutional footing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8d58e1e194c3b85a94a562ef8807a016?utm_source=openai))

The redistricting war remains the most obvious partisan prize

While the data and mail-ballot cases focus on process, the redistricting battle is about power in its most literal form: who gets seats. The high-stakes struggle began when Texas moved to redraw its U.S. House map in a way Republicans said would improve their political performance, and opponents said would dilute Latino and Black voting strength. AP reported in August 2025 that the Texas House approved the new map and that Republican Gov. Greg Abbott vowed to sign it quickly. The move set off a tit-for-tat cycle that by February 2026 had reached the Supreme Court, which allowed California to use its new voter-approved congressional map that is favorable to Democrats. AP said the California plan could help Democrats flip up to five seats, while the Texas map had already been allowed to remain in place for the 2026 cycle despite a lower-court ruling that it likely discriminated on the basis of race. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/882eae23a280a68dd6e0fc5a3bf11a0d?utm_source=openai))

The battle is not just theoretical. In November 2025, a federal panel blocked Texas from using its new map in the midterms, but the Supreme Court later allowed it to stay in place for now, and California’s map survived emergency challenges. The practical effect is to freeze, at least temporarily, maps that could alter the House battlefield by as many as five seats in either direction. AP’s February reporting on California made clear that the court’s emergency docket has become a decisive battleground for redistricting even before the merits are fully resolved. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/8362a34b739ea91d37a190eee1b6a6d1?utm_source=openai))

That mid-decade redistricting fight has also spread to other states. AP reported on April 14 that Maryland Democrats rejected Gov. Wes Moore’s push to redraw the state’s congressional map, a setback for a party that had hoped to blunt Trump’s redistricting campaign. The same AP report said Republicans were moving toward special-session redistricting in Florida, while Democrats were trying to advance a Virginia referendum that could authorize a new map. Meanwhile, AP reported in February that a Virginia court had effectively blocked Democrats’ planned referendum, though the Virginia Supreme Court later said the April 21 vote could go forward while the legal fight continued. Those developments show how quickly redistricting can become a national chain reaction once one state breaks the norm of decennial mapmaking. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4481f51e7f1f007be4ba02d91b3bfa63?utm_source=openai))

The strongest defense of mid-decade redistricting is bluntly partisan: if one side is willing to change the map, the other side will respond or lose the advantage. The more principled defense is that state political majorities have authority to respond to changed circumstances and to use ordinary lawmaking to create maps they believe are lawful. But the cost of that logic is that it normalizes a permanent escalation. Independent commissions, once sold as a way to take the poison out of redistricting, now face renewed pressure wherever one party believes a court, voter referendum, or legislative maneuver can deliver a short-term gain. Maryland’s failure to redraw, Virginia’s referendum fight, and California’s and Texas’s competing maps all show how fragile anti-gerrymandering reforms can be when national control of Congress is at stake. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4481f51e7f1f007be4ba02d91b3bfa63?utm_source=openai))

The courts themselves are becoming political terrain

What makes this moment different from earlier election cycles is the way procedural disputes are colliding with the Supreme Court’s expanding willingness to decide standing and election-power questions on an emergency basis. AP reported in January that the Court revived a Republican challenge to a late-arriving-mail-ballot law in Mississippi, a sign that the justices remain open to revisiting the rules governing when ballots may be counted. AP’s reporting on the emergency docket in the Texas and California map cases suggests that redistricting too is now being shaped at least as much by interim orders as by final opinions. That is a significant shift because it lowers the threshold for election litigation and invites more pre-election challenges to state procedures. Combined with the Court’s emergency rulings in map disputes, the trend suggests the judiciary is not merely resolving fights; it is setting the terms of future fights. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/d65b943c27e9e7c5247dc2c14d3dbb15?utm_source=openai))

There are signs of pushback from the other branches. House Democrats launched a task force on April 15 to overhaul ethics rules and protect access to the ballot, and they framed it as part of a broader anti-corruption message heading into the midterms. A separate Democratic “shadow hearing” on democracy issues has also focused on election security and voting rights. Senate Democrats, meanwhile, have warned that any attempt by a president to take over election administration is unconstitutional because the Elections Clause assigns administration to the states and gives Congress, not the executive, the power to set rules for federal elections. Those are partisan arguments, but they also map onto a real separation-of-powers question that is now being tested in court. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/1eeaee9ca4f9ea78ad2d238f379d5991?utm_source=openai))

Some of the deepest uncertainty here is administrative rather than doctrinal. Election offices are already preparing for 2026, and the EAC’s current planning-and-logistics resources stress that local administrators must prepare for interruptions to core services, identify bottlenecks, and build contingency plans for everything from supply-chain disruptions to voter communication. The EAC has also published 2026 election-security grant guidance showing that Congress appropriated $45 million for states and territories this year to improve election administration and security. Those resources underscore how complex the election system already is: it is a mix of state responsibility, federal support, and local implementation. If courts or executives force last-minute changes, the burden falls on local administrators who must reprogram forms, retrain poll workers, adjust mailing systems, and defend procedures in public while still running elections on schedule. ([eac.gov](https://www.eac.gov/election-officials/clearinghouse-resources-planning-logistics?utm_source=openai))

Election security is the language both sides use, but they mean different things

One reason these disputes are so politically durable is that both camps use the same phrase—election security—to mean very different things. For the Justice Department and the White House, security often means tighter voter-eligibility checks, more federal review, and constraints on mail voting. For states, civil-rights organizations, and many election officials, security means privacy, resilience, clarity, and the ability to run elections without federal interference. The EAC’s official materials, which stress contingency planning, accessible design, and support for election administrators, reflect that latter conception: security as operational capacity rather than centralized control. ([eac.gov](https://www.eac.gov/election-officials/clearinghouse-resources-planning-logistics?utm_source=openai))

That distinction is not semantic; it determines whether the public sees a safeguard or a threat. If the federal government collects voter files, some voters will suspect the data will be used for surveillance or purges. If states refuse to share, others will suspect they are hiding noncompliance. If the White House seeks to set ballot-return rules, supporters call it uniformity; opponents call it constitutional trespass. The politics of 2026 are therefore being shaped not only by candidate quality and district lines but by competing narratives about administrative trust. Once trust becomes partisan, every procedural rule can be recast as either protection or suppression. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

How the current rulings could shape the midterms

The practical consequences of these cases will reach beyond the headlines. If the DOJ keeps losing voter-data cases, the federal government’s leverage over state election offices could shrink, at least until Congress acts. If the White House’s mail-voting order is blocked, state deadlines and ballot-receipt rules will likely remain in place for 2026. If the Supreme Court continues to allow aggressive partisan maps, then House control may depend more on court tolerance for redistricting than on campaign strategy in many districts. And if the Court narrows candidate standing or mail-ballot grace periods, election litigation may accelerate even as ballots are being printed. Each path favors a different institutional actor, but all of them make the election system more legally volatile. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

There is also a democratic-credibility cost. The more voters hear about judges, injunctions, emergency appeals, and conflicting rulings, the more likely they are to believe the rules are being written after the fact. That perception can become self-fulfilling if candidates and activists treat every adverse procedural ruling as evidence of illegitimacy. The 2026 cycle is especially vulnerable because many of the disputes concern process rather than policy; voters may not see the direct link between a ruling over voter-roll access in Rhode Island and the eventual count in a competitive House district, but the institutional chain is real. Rules about data, deadlines, and district lines all shape participation and representation. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

The next phase of this story will likely come from three places. First, expect more rulings in the voter-data cases as states continue to resist DOJ demands and as judges decide whether the department’s legal theory can survive repeated challenges. Second, expect new injunctions and appeals in the mail-voting cases, especially as states argue that presidential orders cannot override state election laws in the narrow time window before ballots go out. Third, expect the redistricting fight to remain fluid as appeals move quickly and as state courts, federal courts, and the Supreme Court continue to resolve map disputes in the shadow of the midterms. The most important signal may not be any single ruling, but whether institutions can still agree on who gets to make the rules before the campaign clock runs out. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))

For now, the broad picture is clear. The 2026 midterms are being shaped less by one dramatic crisis than by a steady accumulation of institutional tests. The Justice Department is testing the limits of federal power over state voter files. The White House is testing the limits of executive power over election procedures. The Supreme Court is testing how far it will let parties go in redrawing political maps and challenging ballot rules. And states are testing whether they can still defend their traditional authority without being overwhelmed by the tempo of federal litigation. That is not merely a story about election law. It is a story about whether American democracy can still agree on the rules before it argues about the results. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c79e6f395f4b296ce91d3eeff172365a?utm_source=openai))
