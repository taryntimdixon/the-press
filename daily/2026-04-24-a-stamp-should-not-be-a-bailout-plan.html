A Stamp Should Not Be a Bailout Plan

The Postal Service’s 2026 cash alarm has revived an old habit: asking customers and carriers to absorb a structural mismatch one filing at a time. If Congress wants universal service, it should fund the public obligations openly instead of pretending that higher stamps, temporary surcharges and emergency balance-sheet maneuvers are a durable strategy.

By The Press • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

When a public institution starts financing itself like a distressed operator, the country should stop pretending it is merely going through another rough quarter. That is the clearer lesson of the Postal Service’s spring 2026 sequence: a request for a temporary package surcharge tied to transportation costs on March 25; a notice on April 9 proposing to raise the price of a First-Class Mail Forever stamp from 78 cents to 82 cents in July; and, in the same period, an extraordinary decision to suspend the Postal Service’s employer contributions for Federal Employees Retirement System annuities because of what its own internal materials described as a pending liquidity crisis that could leave USPS out of cash as early as February 2027.

It is tempting to treat these as separate stories. One is about fuel and transportation. Another is about postal rates. Another is about pensions, cash flow and Washington accounting. But together they reveal something larger and less comfortable. The United States keeps insisting that it wants a universal postal network — six-day delivery, broad geographic reach, a nationwide retail footprint, election-mail capacity, medication delivery, and dependable service to places private carriers often price differently or avoid — while continuing to finance that mission as though it were mostly a shrinking product line that ought to survive on postage and package competition alone.

That contradiction is not new. What is new is how plainly it is now showing up in the operating choices. In the span of weeks, the Postal Service moved from routine-looking rate mechanics to emergency-sounding liquidity preservation. The Postal Regulatory Commission’s published market-dominant rate authority for March 27, 2026 showed 4.803 percent available authority for First-Class Mail. On April 9, USPS used that framework to file for price changes that included the proposed 82-cent Forever stamp. Separately, the agency asked the PRC for a temporary 8 percent increase on several competitive package products as a transportation-related bridge measure. Then came the retirement-contribution suspension, which USPS explained in an April FAQ by saying the Board of Governors had decided to stop those employer annuity payments effective April 10 because of the prospect of running out of cash as early as February 2027.

That is not ordinary housekeeping. It is what an unresolved public-policy problem looks like when it finally begins to crowd the balance sheet.

The real question is not whether a stamp should cost 82 cents

By itself, a four-cent increase is easy to trivialize. Many households will barely notice it, and USPS is right that the commission’s system exists precisely to permit regulated increases rather than freeze prices in place. The PRC, not Congress, sets the cap structure for market-dominant products, and USPS makes the filing within that framework. On that narrow point, nothing unusual is happening. The proposed increase is legal. It fits the current regulatory machinery.

But legality is not the same thing as a strategy, and the machinery does not answer the prior civic question. The prior question is what part of the postal network exists because the nation wants a public service and what part should truly be left to product revenue and competition. The PRC can tell us how much rate authority USPS has. It cannot settle the deeper matter of whether universal postal service in the twenty-first century can honestly be treated as self-funding without repeatedly shifting the pressure to customers, workers and communities that lack good alternatives.

That pressure shift is the habit this spring has made impossible to ignore. A temporary surcharge asks package customers to absorb transportation volatility. A stamp increase asks remaining letter-mail users to absorb more of the institution’s structural mismatch. A retirement-payment suspension uses a long-term obligation as a short-term liquidity valve. Each step has its own rationale. Taken together, they amount to a system trying to buy time by charging more where it can, deferring where it can, and hoping that Congress will postpone the larger settlement yet again.

The Government Accountability Office has been warning for years that this is not a normal turnaround story. In December 2025, GAO said USPS has lost money almost every fiscal year since 2007, called its business model unsustainable, and said there is a fundamental tension between the level of service Congress expects and the revenue USPS can reasonably be expected to generate. GAO’s updated postal primer, also issued in December 2025, reiterated that USPS has long been expected to fulfill nationwide service requirements while being financially self-sufficient — and that this mismatch is why USPS’s financial viability has remained on GAO’s High Risk List since 2009.

That should be the frame for any honest opinion about the current rate fight. The problem is not that postage ever rises. The problem is that postage has become one of the country’s favorite ways to avoid admitting that a universal service obligation is, in part, a public budget choice.

The country wants a public network but still talks about a product business

Listen to how postal debates usually unfold. Advocates of the institution emphasize what USPS uniquely does: it goes to every address, supports voting by mail, carries medicines, reaches rural customers, preserves local post office access, and serves as a civic storefront in communities that have lost other forms of public presence. Critics, meanwhile, often talk as though USPS were mostly a legacy seller of First-Class Mail that has been slow to cut costs, slow to modernize and too constrained by labor and politics.

Both descriptions contain truth. Neither is sufficient on its own. The first leaves out real operating inefficiencies and the undeniable fact of long-term volume decline. The second leaves out that private firms do not bear the same obligations and are not asked to keep an integrated national system available on equal terms simply because the public expects it. The conceptual error in Washington is trying to combine the hardest parts of both models: demanding the reach and resilience of a public network while insisting on the financing logic of a stand-alone enterprise.

USPS itself often contributes to the confusion. On one hand, its public statements still note that it generally receives no tax dollars for operating expenses and relies on the sale of postage, products and services. On the other hand, its own strategic and government-relations materials now openly describe the organization as trapped in an unsustainable operating model. Its Delivering for America plan argues that modernization, pricing reform and network redesign can restore financial stability, and USPS says the plan is meant to reverse a projected $160 billion in losses over ten years. But even that ambitious framing keeps running into the same reality: some parts of the mission are plainly civic obligations, not merely commercial offerings, and they do not become less civic because the bill is routed through postage rather than appropriations.

The Postal Service Reform Act of 2022 was important precisely because it acknowledged at least part of this truth. It removed the old prefunding requirement for retiree health benefits and required future postal retirees to enroll in Medicare, among other changes. That was substantial relief. Yet it did not finally answer GAO’s more stubborn question: what level of postal service the nation requires, how those services should be funded, and what institutional structure makes that arrangement sustainable.

That unresolved question is why the same arguments recur every time USPS rates go up or service changes. If the institution were really just a business, the answer would be straightforward: charge what the market can bear, cut unprofitable commitments and redesign the network to maximize returns. But if it is really a national service utility, then some commitments are not reducible to market tests. The public cannot keep demanding one model and paying for the other.

Service changes are not abstractions when the network is local

One reason the present financing approach is politically corrosive is that customers do not experience it as elegant reform. They experience it as paying more while hearing constant warnings that service may be slower, less legible or more uneven than before.

USPS has defended its Delivering for America service-standard refinements as necessary modernization. In a March 20, 2025 release, it said the refinements were estimated to save at least $36 billion over ten years and emphasized that the standard First-Class Mail delivery time would not exceed five days. The second phase took effect on July 1, 2025. The agency’s larger strategic materials likewise say the network transformation is intended to deliver more reliable service and improved work environments.

Those are real claims, and they deserve to be judged with real evidence rather than reflexive nostalgia for the pre-reform network. But the public evidence has been mixed enough that triumphalism is not credible. The USPS Office of Inspector General’s service-performance work continues to track trouble spots around the country, and as of this month the OIG lists multiple open 2026 delivery-operations projects, including Lynchburg, Norfolk, Virginia Beach, Suffolk, and election-mail readiness for the 2026 midterms. Its January 2026 oversight report on Delivering for America said USPS has made meaningful investments in infrastructure, fleet modernization and pricing reforms, but that service performance has been inconsistent and financial outcomes have fallen short of break-even targets.

Local reporting fills in what nationwide talking points often smooth over. In Virginia, WSLS reported on April 20 that a federal audit of the Lynchburg processing center cited delayed mail, package-scanning problems and security concerns. In Michigan’s Upper Peninsula, local reporting has documented prolonged delivery disruptions and community frustration. And Nieman Journalism Lab reported in February that publishers in several states were dealing with subscriber complaints and delivery delays after USPS changes, underscoring that local newspapers — one of the country’s most fragile public goods — are unusually exposed when the mail network becomes less predictable.

That matters because repeated rate increases are easier to justify politically when service is clearly dependable. The reverse is also true. Once the public starts to feel that the institution is charging more while asking for patience, longer windows and understanding about transition costs, every rate filing begins to look less like normal regulation and more like a levy on dependence.

That perception can be unfair to postal workers and even unfair to managers trying to keep a difficult network functioning. But it is a rational public response to the current settlement. A system financed through drift will be judged through drift: another increase here, another local complaint there, another hearing on reliability, another warning that the alternative is worse. Over time, that is a recipe for distrust.

The labor question is not separate from the service question

Postal labor appears in these debates as a line item, a bargaining issue or a political constituency. It should be understood more basically than that. Labor is the mechanism by which universal service happens. The work remains physical, repetitive, geographically dispersed and difficult to standardize away: accepting, sorting, transporting, casing, carrying, redelivering, staffing counters, handling accountable mail and keeping routes functioning in every kind of place and weather.

That is why calls for greater “discipline” too often smuggle in an unspoken premise: that when Congress refuses to settle the public-financing problem, labor should absorb part of the difference. Sometimes that shows up as pressure on wages or staffing. Sometimes it shows up as churn, route strain, worsening morale or a widening gap between public expectations and what the workforce can sustainably deliver. Sometimes it shows up in more symbolic ways, as it did this month, when employer retirement contributions became one of the available emergency levers.

USPS says the FERS action is temporary and that there will not be any immediate detrimental impact to current or future retirees if normal payments are temporarily withheld. That may be true as a near-term actuarial matter. But it is still politically and morally revealing. When an institution starts preserving liquidity by suspending employer retirement contributions while telling the country it may run out of cash within a year, workers are being told that their long-term security is part of the institution’s crisis toolkit.

This collides with a live bargaining moment. The National Association of Letter Carriers formally opened contract negotiations with USPS on February 25, 2026. NALC has emphasized wages, compliance and the stakes for more than 200,000 city letter carriers, and union materials have noted that the current National Agreement is set to expire on May 22, 2026. The American Postal Workers Union, meanwhile, has continued processing retroactive-pay matters and related contract business in 2026 for employees under its own agreements. So the spring liquidity alarm is arriving precisely when workers are asking what a viable postal job is supposed to look like in a system that still demands national reach and daily physical performance.

That timing is not incidental. It goes to the heart of what kind of institution Congress thinks it is preserving. If postal employment is treated only as overhead, then every financial shortfall becomes an argument for labor restraint. If postal employment is understood as infrastructure, then staffing, compensation and workplace stability become part of service policy itself.

That does not mean every union demand should be granted. It does mean the country should stop pretending that a labor-intensive universal network can be preserved indefinitely by squeezing the people who operate it whenever the larger financing model fails to add up.

The best version of the opposing case

There is a serious objection to the argument for more explicit public financing, and it should be met directly. Once Congress starts paying for a larger share of USPS obligations, the Postal Service could become more vulnerable to annual appropriations fights, partisan meddling and pork-barrel politics. A more self-funded model, even an imperfect one, offers a measure of insulation. It also imposes discipline. If the institution can raise rates within regulated limits, redesign old facilities, consolidate operations and use pricing authority to adapt to inflation and changing demand, why rush to ask taxpayers broadly to fund services they may use only lightly?

That argument is not frivolous. It is strengthened by the reality that USPS has already received meaningful relief and flexibility in recent years. The 2022 reform law was substantial. The PRC this month also granted USPS a multi-year temporary conditional waiver related to retirement-obligation rate authority, giving the agency more room to use revenue it had already been provided through that system. Congress has held new hearings on the Postal Service’s financial future. USPS has introduced and defended a ten-year modernization plan. The institution is not simply standing still with a tin cup.

Moreover, the critics are right that declining mail volume is not a myth. House Oversight materials from March cite testimony noting the drop from a historic peak of 213 billion mail pieces per year to about 109 billion today. No opinion worth publishing should pretend that the postal network can be frozen in its high-volume past. Efficiency matters. Network redesign matters. Better cost accounting matters. Some rate increases are unavoidable in a lower-volume environment.

But none of that rescues the underlying contradiction. The anti-subsidy case almost always ends up wanting universal service after all — broad coverage, civic reliability, election readiness, remote-route service and political accountability when things go wrong. Once those commitments are retained, the issue is no longer whether public obligations exist. The issue is whether they will be financed honestly and measured openly, or hidden inside postage, regulatory waivers, workforce strain and emergency balance-sheet maneuvers.

Why the current compromise keeps failing in public

The present settlement asks too much of indirection. It asks rate authority to do the work of policy clarity. It asks customers to interpret repeated price changes as modernization rather than drift. It asks workers to treat emergency financial tactics as temporary technicalities rather than signs of institutional strain. It asks communities to accept local disruption as the inevitable price of national reform, even when transparency about local effects is fragmented and hard to compare.

That is why the debate keeps coming back in such brittle form. If lawmakers want USPS to be substantially self-supporting, they should be willing to redefine the service map and say openly which obligations no longer warrant national cross-subsidy. If lawmakers want something closer to the present universal-service posture, they should admit that some part of that posture belongs on the public ledger. What they should not keep doing is preserving the politics of universalism while outsourcing the cost of that choice to the remaining users of letter mail and the people who sort and carry it.

The distributional consequences also matter. A four-cent stamp increase is negligible to many households. It is not equally negligible to every organization that still depends on the mail at volume, especially local institutions with thin margins. Temporary package surcharges may be absorbable for large shippers that can optimize across carriers and contracts. They are not experienced the same way by smaller businesses or customers in places with fewer alternatives. Service degradations are likewise uneven. Wealthier urban or suburban customers often have more substitutes, more digital workarounds and more delivery options. Rural residents, older residents, local news organizations and communities that still rely heavily on physical access do not.

Universal service is expensive precisely because unequal geography is expensive. If the country believes that inequality of place should be softened — that some national baseline of delivery access, retail presence and civic reliability is worth preserving — then the cost of that choice should be socialized more candidly. Otherwise “self-sufficiency” becomes a polite label for letting the most dependent users subsidize the public’s own ambivalence.

What Congress should actually do

First, Congress should define a core set of postal obligations that are civic in character rather than purely commercial in character, and it should attach an explicit funding mechanism to them. That does not require turning the entire Postal Service into a conventional appropriated agency. It does require acknowledging that some commitments — service to remote communities, election-mail readiness, minimum national retail access, and a basic physical communications network — are public obligations that should not be financed only through recurring pressure on postage and payroll.

Second, any public-service funding should come with auditable benchmarks. The point is not to write a blank check. It is to separate the books enough that the country can tell what it is paying for. If Congress funds a rural-access baseline, then rural-access performance should be measured. If it funds election-mail readiness, then pre-election transparency and performance metrics should be mandatory. A universal-service subsidy that remains vague would invite exactly the meddling and distrust its critics fear. A clearly specified one would make accountability sharper, not weaker.

Third, pricing should become more legible. Customers can absorb rate increases more readily when they understand the service commitment attached to them. They are less likely to trust increases that arrive in a climate of insolvency warnings, temporary surcharges and mixed service evidence. The current system is too easy to experience as drift. Congress and the PRC should want fewer surprises, clearer justifications and better public explanation of what repeated increases are intended to sustain.

Fourth, retirement and pension mechanics should stop serving as hidden pressure valves. The April suspension of FERS employer annuity contributions may be temporary, and the PRC’s recent waiver action may offer breathing room. But breathing room is not a settlement. Long-term obligations should be resolved through statute and transparent oversight, not revisited through emergency liquidity maneuvers whenever the operating model tightens.

Fifth, local transparency around service changes needs to improve. USPS and its regulators already produce a great deal of information, but too much of it is fragmented across national releases, rate dockets, inspector-general reports, trade publications and local news accounts. Communities should not have to reconstruct the impact of network redesign from scattered clues. Where service standards, processing changes or transportation changes affect local delivery, newspapers, legal mail or customer access, before-and-after data should be readily available in plain language.

Finally, labor has to be treated as infrastructure rather than residue. If the country wants a dependable hand-to-hand network, stable staffing and fair bargaining are not side issues. They are part of service design. Postal workers are not a regrettable leftover from a pre-digital age. They are the means by which the remaining public mission is carried out.

A stamp should buy carriage, not concealment

The United States still has time to choose honesty over improvisation. The mail continues to move. The post office remains one of the few federal presences many communities encounter as a matter of ordinary life. USPS still reaches nearly every address, and that fact alone explains why Washington has been able to postpone a real settlement for so long. The institution is resilient enough that policymakers can keep relying on one more filing, one more tweak, one more waiver, one more round of worker anxiety and one more insistence that self-sufficiency is just around the corner.

But spring 2026 should be read as evidence that the improvisation model is breaking down. When an institution requests a temporary package surcharge in late March, files for another stamp increase in early April, suspends employer retirement contributions effective April 10, and warns that cash could run out as early as February 2027, it is not merely fine-tuning prices. It is signaling that mission and financing no longer match.

Congress can continue to dodge that signal by letting stamp buyers, package customers and postal workers absorb the unresolved burden bit by bit. Or it can finally admit that universal service is a public choice that should be financed and judged as one. The first path preserves the fiction of business discipline while extracting a concealed subsidy from the people still using and performing the service. The second would at least align the ledger with the reality the country keeps defending whenever someone suggests dismantling the network.

A stamp should pay for carrying a letter. It should not be asked to rescue the nation from making up its mind about the public infrastructure of everyday life.
