AI’s new power struggle: Washington, utilities and the bill for the next wave of models

A race to build bigger AI systems is colliding with regulators, schools, labor markets and the electric grid — and the fight over who pays, who is protected and who gets to set the rules is intensifying.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 22, 2026 • 4:18 p.m. • 2746 words

The AI industry has entered a new phase that is less about chatbots and more about power: electrical power, political power, market power and the power to set rules. In the first four months of 2026, the center of gravity in artificial intelligence has shifted from novelty to infrastructure, from product demos to policy fights, and from abstract promises about productivity to concrete arguments about who will pay for the grids, chips, water, labor and oversight needed to keep the boom going.

That shift is visible in the public statements of the biggest players. OpenAI is openly talking about industrial policy, backing fellowships and research grants, and inviting a broader policy conversation around the economics of the intelligence age. Google is pairing product launches with infrastructure-first messaging and telling investors and customers that much of its machine-learning investment this year is flowing toward cloud customers and partners. NIST, meanwhile, has started work on a profile for trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure, signaling that the federal government is beginning to treat AI less like a niche software category and more like a systems-level issue. At the same time, federal and state lawmakers are still wrestling with the basics: should AI rules be preempted, standardized or left to a patchwork of states; should firms be forced to disclose more about model risks; and how should consumer protection, labor law and civil rights adapt to tools that now touch hiring, education, medicine, finance and public services? ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

The most important thing to understand about the current AI moment is that it is no longer confined to Silicon Valley. AI has become a systems issue. It now sits inside data centers and utility planning, inside government procurement and military contracting, inside classrooms and human-resources software, inside copyright disputes and cybersecurity policy. It is also moving faster than the institutions meant to govern it. That mismatch is driving much of the tension in 2026. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

The new bottleneck is not code. It is capacity.

The most current signal from the sector is that scale, not just software quality, is now the binding constraint. Google’s Cloud Next 2026 materials say that in 2026, just over half of the company’s overall machine-learning compute investment is expected to go toward its cloud business, and they frame the era of AI agents as one in which infrastructure must evolve to handle the most demanding workloads. Together with OpenAI’s policy push and NIST’s critical-infrastructure work, that suggests the market’s next fight is not about whether AI exists, but whether the infrastructure can absorb it safely and affordably. ([blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/google-cloud/cloud-next-2026-sundar-pichai/?utm_source=openai))

That infrastructure question is increasingly about electricity. Large models require enormous compute, and compute requires power, cooling and transmission capacity. AP has reported throughout 2026 that utilities and policymakers are scrambling to handle the load from data centers, that ratepayers are already feeling pressure in some regions, and that political fights over who should bear those costs are intensifying. Meta’s January deals for nuclear power, Trump’s March meeting with tech firms over data-center electricity, and the record federal loan for utility expansion in Georgia and Alabama all point in the same direction: AI is becoming an energy-policy story as much as a technology story. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0eb051a9a11d96f7ce200e186ad13476?utm_source=openai))

There are two competing readings of that trend. The bullish view says AI is becoming a generational productivity engine and that building more power, more chips and more data centers is simply the cost of progress. Google’s and OpenAI’s public materials lean in that direction, arguing that AI can improve science, education, energy management and public services. The skeptical view says the sector is externalizing costs onto ratepayers, communities and public institutions while the gains remain concentrated among a handful of firms. That critique has gained force because the larger the infrastructure buildout becomes, the harder it is to treat the benefits as purely private. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

Washington is moving, but not in one direction

Federal policy is now one of the decisive battlegrounds. In March, Axios reported that the White House’s AI framework urged Congress to preempt state AI laws that impose undue burdens and to set a minimally burdensome national standard. That stance captures the central political fault line: companies and some federal officials want uniformity; many states and consumer advocates want room to regulate more aggressively. The result is not a coherent national policy; it is a contest over the architecture of AI governance itself. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/20/white-house-ai-plan-trump-framework?utm_source=openai))

That contest is sharpening because the states have not waited. Axios reported in early April that California is positioning itself as a national testing ground for AI rules, and it has been joined by other states and local governments moving on children’s safety, privacy and workplace issues. Industry groups say a patchwork of rules makes compliance expensive and slows deployment. Consumer advocates and many state lawmakers counter that federal preemption would lock in weak protections, especially if Congress moves before there is enough evidence about harms. The debate is no longer theoretical; it is over whether the first generation of AI regulation will be made by states, by agencies or by Congress under pressure from the largest firms in the market. ([axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/03/california-national-testing-ground-ai-rules?utm_source=openai))

NIST’s recent work complicates the anti-regulation argument. The agency is not proposing a ban or a heavy-handed licensing regime. It is developing a profile on trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure, and its AI program says that effort is meant to support economic security, competitiveness and quality of life. That matters because it signals a middle path: not “let the market sort it out,” and not “freeze the technology,” but “apply familiar engineering discipline to a new class of systems whose failures can scale fast.” In other words, the public sector is trying to catch up by treating AI less like consumer software and more like consequential infrastructure. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

OpenAI’s industrial-policy document also deserves attention because it reveals how the biggest private actors are trying to shape the debate before outside rules harden. The company says its ideas are exploratory and meant to start discussion, but the move itself is strategic: it is signaling that the industry wants to define the terms of industrial policy, workforce adaptation and investment incentives. That is not surprising. Firms that may need more chips, more energy and more favorable permitting have a strong interest in framing those needs as national competitiveness issues rather than corporate demands. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

Safety is becoming a contract dispute, not just a research goal

One of the clearest signs that AI safety has entered a new phase is that it is now showing up in procurement fights, not just academic papers. In February, AP reported that Anthropic’s chief executive said the company could not in good conscience accede to Pentagon demands for unrestricted military use of its technology. In early April, AP reported that the Trump administration was appealing a court ruling blocking punitive measures against Anthropic over that dispute. Those developments show that frontier-AI safety is no longer an abstract ethics question; it is now entangled with contracts, blacklists, procurement leverage and national-security policy. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9b28dda41bdb52b6a378fa9fc80b8fda?utm_source=openai))

This is more than a company-versus-government quarrel. It is a test of whether safety commitments in frontier AI are enforceable when they collide with lucrative government business. If one firm resists military or surveillance uses on principle while others accept the contract, the market may punish restraint. If, on the other hand, governments insist on specific guardrails, they may shape industry norms faster than multilateral diplomacy has managed to do. The stakes are unusually high because military and intelligence uses tend to normalize technologies that later spill into civilian settings. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9b28dda41bdb52b6a378fa9fc80b8fda?utm_source=openai))

The safety conversation has also broadened beyond weapons. OpenAI has published child-safety material and sector-specific policy ideas; NIST has continued to develop a Cyber AI Profile for secure adoption of AI; and UNESCO has repeatedly warned that GenAI in education needs human-centered guardrails. These are not equivalent documents, and they do not guarantee the systems are safe. But they show that leading institutions now recognize a basic truth: the danger is not only that a model might hallucinate. It is that AI can be embedded into domains where errors become expensive, discriminatory, dangerous or difficult to reverse. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2025/12/draft-nist-guidelines-rethink-cybersecurity-ai-era?utm_source=openai))

There is still serious uncertainty about how effective current evaluations are. NIST’s AI program has emphasized scientific rigor and measurement challenges, which is another way of saying that the field still lacks widely accepted ways to prove that a system is trustworthy before deployment. That criticism matters because public claims about safety can become marketing if they are not independently testable. In other words, the more AI matters, the more the industry needs a compliance regime that can be checked rather than merely narrated. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

The labor story is real, but the simplest version is wrong

AI and jobs remain one of the most emotionally charged parts of the story, and for good reason. The numbers are messy. IMF officials have warned this year that AI could boost productivity while also putting major pressure on labor markets, and an IMF conference call for papers in April said the technology is reshaping labor markets and structural transformation. Those two facts can both be true. AI can reshape hiring and task allocation long before it produces obvious macroeconomic job losses. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/02/03/sp-md-leveraging-artificial-intelligence-and-enhancing-countries-preparedness?utm_source=openai))

What is easier to see is where pressure is building. Firms are automating parts of coding, customer support, compliance, research and document-heavy workflows. That does not mean those jobs disappear overnight, but it can reduce demand for junior roles, compress career ladders and shift value toward workers who can supervise systems, integrate them into processes or do the remaining human-only work. The labor-market risk, then, is not simply mass unemployment. It is a reshaping of entry points into the middle class and a weakening of the pathways by which people used to learn by doing. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/02/03/sp-md-leveraging-artificial-intelligence-and-enhancing-countries-preparedness?utm_source=openai))

That is why some of the most consequential AI policy discussions now involve education and workforce development. UNESCO’s guidance on generative AI in education says governments should move quickly, protect privacy and build human capacity, while New York City Public Schools released its own AI guidance for educators and school leaders in March. OpenAI and Google are both pushing education-related tools and offers, while other governments and institutions are building training programs and pilots. The underlying argument is that the labor shock, if it comes, will be eased not by banning AI, but by teaching people how to work around it and with it. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

But here too, the counterargument is strong. School systems are under pressure to use AI to save time and personalize instruction, yet the evidence on learning gains is still uneven and concerns about cheating, surveillance and bias remain unresolved. Education institutions also risk normalizing tools before they have clear rules on privacy, student data, age-appropriate use and accountability. The worst outcome would be to treat AI as a magical productivity fix and then discover that it erodes trust in assessment, dulls critical thinking or transfers core educational functions to private vendors without adequate oversight. UNESCO’s cautionary framing is important precisely because it resists the temptation to reduce AI in education to a procurement race. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

Regulation is also about fraud, consumer harm and civic trust

Another reason AI has become a public-institutions story is that the consumer-harm surface keeps widening. The FTC’s January order against IntelliVision is a concrete example: the agency said the company made false or unsubstantiated claims that its facial-recognition software was free of gender or racial bias, had been trained on millions of faces and could not be fooled by a photo or video image. That is the kind of case that may shape future AI enforcement, because it shows regulators are willing to treat unsupported model claims as deceptive business practices rather than harmless hype. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/01/ftc-finalizes-order-prohibiting-intellivision-making-deceptive-claims-about-its-facial-recognition?utm_source=openai))

This is especially important in light of rapid advances in synthetic media and agentic systems. Even when a model is not overtly malicious, its outputs can be used in scams, impersonation, misinformation and targeted harassment. That has made child safety, nonconsensual intimate imagery, financial fraud and election integrity part of the AI policy conversation. The policy response is still fragmented, but the direction is clear: lawmakers are no longer asking only whether AI is innovative, but whether it is governable in the same legal environment that handles consumer fraud and civil rights. ([ftc.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/01/ftc-finalizes-order-prohibiting-intellivision-making-deceptive-claims-about-its-facial-recognition?utm_source=openai))

One complication is that some of the strongest enforcement tools are reactive. Agencies often move after the harm has occurred. That is why the debate over front-end disclosure, evaluation standards and auditability matters. A system that can be used to impersonate a child’s voice or generate a fake executive order should not be governed solely by after-the-fact lawsuits. The rise of AI therefore strengthens the case for ex ante rules: documentation, testing, transparency and clear responsibility when systems are deployed in high-risk settings. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2025/12/draft-nist-guidelines-rethink-cybersecurity-ai-era?utm_source=openai))

The geopolitical layer: AI is now industrial diplomacy

The AI race is also being fought through supply chains, alliances and industrial diplomacy. OpenAI has said building the infrastructure required to power advanced AI presents a historic opportunity to strengthen domestic supply chains and reindustrialize the country, and it has launched a request for proposals focused on U.S.-based manufacturing across the AI supply chain. Google, for its part, is telling the market that infrastructure for AI agents has to evolve and that cloud and compute investments are central to the company’s next phase. Companies are effectively competing to make themselves part of the country’s industrial policy story. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-the-us-ai-supply-chain/?utm_source=openai))

This dynamic helps explain why companies are publishing policy papers and making public commitments on workforce training and grid support. These are not just corporate social-responsibility gestures. They are part of a broader bid to make AI development appear compatible with national interest. Google’s recent announcements about cloud and infrastructure, and OpenAI’s policy paper, read as much like industrial policy as product strategy. ([blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/google-cloud/cloud-next-2026-sundar-pichai/?utm_source=openai))

Yet there is a real danger in collapsing national interest into corporate interest. The fact that AI could improve medicine, logistics or education does not automatically mean every data-center deal or regulatory demand is justified. The public should ask who benefits from subsidies, who bears grid costs, where water and land-use externalities land, and how much labor and tax revenue actually stay in the communities that host the infrastructure. A democratic AI policy would not merely accelerate deployment; it would also insist on distributional accountability. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9a3fbe8a9e68197dd470c7c02d92d7ab?utm_source=openai))

That accountability issue has become more concrete as communities around the country push back against data-center growth, and as state utility fights turn into proxy battles over the AI economy. AP’s reporting this spring on utility elections and ratepayer concerns makes clear that this is no longer a niche environmental complaint. It is a consumer-cost issue, a local-politics issue and, increasingly, a national-industrial-policy issue. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/c4c3719bf4d7ccf6f2c85373ddee1e40?utm_source=openai))

What comes next

The next phase of AI coverage should not be organized around product announcements alone. The deeper story is that AI is becoming a structural force in the economy and the state. The relevant questions are now boring in the best possible way: Who sets standards? Who audits them? Who pays for the power? Who gets protected? Who decides what uses are off-limits? What happens to entry-level work, public procurement, school policy and the civil rights system when AI is embedded in everyday institutions? ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

For the public, the answer may depend on whether policymakers can move beyond the false choice between panic and boosterism. Panic says AI is too dangerous and should be slowed everywhere. Boosterism says the market will solve the rough edges if we just let the technology scale. Neither view is adequate. The evidence in April 2026 points to something harder: AI is already being integrated into critical systems, but the institutions that govern those systems are only partly prepared. That means the urgent task is not to decide whether AI matters. It is to decide whether power over AI will remain concentrated in the hands of the firms building it, or be shared through rules that are transparent, enforceable and legitimate. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

That is the real news in AI right now. The technology has moved from demo to doctrine, from tool to infrastructure, from promise to politics. What happens next will be shaped less by a single breakthrough than by a series of choices about law, labor, energy and public accountability. Those choices are arriving now, and they will determine whether the AI boom becomes a broad public asset or another highly concentrated industry with public costs hidden in plain sight. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))
