Candida auris is turning the patient handoff into an infection-control instrument

CDC says U.S. Candida auris clinical cases reached 6,304 in 2024 and kept rising. But in 2026 the sharper health-system story is less the organism alone than the work of making its status follow patients across admissions, transfers, dialysis visits, rehabilitation stays and long-term care.

Written and Researched by AI • April 24, 2026 • 2:33 a.m.

Public-health stories about infectious disease often gravitate toward the dramatic unit of measure: the case count, the death toll, the pathogen that sounds as if it came from speculative fiction. Candida auris, the drug-resistant yeast that has spread through hospitals and post-acute care facilities in the United States and abroad, certainly offers those ingredients. It is resistant to multiple antifungal drugs. It can cause invasive infection in very sick patients. It can colonize people without making them obviously ill. It can persist in healthcare environments and move through networks of medically complex care. Yet the most revealing health-system story in April 2026 is not only that the organism exists or even that it keeps spreading. It is that hospitals, long-term acute-care hospitals, ventilator-capable nursing facilities, rehabilitation units, dialysis services and home-health pathways are being forced to treat paperwork, screening, transfer calls and environmental cleaning as a unified infection-control function.

CDC’s updated national tracking page says 6,304 clinical cases of C. auris were reported in 2024, and the agency adds that cases have continued to rise each year even though the rate of increase has slowed since 2022. CDC also stresses that the organism spreads easily in healthcare facilities and mostly affects people who are already very sick. Those national numbers matter because they show that C. auris is not a short-lived anomaly. But the numbers can obscure where the pressure is actually concentrated. The front line is often not a dramatic bedside rescue. It is the moment one facility admits a patient from another and must know, quickly and reliably, what organisms, precautions and risks are arriving with them. The organism is not just in a room. It is in the transfer network. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/tracking-c-auris/index.html?utm_source=openai))

That shift helps explain why some of the most consequential recent material is operational rather than theatrical. Missouri warned in January 2026 that all healthcare facility types should be prepared to accept and care for patients infected or colonized with C. auris and said facilities should not refuse admission on that basis. California’s June 11, 2025 health update, still current because it describes ongoing transmission rather than a one-day event, reported continued spread in acute-care hospitals and long-term acute-care hospitals in parts of Northern and Central California since November 2024. Pennsylvania’s screening framework and toolkits translate the problem into workflow: which admissions to screen, how to collect and route specimens, what precautions fit which settings, and how to keep communication from breaking during transfer. Taken together, those sources suggest that the public-health challenge has evolved from recognizing C. auris as a threat to building ordinary, durable routines that keep the threat legible as patients move. ([content.govdelivery.com](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/MODHSS/bulletins/405cd55?utm_source=openai))

A pathogen that exploits institutional seams

C. auris is not a general community threat in the same way as influenza or measles, and precision matters here. CDC says people without risk factors generally do not get infected or colonized. The organism mostly affects patients with severe underlying illness, long or repeated healthcare exposure, invasive devices, or residence in higher-acuity care settings. Patients can carry it on skin and other body sites without symptoms, and colonized patients can still transmit it. That means detection and prevention depend less on whether an individual patient feels sick than on whether institutions know when to test, when to flag, when to isolate appropriately and when to tell the next facility what is coming. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/tracking-c-auris/index.html?utm_source=openai))

Its biology makes those institutional tasks unusually demanding. CDC’s infection-control guidance says C. auris can persist on surfaces in healthcare settings and that some commonly used healthcare disinfectants, especially products that rely only on quaternary ammonia compounds, should not be assumed to work. The agency directs facilities to use EPA-registered products effective against C. auris, and EPA maintains its List P for that purpose. In practice, that is not a minor technical footnote. It means the right chemicals must be on hand, environmental-services staff must be trained on contact times and application, shared equipment must be cleaned before reuse, and room turnover cannot become an afterthought when staffing is tight. ([epa.gov](https://www.epa.gov/pesticide-registration/epas-registered-antimicrobial-products-effective-against-candida-auris-list?utm_source=openai))

Resistance data reinforce why prevention carries such weight. A CDC study published in Emerging Infectious Diseases in February 2026 reported that the Antimicrobial Resistance Laboratory Network tested 8,033 U.S. clinical isolates from 2022 to 2023 and found resistance frequencies of 95% to fluconazole, 15% to amphotericin B and 1% to echinocandins, with pan-resistance below 1%. The proportion resistant to echinocandins remains low, which supports echinocandins as first-line therapy, but that small fraction matters because it narrows already limited treatment options. CDC’s clinical-care page also says reports of echinocandin-resistant and pan-resistant cases are increasing. That combination of facts points in the same direction: treatment still matters, but catching the organism early and preventing spread is carrying more of the system’s burden than any rescue therapy can. ([wwwnc.cdc.gov](https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/eid/article/32/2/25-1043_article?utm_source=openai))

The healthcare system has been pushed, accordingly, toward acting earlier than the old model of waiting for a clinical infection to declare itself. CDC’s newer MDRO prevention materials say targeted organisms can spread long before they are detected and define admission screening as colonization screening at the time of arrival to ensure timely infection-prevention action. For C. auris, that is a major conceptual shift. The question is no longer only what to do after a lab confirms infection. It is increasingly who should be screened before symptoms or clinical cultures reveal anything at all. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/healthcare-associated-infections/php/preventing-mdros/preventing-mdros-faqs.html?utm_source=openai))

Admission screening as routine infrastructure

California’s June 2025 update is one of the clearest recent examples of that upstream approach. The state and local partners reported increasing C. auris cases across Northern and Central California and said there had been ongoing transmission in acute-care hospitals and long-term acute-care hospitals in the Bay Area and Central Valley since November 2024. The alert identified patients with prolonged admissions in healthcare settings, especially high-acuity settings including LTACHs, ventilator-equipped skilled nursing facilities and some intensive-care environments, as being at highest risk of colonization and infection. It recommended admission screening for patients coming from facilities with known transmission, from LTACHs or ventilator-capable skilled nursing facilities, and for certain patients with healthcare exposure outside the United States; it also said high-acuity units with long lengths of stay should consider screening based on local conditions. ([cdph.ca.gov](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/OPA/Pages/CAHAN/Ongoing-Transmission-of-Candida-auris-in-Healthcare-Facilities-in-Northern-and-Central-California-.aspx?utm_source=openai))

Pennsylvania has tried to turn that logic into reusable infrastructure. Its Healthcare Facility Framework for Candida auris Admission Screening says facilities should build policies and procedures for routine admission screening in conjunction with public health and specifically consider patients with recent admissions to higher-acuity long-term settings such as LTACHs and ventilator-capable skilled nursing facilities. The state’s readiness toolkit then drills down into what that means operationally: identifying at-risk admissions, planning specimen collection and shipping, aligning testing pathways, deciding when to use Contact Precautions or Enhanced Barrier Precautions, and preparing staff before a case arrives. That is not glamorous public health. It is repetitive, administrative and local. But it is also the kind of work that determines whether colonization is discovered at the door or only after transmission has already happened. ([pa.gov](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/programs/haip-as/Healthcare%20Facility%20Framework%20for%20Candida%20auris%20Admission%20Screening.pdf?utm_source=openai))

There is a strong reason this matters. A patient colonized with C. auris may have no symptoms obvious enough to trigger suspicion on clinical exam alone. If no one screens, the first sign of a problem may come later: contamination of a room, exposure through shared equipment, a cluster among contacts, or invasive disease in a patient who is already medically fragile. In that sense, admission screening is less like diagnosing a single illness and more like smoke detection in a building. Its value lies in early warning that allows precautions to start before the system is already behind.

Still, screening is not costless, and health officials have not treated it as a one-size-fits-all answer. Labs need capacity. Staff need training. Specimens need to move correctly. Results have to reach the people who can act on them. California’s alert is careful to target higher-risk facilities and patients rather than imply that every setting requires universal screening. Its regional strategy document similarly differentiates between higher-risk and lower-risk environments and ties response intensity to local epidemiology. That nuance matters because blunt screening mandates can overwhelm capacity without delivering proportional benefit. Under-screen and transmission can stay hidden. Over-screen indiscriminately and the surveillance system itself can become strained. ([cdph.ca.gov](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/OPA/Pages/CAHAN/Ongoing-Transmission-of-Candida-auris-in-Healthcare-Facilities-in-Northern-and-Central-California-.aspx?utm_source=openai))

Missouri’s signal: readiness can no longer be treated as a niche obligation

Missouri’s January 2026 update is notable not only because it shows spread in a state outside the earliest, most publicized clusters, but because it frames C. auris as a practical burden across facility types. The Department of Health and Senior Services said it had detected 757 additional cases, including clinical and screening cases, between July 1, 2024 and November 30, 2025, for 829 total identified cases since 2023. The state said cases had been detected in acute-care hospitals, long-term acute-care hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, assisted-living facilities and rehabilitation hospitals, and added that some positive patients were also receiving hemodialysis and home-health services. That last detail widens the frame. Once the organism is moving through regional referral and care networks, responsibility no longer belongs only to a hospital infection-prevention office. ([content.govdelivery.com](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/MODHSS/bulletins/405cd55?utm_source=openai))

Missouri’s guidance reads almost like a blueprint for how public health now understands the terrain. All facility types, it says, should be prepared to accept and care for patients infected or colonized with C. auris. Facilities should not refuse admission based on C. auris or another multidrug-resistant organism. They should use setting-appropriate transmission-based precautions, communicate status during transfer, use EPA products effective against C. auris, and consider screening people arriving from long-term acute care, skilled nursing or rehabilitation settings who also have features such as mechanical ventilation or chronic wounds. The state also says retesting should not be used to discontinue infection-control measures. ([content.govdelivery.com](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/MODHSS/bulletins/405cd55?utm_source=openai))

That guidance reveals a more mature, if less dramatic, phase of the response. In regions with enough cases, containment no longer means pretending the goal is to keep every building untouched forever. It means preventing amplification, reducing silent spread and making sure precautions survive transfer. In other words, readiness becomes part of routine capacity planning. A hospital, rehab center or nursing facility that treats C. auris as someone else’s niche problem is already using an outdated map.

It also raises an uncomfortable equity question. Better-resourced systems can hire more infection-prevention staff, build tighter laboratory relationships and sustain cleaning protocols more reliably. Smaller hospitals, rural facilities and underfunded post-acute centers may have fewer people, higher turnover and weaker procurement leverage. Yet those facilities are often deeply embedded in the patient-transfer chains that determine whether control works. Guidance that tells every facility to do more without grappling with staffing and reimbursement risks hardening a two-tier system: some networks can operationalize vigilance, while others are left to improvise under pressure.

The transfer form as barrier protection

One of the most important through-lines in federal and state guidance is the elevation of interfacility communication from clerical detail to infection-control intervention. CDC says C. auris status should be communicated when a patient is transferred or discharged. Missouri stresses verbal report and transfer documentation. Pennsylvania’s materials treat transfer communication as part of the operational core, not a courtesy. That emphasis is easy to underestimate because the act itself is ordinary: a checkbox, a discharge summary line, a report call, a status flag in the electronic record. But for C. auris, these are not administrative extras. They are part of the protective equipment of the system. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/hcp/clinical-care/index.html?utm_source=openai))

Modern medically complex patients rarely remain in one setting. Someone may move from an acute-care hospital to an LTACH, then to a ventilator-capable skilled nursing facility, then to dialysis, then back to the emergency department, then home with services, and then to rehabilitation. Each move creates a chance for information to drop out. If the receiving facility does not know that a patient is colonized or recently came from a facility with transmission, the consequences can cascade: delayed room placement, delayed gown-and-glove use, missed screening of roommates or unit contacts, incomplete cleaning of shared equipment, or inappropriate assumptions about risk. The failure point may look banal, but the effect is epidemiologic.

CDC’s MDRO containment strategy captures this logic explicitly. Upon identification of a targeted organism, the agency says, facilities and laboratories should promptly notify clinical staff, implement appropriate infection-control measures and notify the transferring facility if the organism was present on admission so investigation can occur there as well. CDC’s broader MDRO prevention materials likewise frame admission screening and interfacility notification as tools for acting before spread broadens. For C. auris, then, a well-designed transfer process does some of the work that isolation carts and cleaning chemicals do. Data flow becomes a form of hygiene. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/healthcare-associated-infections/php/preventing-mdros/mdro-containment-strategy.html?utm_source=openai))

That is why the article’s central image is not really metaphorical. The transfer packet, lab requisition, precaution sign and discharge summary are physical surfaces where public health is made durable or allowed to fray. In a fragmented care system, continuity of infection status is not a nice administrative refinement. It is one of the few ways a region can behave like an actual coordinated system rather than a sequence of disconnected rooms.

Why post-acute care sits near the center of the map

C. auris has also exposed how misleading it is to imagine a clean divide between hospitals and long-term care. The organism exploits the corridor between them, especially where patients have devices, wounds, ventilator dependence or long stays. CDC’s MDRO prevention strategies identify influential facilities as those at highest risk of importation and transmission, often because they care for high-acuity patients or residents with long lengths of stay. That description fits many LTACHs and ventilator-capable skilled nursing facilities precisely. The institution that appears to be “post-acute” on paper may, in transmission terms, be central. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/healthcare-associated-infections/php/preventing-mdros/mdro-prevention-strategies.html?utm_source=openai))

Pennsylvania’s readiness toolkit is especially useful on this point because it spells out how precautions differ by setting. In skilled nursing facilities, it describes Enhanced Barrier Precautions as the recommended approach for residents with C. auris colonization or infection when full Contact Precautions do not otherwise apply. CDC’s nursing-home guidance says Enhanced Barrier Precautions use gowns and gloves during high-contact resident care activities for residents known to be colonized or infected with an MDRO, as well as certain residents at elevated risk because of wounds or indwelling devices. The idea is to reduce transmission risk without defaulting every resident to the kind of room restriction more typical in hospitals. ([pa.gov](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/programs/haip-as/C.%20auris%20Toolkit%20-%20Readiness.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That distinction is not just procedural. Nursing homes are living environments as well as medical ones. Residents eat, socialize, receive therapy and often remain there for long periods. An infection-control policy copied too literally from acute care can be difficult to sustain and can come at a real quality-of-life cost. On the other hand, a laissez-faire approach can enable spread. Enhanced Barrier Precautions are an attempt to occupy the middle ground honestly: more protective than Standard Precautions alone, less socially restrictive than default room isolation for everyone. The policy challenge is not choosing between comfort and control as abstractions. It is designing precautions that are both epidemiologically credible and workable in settings with chronic staffing pressure.

California’s 2025 alert supports a similarly targeted view. It says patients with prolonged stays in high-acuity settings such as LTACHs, ventilator-equipped skilled nursing facilities and certain ICU environments are at highest risk, while extensive transmission has been much less common in regular skilled nursing facilities. That matters because it argues against flattening every long-term-care setting into the same risk category. The evidence points more toward targeted vigilance in the highest-acuity post-acute settings than toward indiscriminate alarm about nursing homes in general. ([cdph.ca.gov](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/OPA/Pages/CAHAN/Ongoing-Transmission-of-Candida-auris-in-Healthcare-Facilities-in-Northern-and-Central-California-.aspx?utm_source=openai))

Laboratory capacity decides whether prevention starts on time

Another reason C. auris keeps revealing systems weaknesses is that identification is not always straightforward. CDC’s laboratory materials say the organism can be misidentified by some traditional biochemical methods and that labs need validated methods, including MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry databases or confirmatory pathways, to identify it accurately. The agency also notes the role of the Antimicrobial Resistance Laboratory Network in identification, colonization screening and susceptibility testing. In practical terms, a facility can have excellent policy language and still lose valuable time if the organism is not recognized quickly or if specimens are not routed correctly. ([wwwnc.cdc.gov](https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/eid/article/32/2/25-1043_article?utm_source=openai))

Pennsylvania’s screening documents devote substantial attention to specimen collection, packaging and shipping. Missouri explicitly tells laboratories to implement methods to detect C. auris and, if needed, send suspicious isolates for additional testing. California’s regional strategy likewise ties response to laboratory readiness and surveillance. These are technical details, but they determine whether containment starts while the first patient is still manageable or after the organism has already moved through beds, equipment and transfers. Surveillance is only as strong as the lab chain beneath it. ([pa.gov](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/programs/haip-as/Healthcare%20Facility%20Framework%20for%20Candida%20auris%20Admission%20Screening.pdf?utm_source=openai))

There is an argument on the other side worth taking seriously. Many facilities are also struggling with other healthcare-associated infections, staffing shortages, catheter-associated infections, drug-resistant bacteria and general environmental-cleaning problems. A narrow focus on one organism can look like another unfunded mandate layered atop a crowded safety agenda. That caution is real. But C. auris is also a stress test for broad capabilities that matter beyond one fungus: rapid lab identification, surveillance, interfacility notification, setting-appropriate precautions and reliable disinfection. Investment in C. auris preparedness is not purely bespoke. It exercises the same muscles required for other targeted multidrug-resistant organisms. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/healthcare-associated-infections/php/preventing-mdros/index.html?utm_source=openai))

The international picture is reinforcing the same lesson

The United States is not confronting a uniquely American quirk. WHO’s candidiasis fact sheet says C. auris can be multidrug resistant, cause invasive disease and lead to hospital outbreaks. WHO’s fungal priority pathogens report placed C. auris in the highest-priority group because of its public-health significance. In Europe, ECDC said in September 2025 that C. auris was spreading rapidly in hospitals, with more than 4,000 colonization or infection cases reported in EU and EEA countries from 2013 through 2023 and a steep rise to 1,346 cases in 2023 alone. ECDC’s message emphasized early detection, surveillance and infection-control preparedness. Different jurisdictions use somewhat different terminology and systems, but the pattern is strikingly familiar: vulnerable patients, healthcare networks, uneven preparedness and the need to identify cases before spread becomes entrenched. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/candidiasis-%28yeast-infection%29?utm_source=openai))

The global context matters for two reasons. First, patient travel and healthcare exposure outside the United States remain relevant enough that California specifically includes certain international healthcare exposures in screening considerations. Second, international spread makes clear that this is not merely a housekeeping oddity inside one state or a transient effect of one outbreak. It is a structural challenge of modern medical care: more patients surviving with complex needs, more movement among care settings, and more opportunity for infection-status information to fragment unless systems actively force continuity. ([cdph.ca.gov](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/OPA/Pages/CAHAN/Ongoing-Transmission-of-Candida-auris-in-Healthcare-Facilities-in-Northern-and-Central-California-.aspx?utm_source=openai))

That does not mean inevitability. CDC’s tracking page says recommended infection-prevention strategies have successfully prevented spread in some areas, especially when implemented before or at first detection. A recent MMWR on containment responses in hemodialysis settings in four states described coordinated health-department action, screening and infection-control measures aimed at limiting transmission in dialysis environments. Those examples matter because they show that early action can still change trajectory, even in settings with medically fragile patients and frequent device use. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/tracking-c-auris/index.html?utm_source=openai))

What the public should and should not hear in this story

For most healthy people, the takeaway is calmer than the healthcare-system takeaway. CDC says people without risk factors generally do not get infected or colonized, and patient-facing explanations consistently note that ordinary daily-life risk for healthy household members is low. This is not a reason for the general public to fear casual encounters in the way people might fear a rapidly spreading respiratory virus. But it is highly relevant to families navigating repeated hospitalizations, dialysis, long-term acute care, ventilator care or complex wound management, because in those pathways infection-status information really matters. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/tracking-c-auris/index.html?utm_source=openai))

Families cannot substitute for infection-prevention programs, but they can help make sure critical information travels. If a patient has a history of C. auris colonization or infection, or has recently stayed in a facility with known transmission, that history should not vanish between settings. It should be in the chart, on the transfer documents and in the handoff call. The burden belongs to institutions, but patients and caregivers often become the last redundant memory system when systems fail.

Healthcare facilities, for their part, have to communicate without stigmatizing. Missouri’s guidance that facilities should not refuse admission based on C. auris status is important not only ethically but epidemiologically. If patients become informally undesirable because they require extra work, then the network becomes less honest about where colonization exists and more likely to push risk downstream. Shared safety requires shared acceptance of responsibility. ([content.govdelivery.com](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/MODHSS/bulletins/405cd55?utm_source=openai))

There is also an unglamorous financing question under all of this. Screening programs, precaution supplies, environmental cleaning, laboratory coordination and infection-prevention staffing all cost money. The facilities carrying much of the burden, especially in post-acute care, are not always the facilities with the strongest staffing or reimbursement margins. If payment systems underwrite those functions weakly, guidance will land unevenly. C. auris is therefore not only a microbiology story. It is also a management and financing story about whether the system will pay for prevention before failure becomes visible enough to force it.

A fungus, but also a test of whether the network can remember

The temptation is to tell C. auris as a horror story about the next superbug. There is some truth in that frame: the organism is dangerous for medically fragile patients, often drug-resistant and capable of causing outbreaks in healthcare settings. But the sharper health story in 2026 is more exacting than fear. C. auris is revealing which parts of the care system can function as a coordinated organism and which parts remain a patchwork of separate institutions connected by imperfect memory.

That is why the material objects in this story matter. The swab collected at admission. The lab route that gets a specimen to the right place. The precaution sign outside a room. The disinfectant label with the correct claim and contact time. The discharge summary line that names prior colonization. The transfer form whose checkbox is filled out clearly enough for the next shift to act before the next roommate arrives. These are not secondary details. They are where infection control becomes physically real.

CDC’s latest tracking confirms that C. auris kept rising nationally in 2024. State guidance from Missouri, California and Pennsylvania shows that health officials increasingly understand the operational terrain: higher-acuity post-acute settings, risk-based admission screening, laboratory coordination, interfacility communication, setting-appropriate precautions and cleaning that actually matches the organism. The unresolved question is whether those measures can be made mundane enough to survive turnover, underfunding and the relentless movement of patients through a fragmented system. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/candida-auris/tracking-c-auris/index.html?utm_source=openai))

If that work holds, C. auris may remain a serious but manageable institutional hazard, concentrated where vulnerability is greatest and addressed through disciplined surveillance and control. If it does not, the warning will keep reappearing not as one spectacular national crisis but as a long trail of missed handoffs, delayed precautions and rooms that looked ready until the next patient crossed the threshold.
