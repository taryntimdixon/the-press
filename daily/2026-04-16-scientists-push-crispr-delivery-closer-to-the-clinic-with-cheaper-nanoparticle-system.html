Scientists push CRISPR delivery closer to the clinic with cheaper nanoparticle system

A new gold nanoparticle platform may help overcome one of gene editing’s biggest bottlenecks: getting CRISPR safely and cheaply into hard-to-edit human stem cells.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 16, 2026 • 4:57 p.m. • 1121 words

The latest race in gene editing is not about writing DNA. It is about delivery.

In a paper published this year in Gene Therapy, researchers reported a modular gold-polymer nanoparticle system that can carry CRISPR ribonucleoproteins into human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells, the blood-forming cells that sit at the center of many gene therapies. The team says the method can be assembled in under two hours, costs less than $70 to treat a million cells, and works with multiple gene-editing enzymes, including Cas9 and Cas12a. For a field where delivery can make the difference between a therapy that is theoretically possible and one that is clinically usable, that is a notable advance. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

The result arrives as gene-editing medicine is moving out of the proof-of-concept era and into the practical one. Just last week, NIH highlighted a separate breakthrough that “shrinks” CRISPR for precision delivery in the body, underscoring how central the delivery problem remains as the technology expands beyond the lab. That broader push reflects a common challenge across the field: the editor itself may be powerful, but it must be packaged in a way that reaches the right cells, in the right amount, without causing collateral damage. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases))

Why delivery is the bottleneck

CRISPR-based therapies have already shown they can work in humans. But the path from a successful edit in a dish to a useful treatment in patients is filled with engineering trade-offs. Blood stem cells are especially demanding: they are precious, difficult to manipulate, and sensitive to stress. Current ex vivo methods often rely on electroporation, which can be harsh on cells and requires specialized equipment. Viral vectors can deliver genetic cargo efficiently, but they can also raise concerns about prolonged expression and integration risks. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

That is why the new study focuses on ribonucleoproteins, or RNPs, the preassembled complexes of guide RNA and nuclease protein. RNP delivery is attractive because it acts quickly and transiently, which can reduce off-target effects and may be easier on cells than methods that keep the editor active for longer. The authors argue that if the delivery system can be made modular, inexpensive and easy to assemble, it could lower barriers for labs and clinics that cannot easily support complex electroporation workflows. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

The researchers’ answer is a hybrid system built around gold nanoparticles and a polymer coating. In the paper, they describe a formulation that improves loading of the CRISPR machinery onto the particles and can be adapted for multiple editors. The broader implication is less about gold itself than about engineering flexibility: a single platform that can be tuned for different nucleases may speed translation and broaden the number of diseases that can be targeted. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

What the new nanoparticle system does

The study reports that the CRISPR-AuNP platform achieved efficient editing in primary human CD34+ hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells while preserving cell viability. It also worked with more than one editor, which matters because the field is diversifying beyond Cas9. Cas12a, for instance, has different targeting rules and can be useful when a particular disease target is better suited to its cut pattern or guide design. The ability to swap editors without rebuilding an entirely new delivery system is one reason the platform is drawing attention. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

Cost and speed are also part of the story. The authors say the nanoformulation can be assembled in under two hours in a PCR tube for less than $70 per million HSPCs treated. That estimate is not a finished therapy price tag, but it is meaningful as a manufacturing benchmark. Gene-editing medicine often carries enormous operational overhead, so a cheaper and more accessible research workflow can influence which approaches move from academic studies to translational programs. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

The paper’s claims fit into a much larger wave of delivery innovation. In March, researchers reported that adding a simple amino-acid cocktail could dramatically boost the power of mRNA therapies and CRISPR editing in lipid nanoparticles, while other teams have reported progress using protein nanoparticles, lipid nanoparticles and other non-viral systems. Taken together, these studies suggest that the field is converging on a central insight: the next leap in gene editing may come less from a brand-new nuclease than from better ways to move existing editors into cells. ([eurekalert.org](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1118947?utm_source=openai))

From blood cells to the clinic

The immediate target for the gold nanoparticle platform is hematopoietic stem cell research, an area with direct clinical relevance for disorders such as sickle cell disease and other blood conditions. That matters because the first CRISPR therapy approvals made clear that edited blood stem cells can produce durable benefits. The new work is not itself a therapy, but it aims at the same biological terrain that is already proving clinically valuable. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

Independent work this year has shown how broad the delivery challenge has become. UBC researchers reported a topical CRISPR-based approach for genetic skin disease, using laser-assisted access and lipid nanoparticles to get editors through the skin barrier. In parallel, a PubMed-indexed study described in situ correction of a congenital ichthyosis mutation in human skin models with a lipid nanoparticle-delivered base editor, while another recent paper reported that lipid nanoparticles can improve ex vivo editing in hematopoietic cells. The common theme is clear: every tissue demands its own delivery solution, and the engineering is increasingly becoming the science. ([eurekalert.org](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1114280))

That is why the new gold nanoparticle study matters even if it remains preclinical. A platform that is inexpensive, modular and compatible with multiple CRISPR enzymes could help standardize how labs approach hard-to-edit stem cells. Standardization is not glamorous, but in biomedicine it often determines whether a promising idea becomes a reproducible method and then, eventually, a therapy. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

There are still major hurdles before such a system could be used in patients. Researchers will need to show consistent editing in more disease-relevant settings, confirm long-term safety, and determine whether the platform can be manufactured at scale under clinical-grade conditions. Even if all of that proves successful, the method would still have to compete with fast-moving alternatives, including improved lipid nanoparticles, engineered viral-like particles and other non-viral systems. But the study adds momentum to a field that has spent years looking for the same thing: a delivery vehicle that is safe, adaptable and practical enough to bring the promise of CRISPR within reach. ([nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41434-025-00591-0.pdf))

For now, the significance is less that gene editing has solved delivery than that the field keeps narrowing the gap. Each advance removes another obstacle between a molecular tool that can rewrite DNA and a medicine that can be used in the real world. That is where the next breakthroughs in CRISPR are likely to be decided: not only in the editor, but in the ride it takes to get there. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases))
