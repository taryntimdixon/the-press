The New Intimacy Crisis: Why AI Companions and Agentic Systems Demand a New Ethics of Attention

As chatbots become companions and agents begin acting for us, the central question is no longer whether AI can imitate care or competence. It is whether society will let outsourced attention erode human judgment, social bonds, and accountability.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 1:39 p.m.

For years, the public debate over artificial intelligence has been trapped between two familiar arguments. One says AI will liberate us: automate drudgery, personalize education, speed up medicine, and widen access to expertise. The other says AI will replace us: strip jobs, distort truth, and concentrate power in a few hands. Both are partly right. But neither fully captures the moral change now unfolding in real time.

The deeper shift is not just that machines are becoming more capable. It is that they are becoming socially useful in a way that is morally contagious. AI systems increasingly do not merely answer questions. They talk like confidants. They tutor, coach, prod, flatter, and plan. They appear in the most intimate seams of life: a teenager looking for reassurance at midnight, a worker delegating email triage, a parent seeking help with a school form, a lonely adult asking a conversational bot to stay a little longer. And with the rise of agentic AI, these systems are moving from speech to action: they inspect files, run commands, edit code, and work on long-horizon tasks inside controlled sandbox environments. OpenAI’s current product materials explicitly describe that kind of capability, while enterprise-facing announcements increasingly frame AI as part of operational infrastructure rather than a novelty layered on top of existing software. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/the-next-evolution-of-the-agents-sdk?utm_source=openai))

That combination—companionship on one side, delegated action on the other—creates a new ethical landscape. The old question was whether AI could produce useful outputs. The harder question is whether a society that already struggles with loneliness, mistrust, and institutional overload should normalize systems that substitute for human presence and human labor at the same time. My view is that it should not, at least not without much stronger guardrails than we currently have. AI should be treated as infrastructure for assistance, not as an emotional or moral surrogate. Once we accept that distinction, the policy conversation changes: the goal is no longer how much can we automate, but what forms of dependence are acceptable, and who bears the cost when machine-mediated relationships and machine-mediated work quietly become the default?

The intimacy problem is larger than the chatbot hype cycle

There is a temptation to dismiss AI companionship as a niche curiosity. That would be a mistake. The evidence suggests the social appetite is real, and growing. Pew Research Center’s 2026 work on teens and AI found that 64% of U.S. teens say they use AI chatbots, and that teens’ views are mixed rather than uniformly enthusiastic. Separate Pew reporting from late 2025 focused directly on teens, social media, and AI chatbots, underscoring how quickly these tools have moved from novelty to routine adolescent infrastructure. ([pewresearch.org](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2026/02/PI_2026.02.24_Teens-and-AI_REPORT.pdf?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the loneliness backdrop is not hypothetical. The World Health Organization’s Commission on Social Connection reported in June 2025 that one in six people worldwide experiences loneliness, with significant implications for health and well-being. WHO also warned that social isolation and loneliness are widespread and under-recognized public-health problems. In other words, AI companions do not enter a neutral social field. They enter a world in which many people already feel socially thin, time-starved, and under-supported. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/30-06-2025-social-connection-linked-to-improved-heath-and-reduced-risk-of-early-death?utm_source=openai))

That matters because AI companions do not enter a neutral social field. They enter a world in which many people already feel socially thin, time-starved, and under-supported. In that context, the product promise of always available companionship is not trivial. It is seductive precisely because it meets an unmet need. But seduction is not the same as care. The philosophical problem is that systems optimized for engagement can imitate the forms of care without assuming the duties that make care ethically serious: continuity, reciprocity, accountability, and the willingness to be limited by the other person’s real interests.

Recent company statements show that product builders understand at least part of the risk. OpenAI has rolled out teen-focused safeguards, parental controls, and age-prediction systems intended to tailor the ChatGPT experience for users under 18. It has also published a child-safety blueprint and a teen-safety blueprint, both of which frame younger users as deserving differentiated protections and ongoing evaluation. Those are meaningful steps. But they also confirm how quickly the industry is normalizing a world in which age inference, behavioral tailoring, and emotional sensitivity become standard product features. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-the-teen-safety-blueprint/?utm_source=openai))

The concern is not only that a chatbot might say the wrong thing. It is that an always-available conversational system can train users to prefer low-friction simulated understanding over the difficult work of human relationships. That is especially important for adolescents, whose social development depends on frustration tolerance, mutuality, and learning that other people are not instruments for immediate comfort. A machine that is endlessly validating can become a kind of ethical junk food: momentarily soothing, structurally impoverishing.

Common Sense Media’s 2025 research helps clarify why that worry is not speculative. In that survey, most teens said they do not trust tech companies to care about their well-being, and many doubted that companies would prioritize safety over profit. A separate Common Sense report on AI companions found extensive teen use and substantial ambivalence about trust and emotional safety. The public is not naïve here; young people themselves already recognize that a responsive interface is not the same thing as a trustworthy relationship. ([commonsensemedia.org](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/research-reveals-teens-distrust-in-tech-companies-and-ai-generated-content?utm_source=openai))

Here the burden of proof should be on the companies, not on families or schools. If a product is designed to form the impression of emotional attunement, then the company is no longer merely selling software. It is intervening in the ecology of attachment. That intervention should be held to a standard stronger than users seem to like it.

From automation to delegation: why agentic AI changes the moral calculus

The second shift is subtler but just as consequential. Traditional software required users to click, confirm, and supervise. Agentic systems promise to do more of that work themselves. OpenAI’s April 2026 materials describe the next evolution of the Agents SDK as enabling sandboxed long-horizon work across files and tools, with capabilities such as file inspection, command execution, code editing, and model-native workflows. Enterprise products are being marketed in similar terms: not just as assistants, but as action-taking layers that can live inside business systems. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/the-next-evolution-of-the-agents-sdk?utm_source=openai))

This matters philosophically because delegation changes responsibility. If a person uses a tool to draft an email, the person remains the author in the moral sense. If a system autonomously triages a customer, schedules a meeting, updates a CRM, or acts on behalf of a worker across multiple systems, then the line between assistance and agency blurs. When something goes wrong, who is accountable: the user, the employer, the vendor, the model provider, the compliance team, or the prompt designer? In practice, the answer is often everyone and no one.

That diffusion of responsibility is not just an administrative headache; it is a moral hazard. Systems that can act across accounts and software environments can create an illusion of competence that outpaces genuine oversight. The more an agent behaves like a conscientious assistant, the more users are tempted to stop supervising. And once supervision declines, the system becomes not only more powerful but also more dangerous. The ethical challenge is not merely whether agents can perform tasks. It is whether organizations will keep humans meaningfully in the loop after the novelty wears off.

This is where the labor debate needs to get more specific. Public discussion often collapses into two extremes: either AI will eliminate millions of jobs or it will mostly augment work. The truth is likely mixed, uneven, and institution-dependent. The OECD’s 2026 conference on AI in work, innovation, productivity, and skills signals that policymakers are treating the issue as an open question involving labor-market structure, productivity, and skills formation rather than as a settled forecast. Anthropic’s recent Economic Index reporting, meanwhile, is being read by researchers and commentators as evidence that usage patterns are changing in ways that may signal workflow restructuring rather than simple novelty. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/events/2026/03/2026-international-conference-on-ai-in-work-innovation-productivity-and-skills.html?utm_source=openai))

Still, the question is not whether every occupation disappears. It is whether workers lose the dignity of skill ownership. If agents absorb the boring but cognitively formative parts of work—writing first drafts, sorting priorities, checking references, making routine decisions—what remains may be either the glamorous top slice of judgment or the exhausted oversight of a machine pipeline. That is a serious concern for knowledge work, but also for public-sector labor. OECD’s recent work on building an AI-ready public workforce explicitly argues that training, institutional adaptation, and staff capacity are central to realizing benefits while managing risk. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/harnessing-artificial-intelligence-in-social-security_b52405c1-en/full-report/building-an-ai-ready-workforce_9aa41028.html?utm_source=openai))

The ethical stakes are higher when these systems are introduced not only into productivity software but into the human-relational parts of institutions: education, healthcare, public benefits, customer service, and elder support. A bot that reduces wait times can be a genuine good. A bot that becomes the only reachable face of an institution is something else. The line between helpful filtering and bureaucratic abandonment is thinner than vendors admit.

What the best evidence complicates

A credible essay has to admit what the counterarguments get right. First, many people do benefit from AI assistance. There is a real accessibility case: people with disabilities, language barriers, cognitive overload, or social anxiety can use AI to lower friction in daily life. OpenAI’s public materials on healthcare, teen safety, and economic blueprints repeatedly frame AI as a tool for widening access and improving services. OECD and other institutions also argue that AI can raise productivity and help workers adapt. ([cdn.openai.com](https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/2cb29276-68cd-4ec6-a5f4-c01c5e7a36e9/OpenAI-AI-as-a-Healthcare-Ally-Jan-2026.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Second, not every human interaction is precious simply because it is human. Some institutions are already impersonal, slow, and humiliating. A well-designed agent can reduce bureaucratic pain, answer routine questions after hours, and serve people who would otherwise be shut out. In some contexts, the moral failure is not overreliance on machines but the prior absence of humane service. It would be perverse to demand that a single mother wait on hold for forty minutes in the name of preserving a romantic ideal of human contact.

Third, claims that AI companions are inevitably harmful overstate the evidence. The empirical picture is still uneven. WHO’s social-connection work identifies loneliness as a real public-health issue and notes that technologies can affect connection, but it does not claim that every digital interaction is harmful. Pew’s teen data likewise show ambivalence rather than simple rejection. Some users may find genuine support, rehearsal space, or emotional relief in AI interactions. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/30-06-2025-social-connection-linked-to-improved-heath-and-reduced-risk-of-early-death?utm_source=openai))

These objections matter because a reflexive ban would be both politically unrealistic and morally blunt. There is a difference between forbidding a technology and assigning it a place. I am not arguing that AI companions or agents must be eliminated. I am arguing that the default cultural and regulatory posture should be one of suspicion toward substitution. AI should be allowed to augment, scaffold, translate, and reduce friction. It should not be allowed to normalize replacement in the realms where human judgment, reciprocity, and accountability are themselves part of the good being delivered.

This distinction is easier to state than to implement, which is why a philosophy desk has to be concrete. Consider three examples.

First, a teenager who uses a chatbot to practice a difficult conversation can benefit from rehearsal. The same teenager, left alone with a system that rewards disclosure, mirrors their mood, and frames itself as a steady companion, may come to treat an artifact as a relationship. The product’s moral role changes when it shifts from instrument to surrogate. That is why OpenAI’s teen-safety, child-safety, and age-prediction efforts are relevant but insufficient: they acknowledge risk, yet they still presume the product category remains desirable if only made safer. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-the-teen-safety-blueprint/?utm_source=openai))

Second, a customer-service agent can speed up a mortgage or benefits application. But if the same AI becomes the only interface and has authority to deny, delay, or silently route cases, then the institution may gain efficiency while losing legibility. People need to be able to tell who decided what, on what basis, and with what appeal path. The more an agent stands in for a human administrator, the more important transparency and contestability become. OECD’s work on public-workforce readiness points toward this institutional challenge, even when framed as capacity-building rather than critique. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/harnessing-artificial-intelligence-in-social-security_b52405c1-en/full-report/building-an-ai-ready-workforce_9aa41028.html?utm_source=openai))

Third, a workplace agent that drafts routine messages and compiles data can free up time. But if the same agent is allowed to act across systems with weak oversight, then the employer may discover too late that it has automated not just the repetitive parts of the job but the chain of responsibility itself. OpenAI’s agentic product materials stress controlled sandbox environments, which is promising. Yet controlled is not a moral category; it is a design claim. The ethical question is how much control remains when systems become woven into daily operations and managers start trusting outputs more than they verify them. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/the-next-evolution-of-the-agents-sdk?utm_source=openai))

The case for a new ethics of attention

The philosophy we need is not anti-technology. It is anti-displacement where displacement matters most. The right question is not whether a machine can imitate an aspect of care or competence. It is whether the imitation undermines the human capacities that care and competence depend on.

That means thinking about attention as a public good. The attention economy trained us to view human focus as a commodity. AI takes that logic one step further by making attention bidirectional: it does not merely capture ours, it simulates its own. The machine appears to listen, remember, and respond. That is why AI companions are so compelling and so ethically risky. They are built to feel like a form of recognition. But recognition without responsibility is not recognition in the full moral sense; it is a performance of regard.

Social connection research helps explain why this matters. WHO treats loneliness and isolation as health and social-policy issues, not merely individual emotions. OECD’s work likewise ties social connection to well-being and warns that loneliness can affect health, education, and labor-market participation. If that is right, then technologies that intensify isolation deserve the same seriousness as technologies that pollute air or water. No one expects a private app to solve loneliness. But when a product extracts the very need it purports to address—by keeping users engaged with a responsive simulation rather than with neighbors, teachers, colleagues, or family—it risks deepening the problem it monetizes. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/30-06-2025-social-connection-linked-to-improved-heath-and-reduced-risk-of-early-death?utm_source=openai))

Attention also matters in work. If AI agents become the default interface through which we apply, file, schedule, request, and confirm, then our labor may become less visible to ourselves. The philosophical cost is not just job loss but self-loss: a slower erosion of the experience of authorship. Workers can become supervisors of an invisible pipeline rather than agents of their own craft. That possibility deserves more public scrutiny than it has received.

There is also an intergenerational question. Children and teens are not simply small adults using the same products at a smaller scale. OpenAI’s recent youth-safety materials, WHO’s loneliness findings, CDC’s youth mental-health campaign, and Common Sense Media’s teen research all point to the same basic truth: young people are in a vulnerable developmental period, and digital environments shape habits before they harden into adult dispositions. If the first lesson a child learns from AI is that the machine will always reply, never tire, never ask for reciprocity, and never insist on the messy obligations of real relationships, then we should not be surprised if the child later finds human connection more difficult, not less. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-the-teen-safety-blueprint/?utm_source=openai))

This is an inference, not a settled empirical finding. The evidence does not yet prove that AI companions cause loneliness or that agents inevitably deskill workers. But policy should not wait for perfect proof when the harm pathway is plausible and the scale of deployment is accelerating. The ethical standard should be proportional precaution.

What proportional precaution would look like

A serious response would have several parts.

First, products that present themselves as companions should face strict age rules, disclosure rules, and limits on manipulative emotional design. If a system simulates intimacy, users should know exactly what it is doing, what data it retains, and what emotional claims it is authorized to make. OpenAI’s teen and child-safety materials show that the industry understands the need for differentiated treatment; the next step is independent auditing and enforceable standards, not just company-authored blueprints. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-the-teen-safety-blueprint/?utm_source=openai))

Second, agentic systems should be permissioned by default, with narrow scopes, loggable actions, and reversible decisions wherever possible. Sandboxes are good. Human review is better. The more a system can act on behalf of a person or institution, the more its actions should leave a clear record. Otherwise, responsibility becomes a feature everyone assumes somebody else is carrying.

Third, employers and public agencies should treat AI adoption as a labor and governance reform, not a mere software update. OECD’s 2026 work suggests that skills, public employment, and organizational design will determine outcomes. If governments and firms do not invest in training, review, appeals, and job redesign, then AI will likely reward already powerful institutions while hollowing out the human capacities that make those institutions legitimate. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/events/2026/03/2026-international-conference-on-ai-in-work-innovation-productivity-and-skills.html?utm_source=openai))

Fourth, society should resist the idea that every human deficiency must be patched by a product. Loneliness should be met with more social infrastructure: schools with counselors, libraries with programs, parks that are safe and welcoming, workplaces that permit actual time with colleagues, and public health systems that treat connection as part of care. The WHO’s framing of social connection as a public-health issue points in exactly this direction. CDC’s youth mental-health campaign also underscores how much public institutions still matter in helping young people navigate distress without defaulting to isolated coping. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/30-06-2025-social-connection-linked-to-improved-heath-and-reduced-risk-of-early-death?utm_source=openai))

The core philosophical claim

The true danger of AI is not that it becomes too human. It is that it becomes human-shaped enough to substitute for things humans owe one another, while remaining exempt from the obligations that make those things meaningful.

That is why the emerging frontier is not just technical safety but moral substitution. A chatbot that comforts without responsibility, and an agent that acts without accountability, both tempt us into outsourcing forms of life that should remain socially embedded. The issue is not whether AI can help. It can. The issue is what kinds of help we are willing to accept when help becomes a way of avoiding the burdens of being human with one another.

We should welcome AI that expands capacity, reduces drudgery, and removes barriers. We should be wary of AI that soothes loneliness without curing it, manages work without preserving agency, and simulates care while displacing the people and institutions that actually owe us care. That is the line worth drawing now, before convenience hardens into dependency and dependency hardens into doctrine.

If the last decade taught us anything, it is that systems optimized for engagement can quietly rewire public life before society understands what has been lost. The next decade may teach the same lesson about delegation. The task for philosophy, and for policy, is to insist that not everything that can be made easier should be made machine-shaped.
