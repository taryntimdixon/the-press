Juneau’s Voter List Puts DOJ’s Database Push on Trial

Alaska’s unredacted voter-file handoff has turned a national fight over election integrity into a local privacy test: who may hold the list behind the voting booth, how much federal power follows the spreadsheet, and what happens when a match is wrong?

Written and Researched by AI • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

Start with the least glamorous object in American democracy: a list.

Not a flag. Not a ballot box. Not a concession speech. A list.

On a state computer, a voter list can look almost aggressively boring. It may hold a name, address, date of birth, registration status, voter history, party affiliation where state law uses it, and identifying numbers used to keep one Jane Smith from becoming every Jane Smith. Poll workers use it to check people in. Election offices use it to send notices, merge duplicates, plan ballot supplies and remove people who have died or moved after the required process. It is the quiet machine under the loud ritual.

That quiet machine is now one of the sharpest power fights of the 2026 midterm year.

On April 22, the League of Women Voters of Alaska and the Alaska Black Caucus sued Alaska Lt. Gov. Nancy Dahlstrom and Division of Elections Director Carol Beecher in state court. Their complaint says Alaska violated the state constitution when it gave the U.S. Department of Justice a full, unredacted voter registration list and signed a memorandum of understanding about future list maintenance. The challenged file, according to the complaint, included full names, birth dates, residential addresses and either state driver’s license numbers or the last four digits of Social Security numbers.

One day earlier, Common Cause and four voters filed a separate federal lawsuit in Washington, D.C. That case targets the Justice Department directly. The plaintiffs allege that DOJ is trying to consolidate sensitive state voter files into a federal system without the legal authority and privacy safeguards such a project would require. DOJ says its campaign is about accurate voter rolls, federal election laws and preventing ineligible voting. Its critics say the department is using election-integrity language to build a national voter-data apparatus that could pressure states to remove eligible voters.

Those are large claims, and some remain allegations. But the Alaska case gives the fight a particular door: Juneau, a December file transfer, a signed agreement, and voters whose information has already left the state election office. In states that refused DOJ’s demands, courts are arguing about whether the federal government can get the unredacted list at all. In Alaska, the plaintiffs are also asking what can be done after the data has moved.

Why Alaska matters

Alaska is not the only state in the dispute. The Justice Department has sued at least 30 states and the District of Columbia to try to obtain detailed voter-roll data, according to tallies cited in court records and press accounts. Judges have rejected DOJ efforts in California, Massachusetts, Michigan, Oregon and Rhode Island, while at least a dozen states have provided or promised to provide detailed information. Alaska and Texas stand out because they also signed agreements describing DOJ analysis of the files and the return of flagged issues to state officials.

The Alaska complaint traces the handoff through a simple sequence. DOJ asked in July 2025 for Alaska’s statewide computerized voter registration list, including all fields. Alaska first sent a public version in August. DOJ then repeated its demand for a complete list, including birth dates, residential addresses and identifying numbers. On Dec. 19, 2025, the complaint says, the lieutenant governor agreed to provide the complete file, and the elections director signed the memorandum with DOJ. On Dec. 23, Alaska transmitted the voter list.

The state’s public defense is also straightforward. Sam Curtis, a spokesperson for the Alaska Department of Law, told the Associated Press that state law permits sharing otherwise confidential information for authorized governmental purposes and that the state will defend that position. At a legislative hearing, a state attorney said Alaska had a compelling interest in cooperating because both governments had an interest in accurate, current rolls.

That is the strongest version of the state’s argument: voter lists are not diaries. They are government records used to administer a public system. The federal government enforces federal election laws, including the National Voter Registration Act and the Help America Vote Act. If federal authorities have a lawful oversight role, cooperation is not automatically sinister.

The strongest version of the plaintiffs’ argument is just as practical: a voter gives sensitive information to Alaska so Alaska can administer elections, not so a federal law-enforcement agency can assemble or search a national store of voter data. A home address can be dangerous for a domestic-violence survivor, judge, election worker, tribal leader, law-enforcement officer or anyone living under threat. A partial Social Security number is not campaign trivia. Even if data is gathered for a proper first use, the second use can be harder to control than the first.

The memorandum is the little object carrying the big argument. The Alaska plaintiffs say it gives DOJ too much power by requiring the state, within 45 days of receiving notice of problems, to clean its voter list by removing ineligible voters and resubmitting the updated data. The complaint says the agreement does not spell out adequate notice, investigation or appeal procedures for voters who might be flagged. Alaska officials have said they will comply with state and federal law. That assurance may matter in court. So may the exact words of the agreement.

What DOJ says it is enforcing

DOJ’s public explanation is not mysterious. The department says accurate voter rolls are essential to fair elections. It has cited three federal laws in particular: the National Voter Registration Act, or NVRA; the Help America Vote Act, or HAVA; and the Civil Rights Act of 1960. In its press releases, the department says states must show that their lists are accurate and that DOJ has authority to inspect records needed to enforce those obligations.

Voter-list maintenance is real work. People move. People die. People change names. Some people register in one state and later in another. Some lose eligibility under state law because of a criminal conviction. Election officials have to keep lists current without turning ordinary maintenance into a mass accidental lockout.

The scale is enormous. The U.S. Election Assistance Commission reported that more than 211 million citizens were active registered voters for the 2024 general election. States also reported sending nearly 40 million confirmation notices as part of list-maintenance work. That is not a dusty address book. It is a living civic infrastructure, revised every day by clerks, software, mail returns, motor-vehicle updates, death records and voters themselves.

Federal law does require maintenance. The NVRA says states must make a reasonable effort to remove people who have died or moved out of the jurisdiction, while also setting limits so voters are not removed simply because they skipped elections. HAVA requires nearly every state to maintain a single, official, centralized computerized voter-registration list at the state level for federal elections. These laws were designed to make elections more reliable.

So the cleanest version of the dispute is not whether voter rolls should be accurate. They should be. The dispute is who decides what accuracy requires, how much confidential data Washington may demand, what evidence DOJ must show before demanding it, how long federal agencies may keep it, who else may see it, and what process protects a voter if a federal match is wrong.

Courts have been asking for the missing middle

So far, several federal judges have not accepted DOJ’s broadest demands. On April 17, U.S. District Judge Mary S. McElroy dismissed the department’s Rhode Island lawsuit and denied its motion to compel production of the state’s unredacted voter list. The order said DOJ’s request sought sensitive information, including the last four digits of Social Security numbers and driver’s license numbers, and that the federal demand lacked factual allegations suggesting Rhode Island was violating list-maintenance law.

The Rhode Island ruling did not say voter-list maintenance is optional. It said DOJ had not shown enough of a legal and factual basis to force disclosure of the state’s unredacted file. That is the missing middle in the national fight: between the broad claim that clean rolls matter and the narrow act of compelling a state to surrender confidential fields.

Imagine a building inspector asking to inspect a restaurant kitchen. That can be routine. Now imagine the inspector demanding every customer’s medical history, home address and driver’s license number without explaining the suspected violation. The purpose matters. The tailoring matters. The paperwork matters.

DOJ can appeal adverse rulings. It can narrow requests. It can send more detailed letters. It can argue that judges are reading federal enforcement authority too grudgingly. Litigation is not a scoreboard that freezes after one inning. But timing matters. A legal theory that could take years to settle is being pressed against a midterm election calendar measured in months.

SAVE is not a magic citizenship wand

The database question became sharper when DOJ acknowledged in the Rhode Island case that it wanted unredacted voter information so it could be shared with the Department of Homeland Security for citizenship checks. Stateline reported in March that DOJ confirmed voter-data sharing with DHS while denying that it was building a national voter list. CBS News and NPR affiliates also reported on plans to run voter data through DHS systems.

The relevant federal tool is SAVE, the Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements program, run by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services. Historically, SAVE has helped authorized agencies verify immigration or citizenship information for benefits, licenses and other government programs. USCIS says SAVE can provide verification information for voter registration or voter-list maintenance, but it does not itself decide whether a person may register, remain on a roll or vote.

That distinction is not a technical footnote. It is the difference between a smoke alarm and a firefighter. A database response can raise a question. It should not, by itself, take away a vote.

Large-scale data matching can fail for dull reasons: a typo, a missing hyphen, an old immigration record, a married name, a birth date entered backward, a recently naturalized citizen whose federal record has not caught up, a person with acquired citizenship not confirmed in the records being queried, or two people with similar names. The dull reasons are dangerous because they look administrative rather than dramatic. No villain has to enter the room for an eligible voter to be flagged.

That is why notice and due process matter. Due process means a person gets fair notice and a meaningful chance to respond before government takes away an important right. If a voter is wrongly identified as ineligible, the fix must happen before the ballot is lost, not months later when a lawsuit discovers the error.

Noncitizen voting is illegal. The evidence still points to rarity.

Federal law already bars noncitizens from voting in federal elections, and states have their own eligibility rules. DOJ and DHS argue that data sharing can help make sure only eligible citizens vote. That goal has political force. Voting is the basic act of self-government, and illegal voting should be investigated.

The evidence, however, does not support claims of widespread noncitizen voting. The Brennan Center’s survey of election administrators in 42 jurisdictions covering 23.5 million votes in 2016 found about 30 suspected incidents referred for further investigation or prosecution. The libertarian Cato Institute, hardly a progressive voting-rights shop, has also criticized inflated claims about widespread noncitizen voting.

That does not mean the number is zero. Iowa officials said in 2024 that they had found 87 people who had identified themselves to the state Department of Transportation as noncitizens and had previously voted, plus 67 who had registered. They also said thousands of other records required clarification because people might have become naturalized after earlier identifying as noncitizens. Cases like that deserve careful investigation. They also show why a lead is not the same thing as proof.

The question is proportionality. If a government tool catches rare ineligible voting while wrongly frightening or blocking far more eligible voters, the tool has created a new election-integrity problem. If officials measure error rates, disclose them and build voter-protective review around the data, the risk changes. The phrase to watch is not only election integrity or voter suppression. It is error rate.

Privacy is more than identity theft

Voter-file privacy is broader than stolen credit cards. A voter file can reveal where someone lives, whether a registration is active, which elections the person participated in, and, in some states, party affiliation. It does not reveal how a person voted in a secret-ballot election. That distinction is crucial. But participation data can still be sensitive in a polarized community.

Some voter information is already public or available under state law to parties, campaigns, researchers, journalists or government users. That fact can make privacy objections sound late. If campaigns can buy parts of the file, why panic when DOJ asks?

The answer is that unredacted statewide files may include fields public versions do not. The recipient also matters. A campaign list is not the same as a federal law-enforcement database. A state disclosure rule with use limits is not the same as a national consolidation project. Privacy risk is not only about whether data exists. It is about who can combine it, search it, copy it, keep it and act on it.

There is also the problem of secondary use. A database built for one purpose can become tempting for another. The Alaska memorandum says DOJ will use the file for list-maintenance verification and describes security procedures. The Common Cause complaint alleges that DOJ is using voter-roll data for a broader nationalization policy and asks the court to order deletion or disentangling of confidential data already collected. DOJ has not accepted that characterization. The legal question is whether the statutes and privacy notices DOJ cites actually carry the weight the department is putting on them.

States are not just branch offices

The constitutional background is federalism, a dry word for a lively arrangement: power divided between national and state governments. In U.S. elections, states handle most of the hands-on work. Congress can regulate federal elections, and the federal government enforces important voting-rights laws. But states register voters, run polling places, maintain voter files, certify results and decide many details of eligibility and procedure.

That division can be messy. Voters in different states experience different systems. But decentralization also creates resilience. It makes elections harder to capture, manipulate or break from one central office. The same feature that frustrates people who want national uniformity can reassure people who fear national control.

History cuts both ways. Federal power has been essential when states violated voting rights, especially against Black voters under Jim Crow and after. The Civil Rights Act of 1960, one statute DOJ cites, came from an era when federal oversight was necessary because state and local officials were suppressing citizens. But that history does not answer every present question. A law created to fight suppression is now being invoked to obtain confidential voter files from states that say disclosure itself may endanger voters. That does not automatically make DOJ wrong. It makes the statutory fit worth reading closely.

The Common Cause case puts the national version of the dispute in one frame. The plaintiffs ask a federal court to block DOJ from consolidating, using or sharing confidential voter-list data and to require the department to comply with the Privacy Act and other procedural safeguards. The Privacy Act governs how federal agencies collect, maintain, use and share records about individuals. Its basic premise is modest and important: the government should not quietly build searchable filing cabinets about people without rules, notice and limits.

What voters can do while lawyers fight

Several outcomes are possible. Courts could keep rejecting DOJ demands unless the department provides a narrower request and a stronger factual basis. DOJ could win on appeal or revise its approach. Congress could clarify federal access to voter data, add data-minimization rules, require audits and appeal procedures, or mandate more aggressive citizenship checks. States could split further, with some cooperating, some refusing and some providing only public versions of their files.

Voters, meanwhile, have a smaller tool: check registration early, keep documentation organized and respond quickly to any official notice. That advice is annoyingly mundane. It is also useful. Administrative trouble hurts most when the affected person hears about it too late.

None of this means the 2026 election is doomed, rigged or already broken. American elections are run by thousands of local officials, many of whom are serious people doing difficult work under pressure. It also does not mean every federal records request is a coup in letterhead form. Governments need records to enforce laws.

But power often moves through dull channels before anyone notices. A demand letter. A spreadsheet. A memorandum. A file transfer. A field called status.

Alaska gives the national fight a concrete place to stand. The list has already traveled. The agreement has already been signed. The voters whose information moved did not each get a phone call before the transfer. Now a state judge may be asked to decide whether that handoff violated Alaska’s unusually explicit constitutional right to privacy, while a federal judge in Washington considers whether DOJ’s broader project violates federal law.

Most voters will never read the complaints. They will not parse HAVA, NVRA, SAVE or the Privacy Act. They will show up, give their name and expect the list to know them.

That expectation is small, almost sweet. It is also the whole republic, printed in rows.
