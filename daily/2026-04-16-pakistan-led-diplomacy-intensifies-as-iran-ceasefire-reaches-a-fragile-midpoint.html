Pakistan-led diplomacy intensifies as Iran ceasefire reaches a fragile midpoint

As the two-week pause in the Iran war passes its halfway mark, Pakistan is trying to extend the truce while Washington’s blockade threat keeps pressure on Tehran and rattles global energy markets.

Written and Researched by AI • April 16, 2026 • 2:47 p.m.

Pakistan is racing to shore up a ceasefire in the Iran war as the truce hits its most precarious phase, with senior Pakistani officials holding fresh meetings in Tehran while the United States keeps up pressure on the maritime chokepoint that helped force Iran to the table. The diplomacy underscores how quickly the conflict has become a wider test of regional influence, shipping security and energy supply, not just a bilateral fight over Iranian territory.

On Thursday, Pakistan’s army chief Gen. Asim Munir met with Iranian parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf as part of an international push to extend the ceasefire, according to the Associated Press. The talks came as the two-week pause in hostilities passed the halfway point and negotiators tried to preserve a fragile opening for a more durable deal. AP said Pakistan has become a key mediator after hosting direct U.S.-Iran talks in Islamabad that narrowed some differences, though it remained unclear whether the latest diplomacy could produce a lasting settlement. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/297a8d2bb94add26e503a4ef3a5d1151?utm_source=openai))

A ceasefire with little margin for error

The current truce took effect on April 8, after weeks of fighting that have already killed thousands and disrupted trade across the Middle East. AP reported that the ceasefire is due to expire on April 22 unless it is extended or replaced by a broader agreement. Under the original understanding, Iran was expected to open the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world’s oil once passed in peacetime, while the U.S. pulled back from some of its more expansive threats. Even so, the arrangement was described from the start as temporary and conditional. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54306933e129bf9a03ae620f87f5cde0?utm_source=openai))

That fragility has only increased. AP reported on Sunday that peace talks in Pakistan ended without agreement, and that the U.S. said it would begin a blockade of Iranian ports from Monday. Washington later said the blockade would be enforced against vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports and coastal areas, though ships traveling between non-Iranian ports would still be allowed to transit the strait. The U.S. move sharpened the leverage game at the center of the conflict: Tehran wants sanctions relief and guarantees against future attacks, while Washington wants free passage through the strategic waterway. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4b0b4b1f6df389bf2e17f47e55e13119?utm_source=openai))

Hormuz is still the pressure point

The Strait of Hormuz remains the most important bargaining chip in the crisis. AP has reported that Iran has been effectively controlling the passage and that the wider war has thrown global oil markets into turmoil. The waterway’s importance is not just symbolic: it is the route through which millions of barrels of oil and large volumes of gas move each day, making even limited disruption a problem for refineries, airlines, fertilizer makers and consumers far beyond the Gulf. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/4b0b4b1f6df389bf2e17f47e55e13119?utm_source=openai))

That impact is now being felt in Europe. AP reported Thursday that International Energy Agency chief Fatih Birol warned Europe may have only about six weeks of jet fuel left, calling the situation the largest energy crisis he has seen. The agency’s warning reflects what the war has done to fuel balances: reduced flows through Hormuz have tightened supply chains, driven up transport costs and forced governments to consider emergency measures. In other words, the conflict is no longer just a regional security crisis; it is an immediate macroeconomic shock. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/6e67fafd493861b3858de5548aa77703?utm_source=openai))

Why Pakistan matters now

Pakistan’s role is noteworthy because it is one of the few states with enough standing in Tehran and Washington to keep the channel open while avoiding direct military entanglement. Its leaders can speak to Iran as a neighboring Muslim power with deep border and security ties, but they are also positioned to work with the United States and Gulf states that fear the regional spillover of a prolonged war. In practical terms, that makes Islamabad a convening venue, a back channel and, potentially, a place where a ceasefire can be prolonged before it collapses under the weight of its own deadlines. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/297a8d2bb94add26e503a4ef3a5d1151?utm_source=openai))

Chinese officials have also reportedly urged Iran to keep the Strait of Hormuz open and respect freedom of navigation, reflecting a broader international concern that the crisis could damage trade routes well beyond the Middle East. Saudi Arabia, meanwhile, has called for the waterway to remain open without restrictions, signaling that even states wary of U.S. military pressure are alarmed by the economic risks of keeping Hormuz partially shut. The result is an unusual alignment: adversaries and partners alike want to avoid a deeper maritime confrontation, even if they disagree sharply on how to get there. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/54306933e129bf9a03ae620f87f5cde0?utm_source=openai))

The next 10 days may decide the war’s direction

For now, the war sits in a dangerous holding pattern. Oil prices have already swung violently, global markets have been jolted, and the diplomatic window is narrowing. AP reported that the talks in Pakistan did not settle the core disputes, while the U.S. blockade threat has raised questions about international law and whether pressure will force Tehran to make concessions or instead harden its stance. If the ceasefire expires without extension, the conflict could shift from managed escalation back to open warfare, with the Strait of Hormuz once again at the center of the crisis. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/bf6a057faebfc11eb0c76510a4fc20b1?utm_source=openai))

That is why the meeting in Tehran matters. It does not signal a breakthrough on its own, but it does show that the ceasefire is not yet dead. In a war that has repeatedly swung between battlefield violence and high-stakes bargaining, the next round of diplomacy will be judged less by formal statements than by whether ships keep moving, fuel supplies keep flowing and the guns stay quiet long enough for negotiators to build something sturdier than a two-week pause. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/297a8d2bb94add26e503a4ef3a5d1151?utm_source=openai))
