The Harvest Wage Fight Has Reached the Produce Cooler

A court fight over H-2A farmworker pay is also a food-price story, but not in the simple way shoppers may expect. Growers want a lawful seasonal workforce they can afford, workers fear a wage floor that pulls down the whole rural labor market, and any savings would pass through a grocery chain with many hands on the margin.

By The Press • April 25, 2026 • 7:21 p.m.

The most revealing wage fight in the economy this spring is not taking place at a factory gate, a downtown office tower or a familiar bargaining table. It is unfolding in a more dispersed setting: fields, orchards, packing sheds, employer housing, farm-labor buses and a federal courtroom in Fresno. The subject is the wage floor for H-2A temporary agricultural workers. The household version of the question is more direct: if the government makes seasonal harvest labor cheaper for farms, will the grocery bill go down?

The honest answer is that it might help keep some crops planted and picked, but it should not be sold to shoppers as a clean discount at the produce case. A wage rule can lower one input cost for growers while the retail price of lettuce, berries, melons, onions, tomatoes or apples is still shaped by weather, water, cooling, packing, freight, shrink, store labor, buyer power, fuel, advertising, rent and pricing strategy. The worker can lose income without the shopper receiving an equal gain.

That distinction matters because the H-2A program has outgrown its old status as a farm-policy specialty. It has become part of the country’s cost-of-living machinery. The program allows agricultural employers to bring in foreign seasonal workers after certifying that they cannot find enough able, willing, qualified and available U.S. workers and that hiring foreign workers will not adversely affect similarly employed U.S. workers. In fiscal 2025, the Labor Department certified 398,258 H-2A positions, up from about 385,000 in fiscal 2024 and more than seven times the roughly 48,000 positions certified in fiscal 2005. In the first quarter of fiscal 2026, employers requested 65,123 positions and 62,341 were certified. Florida, Washington, Arizona, Texas, Louisiana and California led the early-season certifications.

The wage number at the center of the fight is the Adverse Effect Wage Rate, or AEWR. It is not an ordinary minimum wage. It is a special H-2A wage floor meant to keep the admission of guestworkers from depressing the pay and working conditions of U.S. workers in comparable jobs. Because so much labor-intensive agriculture now depends on immigrant seasonal labor, the AEWR has become a quiet but powerful economic lever. It determines how much of the cost of a legal seasonal workforce is borne by growers, workers, retailers, consumers, taxpayers or some moving combination of all of them.

The Trump administration’s October 2, 2025 interim final rule changed the way the AEWR is calculated for non-range H-2A jobs. It took effect immediately, invited comments afterward, shifted wage setting toward Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics data, created skill-level distinctions, and added a downward housing-related adjustment for H-2A workers who receive employer-provided housing. The rule says that adjustment may not exceed 30 percent of the applicable hourly AEWR. It also makes a large admission: the new method is expected to reduce wage floors and transfer money from workers to employers. The Department of Labor estimated average annual transfers of about $2.42 billion, including both the housing adjustment and the wage-methodology change, while saying it could not quantify all impacts on corresponding U.S. workers.

That is why the rule has produced two economic stories at once. The grower story is about survival in a sector that says it cannot harvest crops without a lawful, timely and affordable workforce. The worker story is about a wage floor that may not stay confined to visa holders. The consumer story is about the space between those two claims: lower harvest labor costs may stabilize supply, but they do not automatically become lower supermarket prices.

A visa program became a labor market

The H-2A program was designed as a safety valve for seasonal farm labor. Employers must recruit U.S. workers first, secure certification through the Labor Department, and meet program rules that include wage, housing, transportation and work-hour guarantees. For workers who cannot reasonably return home at the end of the day, employers generally must provide housing. They must provide safe transportation between housing and the worksite. They must also guarantee at least three-fourths of the work hours in the contract period. These obligations are not abstractions. They become buses, bunkhouses, kitchens, inspections, legal fees, payroll systems, supervisors, recruitment paperwork and compliance risk.

Those costs help explain why growers describe H-2A as both essential and expensive. The program’s rapid growth does not mean it is cheap. It means employers have been willing to pay a premium for a workforce that arrives when crops need to be picked. A strawberry field, apple orchard or vegetable operation cannot postpone harvest until the broader labor market loosens. Biological time is not accounting time. Heat, rain, disease and ripeness can narrow the window further. A crew that arrives on schedule, even at a higher cost, may be the difference between a marketable crop and a loss.

The economic pressure is especially sharp in fruits, vegetables, nursery products and other labor-intensive crops. USDA’s farm-labor overview notes that wages and salaries plus contract labor represented 40 percent of production expenses for fruit and tree-nut operations and 42 percent for greenhouse and nursery operations in the 2022 Census of Agriculture, compared with 12 percent for all farms. Those averages conceal even sharper pressure at the moment of harvest, when a large share of the year’s revenue can depend on a short burst of labor.

The program’s growth also reflects the limits of the domestic farm-labor pool. USDA’s Economic Research Service says H-2A certified positions rose from just over 48,000 in fiscal 2005 to around 385,000 in fiscal 2024, while noting that a certified job does not necessarily equal a visa or a unique worker. The Labor Department’s National Agricultural Workers Survey, which excludes H-2A workers and livestock workers, has long shown that crop agriculture relies heavily on foreign-born workers. ERS summarizes recent NAWS-based data as showing that 42 percent of hired crop farmworkers in 2020-2022 lacked work authorization, while 32 percent were U.S.-born, 7 percent were naturalized citizens and 19 percent were other authorized immigrants.

Those figures make the politics of the wage rule unusually uncomfortable. The United States has relied on immigrant labor to keep labor-intensive agriculture functioning. It has also failed to build an immigration and labor system that makes that dependence stable, legal, adequately protected and affordable. The H-2A wage fight is the point at which those failures meet the grocery aisle.

The rule is a cost cut, but not only a cost cut

The Labor Department’s case for the October rule is that the old wage structure was not sufficiently tied to the actual job being offered and that farm employers faced a more urgent labor-supply problem after the Farm Labor Survey was discontinued and after litigation affected the 2023 AEWR rule. The department invoked the Administrative Procedure Act’s good-cause exception to bypass ordinary pre-enforcement notice and comment, saying delay would be contrary to the public interest and impracticable. It also linked the issue to domestic food supply, global competitiveness and the availability of legal agricultural labor.

That argument is not imaginary. Many farms operate in markets where they cannot simply pass every cost increase to buyers. A grower may sell to a national retailer with substantial bargaining power, compete with imports from countries with different wage structures, face a temporary glut from another region, or absorb losses from weather and disease. The same farm may be carrying higher insurance, interest, water, equipment, fertilizer, compliance and fuel costs. USDA’s February 2026 farm income forecast projected net farm income at $153.4 billion for 2026, down slightly in nominal terms and down 2.6 percent after inflation from 2025. It also projected production expenses near current high levels, with cash labor expenses at $53.9 billion, up 2.2 percent from the expected 2025 level.

The April 2026 Beige Book added another layer. The Federal Reserve’s national summary reported that many districts saw consumer financial strain, rising price sensitivity and input cost increases outpacing selling price growth. The Atlanta district reported flat to slightly down agricultural conditions amid margin pressure from elevated input costs, especially fertilizer. The San Francisco district reported that elevated input costs and low selling prices weighed on some farmers, with several borrowers carrying over loans and some growers lacking financing for 2026 crops. In that environment, a grower-side plea for relief cannot be dismissed as only a request for cheap labor.

But the worker-side objection is also not theoretical. The AEWR is designed precisely because H-2A labor is not a normal free labor market. A guestworker’s visa status is tied to the sponsoring employer. Workers may have limited ability to change jobs, bargain, complain or walk away without jeopardizing their season. Domestic workers who work alongside H-2A crews are affected by the same local wage norms, contractors, harvest calendars and employer expectations. If the legal guestworker wage floor falls, it can become a reference price for the surrounding labor market.

That is the core of the lawsuit filed in November 2025 by the United Farm Workers, the UFW Foundation and individual farmworkers in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of California. The plaintiffs argue that the rule violates the statutory duty to prevent adverse effects on U.S. workers, is arbitrary and capricious, and was improperly issued without ordinary notice-and-comment procedures. Their challenge focuses on the two-tier wage structure, the housing adjustment, the use of OEWS data that worker advocates say is not farm-establishment-specific, and the way job duties are classified.

Farmworker advocates have put much larger numbers on the feared wage loss. The Economic Policy Institute, which opposes the rule, estimated that H-2A workers could lose roughly $1.7 billion to $2.2 billion annually under common scenarios, and that all farmworkers could lose $4.4 billion to $5.4 billion annually once spillovers to domestic workers are included. Those estimates are disputed by the rule’s defenders, but the dispute is not over whether the rule lowers labor costs. The Department of Labor itself describes the change as producing transfers from workers to employers. The fight is over whether those transfers are lawful, justified and economically necessary.

The housing adjustment is the hinge

The rule’s most politically revealing feature is the housing adjustment. H-2A employers have long been required to provide housing at no cost to workers who cannot reasonably return to their homes the same day. The Labor Department’s October rule treats that housing as non-wage compensation that should be recognized in the wage formula. It says domestic farmworkers often face significant housing burdens while H-2A workers receive employer-provided lodging, and it therefore establishes a downward hourly adjustment based on statewide fair-market rent calculations, capped at 30 percent of the AEWR.

There is a real problem hidden inside that rationale. Farmworker housing is expensive, scarce and often inadequate in many agricultural regions. Domestic farmworkers may pay high rents, double up in crowded housing, commute long distances or move with unstable seasonal work. If the policy question were how to improve the housing security of domestic farmworker families, there would be a strong case for direct investment, enforcement and rural housing programs.

The October wage rule answers the housing problem differently. It lowers the cash wage of H-2A workers because they receive employer-provided housing. Worker advocates argue that this converts a statutory employer obligation into a wage deduction and ignores the control employers gain when workers live in employer-controlled housing. It also does not provide domestic workers with rent relief. A domestic farmworker facing high rent in a California, Washington, Georgia or Florida agricultural county does not receive cheaper housing because an H-2A worker’s cash wage is reduced.

That is why the housing provision has become a symbol for the whole dispute. The grower sees housing as a major program cost layered on top of wages. The worker sees housing as a condition of a tied employment relationship, not a benefit that should be used to cut pay. The consumer does not see the housing at all. By the time a carton of strawberries reaches a store shelf, the cost of bunk space, transportation, compliance and the worker’s remittance obligations have disappeared into the broader price.

The grocery promise is weaker than the labor effect

The most tempting consumer conclusion is simple: if harvest labor costs less, produce should cost less. That is a poor map of the food chain. Farm labor is critical, but retail food prices are not a one-for-one pass-through of field wages. USDA’s price-spread data show why. The farm share of retail food varies by product, and the spread between farm value and retail value includes sorting, washing, trimming, bagging, cooling, processing, transportation, wholesaling, retailing and other marketing activities. Fresh produce gives farmers a more direct role than many processed foods, but the retail price still reflects many post-farm costs and decisions.

That is especially true for produce because items differ so widely. Lettuce, tomatoes, apples, carrots, berries and melons have different labor needs, perishability, freight distances, import exposure, packaging, shrink rates and retail pricing strategies. A wage change that matters a great deal to a berry operation may barely move the national food-at-home index. A short weather shock in one growing region can move a specific item sharply without saying much about grocery inflation overall. Tomatoes can spike while lettuce falls. Fresh vegetables can rise faster than fresh fruits. The shopper experiences all of that as one cart, but the supply chains are not one chain.

The latest price data reinforce the point. In March 2026, the BLS food-at-home index was down 0.2 percent on a seasonally adjusted monthly basis and up 1.9 percent from March 2025. Fresh fruits and vegetables were up 4.2 percent from a year earlier, fresh vegetables were up 7.5 percent, lettuce was up 13.8 percent and tomatoes were up 22.6 percent. USDA’s April 2026 Food Price Outlook projected food-at-home prices to rise 2.4 percent in 2026, slower than their 20-year historical average of 2.6 percent, while food-away-from-home prices were projected to rise 3.6 percent, slightly faster than their 20-year average.

Those figures do not make field labor unimportant. They show that the grocery bill is built from many layers. The same household may see restaurant prices rise faster than grocery prices, fresh vegetables rise faster than fresh fruit, eggs fall sharply from a year earlier, beef remain expensive and coffee push up beverage prices. H-2A wages can matter deeply to the supply of berries, vegetables, fruit and nursery products without producing a visible markdown in the weekly cart.

There are several reasons savings may not flow to consumers. Growers may use them to cover fertilizer, water, energy, interest, insurance or debt. Farm labor contractors may absorb part of the margin. Retailers may keep prices steady because consumers have already shown they will pay. A lower wage floor may prevent acreage cuts rather than reduce the price of already-planted acreage. It may slow import substitution rather than lower retail prices. It may keep a farm operating through a difficult season, which is a real supply benefit, but not the same as an immediate consumer discount.

The strongest consumer case is stability, not cheapness

The strongest argument for the rule from a household-budget perspective is not that salad will suddenly be cheaper. It is that a legal labor channel with lower costs may keep more domestic production in place, reduce the risk of crops going unharvested and limit pressure to move production offshore. USDA research on fresh fruit and vegetable industries has described the adjustment menu when labor costs rise: growers may use mechanical aids, reduce the number of harvest passes, change management practices, invest in mechanization, shift crops, expand use of H-2A workers or reduce domestic production. Some of those adjustments improve productivity. Others reduce the amount or quality of domestic supply.

That stability argument deserves attention. A country that wants American-grown produce cannot ignore whether farms can legally hire workers during short harvest windows. It also cannot ignore that many labor-intensive crops face import competition from countries with different labor, environmental, water and compliance costs. If the domestic system becomes too expensive or too uncertain, production can move. Once it moves, the consumer may not see the labor conditions behind the replacement supply.

But stability should be named honestly. It is not the same as affordability, and it is not free. If the wage rule lowers the cash pay of H-2A workers and exerts downward pressure on domestic workers, the first people to finance that stability are farmworkers. They finance it through lower wages, smaller remittances, tighter rent budgets and less room for months when work slows. That cost is not visible in the grocery aisle, but it is part of the economic design.

The court fight has made that design unusually explicit. At a March 18, 2026 hearing in Fresno, farmworkers and advocates rallied outside the federal courthouse while lawyers argued inside over whether the rule should be paused. Local reporting described the proceeding as a fight over whether a wage cut for H-2A workers would ripple through the broader agricultural workforce. Grower-side groups, including WAFLA and a coalition of agricultural organizations, have urged the court to reject the challenge, warning that blocking the rule would disrupt 2026 planning and inject uncertainty into operations that had already calculated wages, financing and hiring needs.

The resulting policy conflict is not a clean morality play. Some farms really do face severe labor and cost pressure. Some workers really do have little bargaining power in a visa system tied to employers. Some households really are tired of high food prices. All three facts can be true at once. The problem is that the October rule tries to reconcile them inside a wage formula, and a wage formula cannot carry the full weight of immigration policy, housing policy, farm finance, food affordability and labor standards.

The missing policy is not only a wage formula

A more durable food-labor policy would separate problems now being forced into the AEWR. If the problem is farmworker housing, lawmakers could expand and enforce farmworker housing programs rather than using employer-provided H-2A housing to justify a cash-wage reduction. If the problem is domestic recruitment, enforcement could examine whether jobs are advertised in ways that U.S. workers can realistically take, whether transportation and schedules make the work feasible, and whether contractors are shaping local labor markets in ways that suppress pay.

If the problem is legal labor supply, Congress could modernize agricultural immigration law instead of leaving agencies to stretch old statutory language around a workforce that has already changed. If the problem is food affordability, policymakers could use nutrition assistance, retail competition policy, freight resilience, regional supply investment and antitrust scrutiny rather than expecting field-wage cuts to solve the grocery bill. If the problem is farm margins, farm policy could address credit, insurance, disaster assistance, water infrastructure and market concentration more directly.

There is also a data problem. H-2A certification data show positions approved, not necessarily workers present, hours worked or final pay after all contract realities. NAWS is essential for understanding crop workers, but it excludes H-2A workers and is not a real-time labor-market instrument. The Farm Labor Survey historically played a central role in wage setting, but USDA’s discontinuation of the survey was one of the reasons DOL cited for emergency rulemaking. OEWS is broad and detailed, but worker advocates and some agricultural analysts object that it is not built around farm establishments in the same way. Retail price data show what shoppers pay, not which actor captured the margin between the field and the checkout lane.

A country trying to manage food prices, legal migration and rural labor markets through administrative formulas needs better and faster information. It needs to know not just how many positions were certified, but how many workers arrived, how many hours were worked, what workers actually took home, how contractors set bids, how wage floors affected domestic workers, how much acreage shifted, and whether any savings reached consumers. Without that, the debate becomes a series of claims made from different points in the supply chain.

The H-2A wage fight is therefore larger than its bureaucratic language. It is a small-looking administrative change with a large economic confession inside it. The United States wants low food prices, a legal agricultural workforce, secure domestic production, higher wages for people doing difficult work, and less dependence on unauthorized labor. The current system cannot deliver all of those goals at once. The October rule chooses one balance point by making legal seasonal labor cheaper for many employers. The lawsuit asks whether that choice violates the statute that is supposed to protect U.S. workers from adverse effects.

Households will not experience the answer as a line in the Federal Register. They will experience it indirectly: in whether a crop is harvested, whether a farm shifts acreage, whether a contractor can staff a crew, whether a rural rental market tightens, whether a remittance shrinks, whether a store keeps a price steady, and whether a produce item is available when weather and labor collide. The produce aisle begins far from the checkout scanner. This spring, it begins at a wage floor.
