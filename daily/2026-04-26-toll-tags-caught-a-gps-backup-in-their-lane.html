Toll Tags Caught a GPS Backup in Their Lane

A useful strip of 900 MHz spectrum helps meters report, toll tags answer gantries, warehouses read goods and security devices check in. NextNav wants part of it remade for a terrestrial GPS complement. The fight has become a practical test of how much disturbance Washington will accept in the quiet machinery of everyday wireless life.

By The Press • April 26, 2026 • 3:22 a.m.

The radio fight of the week is not taking place in a scene that looks like the future.

It is hiding in a windshield toll tag. It is in the electric meter on the side of a house. It is in the warehouse reader that notices a pallet, the security sensor that checks whether a door opened, the retail tag that helps a store find a jacket, the railroad device that helps identify equipment, and the amateur-radio node that may be switched on when ordinary networks are down.

Many of those devices use, touch or crowd around the same useful neighborhood of the airwaves: 902 to 928 megahertz. The band is not glamorous. It is not the headline midband spectrum of the 5G boom, not Wi-Fi’s best-known territory, not satellite broadband. It is a modest, low-band workhorse whose signals travel reasonably well through walls and across utility yards and whose chips can be cheap enough to disappear into everyday equipment.

Now NextNav, a geolocation company, wants the Federal Communications Commission to reorganize that room.

The company has asked the FCC to reconfigure the lower 900 MHz band so it can support a ground-based positioning, navigation and timing system — a terrestrial complement and backup to the satellite-based Global Positioning System — built around 5G technology. NextNav’s requested plan would create a five-megahertz uplink at 902-907 MHz and a 10-megahertz downlink at 918-928 MHz, move remaining non-multilateration Location and Monitoring Service licensees into 907-918 MHz, and exchange the company’s existing Location and Monitoring Service holdings for a single nationwide 15-megahertz flexible-use license.

That is the formal version. The plain-English version is harder: a company wants Washington to let it build a private, nationwide layer of GPS backup in a band already occupied by a dense ecology of quiet devices.

The need for GPS resilience is not invented. GPS is powerful, free to users and deeply embedded in modern life. It is also vulnerable to jamming, spoofing, space-weather disruptions and local outages. Federal agencies have repeatedly treated positioning, navigation and timing — usually shortened to PNT — as a national infrastructure issue. A phone’s blue dot is only the consumer face of the system. Precise timing and location data also matter to communications networks, emergency response, banking, ports, farms, power systems, transportation and public safety.

But the fight over NextNav is not a clean argument between progress and inertia. It is a fight between two different definitions of resilience.

One definition says the country should stop leaning so heavily on satellites before a crisis proves how dependent it has become. The other says a GPS backup should not be created by endangering the meter networks, toll systems, RFID readers, alarm devices and public-interest wireless uses that already make the physical economy legible.

As of April 26, 2026, the dispute has moved beyond the engineer’s notebook. On April 22, the House Appropriations Committee said it had approved the fiscal 2027 Financial Services and General Government appropriations bill and adopted an en bloc amendment that included language prohibiting the FCC from reconfiguring or repurposing the lower 900 MHz band. Broadband Breakfast reported that the amendment would bar FCC funds from being used to finalize, implement or enforce rules or orders that would reconfigure or repurpose 902-928 MHz in a way that authorizes high-power terrestrial operations. That does not settle the FCC proceeding, and appropriations language can change. It does show that a spectrum docket about toll tags and meter radios has become a live congressional issue.

The FCC record is also still moving. The commission opened WT Docket No. 24-240 in August 2024 to seek comment on NextNav’s petition. In March 2025, it opened a broader inquiry into PNT technologies and solutions in WT Docket No. 25-110, explicitly saying the NextNav petition remained separate and ongoing. In March 2026, an FCC proposed rule titled “Promoting the Development of Positioning, Navigation, and Timing Technologies and Solutions” was received at the Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs. Incumbent-device groups and NextNav supporters have read that as a sign the FCC may be preparing another PNT rulemaking step, though that OIRA entry concerns the broader PNT docket, not a final decision on NextNav’s 900 MHz petition.

This is the odd charm of spectrum policy. The argument is invisible until something fails. Then everyone discovers they were sharing the same hallway.

The band with too many quiet jobs

Radio spectrum is often described as real estate, but the metaphor is only partly useful. A parcel of land can hold one building at a time. A frequency band can hold many uses if devices are weak enough, brief enough, coordinated enough or legally subordinate enough to avoid breaking one another.

The 902-928 MHz band became useful because of its physical personality. It sits low enough that signals can travel farther and penetrate walls better than many higher-frequency systems. It is also high enough to support small antennas and inexpensive electronics. For many devices, the job is not to carry a movie or a video call. It is to wake up, send a short message, receive a short command and go back to sleep.

That profile made the band attractive for telemetry, identification and low-data-rate control. A meter can report usage. A toll tag can answer a roadside reader. A warehouse label can identify a box. A farm or utility sensor can send a short status report. A smart-home device can tell a hub that a door opened. A security device can check in. A hobbyist node can pass a simple message.

Many of these devices operate under Part 15 of the FCC’s rules. Part 15 is the unlicensed bargain behind much of modern wireless convenience. A device can operate without an individual license, but it must not cause harmful interference and generally must accept interference from authorized users. That legal position can sound harsh, but it is one reason cheap wireless devices spread so widely: the regulator did not need to license every sensor, tag and remote.

The band also contains licensed and specialized uses. The FCC’s 2024 public notice described a layered hierarchy: federal radiolocation and industrial, scientific and medical equipment on a primary basis; Location and Monitoring Service licensees; non-M-LMS licensees; amateur radio on a secondary basis; and Part 15 devices operating without the ordinary protections of licensed services. The agency said there were approximately 1,500 active non-M-LMS licenses in the band when it sought comment on NextNav’s petition.

That hierarchy is complicated, but its social rule is simple: the band works because most devices are small, intermittent or constrained. The etiquette changes when a stronger neighbor arrives.

NextNav argues that the change is necessary. Its petition says a 5G-based terrestrial PNT network could complement and back up GPS, help indoor and urban positioning, support public safety and leave excess low-band capacity available for mobile broadband use through partnerships. The company has filed studies and supplemental analyses arguing that 5G operations can coexist with unlicensed devices, UHF RFID systems, security devices and tolling operations. In January 2026, it said tolling operations could coexist with its proposed lower 900 MHz 5G PNT operations, and that any retuning burden would fall on some reader equipment rather than ordinary consumer transponders.

Opponents hear something different. To them, NextNav is not merely proposing a GPS backup. It is asking the FCC to turn a shared, irregular, low-power band into a nationwide high-power flexible-use license for one company, with broadband value attached. The FCC itself raised the issue in its public notice, asking about the potential windfall NextNav might receive from the spectrum swap and how the commission should address it.

The important point is that no side disputes the band’s value. The fight is over who gets to redefine that value.

GPS is not broken. That is part of the problem.

GPS is one of the most successful public technologies in history partly because it usually vanishes. Drivers do not think about orbiting atomic clocks when their phones announce a turn. Banks do not advertise satellite timing when transactions are time-stamped. Cell networks do not tell subscribers that precise time helps towers coordinate. Farmers, ships, surveyors, emergency responders and delivery fleets simply use it.

That invisibility creates a policy trap. It is difficult to pay for a backup to a system people experience as free and reliable.

Federal warnings have accumulated for years. A 2020 executive order framed PNT as a largely invisible utility whose responsible use is important to critical infrastructure. The Department of Transportation’s Complementary PNT Action Plan, updated in March 2024, set lines of effort around stakeholder engagement, specifications and standards, field trials, a federal PNT services clearinghouse and acquisition support. CISA has warned that reliance on GPS as the primary and sometimes sole source of PNT data leaves critical infrastructure exposed to disruption or manipulation. NIST has published cybersecurity guidance for systems that use PNT data.

The economic scale is large, even if the exact number depends on assumptions. A NIST-sponsored RTI study estimated that GPS generated roughly $1.4 trillion in U.S. private-sector economic benefits from the 1980s through 2017 and that a loss of GPS service could average about $1 billion per day in national impact. The study also warned that losses could be higher during critical planting periods because of precision agriculture.

Those numbers should not be read as a universal timer for every outage. A local jammer, a spoofing incident near an airport, a regional timing failure and a nationwide multiweek disruption would have different consequences. But the scale point is hard to dismiss: GPS is not just a navigation convenience. It is an input to the operating system of the economy.

NextNav’s strongest argument begins there. If GPS is essential and vulnerable, then waiting for a perfect government-built replacement may be a mistake. The company says a market-based terrestrial service using 5G technology could move faster and support uses satellite signals struggle with, including indoor and urban positioning and vertical location for emergency response.

That is the attractive version of the plan: use private investment and the value of spectrum to build an additional layer of national resilience.

It is also where the hardest questions begin. If a GPS backup is a national-security necessity, should the answer be a nationwide license for a single company in a band that already serves public-interest systems? If the project is sold as no-cost resilience, should replacement, retuning or mitigation costs borne by utilities, toll agencies, retailers, alarm-system owners and consumers be counted as part of the bill? If 5G broadband is part of the value proposition, how much of the proceeding is about PNT and how much is about monetizing a low-band asset?

The FCC’s own questions suggest the commission understands that distinction. In the 2024 notice, it asked whether a terrestrial PNT system would provide ubiquitous nationwide coverage similar to GPS, what infrastructure would be required, what costs would be involved, what performance requirements would ensure intensive use of the spectrum, and whether license conditions would be needed to make sure a GPS-complementing system is permanently implemented and maintained.

Those are not academic questions. They are the difference between a backup and a business plan that mentions backup.

Interference is not one thing

To a non-engineer, interference can sound binary. Either a device works or it does not. Real radio systems live in the middle.

A toll reader may work in one lane and fail at another angle. A warehouse RFID system may read well when the room is sparse and badly when tags, reflections and noise multiply. A utility mesh may tolerate extra noise until a few weak links become unreliable. A security sensor may send only a few bits, but it may need those bits to arrive at the moment a door opens or a smoke alarm reports. A railroad or utility system may have been designed for long service lives, not quick replacement cycles.

That is why the proceeding has become a duel of studies.

NextNav says its analyses support coexistence. Its public materials point to engineering work on unlicensed Part 15 technologies, supplemental coexistence analysis, RFID studies, tolling studies and responses to security-industry objections. It has argued that opponents exaggerate the power and interference profile of its proposed system and that a rulemaking is the proper venue to test and refine conditions.

Opponents say the company’s studies are too narrow, too theoretical or too forgiving of real-world complexity. The Security Industry Association, citing a Pericle Communications Company report, warned that the proposal could severely limit the range and effectiveness of lower 900 MHz security and life-safety devices. The Utilities Technology Council said NextNav’s proposal threatens advanced metering infrastructure and supervisory control and data acquisition systems. Tolling interests have warned of missed reads, higher transaction costs, billing errors and loss of trust. RFID groups worry about UHF RFID deployments in retail, logistics, manufacturing, aviation and health care. Amateur radio groups and open-wireless advocates see a shared band being squeezed by a nationwide commercial license.

The installed base is difficult to count because the band’s value is precisely that it is diffuse. A utility may know its meters. A toll authority may know its gantries. A retailer may know its readers. A warehouse may know some of its equipment but not every supplier’s tag. A homeowner may not know that a lock, alarm sensor or leak detector uses a lower 900 MHz protocol. A hobbyist node or emergency mesh device may never appear in a commercial inventory.

That uncertainty cuts both ways. It can allow opponents to imply a worst-case universe of vulnerable devices. It can also allow proponents to understate transition costs because nobody has a perfect list of what would need protection, retuning, shielding or replacement.

Field testing matters, but it cannot carry the whole burden. Broadband Breakfast reported that the FCC had approved NextNav coexistence testing with railroad equipment in Colorado, in addition to the company’s San Jose field testing. The San Jose work followed an FCC experimental license granted in late 2025, with the test network planned to operate through Dec. 1, 2027. Such testing can generate useful evidence. It will not automatically answer how every deployed meter, toll reader, RFID system, alarm sensor and amateur use behaves nationwide under different density, terrain, weather and building conditions.

A coexistence result in one configuration is evidence, not absolution. A failure in one configuration is evidence, not a full indictment. The commission’s task is to ask where the margins are, how failures would be detected, who must fix them, how fast they can be fixed and who pays.

The cheapest radio can be the most expensive one to move

Old infrastructure becomes political when it works just well enough to be forgotten.

A smart meter is not as dramatic as a power plant. But replacing meters early is expensive because the radio is only one piece of a system. A utility has trucks, labor schedules, customer-access problems, billing software, data systems, outage-management practices, rate cases and procurement cycles. A modest radio conflict can turn into a capital project.

Tolling has the same hidden depth. A windshield tag looks disposable. The system behind it is not. A toll road, bridge or tunnel may rely on gantries, roadside readers, cameras, back-office account systems, enforcement procedures and interoperability arrangements. If readers need retuning, filtering, shielding or replacement, the consumer may never see the technical fix. The cost will still land somewhere.

Retail and logistics face a different kind of fragility. RFID works because it turns objects into data. A tag can help a store know whether a coat is in the back room, a hospital track supplies, an airline manage parts, a manufacturer follow components or a warehouse locate inventory. The system is mundane until reads become unreliable. Then a small drop in performance can become labor, shrinkage, delay and customer-service trouble.

This is the practical center of the NextNav fight. The lower 900 MHz band is not merely a channel chart. It is sunk cost, procurement memory, field labor, replacement cycles and operational habit.

NextNav’s response is that spectrum policy cannot give every incumbent a permanent veto. That is true. The FCC has often repurposed bands when a new use promised greater public benefit. Existing investments are not automatically sacred. If a terrestrial GPS complement could reduce a serious national vulnerability, some transition burden might be justified.

But the accounting has to be public and honest. A proposal does not become no-cost because the federal government is not writing the first check. Costs can move to utility bills, toll budgets, alarm-system replacements, retail infrastructure, municipal systems or consumer equipment. If Washington is weighing national resilience against incumbent disruption, the disruption should not be treated as a rounding error.

There are other GPS complements, but none is magic

If NextNav were the only possible GPS complement, the policy question would be narrower. It is not.

The FCC’s 2025 PNT inquiry asked about a broad range of technologies and policy paths: space-based systems, terrestrial systems, broadcast-based timing, mobile-broadband positioning, testbeds, receiver equipment, public-private partnerships and rule changes. DOT’s earlier complementary PNT work pointed toward diversity rather than a single replacement. NTIA’s 2025 inventory of complementary, alternative and augmentative PNT solutions listed options including GPS modernization, low-Earth-orbit satellite approaches, eLoran, broadcast-based timing, mobile broadband systems, time-over-fiber and inertial navigation.

Each alternative has a catch.

Low-Earth-orbit PNT systems may offer stronger or different signals, but they still depend on space systems, receiver adoption and commercial deployment. eLoran has long had supporters because low-frequency terrestrial signals are difficult to jam over wide areas, but U.S. deployment has lagged for years. Broadcast Positioning System proposals would use ATSC 3.0 television infrastructure for timing, but ATSC 3.0’s broader transition is itself politically and commercially tangled. Fiber timing can be excellent for fixed critical sites, but it does not solve every mobile location need. Inertial systems can bridge gaps, but sensors drift unless corrected.

The phrase agencies often use is “system of systems.” It sounds bureaucratic because it is. It is also probably right. A resilient PNT future is unlikely to be one perfect substitute for GPS. It may be a stack: stronger and modernized satellite signals, smarter receivers, terrestrial timing, fiber for fixed critical infrastructure, satellite diversity, local sensors, sector-specific procedures and operating plans for degraded service.

NextNav can fit inside that universe. The question is whether its requested 900 MHz reconfiguration is the right price of admission.

The FCC has to decide what kind of proof counts

The FCC is comfortable with interference arguments. It is less comfortable with national mood. This proceeding has both.

On one side, GPS vulnerability is easy to dramatize and hard to ignore. Wars and regional conflicts have made GNSS interference a regular operational problem in parts of the world. Commercial aviation, maritime operators, military planners and infrastructure owners understand that signals from space can be jammed or spoofed. The United States does not need to wait for a domestic emergency to admit that dependence creates risk.

On the other side, the incumbent lower 900 MHz users are not defending nostalgia. They are defending systems built under rules the FCC created. Many are low-power, unlicensed or secondary users, but they are not trivial users. They are embedded in public safety, utilities, commerce, transportation, homes and experimentation.

The commission’s hardest task is to avoid being dazzled by either side’s abstraction.

A GPS backup is an abstraction until it keeps a cell network timed, a port operating, a rescue crew oriented or a financial system synchronized. Interference is an abstraction until a meter stops reporting, a toll gantry misses a tag, a security sensor fails, a railcar is misidentified or a warehouse inventory system becomes unreliable. Both harms are real. Neither should be waved away.

The useful next step is not slogan but evidence that looks like the world: dense deployments, old equipment, mixed vendors, warehouses, toll plazas, substations, rail yards, apartments, hospitals, farms, weather, reflective metal, urban canyons and emergency conditions. Testing should measure not only whether devices work once, but how much margin remains, how failures appear, who sees them and how they are remedied. Transition costs should be estimated in public. If a nationwide PNT license is granted, obligations should be enforceable: coverage, timing performance, uptime, emergency access, cybersecurity, reporting, interoperability and consequences for underbuilding.

If the public benefit is a GPS backup, the public should know exactly what backup it is getting.

A shared hallway, not an empty room

The lower 900 MHz fight matters because it reveals how digital life is actually built. The internet is not only clouds, apps and artificial intelligence. It is also radios in meters, readers over lanes, tags on boxes, clocks in networks, antennas on poles, sensors in doors and rules in federal dockets.

The band at issue is easy to underestimate because its devices do not behave like stars. They do not stream movies. They do not launch rockets. They do not answer questions in a human voice. They report, identify, unlock, count, measure and synchronize.

That is why the dispute is revealing. NextNav is trying to solve a real vulnerability in the national machine. Its critics are defending another part of the same machine. The public interest is not automatically on either side. It sits in the accounting: risk reduced, risk created, money spent, obligations enforced, alternatives considered and failure modes anticipated before they become expensive.

Spectrum policy often sounds like a dispute over invisible property. This one is more like a building renovation. The landlord has discovered that the emergency exit may not be strong enough. A contractor has offered to rebuild it. The tenants are pointing out that the proposed route runs through the mailroom, the meter closet, the loading dock and the hallway where everyone’s keys already work.

Maybe the renovation is necessary. Maybe this contractor has the best plan. Maybe another door, or several smaller doors, would be safer.

But the room is not empty. The quiet machines are already there.
