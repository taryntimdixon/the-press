Sanriku’s One-Week Warning Clock Runs Out in Sendai

Japan ended its rare Hokkaido-Sanriku subsequent-earthquake advisory after a week of grab bags, station delays and coastal caution. The lesson is not that danger vanished; it is that public science now has to explain risk without pretending to know the hour.

By The Press • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

Sendai Station did not look like the end of the world this week. It looked like a place where people still had offices, classes, buses, bags of groceries and trains to catch.

That ordinary motion is the point of Japan’s latest earthquake story. On April 20, a powerful quake struck off the Sanriku coast, the Pacific-facing shoreline that runs along northeastern Honshu. Tsunami warnings and advisories sent coastal communities into their practiced choreography: move away from the water, listen for official updates, do not go to the harbor to look. Bullet trains were temporarily stopped. Screens changed. People waited on platforms. Local governments and institutions began the quieter work of translating a large offshore shock into checklists: water, flashlights, medicine, family contacts, evacuation routes, shoes near the bed.

Then came the more complicated message. The Japan Meteorological Agency, working with the Cabinet Office, issued an “Off the Coast of Hokkaido and Sanriku Subsequent Earthquake Advisory.” The phrase is clunky in English and technical in Japanese. Its meaning is more direct: after a magnitude 7-class earthquake in or near the Japan and Chishima trench hazard zone, the chance of a still larger earthquake may be temporarily higher than usual. Not high. Not certain. Higher.

At 5 p.m. Japan time on Monday, April 27, the special one-week call for extra caution ended. Jiji Press, carried by Nippon.com, reported that JMA and the Cabinet Office closed the special advisory period after one week, while stressing that the end of the period did not mean the region was free of large-earthquake risk. Miyagi Prefecture posted the same practical message: the government’s special call ended at 5 p.m., but everyday preparation should continue.

That distinction is the whole story. The advisory was not a prophecy. It was a public test of how a scientifically advanced, disaster-literate country tells people to live with a changed probability.

What happened off Sanriku

The April 20 earthquake occurred at about 4:53 p.m. Japan time off Sanriku. JMA’s hypocenter list records a magnitude 7.7 event at 16:52:58, at 39 degrees 50.5 minutes north and 143 degrees 9.4 minutes east, with a listed depth of 19 kilometers. The U.S. Geological Survey measured the same broad event differently, at magnitude 7.4. Those numbers are not a newsroom contradiction. Earthquake agencies may use different data, magnitude scales and update cycles, especially in the first hours after a large offshore rupture.

On land, the number that mattered to residents was not only magnitude but shaking intensity. Japan’s seismic intensity scale, often called shindo, measures what people and structures experience at a specific place. A single earthquake has one source magnitude but many local intensity readings. Nippon.com, citing JMA data, reported upper 5 shaking in Hashikami, Aomori Prefecture, on Japan’s 0-to-7 scale. JMA’s English guide explains that its intensity scale includes 0 through 4, then 5 lower, 5 upper, 6 lower, 6 upper and 7. Upper 5 is the kind of shaking that can make walking difficult, knock unsecured objects around and turn a familiar room into something unreliable.

The sea responded too, though not with the catastrophe that a first warning must consider. AP reported that authorities detected an approximately 80-centimeter tsunami at Kuji port in Iwate Prefecture and a smaller 40-centimeter wave at another Iwate port before tsunami alerts were lifted. Earlier warnings had included the possibility of waves up to 3 meters in some areas. That gap between warned-for and observed wave height can make a warning look excessive after the fact. It is not so simple. JMA’s tsunami system is designed to estimate whether destructive waves may be generated and to warn coastal regions before the full sea record is known. A tsunami does not have to be movie-sized to sweep a person off a quay, move debris or make a harbor lethal.

AP reported two people injured after falls, in Aomori and Iwate, and said Shinkansen bullet trains connecting Tokyo and northern Japan were temporarily suspended. The Nuclear Regulation Authority reported no abnormalities at nuclear facilities in the region, according to AP. Those details matter because earthquakes are not only a shaking story. They are a transport story, a port story, a medical story, an energy story, and a trust story.

Risk, not prediction

The subsequent-earthquake advisory began at 7:30 p.m. on April 20. JMA’s advisory text said the Sanriku earthquake had been reviewed as a moment magnitude 7.4 event that met the criteria for the advisory because it could affect the expected source region of a huge earthquake along the Chishima and Japan trenches. The agency said the possibility of a new magnitude-8-class-or-larger earthquake had become relatively higher than normal in the expected source region from off Nemuro, Hokkaido, to off Sanriku, northeastern Honshu.

It also said, in plain terms, what the advisory did not mean. It did not mean that a large earthquake would definitely occur during a specific period. JMA’s April 20 advisory cited global statistics: among 1,529 earthquakes of moment magnitude 7.0 or greater from 1904 to 2021, 19 were followed within seven days and within 500 kilometers by a magnitude 7.8-or-greater earthquake. That is about 1%. AP summarized the official comparison as roughly a 1% chance during the advisory period, compared with about 0.1% in normal times.

A one-in-100 chance is easy to misunderstand. If the hazard were a rainy picnic, 1% might be ignored. If the hazard is a giant trench earthquake and a tsunami reaching broad sections of coast, 1% becomes a number governments cannot simply leave inside a technical file. Cabinet Office materials estimate that a maximum-class Japan Trench earthquake could cause up to about 199,000 deaths, and a maximum-class Chishima Trench earthquake up to about 100,000 deaths, under severe assumptions. Those are planning scenarios, not forecasts for this week. But planning scenarios exist to force society to ask where roads, shelters, nursing homes, ports, schools and phone systems fail first.

That is why the Hokkaido-Sanriku advisory sits in a narrow lane between silence and alarm. Officials were not saying “run now.” They were saying “be able to run if the shaking or tsunami warning comes.” Sendai City’s public guidance put it cleanly: people should continue normal life and social and economic activity, but for about a week after an advisory they should be more alert than usual, ready to evacuate quickly in tsunami evacuation areas, and clear that immediate advance evacuation was not being ordered.

That is a demanding sentence to put into everyday life. It asks a household to be calm but not casual. It asks a hotel to reassure guests while showing them the high ground. It asks a school to keep teaching while checking pickup plans. It asks a care facility to think beyond “grab bag” and toward staffing, oxygen, medication, elevators, wheelchairs, backup power and transport. It asks local officials to say something serious without making the public hear something certain.

The week became a checklist

The most interesting part of the advisory was not the phrase itself. It was what happened after it left JMA’s website and entered municipal pages, campus notices and multilingual help desks.

Tohoku University in Sendai posted a notice to students, faculty and staff after the Sanriku earthquake. The university asked them to review departmental disaster-response protocols and emergency contact networks, and to prioritize personal safety. That is not dramatic language. It is the language of institutions that understand a warning has value only if people already know what to do when minutes matter.

Sendai City’s updated advisory page is almost a miniature manual for living with uncertain hazard information. It explains that a “preceding earthquake” can be followed by a “subsequent earthquake,” that the advisory is issued by JMA and the Cabinet Office when the possibility of a large follow-on event is judged to have risen, and that Sendai is within the affected municipal area. Its practical list is wonderfully uncinematic: sleep in a way that makes evacuation easier, keep emergency items close, avoid damaged buildings and unstable walls, watch landslide risk, turn up the volume on devices that receive emergency information, keep a radio nearby, check water and food stocks, review hazard maps, secure furniture, prevent window-glass scattering, check smoke alarms and seismic breakers, and keep extinguishers reachable.

Miyagi Prefecture’s page took a similar tone. It said the advisory is intended not to stop social and economic activity but to encourage disaster-prevention action while daily life continues. It listed ordinary preparedness steps — confirming evacuation sites and routes, checking family contact methods, securing furniture, preparing an emergency bag, storing water and food — and added advisory-specific actions such as sleeping ready to evacuate and carrying emergency items.

In Sapporo, the International Communication Plaza published English-language guidance for foreign residents. Its wording was gentle but useful: the advisory did not mean a large earthquake would definitely happen, but being prepared could help people feel safer. It told readers to keep extra water and food at home, have a flashlight and batteries ready, talk with family and friends about meeting places, check safe evacuation spots on higher ground if near the ocean, and rely on trustworthy information. Translation is not a decorative service in a disaster. It is infrastructure. A person who does not fully understand the warning cannot fully act on it.

The same is true for tourists and newcomers. A resident may know which road climbs inland. A visitor may know only where the station exit, coffee shop or hotel lobby is. A coastal warning system that assumes local knowledge will always leave someone behind. The advisory’s practical success depends on signs, staff, maps, multilingual pages and repeated drills that make a strange place legible under stress.

Monday morning shook Hokkaido

As the Sanriku advisory week approached its scheduled end, Hokkaido shook again. Early Monday, April 27, an inland earthquake struck the Tokachi region. AP reported a magnitude 6.2 quake, no tsunami advisory, and no immediate damage or casualties. It said the U.S. Geological Survey placed the quake about 18 kilometers west of Sarabetsu at a depth of 81 kilometers and measured it at magnitude 6.1. Nippon.com, citing Jiji Press and JMA, reported an estimated JMA magnitude of 6.2, a depth of 83 kilometers and upper 5 shaking in Urahoro.

The public question was obvious: was this the feared next quake?

JMA officials said no. Ayataka Ebita, director of JMA’s Earthquake and Tsunami Observation Division, said the Hokkaido quake was not believed to be directly related to the April 20 Sanriku event. Nippon.com reported his explanation that the Tokachi quake originated deep within an oceanic plate subducting beneath a continental plate and was not one of the subsequent quakes covered by the advisory. AP likewise reported that JMA said the Monday quake was outside the advisory area and that the special awareness week would end as planned.

That may sound like technical hair-splitting to anyone whose dishes rattled before breakfast. It is not. Location, depth and mechanism are the grammar of earthquake science. A shallow plate-boundary event in the trench zone is not the same as a deeper intraslab earthquake within the descending plate, even when both are frightening. If agencies blur those distinctions, they gain simplicity and lose credibility. If they over-explain them, they risk sounding cold at the exact moment people want reassurance. This is the awkward music of public science: evidence, fear and plain language all have to keep time.

The word “foreshock” is a trap

Earthquake language adds another problem. People often want to know whether a large earthquake was a foreshock. The honest answer is almost always: no one can know yet. The U.S. Geological Survey explains that a foreshock is an earthquake that precedes a larger earthquake in the same area, but the label can only be assigned after the larger earthquake occurs. Until then, the first event is simply an earthquake in an active sequence.

The USGS also says that, around the world, the chance that an earthquake will be followed by a larger nearby earthquake within a week is about 5%, though that value varies with the activity level of the sequence. Japan’s Hokkaido-Sanriku advisory uses a narrower, trench-specific public-warning framework focused on the chance of a magnitude-8-class-or-larger follow-on event after a magnitude-7-class event in or near the Japan and Chishima trench source region. That is why the Japanese advisory’s public number was about 1%, not the broader USGS foreshock figure. Both ideas point to the same communication problem: the most consequential question often cannot be answered in the way the public wants it answered.

That does not make the advisory useless. It makes it harder to use well. A warning that says “the probability is higher but still low” asks citizens to handle two ideas at once. Many people can. But the message has to be repeated in useful forms: what to do tonight, what not to do, where to go if the sea alarm sounds, when the special period ends, and why the end of the period does not mean safety has been certified.

JMA’s own April 20 advisory tried to do this. It said the chance of a later event is highest soon after the first earthquake and declines with time and distance, but does not become zero. It also warned that magnitude-8-class earthquakes often happen suddenly even when no subsequent-earthquake advisory has been issued. That last point is easy to overlook and vital: the advisory is not a gate that danger must pass through before it reaches the coast.

The second disaster is information

The Sanriku earthquake also showed a more modern aftershock: misinformation. AFP fact-checkers documented old earthquake and tsunami videos falsely recirculated as if they showed the April 20 event. AFP found that clips being shared online actually showed earlier disasters or other events, including tsunami footage from 2025, the 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake and the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake and tsunami.

That pattern is now predictable. When the ground moves, old footage moves too. Dramatic video can outrun a careful notice explaining that the observed tsunami at Kuji was about 80 centimeters, that a special advisory is not an evacuation order, or that Monday’s Tokachi quake was not considered the advisory’s feared follow-on. False images can make people panic, but they can also have the opposite effect: if residents later learn the viral clip was old, they may discount the next real warning. In disaster communication, accuracy is not a luxury. It is part of the evacuation route.

Japan is better prepared than most countries for earthquakes and tsunamis, but even Japan cannot solve the human side of probability by engineering alone. Seawalls, sensors, sirens, train protocols and official websites matter. So do trust, habit, neighborhood knowledge and the ability to reach people who do not speak Japanese, do not own smartphones, cannot climb quickly, cannot leave work easily, cannot afford spare supplies, or live alone.

Why this is a global story

Japan’s advisory is unusually specific, but the civic problem is global. Flood forecasts, heat alerts, wildfire evacuation watches, volcanic unrest bulletins, epidemic notices and aftershock forecasts all sit somewhere between certainty and silence. They ask the same question: when does uncertainty become information the public deserves to have?

The United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction and the World Meteorological Organization have pushed the Early Warnings for All initiative, launched in 2022 with the goal of protecting everyone on Earth with early-warning systems by 2027. UNDRR describes effective warnings as people-centered, end-to-end and multi-hazard, not merely technical alerts. The four pillars are risk knowledge, detection and monitoring, warning communication, and preparedness to respond. The Sanriku week is a case study in why all four are necessary.

Detection without trust does not save lives. Communication without clear action creates noise. Preparedness without equity leaves the hardest-to-move people in the most danger. And risk knowledge that stays on a government PDF does not help the hotel clerk who needs to point guests toward higher ground.

Japan’s one-week advisory ended without the feared larger trench earthquake. That is good news. It is also the moment when warnings become vulnerable to bad interpretation. Some people will be tempted to call it a false alarm. But a probabilistic advisory does not fail because the low-probability event does not occur. It fails if people misunderstand it, cannot act on it, or lose trust because officials overstate or understate what is known.

The right audit now is practical. Did residents in the 182 municipalities understand the advisory? Did older people and people with disabilities get help turning advice into action? Did multilingual notices reach foreign residents and visitors? Did schools, hospitals, nursing homes, ports, hotels and rail operators know what extra readiness meant? Did the advisory’s name itself make sense to people outside government and seismology? “Subsequent earthquake advisory” is accurate, but accuracy and memorability are not the same thing when the room starts moving.

There is also a fairness test. Preparedness is easier for people with money, storage space, flexible jobs, cars, strong legs and stable housing. A grab bag costs money. A week’s water takes space. A quick evacuation is simpler for a healthy adult than for a bedbound patient, a parent with toddlers, a worker on a night shift, or a tourist who cannot read the local sign. The same advisory reaches everyone. The ability to act on it does not.

What remains after the clock

Sanriku has taught Japan terrible lessons before. The March 11, 2011, Tohoku earthquake and tsunami remain within living memory and physical landscape: seawalls, rebuilt towns, evacuation markers, memorials, drills, school lessons and grief. The danger of invoking 2011 too casually is that every advisory can sound like dread. The danger of not invoking it is that preparation becomes abstract.

The better lesson is smaller and more useful: the first minutes matter, and the preparation for those minutes happens on boring days.

Monday’s advisory expiration did not close the Japan Trench. It did not make northern Japan safe from earthquakes. It ended one specific week in which officials judged the probability of a very large follow-on earthquake to be relatively elevated after one specific offshore event. The larger hazard remains, as it did before April 20 and as it will after April 27.

So the scene to hold is not a wall of water or a cracked highway. It is a station concourse, a municipal webpage, a campus notice, a translated help-desk post, a flashlight with fresh batteries, a family message thread, a care-home manager checking a roster, a train passenger looking at a delay board, a seismologist explaining why one frightening quake does not equal another, and a coast keeping its blue evacuation signs in place after the headline fades.

Sendai can breathe out, but not all the way. The train boards keep changing. Convenience stores keep selling rice balls, bottled tea and batteries. The advisory clock has stopped. The work it was pointing to has not.
