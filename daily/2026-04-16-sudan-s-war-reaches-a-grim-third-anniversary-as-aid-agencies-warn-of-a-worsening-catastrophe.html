Sudan’s war reaches a grim third anniversary as aid agencies warn of a worsening catastrophe

As diplomats gather in Berlin, Sudan’s conflict has become the world’s largest humanitarian crisis, with famine risks, mass displacement and a struggle for access that relief workers say is still being met too slowly.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 16, 2026 • 4:57 p.m. • 842 words

Sudan’s war entered its fourth year this week with no clear path to a ceasefire and an increasingly urgent warning from aid agencies: the conflict has become not just a battlefield disaster, but the world’s largest humanitarian crisis. On the third anniversary of fighting that began on April 15, 2023, the United Nations said the country is now suffering from mass displacement, deepening hunger and repeated attacks on civilians, while diplomats and relief officials gathered in Berlin to search for a way to blunt the worsening toll. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/sg/statements/2026-04-15/secretary-generals-video-message-the-third-international-humanitarian-conference-for-sudan?utm_source=openai))

The anniversary has landed with particular force because the humanitarian picture has deteriorated even further since last year’s warnings. The U.N. has described Sudan as the world’s largest humanitarian, protection, displacement and hunger crisis, and relief officials say the war has shattered what was once a country with significant agricultural and institutional capacity. In Berlin, U.N. humanitarian chief Tom Fletcher urged leaders to move beyond expressions of concern and toward concrete steps that would protect civilians and restore access to people cut off by fighting. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-14.html?utm_source=openai))

A conflict that has spread far beyond the front lines

Sudan’s war began when tensions between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces exploded into open fighting in Khartoum and other cities in April 2023. Since then, the conflict has displaced millions, driven entire communities from their homes and left huge areas of the country dependent on aid that often cannot get through. The U.N. and humanitarian groups say the crisis now extends well beyond military lines, touching health systems, education, markets and food supply chains across the country. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/3f88dcd4422c9dea3d0da1b3bbfdbf40?utm_source=openai))

The war’s human cost is visible not only in displacement but also in hunger. U.N. agencies say Sudan has become a test of the international system’s ability to respond to a crisis that is simultaneously political, military and humanitarian. The conflict has created conditions in which families flee repeatedly, local economies collapse and aid deliveries remain irregular or blocked. That combination has made famine prevention one of the most urgent goals for international agencies, even as the broader war shows little sign of easing. ([ungeneva.org](https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/news/2026/04/117610/world-failing-sudan-war-enters-fourth-year-un-relief-chief-warns?utm_source=openai))

Berlin meeting highlights urgency, but not a breakthrough

The Berlin conference was designed to rally diplomatic attention and funding at a moment when Sudan risks sliding further from the world’s focus. The U.N. secretary-general, in a video message to the gathering, called the conflict a tragic milestone and said it has created the world’s largest humanitarian crisis. Aid officials used similarly stark language, warning that the scale of suffering cannot be met with rhetoric alone. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/sg/statements/2026-04-15/secretary-generals-video-message-the-third-international-humanitarian-conference-for-sudan?utm_source=openai))

Still, the meeting underscored a familiar problem: the international community can assemble, issue declarations and pledge support, but it has struggled to shape the behavior of the warring sides. Human Rights Watch said leaders meeting in Berlin should commit to time-bound measures to protect civilians and hold those responsible for serious crimes to account. That plea reflects a broader frustration among aid workers and rights groups that Sudan’s war has become one of the most documented but least resolved atrocities of the era. ([hrw.org](https://www.hrw.org/news/2026/04/14/sudan-world-leaders-need-to-act-on-ongoing-atrocities?utm_source=openai))

Some of the difficulty lies in the war’s political complexity. The two main belligerents have each sought outside support, while regional actors have tried to shape a peace process that remains fragile and incomplete. At the same time, Sudan’s civilian institutions have been badly weakened, limiting the domestic pressure that might otherwise push the sides toward compromise. The result is a conflict with multiple fronts and few obvious levers for immediate de-escalation. ([digitallibrary.un.org](https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/4011515/files/S_2023_355-EN.pdf?utm_source=openai))

What comes next for a country in free fall

Relief officials say the immediate priorities are straightforward even if the politics are not: protect civilians, keep border crossings and aid corridors open, and provide enough resources to prevent hunger from turning into large-scale famine. The U.N. has repeatedly warned that many people are already beyond the reach of normal assistance, and that the war has forced hospitals, schools and local services to close or operate in a degraded state. The concern is that each month without a settlement makes recovery more expensive and less likely. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/highlight/2026-04-14.html?utm_source=openai))

There is also a broader regional risk. As the war continues, it increases pressure on neighboring states hosting refugees and complicates security and logistics across parts of northeast Africa and the Red Sea corridor. That matters not only for Sudanese civilians but for a region already absorbing shocks from other conflicts and economic strains. International agencies are therefore treating Sudan less as a single-country emergency than as a destabilizing crisis with spillover effects. ([unsdg.un.org](https://unsdg.un.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/UN%20Sudan%20Interim%20Cooperation%20Framework%202026-2028.pdf?utm_source=openai))

For now, the anniversary has served less as a turning point than a reminder of unfinished business. Sudan remains locked in a war that has outlasted many early assumptions about speed, containment or negotiation. The latest warnings from the United Nations and rights groups suggest the world is still reacting to the crisis rather than shaping it. Unless the diplomacy in Berlin and beyond produces real access, real protection and sustained pressure for talks, Sudan’s fourth year of war may be even more punishing than the third. ([un.org](https://www.un.org/sg/en/content/sg/statements/2026-04-15/secretary-generals-video-message-the-third-international-humanitarian-conference-for-sudan?utm_source=openai))
