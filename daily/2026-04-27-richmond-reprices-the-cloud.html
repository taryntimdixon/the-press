Richmond Reprices the Cloud

Virginia’s unfinished budget has turned a data-center tax break into a practical question for every place chasing AI: who pays for the power lines, water pipes, land, schools and quiet nights behind modern computing?

By The Press • April 27, 2026 • 11:21 a.m.

In Richmond, the cloud has acquired a price tag big enough to hold up a state budget.

That is not how people usually talk about data centers. The buildings themselves often hide in plain sight: long, window-light industrial boxes with chillers, transformers, security fencing and backup generators, built to keep rows of servers alive every second of the day. Their product feels weightless. A photo opens on a phone. A map route appears. A chatbot answers. A payroll file lands where it should. The work seems to happen somewhere else.

Virginia has become one of the places where somewhere else is very much somewhere. Northern Virginia is the largest data-center market in the United States, according to CBRE’s latest market reporting, and one of the world’s densest clusters of internet infrastructure. In Loudoun County, data centers are not a science-fiction abstraction. They are a budget line. The county’s fiscal 2026 materials say data-center revenue generates 38 percent of its general fund. In neighboring Prince William County, a courthouse fight over the Prince William Digital Gateway has made data centers a land-use, history and neighborhood issue as well as a tax-base issue. In Richmond, they have become the main reason the General Assembly left an April 23 special session without a completed two-year budget.

The immediate fight is narrow on paper. Virginia’s Data Center Retail Sales and Use Tax Exemption lets qualifying data-center operators and their tenants avoid sales and use tax on eligible computer equipment, enabling software and related items. For a household, sales tax is the small extra at the register. For a data center, where servers, chips, networking gear and storage systems are bought in enormous waves and replaced on a fast cycle, it can become a mountain. The Virginia Department of Taxation’s January 2, 2026 report put the value of the exemption at about $1.3 billion in fiscal 2024 and $1.9 billion in fiscal 2025.

The Senate has pushed to end the exemption for data-center computer equipment and software on January 1, 2027, eight years before the current 2035 sunset. The House has favored keeping the incentive, but with new energy and environmental conditions. Gov. Abigail Spanberger called lawmakers back to Richmond for an April 23 special session to finish the 2026-2028 budget. They did not finish it. By April 24, Virginia reporters were describing a budget still stuck on the same question: should the state keep subsidizing the machinery of the cloud, or begin treating that machinery as mature infrastructure that must pay more of its own way?

That question is not only Virginia’s. Ireland, Singapore and Australia have all moved from open-ended recruitment of data centers toward more conditional welcome mats. Their versions differ, but the pattern is familiar. The first generation of policy asked how to attract computing. The current generation asks how to house it without surprising electric customers, draining local water plans, flattening land-use politics or writing tax checks that age badly.

One tax break, many machines

A data center is not just a building with computers. It is a building designed to keep computers alive under relentless conditions. It needs grid power, backup power, cooling, fiber-optic connections, security, fire systems, stormwater controls and enough land to place all that equipment somewhere people can tolerate it. Some facilities rent space and power to many customers. Others are hyperscale sites, meaning very large campuses built for cloud giants or other heavy users. Artificial intelligence, or AI, adds pressure because training and running large models can require dense clusters of specialized chips that draw a great deal of power and produce a great deal of heat.

Virginia’s exemption was created when the commonwealth was competing to become a digital-infrastructure hub. It worked, at least by the simplest measure. The industry came. Fiber routes, reliable power by U.S. standards, proximity to federal agencies, a deep pool of contractors, and the self-reinforcing advantage of being near other data centers helped Northern Virginia pull ahead. CBRE reported that Northern Virginia’s data-center inventory reached roughly 4,039.6 megawatts in 2025, after more than one gigawatt of new capacity was delivered that year. Its colocation vacancy rate was 0.5 percent, a real-estate way of saying that almost nothing was sitting empty.

That success has changed the politics of the incentive. A tax break meant to coax an industry into being now applies to a sector that some of the world’s richest companies consider essential. Cloud providers are racing to secure power, land and equipment years in advance. Industry advocates argue that the exemption is one reason Virginia won the cluster in the first place, and that weakening it could push future growth to Texas, Georgia, Ohio or other states with open land, grid ambitions and aggressive economic-development offices.

The state’s own evidence is not a postcard for either side. The January 2026 tax report is required to discuss costs and benefits, and it points to both. The exemption reduces state revenue that could otherwise flow into schools, transportation, public safety, tax relief or other public uses. But data centers also create local property-tax revenue, construction work, utility investment and some permanent jobs. The Northern Virginia Technology Council, citing a Mangum Economics analysis, said in March 2026 that data centers generated nearly $40 billion in statewide economic output over 2024 and 2025 and supported more than 112,000 jobs when direct, indirect and induced effects are counted. Those are broad economic-impact measures, not the same thing as full-time workers inside the buildings, but they help explain why many local officials are careful about declaring the boom a bad bargain.

The sharper issue is distribution. State taxpayers give up sales-tax revenue. A smaller set of localities collect large property-tax checks. Residents near the buildings get the noise, traffic, land-use change and visual bulk. Utility customers across a wider region may share the costs of generation and transmission, depending on how regulators assign them. The cloud may be global, but the invoices arrive locally and unevenly.

Loudoun’s bargain looks different up close

Loudoun County shows the upside better than almost anywhere. Its fiscal 2026 budget story says data centers generate 38 percent of the county’s general fund revenues. That money helps pay for schools, deputies, parks, libraries, social services and roads while holding down the residential tax pressure that might otherwise land on homeowners. Data centers do not send many children to school. They do not call 911 as often as a subdivision. They bring taxable equipment, and a lot of it.

But even a windfall can become a dependency. Loudoun has created a Revenue Stabilization Fund to manage gaps between budgeted and actual data-center tax revenue. The fiscal 2026 adopted budget included $39.7 million for that fund, and the budget story listed a $119.7 million fund balance. That is a sober admission that server economics can jolt a county budget. Equipment values depreciate. Replacement cycles shift. State law can change. A company can delay a buildout. A county that builds daily services on an unusually rich and unusual tax base has to ask what happens when the timing goes sideways.

Prince William County shows the harder land-use edge. Its long fight over the Prince William Digital Gateway, a proposed corridor of data centers near Manassas National Battlefield Park, turned digital infrastructure into a kitchen-table issue for residents who did not think they were signing up to host such a large piece of the internet. On March 31, 2026, the Court of Appeals of Virginia upheld rulings that voided the project’s rezonings because of problems with public notice and the approval process. On April 14, the county board voted to withdraw from appeals, while developers could still seek review on their own. Supporters saw a lost chance for tax revenue. Opponents saw a reprieve for neighborhoods, rural land, historic landscape and public process.

Those arguments can get cartoonish quickly: jobs versus trees, tax base versus NIMBYs, progress versus nostalgia. The actual trade is messier. A data center can be quieter and cleaner than many industrial uses, and it can produce more local revenue than a warehouse. It can also be a poor neighbor if backup generators, cooling systems, substations, building walls and transmission corridors are placed without care. Virginia’s 2024 Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission study said one-third of data centers were located near residential areas and warned that the industrial scale of the buildings makes them largely incompatible with homes when planning is weak.

That is why the tax fight is really a planning fight wearing a green eyeshade. If Virginia keeps the exemption, lawmakers can still require better siting, water reporting, noise studies, energy planning and cost allocation. If Virginia ends it, developers may still build where power, fiber and customers make sense. The choice is not cloud or no cloud. It is what rules attach to the next round of machines.

The grid is the table underneath

The electric grid has made the debate more urgent. In old economic-development language, a new employer asked for roads, sewer, perhaps a training grant. A large data center asks for electricity on the scale of a town, a county or, in the biggest AI proposals, something larger. A megawatt is one million watts. A gigawatt is one thousand megawatts. Once a state is talking in gigawatts, it is no longer merely discussing buildings. It is discussing power plants, substations, high-voltage lines, fuel risk, interconnection queues and who pays if demand forecasts prove wrong.

PJM Interconnection, the regional grid operator that coordinates wholesale electricity across Virginia and a large part of the eastern United States, has had to build data-center growth into long-term load forecasts. The Dominion zone, which includes much of Virginia’s data-center territory, is one of the places where power planning has become inseparable from computing. PJM’s 2026 forecast materials include a data-center adjustment requested by Dominion Energy and continue to show data centers as a central driver of expected load growth in the region.

Dominion Energy Virginia says large users pay for the electricity they use. Critics respond that the larger question is not just the energy flowing through today’s meter. It is the cost of building a bigger system for tomorrow’s peak demand and reliability needs. The Virginia State Corporation Commission, which regulates utilities, has begun separating large-load customers such as hyperscale data centers into a new rate class and has directed more work on cost allocation. That is regulator language for a very human question: should a household in Norfolk, Richmond or Roanoke pay more because a data campus in Northern Virginia needs new wires?

There is no perfectly simple answer. Utilities build shared systems. New large customers can spread some fixed costs over more electricity sales, which can help rates in some circumstances. But if growth forces expensive new transmission and generation, and if those assets are built for demand that arrives late, arrives differently or does not arrive at all, ordinary customers can be exposed. Data-center developers want speed and certainty. Regulators want evidence. Residents want bills they can understand. The machines want electricity now.

AI makes the timing problem worse because demand is both enormous and uncertain. In April 2026, the International Energy Agency said data-center electricity demand grew 17 percent in 2025 and that power use from AI-focused data centers grew even faster. Its Energy and AI work estimated that data centers accounted for about 415 terawatt-hours of electricity use in 2024, about 1.5 percent of global consumption, and could more than double by 2030. A terawatt-hour is a billion kilowatt-hours; it is the kind of unit that appears when an issue has left the server room and entered the power system.

But uncertainty matters. Not every announced campus is built. Not every power request becomes real load. Chips become more efficient. AI business models may cool. Some workloads can move to places with cheaper power, easier permitting or better climate conditions. Some data centers may become more flexible, meaning they can reduce or shift demand at stressed hours. A good policy has to be tough enough to protect the public and flexible enough not to chase ghosts.

Water is quieter until it is not

Water has not dominated Virginia’s debate as much as electricity, but it belongs in the same systems story. Data centers use water directly when cooling systems evaporate it to remove heat. They also use water indirectly through power plants that need water for cooling or steam cycles. The exact amount varies widely. A facility with air cooling may use little water on site. A dense AI facility using evaporative cooling can use more. Climate, chip density, cooling design, local water sources and drought planning all matter.

JLARC found that data-center water use in Virginia was currently sustainable at the statewide level, while warning that local limits and groundwater-management areas deserve closer attention. That is a useful caution against easy slogans. A data center is not automatically a water villain. It is also not automatically harmless. The question is where it is, how it cools, what else needs the same water, and what happens in dry years.

Australia has put that logic into national expectations. On March 23, 2026, the Australian government released expectations for data centers and AI infrastructure developers, telling operators that new projects should not push up energy prices, should pay their full share of grid connection costs, should support the clean-energy transition, and should use water efficiently and sustainably. A month later, Microsoft announced a A$25 billion commitment in Australia through 2029 and said it would align with those expectations. Whether the details prove strong enough is still an open question, but the sequence is revealing: governments are beginning to write house rules before the next wave of compute arrives.

Singapore offers another model. After years of constraint on new data-center growth, it published a Green Data Centre Roadmap in 2024. The roadmap aims to provide at least 300 megawatts of additional capacity in the near term, with more capacity tied to green energy deployments, efficiency standards and updated building requirements. Ireland, where data centers grew from 5 percent of national electricity demand in 2015 to 22 percent in 2024, has moved toward a connection policy that explicitly weighs grid constraints, security of supply and renewable-energy targets. These are different jurisdictions with different grids. Still, they are all asking the same basic question Richmond is asking: how do you invite digital infrastructure without letting it write the whole infrastructure plan?

The jobs argument needs a sharper ruler

Supporters of the Virginia exemption often point to jobs, and they are right that data centers support work beyond the small crews inside each building. Construction workers pour concrete, install switchgear, run cable and build substations. Electricians, engineers, security staff, cleaners, equipment vendors, fiber contractors and local suppliers all touch the industry. Electrical workers and construction trades have reason to care about whether the pipeline continues.

But data-center jobs should be counted carefully. A hospital, factory, university or call center may employ many people every day on one site. A data center can cost billions and employ dozens or a few hundred once operating, depending on its size and function. Economic-impact studies include indirect and induced jobs, which are real categories but can blur the difference between a permanent technician job, a temporary construction job and a restaurant job supported by local spending. The public bargain looks different depending on which type is being promised.

That does not make the industry unworthy. It means lawmakers should ask a more precise question: what kind of job, for whom, for how long, at what wage, with what training path, and compared with what other use of the same land, power and tax expenditure?

Virginia’s debate has also exposed a rural-development hope. If data centers can move beyond the crowded Northern Virginia core, they might bring revenue to communities that need it. But JLARC noted that distressed areas may have trouble attracting the industry because data centers need power access and large, flat sites. Fiber and latency, the time it takes data to travel, also matter for some uses. The cloud does not float to a locality simply because the locality wants it.

This is where the phrase economic development can become too soft. Development is not just a ribbon-cutting. It is the physical ability to plug in, cool, connect, hire and govern a project. A county with land but no transmission capacity does not have the same asset as a county with land, substations, fiber routes and trained contractors. A tax exemption cannot conjure all of that by itself.

What compromise might look like

The current Virginia stalemate could end many ways. Lawmakers could preserve the exemption largely as it is. They could set a nearer sunset date. They could narrow which equipment qualifies. They could attach energy-efficiency standards, water reporting, clean-power requirements, local-impact rules or better public disclosure. They could design a new fee aimed at very large electricity users. They could split revenue between the state and localities in a way that acknowledges who pays and who hosts. They could also postpone the hard choices, which is its own choice.

A durable compromise would probably avoid two temptations. The first is pretending the data-center boom is pure gravy. It is not. It demands land, power, water planning, regulatory time and public trust. The second is pretending the boom is pure extraction. It is not that either. The same infrastructure that powers AI image generators also powers hospital records, bank payments, weather models, small-business websites, emergency communications and the boring, essential software that keeps daily life from becoming a scavenger hunt.

The better frame is infrastructure citizenship. If data centers are now critical infrastructure, they should behave like it. That means paying predictable taxes or fees, sharing enough information for planners to distinguish real projects from speculative queue-sitting, designing for flexibility where possible, funding grid upgrades that are clearly caused by their demand, reducing water stress, and accepting land-use rules that treat neighbors as people rather than obstacles. In return, states should offer clear timelines, consistent standards and honest accounting instead of surprise politics after companies have already spent millions on sites.

That may sound bland. It is not. Clear rules are the difference between a data center that strengthens a region and one that turns every substation into a town-hall brawl.

The cloud comes home

For years, the internet trained people to think of digital life as a service that appeared without much visible machinery. Virginia’s budget fight is a reminder that the machinery was always there. It just had a good hiding place.

The next phase will be harder to hide. AI tools require chips. Chips require electricity. Electricity requires wires, fuel, turbines, solar panels, batteries, substations, operators and regulators. Cooling requires air or water or both. Buildings require land. Tax incentives require someone to give up revenue. Local revenue requires someone to host the buildings. Every prompt typed into a glowing box rests on unglamorous systems that can be mapped, permitted, taxed, repaired and argued over.

Richmond has not settled the argument. That is frustrating if you need a budget. It is also clarifying. The state that became the capital of the American cloud is now being forced to decide whether its old bargain still fits the new machines.

The answer may not be yes or no. It may be: yes, build the cloud here, but bring a better receipt.
