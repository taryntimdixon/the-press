America’s AI boom is hitting a very physical limit: power, not code

A race to build more AI infrastructure is colliding with the grid, the labor market, and a growing political backlash over who pays for the costs.

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 11:22 a.m.

In Washington, Silicon Valley, and utility boardrooms across the country, the same question is getting louder: not whether artificial intelligence can keep improving, but whether the electricity system can keep up. The answer now appears to be a cautious maybe. Federal energy officials say data centers consumed about 4.4 percent of U.S. electricity in 2023 and could rise to between 6.7 percent and 12 percent by 2028. The Department of Energy has framed that surge as part of a broader industrial expansion, not just a software trend, and it has also said data center electricity use rose from 58 terawatt-hours in 2014 to 176 terawatt-hours in 2023. Critics see a warning sign: AI may be scaling faster than the grid, permitting systems, and public tolerance for the cost. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/node/4847688?utm_source=openai))

This is no longer a niche infrastructure story. It is becoming the defining public-interest question around AI in 2026 because the bottleneck is visible in household electricity bills, local zoning fights, water use disputes, school policies, workforce planning, and federal preemption debates. The White House has urged Congress to take a light-touch approach and preempt many state AI laws, while state governments continue to move ahead with their own rules and communities near proposed data center campuses push back over water, pollution, land use, and grid strain. Those are not separate conversations. They are all the same AI story, now entering its physical phase. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

Why this is the AI story of the moment

For the past two years, AI coverage has often centered on model capability, product launches, and the abstract risks of generated text, hallucinated answers, or future superintelligence. Those issues still matter. But in April 2026, the most consequential pressure point may be something much more ordinary: transformers, substations, gas turbines, permits, cooling systems, and land. The Energy Department’s latest figures show that data centers’ share of total annual U.S. electricity consumption has already more than doubled from 1.9 percent in 2018 to 4.4 percent in 2023, with projections of 6.7 percent to 12 percent by 2028. If the high end of those forecasts proves accurate, data centers could account for roughly one-eighth of U.S. electricity within just a few years. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/geothermal/geothermal-and-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

That scale changes the politics. A technology that once seemed to float above the economy now competes with homes and factories for power. It also changes the economics. When the grid gets congested, developers build private generation, negotiate special utility arrangements, or cluster near cheap and abundant power. That may speed AI deployment for companies that can afford it, but it can also shift costs and risks onto neighbors who never asked for the project. Analysts and policy groups disagree on how much of the burden will land on consumers, but even industry-friendly institutions acknowledge that data-center expansion can create local stress and pricing concerns if utilities and regulators fail to adapt. ([itif.org](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/06/five-concerns-about-ai-data-centers-and-what-to-do-about-them/?utm_source=openai))

The numbers behind the boom

The basic trend line is hard to dispute. DOE says U.S. data center electricity use climbed from 58 TWh in 2014 to 176 TWh in 2023 and could rise to 325 TWh to 580 TWh by 2028. That is not just a bigger internet. That is an energy-system event. DOE has also launched initiatives to help the grid keep pace, including its Speed to Power effort, which it says was launched in September 2025 to accelerate large-scale transmission and generation projects. NIST, meanwhile, said on April 7, 2026, that it released a concept note for an AI Risk Management Framework profile focused on trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure. The government is effectively admitting that AI is now a systems-infrastructure issue, not just a software issue. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/node/4847688?utm_source=openai))

Industry forecasts are similarly striking, though they should be treated cautiously because projections can swing with hardware efficiency, model design, and deployment patterns. Gartner has warned that power shortages could restrict a large share of AI data centers by 2027, while Bloomberg has argued that AI-related electricity demand may climb sharply through 2025 and 2026 even as efficiency improves. Those estimates are not official statistics, but they help explain why utilities, chip companies, cloud vendors, and local governments are suddenly speaking the language of megawatts and queue times. ([bloomberg.com](https://www.bloomberg.com/professional/insights/artificial-intelligence/ai-energy-demand-to-climb-in-2025-26-despite-efficiency-gains/?utm_source=openai))

What makes April 2026 different from the usual cycle of AI hype is that the energy story is now being reinforced from multiple directions. Federal agencies are warning about demand growth. Companies are publicly discussing dedicated power strategies. Independent researchers and policy shops are publishing papers on workload power profiles, grid bottlenecks, and regional stress. And reporting around specific megaprojects suggests that some proposed campuses may already be running into delays. The precise share of projects affected is disputed, but the possibility of delays itself is telling: the physical world is starting to push back. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/femp/articles/technology-changes-energy-efficiency-principles-remain-steadfast-data-center?utm_source=openai))

The political backlash is broadening

The backlash is no longer limited to environmental activists. It now includes lawmakers, labor groups, consumer advocates, and residents who see AI as a driver of rising power costs and a drain on scarce water and land. In March, AP reported that Senators Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez introduced a bill to impose a moratorium on new data centers until national safeguards are in place. Their bill may not pass, but it underscores how the politics of AI have shifted from abstract algorithmic risk to concrete infrastructure conflict. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/65651bd28c3d911d18eeb46cd54f4c75?utm_source=openai))

At the federal level, the White House’s March 20 blueprint took the opposite approach: keep regulation light, protect innovation, and preempt state rules that are considered too burdensome. The document emphasized children, electricity costs, intellectual property, censorship, and education. That mix is revealing. It shows the administration understands that AI politics now spans more than one committee jurisdiction. But the preemption argument is likely to collide with state efforts already underway, especially in places where lawmakers feel Washington has moved too slowly or is too close to the industry. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

California remains the clearest example of a state trying to set its own guardrails. In September 2025, Governor Gavin Newsom signed a law that AP said aims to prevent people from using powerful AI models for potentially catastrophic activities such as building a bioweapon or shutting down a bank system. That state-first model now sits under pressure from the opposite direction: a federal preference for uniformity and lighter rules. Whether AI is governed by a patchwork of state laws or a single national framework is no longer a technical legal debate. It is a contest over who gets to define the social cost of AI. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/9f888a7cbaa57a7dec9e210785b83280?utm_source=openai))

What companies are saying versus what they are doing

The biggest AI companies are trying to position themselves as public-interest actors, not just software vendors. OpenAI’s April 6 “Industrial policy for the Intelligence Age” proposes a slate of policy ideas meant to expand opportunity and build resilient institutions. Anthropic updated its Responsible Scaling Policy effective April 2, 2026. OpenAI also said on April 14 that it was scaling “Trusted Access for Cyber” and fine-tuning a cyber-permissive variant of GPT-5.4 for defensive use. These moves matter because they suggest the industry knows it needs a broader legitimacy story than “trust us, it’s useful.” ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

But public policy language and operational reality are not the same thing. A company can publish safety principles while still accelerating deployment, seeking new government contracts, or pushing for policy environments that favor scale. OpenAI’s agreement with the Department of War says the company will not use its technology for high-stakes automated decisions such as social-credit systems and that it expects the arrangement to support dialogue on privacy and national-security challenges. That is a sign of governance progress, but it is also a reminder that frontier AI is moving deeper into national-security infrastructure. The same companies asking for trusted, guarded deployment are also asking for permission to expand their footprint into more sensitive institutions. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/our-agreement-with-the-department-of-war/?utm_source=openai))

Google, meanwhile, is promoting AI in productivity tools, health, and public-interest support work. On April 7 it posted an update on its mental-health work, including AI-powered simulations to train staff and volunteers for critical conversations. In March it also described AI-enabled health tools and support features. These initiatives show how AI is becoming embedded in everyday institutional workflows, from healthcare and schools to office software and crisis support. They also show why harm analysis can’t focus only on dramatic failures. A model that saves time for one group can also shift labor burdens, data exposure, and dependency for another. ([blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/health/mental-health-updates/?utm_source=openai))

Workplace adoption is rising, but confidence is not

The labor-market story is more complicated than both the boosters and the doomsayers suggest. Gallup’s latest workplace surveys show AI use at work continuing to rise. As of February 2026, Gallup said 13 percent of employees use AI daily and 28 percent use it a few times a week or more. Yet Gallup also found that many workers who have access to AI do not use it, often because they prefer their old way of working, do not see a clear use case, or worry about privacy and ethics. In other words, adoption is not the same as transformation. ([gallup.com](https://www.gallup.com/workplace/704225/rising-adoption-spurs-workforce-changes.aspx?utm_source=openai))

That distinction matters because companies and policymakers often talk as though deployment automatically equals productivity. The evidence is much messier. Gallup’s April 2026 reporting said employees who use AI often report productivity and efficiency gains, but it also found that the technology has not fundamentally changed how most people do their work. An Atlanta Fed analysis published in March 2026 similarly suggested that executives see workflow changes, but not the kind of sweeping productivity jump that the most optimistic AI narratives imply. The broader lesson is that AI is more often reorganizing work than simply replacing it. ([gallup.com](https://www.gallup.com/workplace/704225/rising-adoption-spurs-workforce-changes.aspx?utm_source=openai))

That is also why the labor impact cannot be reduced to headline job-loss estimates. The Bureau of Labor Statistics has said AI effects on occupations are uncertain and likely to vary by field, with exposure especially notable in computer, legal, business, financial, and architecture-and-engineering jobs. The International Monetary Fund has also published work in 2026 on skill gaps and the labor adjustments that AI may bring. AI can compress some tasks, expand others, and alter the balance inside a job long before it eliminates the job itself. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/node/4847688?utm_source=openai))

The education system is now part of the story

Schools and universities are no longer on the sidelines of AI policy. UNESCO has spent the last two years building a global framework around generative AI in education, issuing guidance for policy-makers and competency frameworks for teachers and students. UNESCO’s position is clear: AI can support learning, but institutions need policies, training, and safeguards before scaling adoption. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/digital-education/ai-future-learning/guidance?utm_source=openai))

In the U.S., the education stakes are especially high because the same technology that schools may use for tutoring, planning, and administrative efficiency also complicates assessment, plagiarism enforcement, privacy, and equity. The Education Department issued guidance in July 2025 saying AI may be used across key educational functions if it aligns with applicable federal requirements. Gallup’s April 2026 reporting adds a new layer: the share of K-12 students saying their school has AI rules jumped from 51 percent in 2025 to 74 percent in 2026, which suggests institutions are catching up, but unevenly and often reactively. ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-issues-guidance-artificial-intelligence-use-schools-proposes-additional-supplemental-priority?utm_source=openai))

Even where AI is welcomed, the distribution of benefits is uneven. Teachers may save time on lesson planning or drafting. Students may get more personalized support. But schools also face risks that a private vendor captures the data trail, shapes the default curriculum, or creates dependency on systems that districts cannot afford to audit properly. UNESCO’s recent work repeatedly returns to the same warning: access, capacity, and governance matter as much as the tool itself. That caution should be taken seriously, especially in public education, where the mandate is not speed but learning. ([unesco.org](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-teachers?utm_source=openai))

Public institutions are trying to catch up

One reason the AI debate keeps getting stuck in ideological shorthand is that public institutions are moving at different speeds. NIST is trying to build risk-management profiles for critical infrastructure. DOE is trying to make the grid and energy supply more AI-ready. The EEOC has long warned that algorithmic hiring tools can violate disability and civil-rights law, and its guidance remains a reminder that AI governance is not only about safety from catastrophic misuse; it is also about discrimination in the ordinary mechanics of employment. ([nist.gov](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/nist-information-technology-laboratory-itl-ai-program?utm_source=openai))

That administrative catch-up is happening under political pressure. Some of the loudest calls for AI acceleration now come paired with demands to reduce oversight. But the more AI enters hiring, education, healthcare triage, cybersecurity, and public administration, the harder it becomes to argue that lighter regulation is inherently better regulation. The public sector already knows this from older technologies. Software procurement failures, biased screening systems, and opaque scoring tools have repeatedly shown that scale without accountability can create new kinds of institutional harm. AI simply increases the speed and scale of the problem. ([eeoc.gov](https://www.eeoc.gov/newsroom/us-eeoc-and-us-department-justice-warn-against-disability-discrimination?utm_source=openai))

What the counterarguments get right

The strongest pro-growth argument is not frivolous. AI can increase productivity, lower the cost of some services, assist scientific research, and help public institutions do more with less. OpenAI’s policy materials, Anthropic’s scaling framework, and Google’s product updates all reflect a real belief across the industry that AI is becoming a general-purpose tool. And there is some evidence that organizations using AI are seeing at least partial gains, especially in managerial, technology, and office-based roles. In healthcare and public service settings, workers are already using AI to find information, draft materials, and triage routine work. ([openai.com](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age?utm_source=openai))

The energy pessimists can also overstate their case. Historical tech revolutions often triggered early alarms about resource scarcity that later eased as efficiency improved. Data centers have become more efficient over time, and AI hardware and software are advancing quickly enough that some forecasts may prove too high. Bloomberg’s and Gartner’s projections are useful, but they are not destiny. If models become more efficient, if organizations shift from training to lighter inference workloads, or if utilities expand transmission and generation faster than expected, the worst-case scenarios could soften. That is the case for policy planning, not panic. ([bloomberg.com](https://www.bloomberg.com/professional/insights/artificial-intelligence/ai-energy-demand-to-climb-in-2025-26-despite-efficiency-gains/?utm_source=openai))

Still, the burden of proof is shifting. Optimists now have to show not just that AI can be useful, but that it can be deployed at scale without forcing ratepayers, students, workers, and local communities to absorb hidden costs. Skeptics, in turn, have to show that restraint can be targeted enough to avoid freezing useful innovation. That is the real policy challenge in 2026: not whether to accelerate or stop, but where to draw the line between legitimate public gain and private accumulation of infrastructure costs. ([itif.org](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/06/five-concerns-about-ai-data-centers-and-what-to-do-about-them/?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

The next few months will likely decide whether AI’s infrastructure problem remains manageable or becomes a nationwide political fault line. Watch for three things. First, whether more states try to regulate model behavior, data center siting, or AI disclosure even as the White House pushes for preemption. Second, whether utilities begin imposing longer wait times, higher interconnection costs, or special tariffs on large AI campuses. Third, whether workplace and education adoption continues to rise even as workers and students express more skepticism about trust, privacy, and reliability. Those three trends together will tell us more about AI’s real-world trajectory than any product launch. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

If the boom continues, the winning institutions will be the ones that understand AI not as a single tool but as a stack: model, software, data, labor, electricity, law, and trust. If it stumbles, it will probably not be because the models stopped improving. It will be because the surrounding system could not adapt quickly enough. That is why the most important AI beat story this week is not a chatbot feature or a benchmark score. It is the increasingly physical politics of building intelligence at scale. The machines may be digital. The constraints are decidedly not. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/node/4847688?utm_source=openai))
