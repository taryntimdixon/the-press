New CRISPR design shrinks gene-editing tools enough to fit standard delivery vehicles

An NIH-funded team says it has engineered a smaller, more efficient CRISPR system that could help move gene editing from cells in a dish into treatments delivered directly inside the body.

By The Press • April 16, 2026 • 2:47 p.m.

A federal research team has reported a CRISPR advance that could help solve one of gene editing’s biggest practical problems: getting the molecular machinery into the right cells inside the body. In a news release Monday, the National Institutes of Health said an NIH-funded group identified a naturally occurring enzyme, Al3Cas12f, that is small enough to fit into adeno-associated virus vectors, then engineered it into a more powerful version for editing human cells. The result, if it holds up in further studies, could broaden the range of diseases for which in vivo gene editing is feasible, including cancers and neurological disorders such as ALS.

The central challenge is simple to state and hard to solve. Many of the most widely used CRISPR systems rely on proteins that are too large to package into standard delivery vehicles. That has confined much of today’s gene-editing medicine to ex vivo approaches, in which cells are removed from a patient, edited in the lab and returned. The new work aims at the harder prize: delivering a compact editor directly to tissues in the body and making it work well enough to matter clinically. NIH said the enhanced enzyme reached 90% efficiency in a commonly edited region of the genome, while the engineered Al3Cas12f RKK variant boosted editing from less than 10% to more than 80% across tested targets. Those are early laboratory numbers, but they mark a substantial improvement for a tool designed around size constraints as much as precision. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

Why size is the bottleneck

Gene editing has moved fast enough that the field now has a clear hierarchy of problems. The chemistry is increasingly powerful; delivery remains the choke point. AAV vectors are among the most common targeted delivery systems in gene therapy, but they can only carry a limited payload. Larger CRISPR enzymes, and the RNA guides and repair templates that often travel with them, have made in vivo editing difficult to scale. A compact editor can fit into those vectors more readily, which matters because the vector is often what determines whether a treatment can reach the liver, the nervous system or another organ without invasive procedures. NIH’s release frames Al3Cas12f as a way to move past that size ceiling rather than merely around it. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

That makes the finding more than a technical curiosity. If editors can be delivered directly to specific tissues, doctors could potentially treat diseases in places that are hard to access by taking cells out of the body first. That includes many solid tumors, along with inherited disorders in organs where cell harvesting is difficult or dangerous. The NIH release says the advance could expand treatment options for cancer, ALS and other diseases, but it is important to note that “could” still carries a lot of scientific work. The new system was reported in human cells, not in patients, and the path from cell-culture success to safe human therapy is long. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

What the researchers changed

According to NIH, researchers at the University of Texas at Austin used imaging and machine learning tools to study the enzyme’s structure. They found that the naturally occurring protein forms a more stable and tightly connected complex than comparable enzymes of similar size. That stability appears to matter because smaller CRISPR systems often trade compactness for weak performance. The team then engineered the Al3Cas12f RKK variant, which improved performance dramatically in human cells. In practical terms, the work suggests that a miniaturized CRISPR machine does not have to be a fragile one. It can be compact and more active than its unmodified counterpart. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

That kind of gain is exactly what gene-editing developers have been chasing. Over the past few years, scientists have built a growing toolkit of CRISPR-related enzymes, base editors and prime editors, each with different strengths. Yet delivery has remained the limiting factor, especially for therapies that need to reach the body as a whole. A smaller editor can simplify the logistics, reduce the amount of genetic cargo that has to be packaged, and potentially improve the odds that a therapy reaches enough cells to have a meaningful effect. None of that guarantees success, but it does make a direct-in-body strategy more realistic. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

What it could mean for patients

The most immediate implication is not a new treatment, but a broader therapeutic design space. Ex vivo gene therapies have already produced important breakthroughs, including the first approved CRISPR-based treatment for sickle cell disease. But those therapies are expensive, labor-intensive and available only for certain diseases and patients. A compact editor that can be delivered in vivo could eventually reduce some of those barriers by eliminating the need to extract and re-infuse cells. It could also open doors for organs and diseases where cell extraction is not practical at all. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

Still, several hurdles remain. Scientists will need to confirm that the editing system stays accurate when moved from cell lines into animals and, later, humans. They will need to test whether the editor cuts only where intended, whether the immune system reacts to it, how long it persists, and how efficiently it reaches the right tissue. They will also need to show that the delivery vehicle can carry the editor safely and consistently at therapeutic doses. Those are not minor details; they are the difference between a promising molecular tool and a real medicine. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))

Even so, the timing of the report is notable. Gene editing is entering a phase in which the question is less whether the technology works in principle and more where it can be made practical. Smaller, more stable editors are one answer. Better delivery vehicles are another. This week’s NIH-backed result suggests the two problems may be converging: the enzyme is finally getting small enough for standard carriers, and the carrier may finally be good enough to take advantage of it. If that convergence continues, the next generation of CRISPR medicine may look less like a laboratory trick and more like an ordinary clinical tool. ([nih.gov](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-funded-breakthrough-shrinks-crispr-precision-delivery-body?utm_source=openai))
