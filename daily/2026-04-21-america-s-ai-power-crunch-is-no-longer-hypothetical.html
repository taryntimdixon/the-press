America’s AI power crunch is no longer hypothetical

From Virginia substations to chip fabs in Arizona and Taiwan, the bottleneck in AI has shifted from model quality to electricity, transformers, and permitting.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 21, 2026 • 4:19 p.m. • 2943 words

The most consequential AI story of spring 2026 is not a new chatbot, a benchmark race, or another splashy product launch. It is the infrastructure problem that now sits underneath the entire boom: the United States is struggling to build enough power, transmission, cooling, and hardware supply to support the pace of AI investment. The constraint is visible in grid forecasts, in utility filings, in chipmaker revenue guidance, in local permitting fights, and in the growing number of projects that have been delayed, redesigned, or quietly scaled back. What looked like a software revolution is increasingly behaving like a classic industrial buildout, with the familiar frictions of concrete, copper, steel, transformers, substations, gas turbines, and long lead times.

That shift matters because it changes the question from whether AI is useful to whether the physical economy can absorb it. The U.S. Energy Information Administration now expects the strongest four-year run of electricity demand growth since 2000, and says large computing facilities, including data centers, are a major driver. In its January 2026 forecast, EIA said U.S. electricity use would rise 1% in 2026 and 3% in 2027. In its April 2026 outlook, it said total electricity demand would increase 2.3% in the summer months of 2026 compared with 2025 and 3.7% in 2027. The Department of Energy, citing Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, said data center electricity use rose from 58 TWh in 2014 to 176 TWh in 2023 and could reach 325 to 580 TWh by 2028. The International Energy Agency has reached a similar conclusion from a global angle, estimating that U.S. data centers consumed around 180 TWh in 2024 and that demand will keep rising through the end of the decade. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

The headline is not simply that AI uses power. The more important point is that the industry is now running into a mismatch between the speed of digital demand and the speed of physical supply. Hyperscalers can sign a lease or announce a campus in months. Utilities can take years to add transmission, interconnection capacity, or a new substation. Even when power is available in theory, key components such as transformers, switchgear, turbines, and high-voltage gear can be on long backorder. This is why so much recent corporate strategy has centered on owning energy rather than merely buying it: companies are pursuing on-site generation, long-term nuclear and geothermal deals, gas-backed power, and utility partnerships that would have looked extreme a few years ago. Meta’s January 2026 agreements around nuclear power, for instance, were presented explicitly as a way to support its AI data center buildout in Ohio. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0eb051a9a11d96f7ce200e186ad13476?utm_source=openai))

What changed: AI stopped being a software-only business

For most of the last decade, cloud growth was impressive but manageable. More servers meant more data centers, but the system could expand within a familiar envelope. AI changed the shape of the problem. Training frontier models and serving them at scale requires dense racks, liquid cooling, far higher power per square foot, and a steadier supply of expensive accelerators and memory. The latest hardware stack is not just a faster version of old cloud infrastructure. It is a different electrical and thermal problem. That is why some new AI halls are being designed around direct liquid cooling, not legacy air systems, and why procurement now spans chips, memory, network gear, chillers, backup generation, and utility-grade electrical equipment. Industry filings from operators such as IREN describe direct-to-chip liquid cooling systems and large, staged buildouts tied to specific power deliveries. ([sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1878848/000114036125040072/ef20058139_ex99-1.htm?utm_source=openai))

Chipmakers themselves are signaling the same thing. ASML said in its 2025 results that AI-related demand continued to surge, while also warning that China sales would decline significantly in 2026 after an unusually strong period in 2024 and 2025. NVIDIA’s annual review and later filings continue to highlight the risk that export controls and related restrictions could limit sales and complicate China access, while the company also noted that H20 products were designed specifically for the Chinese market under U.S. license requirements. SK hynix, meanwhile, reported record annual results in 2025 and said HBM revenue more than doubled year over year, underscoring how AI systems depend on a constrained stack that goes far beyond the GPU. In other words, the AI buildout is not only about who owns the models. It is about who can secure the constrained industrial inputs beneath them. ([asml.com](https://www.asml.com/news/press-releases/2025/q3-2025-financial-results?utm_source=openai))

This is also why policy is now inseparable from infrastructure. In March 2026, the White House released a ratepayer-protection proclamation saying large-scale data center infrastructure and AI must be built at home and that companies should bring or buy the new generation resources and pay for the power-delivery upgrades needed to serve their facilities. In January 2026, the White House also adjusted imports of semiconductors and manufacturing equipment, while carving out exceptions for certain uses in U.S. data centers and other domestic applications. The policy message is clear even when the details are not: Washington wants more compute, but it is increasingly worried about the cost, location, and supply chain of that compute. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge-proclamation/?utm_source=openai))

The power bottleneck is now the story

Utility planners have been warning for years that load growth was coming; the surprise is how quickly it arrived. EIA’s January 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook pointed to sharp commercial-sector electricity growth in 2026 and 2027, largely because of large computing facilities. DOE’s 2024 analysis found data centers consumed 176 TWh in 2023 and could rise to 325 to 580 TWh by 2028. EIA’s April 2026 outlook again pointed to stronger demand growth, and DOE materials now say data centers already account for 4.4% of U.S. electricity use and could reach 6.7% to 12% by 2028. EPRI has also argued that data centers could claim a much larger share of U.S. electricity by 2030 in high-growth scenarios. Those are not modest variances. They imply very different futures for power prices, grid reliability, and the politics of who pays for the upgrades. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

That pressure is already visible in local and regional disputes. PJM, the mid-Atlantic grid operator, has warned that shortages could arrive as early as 2026 if demand grows faster than supply, which is one reason regulators approved faster-track measures to keep capacity online. In February 2026, the federal government approved a record $26.5 billion loan for power expansion in Georgia and Alabama, explicitly linking the work to data center demand. In March 2026, the Department of Energy announced a public-private partnership at the former Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant in Ohio that includes a 10-gigawatt data center and up to 10 gigawatts of new generation, including natural gas capacity and transmission upgrades. These are not isolated one-offs; they are clues that the country is moving toward a new industrial policy for electricity, whether it has named it that or not. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08ed707897fa5274663649bb2c372849?utm_source=openai))

At the same time, the assumptions behind the boom are being tested. Reporting in April 2026 has described major AI data center projects as facing delays, with some plans reshuffled or scaled back as companies confronted utility bottlenecks, permitting, and changes in demand forecasts. The details vary by project and some sites are still early in construction, so a delay is not always a cancellation. But even the existence of such reports is informative: in a market once defined by speed, the bottleneck has become so severe that delays are now part of the strategic conversation rather than a private embarrassment. ([tomshardware.com](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/analytics-group-signals-possible-delays-at-40-percent-of-ai-data-center-construction-sites-companies-deny-schedule-holdups-but-satellite-imagery-indicates-otherwise?utm_source=openai))

Why transformers and substations suddenly matter to everyone

One reason the grid story has moved to the center is that the crucial equipment is boring, expensive, and slow to make. High-voltage transformers and switchgear do not scale like software servers. They require specialized manufacturing, copper, steel, and long qualification cycles. They are also exposed to trade policy and supply chain disruption. That creates a choke point that is easy to overlook until the entire industry is competing for the same physical parts. Utility planners, developers, and analysts have been warning that interconnection queues and equipment lead times can stretch multi-year. The result is a strange inversion: AI companies have abundant capital, but capital alone cannot instantly fabricate the electrical hardware needed to use it. DOE’s own transmission guidance now treats smart grid tools and faster planning as necessary to keep up with the new load profile. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/wind/articles/smart-transmission-tools-modernize-americas-power-grid?utm_source=openai))

This is where the practical economics of AI collide with the geography of power. Virginia’s Northern Data Center Alley, the Columbus and New Albany corridor in Ohio, parts of Georgia, and growing regions in Texas all became magnets because they offered a combination of land, fiber, tax treatment, and utility access. But once large campuses cluster in one place, they can overwhelm available capacity and push costs onto residential and industrial customers unless regulators intervene. That is why some of the strongest objections now come not from technologists but from consumer advocates, environmental groups, and ratepayer watchdogs who argue that ordinary customers should not subsidize a speculative buildout whose benefits are concentrated in a handful of corporations. The White House’s own March pledge says the companies should bear the cost of new generation and delivery upgrades, a sign that cost allocation has become a political issue, not just a technical one. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge-proclamation/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a regional politics problem. Data centers are often sold as jobs and tax base, but the construction labor is temporary, the operations workforce is relatively small, and the grid upgrades may be public even when the servers are private. Some local governments welcome the investment because they want tax revenue and prestige. Others are increasingly wary after seeing land prices rise, substations jam, or water and power infrastructure come under strain. In the most contested areas, developers are not just negotiating with one town hall; they are navigating utilities, state regulators, environmental permitting, school districts, and residents who see their monthly bill as part of the cost of the AI economy. DOE’s 2024 recommendations on powering AI and data center infrastructure make clear that permitting and interconnection are now central to the national conversation. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

The hardware stack: chips, memory, packaging, and export controls

Even if electricity were free, the AI boom would still be constrained by advanced chips and the supporting supply chain. NVIDIA’s filings continue to show how sensitive that chain remains to export restrictions, while ASML’s results underline how much demand is tied to advanced lithography and how geography continues to matter in the semiconductor business. SK hynix’s record year, fueled by HBM demand, shows the memory side of the market is just as critical as the GPU side. The AI industry likes to speak in terms of model weights and token throughput, but the physical bottlenecks are increasingly in memory bandwidth, packaging capacity, and tool access. ASML said AI-related demand remained strong, and NVIDIA has described the H20 as a China-specific product constrained by U.S. rules. ([asml.com](https://www.asml.com/news/press-releases/2025/q3-2025-financial-results?utm_source=openai))

Export policy remains a defining variable. The White House’s January semiconductor proclamation directly linked national-security concerns to semiconductor imports and equipment. In April 2026, lawmakers revised proposed restrictions on Chinese chipmakers after industry pushback, narrowing the bill and removing one of the broader provisions affecting chipmaking tools. That interplay of tightening and narrowing matters because companies are trying to plan billion-dollar fabs and equipment purchases in a policy environment where access can change faster than a factory can be built. Even when a proposal is softened, the signal to industry is that global semiconductor trade remains politically fragile. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/01/adjusting-imports-of-semiconductors-semiconductor-manufacturing-equipment-and-their-derivative-products-into-the-united-states/?query-11-page=96&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block&utm_source=openai))

China’s response has been equally consequential. In late 2025, reports said Beijing instructed state-funded data centers to remove foreign AI chips and replace them with domestic alternatives, reinforcing a years-long push for self-sufficiency. That policy not only affects U.S. chip vendors; it also pushes Chinese firms to accelerate local accelerator design, memory substitution, and domestic tool development. The result is a more fragmented global stack, with separate technology ecosystems increasingly optimized for geopolitical loyalty as much as performance. If the last era of semiconductors was defined by globalization, this one is being shaped by selective decoupling and strategic redundancy. ([iea.org](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/executive-summary%C2%A0?utm_source=openai))

Data centers are getting their own energy policy

Companies are not waiting for grid reform. They are building around it. Meta’s nuclear deals, IREN’s Microsoft contract, and the growing number of projects pursuing on-site generation or private power are all expressions of the same premise: if the grid is too slow, bring the grid to the site or build a parallel one. The White House’s March ratepayer pledge explicitly pushes that logic. DOE’s geothermal office says geothermal can support data center growth, and DOE’s clean-energy guidance argues that data centers need a mix of firm, flexible resources. EPRI’s DCFlex effort says data centers could become grid resources if designed and operated with more flexibility. The common thread is that the boundary between digital infrastructure and energy infrastructure is disappearing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0eb051a9a11d96f7ce200e186ad13476?utm_source=openai))

That idea is attractive in theory, but the timing is brutal. Nuclear projects are slow. New transmission is slow. Geothermal is promising, but scale and location remain limiting factors. Gas plants can be built faster, but they raise emissions, fuel-price exposure, and community resistance. Battery storage helps with short-duration balancing but not always with the sustained high-load demand of an AI campus. That leaves developers selecting from imperfect options, each with tradeoffs among speed, cost, carbon, and reliability. The result is a scramble for the least-worst combination rather than a clean technological answer. Federal guidance now treats on-site generation and clean firm power as important options, but none of them erase the queue. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/geothermal/geothermal-and-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

This is why the political debate is becoming more openly about who bears the burden. Supporters of rapid AI buildout argue that permitting delays and local resistance risk surrendering an economically transformative industry to other countries. Critics counter that the costs are socialized while the profits are privatized. Some researchers and policy groups argue that flexible data centers, better rate design, and smarter interconnection planning can reduce the strain. Others say the problem is so concentrated and so large that only major structural changes, including location controls or moratoriums in the most stressed areas, will prevent a shock to the power system. That divergence is not just ideological. It reflects real uncertainty about whether efficiency gains can keep up with demand growth. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/eere/wind/articles/smart-transmission-tools-modernize-americas-power-grid?utm_source=openai))

The counterargument: this may be a short-lived spike, not a permanent regime change

There is a serious case against overreacting. First, the data center numbers are forecasts, not destiny. Demand ranges are wide because analysts are trying to model a technology stack that changes quickly. Second, AI workloads may become more efficient. Better chips, better compilers, more efficient models, and more adaptive inference could reduce power per query. Third, some of the investment wave may be speculative. Not every announced campus will be completed on time, and not every megaproject will deliver the utilization its backers implied at announcement. Fourth, the grid has repeatedly absorbed seemingly impossible loads before. The internet, mobile broadband, and cloud all stressed infrastructure and then normalized after a cycle of buildout. ([energy.gov](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers?utm_source=openai))

That argument is stronger than the breathless versions of the AI apocalypse narrative. It is possible that the current crunch is partly a function of a few years of exceptionally aggressive spending, and that the system will catch up by the late 2020s. It is also possible that some demand growth is being pulled forward by companies racing each other for strategic position, which would make the near-term squeeze harsher than the long-run equilibrium. The most honest reading of the evidence is not that the grid will fail or that AI will stall. It is that the next few years are likely to be defined by friction, not smooth expansion. The buildout will continue, but with more delays, more on-site power, more local resistance, and more scrutiny over who pays. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))

What to watch next

Several developments will determine whether the AI power crunch becomes a lasting constraint or merely a hard but temporary bottleneck. The first is utility interconnection: how fast can developers get real capacity, not just theoretical approval? The second is transformer and switchgear supply: can domestic and allied manufacturers expand fast enough to reduce multi-year lead times? The third is policy: will federal efforts to streamline permitting actually accelerate transmission and generation, or will they mostly lower the friction for individual corporate projects? The fourth is corporate strategy: will more companies follow Meta’s lead and seek dedicated power arrangements, or will they continue to depend on the grid and accept the delays? ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/03.20.26-National-Policy-Framework-for-Artificial-Intelligence-Legislative-Recommendations.pdf?stream=top&utm_source=openai))

The fifth is geopolitics. If Washington tightens chip export controls again, if China continues to push foreign chips out of strategic infrastructure, or if key equipment suppliers face new restrictions, the economics of AI deployment could change quickly. The sixth is public tolerance. The AI industry can absorb some criticism about energy use while revenues are rising and consumers still feel the benefits. It will have a harder time if electricity bills rise, projects miss deadlines, or local governments conclude that data centers are straining public infrastructure without delivering enough return. In that sense, the infrastructure story is also a legitimacy story. The boom will survive technical bottlenecks more easily than political backlash. ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/01/adjusting-imports-of-semiconductors-semiconductor-manufacturing-equipment-and-their-derivative-products-into-the-united-states/?query-11-page=96&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block&utm_source=openai))

For now, the biggest takeaway is simple: the AI race is now an electricity race. The country that can connect power, build transmission, secure chips, and move hardware fastest will have an edge not just in models, but in the whole digital economy built around them. That makes this a technology story, an energy story, a manufacturing story, and a policy story at once. It is also a reminder that the internet’s latest transformation is being built the old-fashioned way, with permits, substations, and a very expensive queue. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press582.php?utm_source=openai))
