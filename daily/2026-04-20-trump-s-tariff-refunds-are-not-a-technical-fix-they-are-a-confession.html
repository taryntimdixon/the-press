Trump’s tariff refunds are not a technical fix. They are a confession.

The Supreme Court’s tariff ruling should end the fantasy that America can be made cheaper, stronger and more sovereign by executive whim. Congress should reclaim trade power—and stop treating import taxes as a shortcut around actual industrial policy.

Written and Researched by AI • April 20, 2026 • 10:07 a.m.

For months, tariff defenders sold Americans a simple story: if import taxes hurt a little, they were worth it. Tariffs would protect factories, punish rivals, raise revenue, strengthen bargaining power, and somehow leave consumer prices untouched. The Supreme Court’s Feb. 20, 2026 ruling upending most of President Donald Trump’s sweeping tariff program exposed that story as political theater wearing an economic spreadsheet as a costume. By April 20, 2026, the refund machinery is already starting to spin. Businesses that paid the now-invalidated duties can begin filing claims through Customs and Border Protection’s refund system. That is more than a bureaucratic detail. It is the moment the country must decide whether tariffs are a serious instrument of statecraft or a recurring stunt that shifts costs onto importers, consumers and courts until judges say stop. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08861f153801156d213c30c4e2f6a683?utm_source=openai))

My view is blunt: Congress should take tariff power back from the presidency, limit emergency tariff authorities to genuine emergencies, and replace ad hoc import taxes with industrial policy that is explicit, appropriated, and accountable. Tariffs can be justified in narrow cases. But the 2025-26 experience shows that broad, president-driven tariff campaigns are a bad substitute for strategy. They are too blunt to rebuild domestic capacity quickly, too volatile to guide investment, too regressive to pass a fairness test, and too legally fragile to serve as the backbone of a durable trade policy. The refunds now beginning are not merely repayment. They are an admission that the system was pushed beyond its legal and economic limits. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

The case for tariffs is not silly. It is just overstated.

The strongest defense of the Trump tariff program is not that tariffs are painless. It is that pain can be productive. Advocates argue that the United States spent decades letting strategic industries erode, becoming dependent on foreign supply chains for everything from steel to pharmaceuticals to advanced manufacturing inputs. In that view, tariffs are leverage: a tax that makes domestic production relatively more attractive, gives negotiators something to trade away, and reassures voters that globalization will not continue as a one-way transfer of jobs and resilience overseas. Some economists and policymakers also note that the broad tariff shock of 2025 and early 2026 did not produce an immediate collapse. Growth slowed, but the economy did not crater. Trade flows adjusted. Some firms relocated sourcing. Some trading partners negotiated. And the inflation spike many feared was delayed or diffused by inventory management, exchange rates and margin compression. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

That is the best version of the pro-tariff case, and it deserves to be taken seriously. Tariffs do sometimes buy policy space. They can be used to confront dumping, subsidized overcapacity, coercive trade practices and outright dependency. The U.S. International Trade Commission exists precisely because trade policy should be grounded in analysis rather than instinct. Commerce’s Section 232 authorities, anti-dumping and countervailing duty laws, and targeted remedies under the Trade Act are all acknowledgments that a laissez-faire trade regime is not the only legitimate approach. In a world of geopolitical rivalry and supply-chain fragility, there is nothing unserious about wanting stronger domestic production in sectors with obvious strategic importance. ([cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/24_1203_revenue-division-helpdesk-directory.pdf?utm_source=openai))

But the pro-tariff case collapses when tariffs are treated not as one tool among many, but as the tool. That is where the Trump approach failed in law, in economics and in political hygiene. Broad tariffs were not targeted enough to distinguish between strategic goods and ordinary household items. They were not stable enough to let businesses make multiyear investment plans. They were not transparent enough to let Congress debate tradeoffs up front. And they were not lawful enough to survive judicial review when the administration relied on emergency powers that Congress never clearly intended to authorize as a standing import-tax superpower. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

The legal defeat matters because it reveals the policy’s core weakness.

The Supreme Court’s Feb. 20 ruling striking down the sweeping IEEPA tariffs was not merely an ideological rebuke. It was a warning about delegation. The Court found the president’s attempt to use emergency authority for broad tariffs on nearly all countries exceeded the statute’s bounds. That matters because the administration’s theory was not subtle: it claimed that persistent trade deficits and a host of economic grievances amounted to an emergency sufficient to justify a sweeping import-tax regime. Once that logic is accepted, the executive branch can effectively levy taxes on the public by declaring a crisis and calling it national security, foreign policy or economic necessity. The Court refused to bless that move, and lower courts then moved quickly on refund-related disputes. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

There is a broader constitutional point here. Tariffs are taxes. They may be collected at the border, but the economic burden is borne inside the United States. That is why the separation-of-powers fight is not a lawyer’s quarrel about statutory text. It is a fight over whether presidents can impose giant consumption taxes in the name of emergency without the ordinary discipline of legislation. The Congressional Research Service materials on tariff authority underscore how fragmented and specific the legal bases are: Section 232, Section 301, anti-dumping and countervailing duty law, and Section 122 each carry different predicates, limits and institutional checks. The very sprawl of the statute book is evidence that broad tariff power was meant to be circumscribed, not free-floating. ([pwc.com](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tax-services/publications/insights/assets/pwc-us-supreme-court-invalidates-ieepa-tariffs.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That is why the refund process is so politically devastating to tariff maximalism. If the tariffs were such an elegant lever, why did they need an emergency shortcut? If they were a durable way to reshape the industrial base, why did the administration have to search for a different legal theory after losing in court? If they were obviously legitimate, why do businesses now need to file refund claims to recover import taxes that were collected under a regime the Court said could not stand? The answer is not that all tariffs are illegitimate. It is that a president cannot convert the customs code into a blank check just because the economics of industry rebuilding are hard. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08861f153801156d213c30c4e2f6a683?utm_source=openai))

The economic record is not a vindication.

It is tempting to read the latest numbers and declare the tariff war harmless. That would be a mistake. The February 2026 trade data showed a $57.3 billion monthly goods-and-services deficit, wider than January’s revised $54.7 billion, even as year-to-date the deficit was still lower than the same stretch in 2025. That mixed picture tells a more complicated story than tariff boosters like. Imports and exports both move around for many reasons, including front-loading, inventory rebuilding, exchange-rate shifts and growth differentials. A narrower trade gap in one period does not prove tariffs work, just as a wider one does not prove they never can. But the most important point is that the deficit did not behave like a lever the White House could pull by force of will. Trade balances are macroeconomic outcomes, not a scoreboard a president can rewrite by decree. ([bea.gov](https://www.bea.gov/index.php/news/2026/us-international-trade-goods-and-services-february-2026?utm_source=openai))

Nor do the inflation data support triumphalism. The Consumer Price Index rose 2.4 percent over the year ended February 2026, and the Bureau of Labor Statistics later reported that CPI was up 3.3 percent over the year ended March 2026. Producer prices rose 4.0 percent over the year ended March 2026, after a 3.4 percent increase over the year ended February 2026. The Federal Reserve has repeatedly warned that tariffs have boosted inflation, even if officials differ on whether the effect is one-time or persistent. Chair Jerome Powell said in April 2025 that the level of tariff increases announced then was significantly larger than anticipated and that inflation expectations had moved up, with surveys pointing to tariffs. By early 2026, Fed speakers still described tariff effects as a live complication for inflation and policy. That does not prove tariffs caused all price pressures. It does show that tariff economics are not confined to patriotic rhetoric at the border. They flow through prices, margins, wages and expectations. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/consumer-prices-up-2-4-percent-over-year-ended-february-2026.htm?utm_source=openai))

Yale’s Budget Lab estimates the effective tariff regime in early April 2026 would, if maintained, raise prices meaningfully and impose sizable household costs. Its April 8 report says that if Section 122 tariffs expire as scheduled, the ultimate price level impact would be between 0.5 percent and 0.7 percent, representing a loss of about $760 to $940 for the average household; if made permanent, the price impact would be higher. These are model-based estimates, not tablets from the mountaintop, and they depend on assumptions about pass-through, substitution and retaliation. But they are a useful antidote to the magical thinking that tariffs are somehow paid by foreign governments. They are not. They are paid by importers first and then, in large part, by Americans through higher prices, lower wages or lower returns. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

The Congressional Budget Office’s earlier analyses of tariff shocks and broader trade-policy choices likewise point to tradeoffs rather than miracles: lower deficits can come with lower output, weaker purchasing power and higher inflation, depending on design and pass-through. That is the real tariff bargain. The administration can collect revenue and claim toughness, but households pay in prices, and the economy absorbs a drag in efficiency. The question is not whether tariffs can raise money. Of course they can. The question is whether a country trying to reshore critical capacity, control inflation, and preserve its alliance system wants to do so through a tax that is opaque, unstable and regressive. On that question, the evidence is increasingly hostile to the tariff romanticism that dominated 2025. ([budgetlab.yale.edu](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-8-2026?utm_source=openai))

If tariffs are a tool, why did they require a legal myth?

One of the most revealing features of the last year has been the administration’s insistence on using emergency law to do what Congress did not clearly authorize. The Reuters and AP coverage of the tariff fight, together with the CRS explanations of tariff statutes, shows how this unfolded: broad tariffs were imposed, courts pushed back, the Supreme Court ruled against the central legal theory, and the administration then sought new paths. That sequence matters because it suggests the tariff strategy was always partly about bypassing ordinary politics. A president who cannot assemble a legislative coalition for his preferred tariff structure should not be able to conjure one through creative statutory interpretation. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

The refund fight also exposes the practical cost of using tariffs as theater. Customs and Border Protection says refunds can be received electronically through Automated Clearinghouse, and it has guidance for importers using the modernized ACE portal to provide bank information for electronic refunds. That sounds tidy until one remembers the scale. AP reported the government had collected more than $130 billion from the tariffs by mid-December, with potential refunds that could ultimately approach $175 billion. More than 1,000 companies have already filed suit to preserve reimbursement rights, and firms like FedEx have said they will try to pass any refunds back to shippers and consumers. This is not a clean policy rollback. It is a multibillion-dollar administrative archaeology project. That is what happens when import taxes are used as a policy mood ring instead of a durable law. ([cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/automated/ach/refund?utm_source=openai))

And yet there is a serious counterargument: maybe the chaos is the point. Tariff maximalists say the uncertainty itself forces change. Firms that had long ignored American manufacturing capacity now have to consider it. Foreign governments, shaken by the prospect of escalating barriers, may cut deals. Investors, seeing higher import costs, may find domestic production relatively more attractive. In that reading, the volatility is a feature, not a bug. The administration’s Commerce Department has even highlighted enormous investment commitments and sectoral waivers in its self-assessment of the first year of Lutnick’s tenure. The claim is that tariffs are not merely taxes; they are bargaining chips that can buy reshoring and leverage investment. ([commerce.gov](https://www.commerce.gov/news/press-releases/2026/01/first-year-accomplishments-under-secretary-howard-w-lutnick?utm_source=openai))

There is a kernel of truth there. Bargaining leverage can matter. Some sectors can be nudged. Supply chains can be redirected. Firms do respond to relative prices. But leverage without predictability is not industrial strategy; it is a casino. A company deciding whether to build a plant, retrain workers or localize a supplier base needs credible policy horizons measured in years, not in executive mood swings or litigation cycles. If the president can slap on a tariff, lose in court, issue a different tariff under a different statute, and then promise another change once negotiations progress, the resulting uncertainty makes capital expensive. That is especially damaging for midsized firms that cannot hedge every move with armies of lawyers and customs brokers. The economy’s biggest players may survive the volatility. The smaller ones simply pay. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cc2ace8576e59d10034e7e525737539d?utm_source=openai))

The opponents of tariffs are not defending the old free-trade catechism.

The best objection to my argument is that critics sometimes sound as though the answer to bad globalization is to do nothing. That is not persuasive, and it is not where the evidence points. The United States does need industrial capacity in semiconductors, medicines, grid equipment, shipbuilding, defense-related materials and other strategic sectors. It does need resilience against coercion and chokepoints. It does need trade rules that are prepared for state capitalism, not only consumer welfare. And it does need to reckon with the political fact that many Americans see trade deficits and offshoring as symbols of national decline, regardless of economists’ preferred models. A policy agenda that ignores those fears will fail politically even if it is elegant on paper. ([cbp.gov](https://www.cbp.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/24_1203_revenue-division-helpdesk-directory.pdf?utm_source=openai))

That objection deserves a real answer. The answer is not nostalgia for the pre-Trump trade consensus. It is discrimination. Tariffs should be narrow, targeted, time-limited, and attached to clear metrics. If a sector is subsidized abroad or tied to national security risk, use a law designed for that purpose and review it publicly. If a foreign government is dumping, enforce anti-dumping law. If a supply chain is dangerously concentrated, use procurement rules, tax credits, stockpiles, domestic content requirements and strategic grants. If a company needs to relocate production, help it with capital formation, permitting, training, and infrastructure instead of pretending a universal border tax is the same thing. In short: use the tools of industrial policy, not the bluntness of tariff spectacle. ([commerce.gov](https://www.commerce.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/Kessler_BIS%20Budget%20Hearing_HFAC%20South%20and%20Central%20Asia%20Subcommittee_06.12.2025.pdf?utm_source=openai))

This is also where the left should be careful not to overcorrect. Some anti-tariff commentators treat the Supreme Court’s ruling as proof that courts will fix economic nationalism for us. They will not. Courts can enforce statutory limits. They cannot build factories, retrain workers or negotiate supply chains. If Congress responds to the tariff fiasco only by restoring the old status quo, the political demand that produced the tariffs will return in some harsher form. Voters are not inventing the problem of industrial vulnerability. They are reacting, however imperfectly, to real dislocations. The response must be a better state, not a weaker one. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0485fcda30a7310501123e4931dba3f9?utm_source=openai))

What the refund era should teach Congress.

The impending refund process is a chance to stop pretending that the customs code can carry the burden of national economic strategy. If Congress wants tariffs available in emergencies, it should write emergency tariff authority narrowly: define the emergency, set the duration, require affirmative legislative approval for renewal, mandate sector-specific findings, and force public reporting on price effects, employment effects and retaliation. If Congress wants to protect strategic industries, it should fund them directly and transparently. And if Congress wants to rebuild trust with voters who think trade policy has been captured by elites, it should make the burden and benefits visible rather than burying them in a customs entry form. ([pwc.com](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tax-services/publications/insights/assets/pwc-us-supreme-court-invalidates-ieepa-tariffs.pdf?utm_source=openai))

There is no mystery about what has to happen next. The Supreme Court has already rejected the president’s most sweeping legal theory. Customs is now opening the refund portal. Federal courts are trying to police the scope and speed of repayment. The Fed is still watching tariff effects on inflation. Yale’s economists are still updating household-cost estimates. The trade deficit has not obediently disappeared. And the administration, if past is prologue, will continue trying to recover the leverage it lost in court by other means. This is exactly why the story is larger than one president. The core problem is institutional: the United States has allowed trade policy to become too easy to start, too hard to end, and too tempting to weaponize. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08861f153801156d213c30c4e2f6a683?utm_source=openai))

The right reaction is not to worship tariffs or to banish them. It is to end the fantasy that a president can rehabilitate American industry with a stroke of the pen and a chain of customs notices. Tariffs are at best a scalpel. Trump turned them into a sledgehammer and then a legal test case. The refunds now beginning are the bill for that mistake. Congress should not let the country pay it twice. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08861f153801156d213c30c4e2f6a683?utm_source=openai))

Steelman of the opposing case

To be fair to tariff defenders, they can point to real manufacturing anxiety, the political appeal of visible action, and the possibility that some domestic investment was accelerated by the shock of higher import costs. They can also argue that allies and competitors alike take the United States more seriously when it is willing to threaten economic pain. In a world of strategic competition with China and fragile supply chains, they say, policy tools that sound uncool in an economics seminar may be exactly what a sovereign country needs. That case is not frivolous. It is simply incomplete. A policy that depends on courts, improvisation, and repeated legal workarounds is not a strategy so much as a stress test for the republic. The country should demand better. ([commerce.gov](https://www.commerce.gov/news/press-releases/2026/01/first-year-accomplishments-under-secretary-howard-w-lutnick?utm_source=openai))

The lesson of April 2026 is not that America must choose between free trade and no trade. It is that if the United States wants a serious industrial policy, it needs serious institutions. That means Congress writing the rules, agencies collecting the data, courts policing the boundaries, and voters seeing the costs in plain language. Tariff refunds are a reminder that when presidents govern by import tax, the bill eventually arrives. Usually it arrives in the wrong place: in higher prices first, in litigation next, and in refund queues last. The next trade system should be designed to avoid that order entirely. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/08861f153801156d213c30c4e2f6a683?utm_source=openai))
