EU copyright rules sharpen pressure on AI developers as Washington pushes back

A new phase of AI regulation is forming on both sides of the Atlantic, with the European Union pressing ahead on copyright and transparency requirements even as the White House asks Congress to take a lighter-touch approach.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 16, 2026 • 4:57 p.m. • 1059 words

The fight over artificial intelligence is shifting from model size and product launches to a more basic question: who gets to control the data that makes generative AI work.

In Europe, policymakers are moving to tighten the rules around copyright, transparency and safety for general-purpose AI models. In Washington, the White House is urging Congress to avoid a patchwork of state laws that could slow the industry’s growth. The result is a widening transatlantic divide over how aggressively governments should regulate the systems that power chatbots, image generators and video tools.

The debate matters because the economics of AI depend on scale. Frontier models are trained on enormous collections of text, images, audio and video, much of it scraped from the open web or licensed through private deals. That has turned news organizations, publishers, artists and other rights holders into reluctant stakeholders in the AI boom. Their material helps make these systems useful, but they are increasingly asking whether they are being compensated fairly for it.

Europe moves from principles to enforcement

The European Union has already put the AI Act into force in phases, and it is now filling in the practical rules for general-purpose AI. Last year, the bloc released a code of practice designed to help companies comply with the new regime, including obligations tied to transparency, copyright and the safety of advanced systems. The AI Act’s framework is built around risk, but its general-purpose rules are especially consequential for companies that build foundational models used across the market. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a3df6a1a8789eea7fcd17bffc750e291?utm_source=openai))

That shift is crucial for AI developers because Europe is not merely asking for disclosures in theory. The point of the code of practice and the underlying law is to force companies to document how their models were trained, what kinds of copyrighted material may have been used and what steps were taken to comply with the bloc’s copyright rules. European lawmakers have also kept pressure on the issue through parliamentary work on generative AI and copyright, underscoring that training-data questions are now central to the policy debate. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a3df6a1a8789eea7fcd17bffc750e291?utm_source=openai))

For companies that want access to the European market, that means compliance can no longer be treated as an afterthought. The EU approach is likely to reward firms that can explain their data pipelines and prove they have risk controls in place. It also increases the odds that model providers will need to negotiate more licenses with publishers, media companies and other content owners rather than assuming broad public-web access is enough. That is an inference from the EU’s copyright and transparency framework, but it is the direction the rules are clearly pushing. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a3df6a1a8789eea7fcd17bffc750e291?utm_source=openai))

Washington wants speed, not a state-by-state fight

While Brussels is sharpening its framework, the White House has taken a markedly different stance. In March, it urged Congress to preempt state AI laws it considers too burdensome, arguing that the United States needs a lighter-touch national approach if it wants to stay competitive. The administration’s position reflects a familiar industry argument: that too many overlapping rules could slow deployment, raise costs and make it harder for American firms to compete with China and Europe. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

But federal restraint has not settled the issue. State governments have continued to pass their own rules, and the policy gap has widened over issues such as consumer disclosure, government use of AI and platform accountability. Texas, for example, already has a law requiring some government agencies and health care providers to disclose when AI is interacting with consumers. The broader pattern suggests that even if Congress eventually creates a federal framework, state-level AI oversight is likely to remain politically durable. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/479eb3d0a50fe7237678a9bfb146ac7a?utm_source=openai))

The White House’s approach also reflects the commercial reality that AI is now an infrastructure story, not just a software story. Federal officials have repeatedly tied AI leadership to energy, computing capacity and data-center expansion, arguing that the country must build more power generation and faster permitting if it wants to support the industry. That has made AI policy inseparable from debates over utilities, grid reliability and industrial planning. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/62e8118b069f36aa9d0844f904047933?utm_source=openai))

Copyright is becoming the industry’s defining test

For the AI industry, copyright is no longer just a legal nuisance. It is becoming a strategic issue that affects product design, licensing costs and public legitimacy. The lawsuits brought by news publishers and other rights holders have already forced companies to defend the way they train models and answer user queries. In one prominent case, a federal judge allowed The New York Times and other newspapers to continue pursuing claims against OpenAI and Microsoft, keeping the core dispute alive. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cc19ef2cf3f23343738e892b60d6d7a6?utm_source=openai))

That litigation has changed the incentives around training data. A few years ago, AI companies often framed the open web as a free resource. Now the market increasingly looks like a licensing business, with some publishers striking agreements while others litigate. Disney’s licensing deal with OpenAI last year showed how a major rights holder can choose partnership over confrontation, even as critics warned that such deals could normalize the use of protected characters in AI-generated video. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/df8be1fe52e9b9c46d965577d3974d3b?utm_source=openai))

The underlying tension is simple: AI companies want abundant data to keep improving their systems, while creators want recognition, control and compensation. Europe is leaning into formal compliance and rights management. The United States, at least for now, is leaning toward growth and innovation, with regulators hoping the market can sort out some of the hardest questions. That divergence could shape where companies choose to launch products, where they license data and where they build the next generation of models. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a3df6a1a8789eea7fcd17bffc750e291?utm_source=openai))

There is also a broader political consequence. As AI becomes embedded in search, video, customer support and enterprise software, the old policy divide between “tech regulation” and “copyright law” is fading. Training data, model transparency, consumer disclosure and energy use are all part of the same story now. The next phase of AI policy will not be won by the company with the flashiest demo. It will be won by the company that can prove it has a lawful, durable and scalable right to operate. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a3df6a1a8789eea7fcd17bffc750e291?utm_source=openai))

For now, the clearest signal is that governments are no longer willing to let the industry set its own terms. Whether through Europe’s detailed rulebook or Washington’s more permissive framework, AI is entering a period of political bargaining that will decide who pays, who profits and who gets to train the next generation of models.
