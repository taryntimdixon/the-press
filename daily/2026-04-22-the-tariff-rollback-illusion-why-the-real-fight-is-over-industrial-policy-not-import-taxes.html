The tariff rollback illusion: why the real fight is over industrial policy, not import taxes

The Supreme Court clipped the broadest tariff authority, but the deeper question remains: should the United States build resilience with blunt border taxes or with targeted industrial policy that actually grows capacity?

By The Press • April 22, 2026 • 11:22 a.m.

The most important thing to understand about the tariff fight in 2026 is that it is not really a fight about tariffs. It is a fight about what kind of state the United States wants to be: one that makes policy by taxing imports until trading partners blink, or one that uses durable industrial policy, targeted public investment, and rules-based trade enforcement to strengthen the economy without turning every shopping cart into a battlefield.

That argument has become more urgent, not less, since the Supreme Court’s 2025 ruling that curbed the administration’s use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act to impose broad import duties. A recent Congressional Research Service summary says the court affirmed that IEEPA does not authorize the tariffs at issue and sent parts of the case back for further proceedings, which means the legal fight is still shaping the policy landscape in 2026. The White House has continued to rely on other tariff channels and trade actions, while supporters present the new regime as both leverage and revenue. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11332?utm_source=openai))

My view is blunt: the United States should stop treating tariffs as a universal economic medicine. Tariffs can be a bargaining chip, a narrow corrective, or a contingency tool in a genuine emergency. But when they become the center of economic policy, they turn into a tax on competence. They punish import-intensive manufacturing, raise costs for consumers, invite retaliation, and make the country look as if it has substituted theater for strategy. If the goal is to rebuild American productive capacity, the better path is not perpetual border taxation. It is investment in energy, infrastructure, workforce development, permitting reform, research, and targeted competition policy that helps firms produce more in the United States instead of simply paying more at the border. That case is especially strong now that the newest research and official inflation data show tariff costs moving through the system rather than disappearing into some foreign pay-as-you-go fantasy. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/consumer-prices-up-3-3-percent-over-the-year-0-9-percent-over-the-month-in-march-2026.htm?utm_source=openai))

The legal reset did not end the economic argument

The court’s tariff ruling mattered because it forced an old constitutional question back into the center of economic policy: who gets to tax imports, under what authority, and with what constraints. Congress’s power over tariffs is not incidental; it is built into the constitutional structure and the trade statutes that actually authorize tariffs. The CRS review of IEEPA tariff litigation makes clear that the administration’s emergency-based tariff theory is not the same thing as Congress granting the executive a free hand over trade policy. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11332?utm_source=openai))

That legal pivot matters because it reveals the administration’s theory of trade policy: tariffs are not merely a means to protect a few strategic sectors; they are a general-purpose lever to force foreign concessions, raise revenue, and redirect commerce. The problem is that a lever is not a plan. The IMF’s April 2026 consultation with the United States projects 2.4 percent growth in 2026 while also saying fading tariff effects and lower oil prices should bring core PCE inflation back to 2 percent during the first half of 2027. In other words, the IMF’s baseline is not that tariffs are harmless; it is that the economy is proving resilient despite them, while the tariff impulse itself is expected to fade. The OECD’s spring outlook likewise warns that a tariff-ratcheting layer reduces global activity. That is not a ringing endorsement of tariffs as a growth strategy. It is a caution that the economy is absorbing a policy shock, not celebrating it. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

The best defense of tariffs starts from a real grievance. The United States is not imagining its industrial problems. It has suffered for decades from hollowed-out manufacturing communities, supply-chain dependence in critical sectors, and a trade system that too often rewards capital mobility over labor security. The USTR’s 2026 National Trade Estimate report catalogs persistent foreign barriers to U.S. exports and frames the administration’s response as an effort to level the field for American workers. That report also reminds us that the trade debate is not only about what the United States does at its border, but about what other governments do to shield their own firms. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-releases-2026-national-trade-estimate-report?utm_source=openai))

There is a serious pro-tariff case for a narrow set of circumstances. If a country is dumping heavily subsidized goods, if a dependency creates military vulnerability, or if a sector is so concentrated abroad that the market is clearly failing, tariffs can buy time for domestic adjustment. Tariffs also create leverage in negotiations, and in some cases they can redirect sourcing or induce partners to lower their own barriers. That is the strongest version of the argument, and it should not be caricatured. The United States has real security interests in steel, semiconductors, critical minerals, pharmaceuticals, and certain defense-adjacent supply chains. No serious critic of tariffs thinks all trade is equally benign or that every import is worth preserving at all costs. The issue is scope, duration, and discipline. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-releases-2026-national-trade-estimate-report?utm_source=openai))

But the strongest pro-tariff case is also a case for restraint. It argues for precision, not maximalism; for time limits, not permanence; for sector-specific targeting, not a whole-economy import tax. Once tariffs become broad and recurring, they stop looking like strategic protection and start functioning as a hidden consumption tax with a nationalist accent. That is especially true when the tariffs apply to inputs that American companies need to make the very goods policymakers claim to want more of. Tariffs on raw materials, components, machine tools, chemicals, and industrial intermediate goods do not magically create factories. They raise the cost of building factories. Brookings has emphasized that even when aggregate macro damage looks modest, tariffs can still raise costs for sectors that rely on imported inputs, including housing and construction. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

The price mechanism is doing exactly what economists said it would do

One reason tariff politics persists is that the macroeconomic damage is often delayed, diffuse, and easy to deny. But the available 2026 data increasingly show the burden moving through the system. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that consumer prices rose 3.3 percent over the year through March 2026, while producer prices rose 4.0 percent over the year through March 2026. The Federal Reserve’s April note on tariff effects estimated that tariff changes through November 2025 raised core goods PCE prices cumulatively by 3.1 percent through February 2026 and boosted core PCE prices overall by 0.8 percent. The Fed explicitly noted that its analysis did not include effects of the February 2026 Supreme Court ruling on IEEPA tariffs. In plain English: tariffs are showing up in the prices Americans pay, and the evidence is now coming from the institutions that measure inflation, not just from think tanks with a position to defend. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2026/consumer-prices-up-3-3-percent-over-the-year-0-9-percent-over-the-month-in-march-2026.htm?utm_source=openai))

The best counterargument is that the economy has not cracked. It is true that the IMF sees U.S. growth rising to 2.4 percent in 2026, and Brookings has argued that the aggregate macro impact of the 2025 tariff surge has so far been smaller than many feared. In its spring 2026 discussion, Brookings said the overall effect in one model was roughly between minus 0.13 percent and plus 0.10 percent of GDP. That is important. It means apocalyptic language is not always warranted, and it means that some businesses and consumers have adapted faster than critics expected. The economy did not instantly collapse under the weight of tariffs. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))

But “the economy did not instantly collapse” is an extremely weak standard for policy success. A tax can be inflationary, regressive, and inefficient without causing recession. A policy can be politically potent and economically wasteful at the same time. The question is not whether the United States can survive tariffs; it is whether tariffs are the best way to achieve the stated ends. The newest research suggests they are not. NBER’s April 2026 working paper by Pablo Fajgelbaum and Amit Khandelwal estimates that, in its baseline, 90 percent of tariffs are passed through to tariff-inclusive prices paid by U.S. importers. That is a devastating number for the “foreigners pay it all” theory. If importers are paying the tariffs and consumers are absorbing the costs downstream, then the tariff is not a free lunch; it is a transfer with frictions, deadweight loss, and political disguise. ([nber.org](https://www.nber.org/papers/w35064?utm_source=openai))

That same NBER paper is also useful because it complicates the anti-tariff case. It finds small net welfare effects in a static framework, with gains from income and revenue offsetting consumption losses depending on terms-of-trade assumptions. This is why a serious opinion essay should not pretend the literature is one-note. There are scenarios in which tariffs shift some bargaining power to the United States or improve the terms on which the country trades. But those scenarios are fragile, and they depend on the rest of the world not retaliating aggressively, firms not re-routing around the tariffs, and domestic policy using the breathing room productively. If the breathing room is just absorbed into higher prices and new lobbying for exemptions, the policy becomes a permanent tax on everybody except the most politically connected. ([nber.org](https://www.nber.org/papers/w35064?utm_source=openai))

The real pro-tariff case is moral, but morality is not a macro strategy

The deeper case for tariffs is not economic. It is moral and political. Tariff supporters believe the world order has treated American workers as a cost center, while elites grew richer from cheap imports, complex supply chains, and financial globalization. They see tariffs as a correction, a way to make trade visible again and force the price of foreign production back onto the political map. This argument is powerful because it speaks to a genuine wound. The communities that lost factories, bargaining power, and status did not lose in abstraction. They lost wages, tax bases, and often the social fabric that comes with stable work. Brookings’ 2026 economic discussion is one of many recent reminders that the policy debate is inseparable from questions of distribution and resilience. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

But moral clarity about the harms of deindustrialization does not prove that tariffs are the best remedy. In fact, the evidence points in the opposite direction. Tariffs are blunt. They do not distinguish between vulnerable domestic producers and firms that simply want less competition. They do not distinguish between strategic sectors and ordinary consumer goods. And they do not automatically produce new capacity; they can just as easily protect inefficiency or create a political constituency that thrives on remaining sheltered forever. That is why industrial policy exists in the first place: to distinguish between sectors, to condition public support on performance, and to make the state a builder rather than a tax collector. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

There is also a crucial distributional issue. CBPP’s 2026 analysis argues that tariffs are highly regressive because they function like sales taxes: they place a heavier immediate burden on families with low and moderate incomes than income taxes do. The same analysis warns that price-hiking tariffs would reduce living standards for households that already have the least room to absorb another increase in everyday costs. That is not just a talking point. Lower-income households spend a larger share of income on goods such as clothing, appliances, and household supplies, which means the pain from import taxes is routed through American checkout counters and budget spreadsheets. ([cbpp.org](https://www.cbpp.org/sites/default/files/2-3-26tax.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Polling now reflects the same reality. A PBS News/NPR/Marist poll found that more than half of Americans say tariffs mostly hurt the U.S. economy, and the survey was conducted in late January 2026. A Washington Post-ABC News-Ipsos poll found that 64 percent of respondents disapprove of Trump’s handling of tariffs. That does not mean public opinion is permanently settled, but it does suggest that the administration’s effort to sell tariffs as broadly beneficial has not been persuasive. And politically, that matters. The tariffs are no longer abstract doctrine; they are hitting cost-of-living concerns in a period when many families are already strained by housing, food, and energy costs. ([pbs.org](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/poll-nearly-two-thirds-of-americans-say-ice-has-gone-too-far-in-immigration-crackdown?utm_source=openai))

The best argument for tariffs is that the alternative has been too weak

The strongest challenge to my position is that the anti-tariff camp has spent decades criticizing protectionism while failing to deliver a credible industrial strategy. That critique is fair. For years, Washington embraced trade liberalization, underinvested in domestic capacity, allowed infrastructure to age, tolerated weak antitrust enforcement in key sectors, and treated trade adjustment as a side program rather than a central economic mission. If the United States had built resilient manufacturing policy earlier, the current tariff backlash might never have gained momentum. In that sense, tariffs are not the cause of American industrial anxiety; they are a symptom of policy failure elsewhere. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

That is why simply saying “free trade is good” is not enough. It is an evasion. The country needs a positive project. Brookings’s spring 2026 assessment notes that tariffs may not have cratered the economy, but the broader point is that the economy has been resilient partly because other forces, including private-sector adaptation and macroeconomic cushioning, have offset some tariff effects. That means policy makers have room to do better than tariffs. They can build around the actual constraints businesses face: permitting bottlenecks, grid congestion, uneven workforce pipelines, fragile supply chains, and chronic uncertainty about whether public policy will change every election cycle. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

Consider the practical alternative. If the goal is reindustrialization, then policy should prioritize the stuff that decides whether a plant gets built and stays open: cheap reliable electricity, faster permitting, ports, rail, roads, training programs, and a tax code that rewards domestic investment rather than financial engineering. If the goal is resilience, then the government should use stockpiles, diversified sourcing, allied coordination, and targeted procurement standards. If the goal is competition with China, then the answer is not a blanket tariff that also hits allies and U.S. producers. It is coordinated export controls on genuinely sensitive technologies, investment screening where justified, and support for strategic sectors that are actually at risk. None of that is as emotionally satisfying as slapping a tax on imports, but policy is supposed to work, not just feel muscular. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-releases-2026-national-trade-estimate-report?utm_source=openai))

There is one more reason to resist tariff maximalism: it confuses leverage with victory. A tariff can force a negotiation. It can even force a concession. But if the resulting deal is followed by more uncertainty, more exemptions, and another tariff round, businesses learn the wrong lesson: the safest investment is not capacity, it is political access. That is poison for productive capitalism. Healthy capitalism rewards firms that innovate, scale, and compete. Tariff-heavy capitalism rewards firms that hedge, lobby, and wait for the next waiver. Recent trade-policy reporting suggests the administration has tried to frame new tariff deals as proof of success, but the more durable test is whether those deals create predictable rules rather than recurring improvisation. ([ustr.gov](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-releases-2026-national-trade-estimate-report?utm_source=openai))

The most honest compromise is not “no tariffs”; it is limited tariffs inside a bigger plan

The fairest conclusion is not that every tariff is bad. It is that tariffs should be demoted from centerpiece to instrument. The United States should keep narrow, legally grounded tariffs available for security-sensitive sectors, anti-dumping cases, and negotiated pressure when there is a credible endpoint. But it should reject the fantasy that broad, economy-wide import taxes can simultaneously raise revenue, revive manufacturing, tame China, strengthen bargaining power, and leave consumers untouched. No advanced economy has ever gotten all of those outcomes at once, and the recent data suggest the United States will not be the first. ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11332?utm_source=openai))

That compromise also demands honesty from tariff skeptics. If they want the public to trust an anti-tariff agenda, they cannot simply promise cheaper imports and call it a day. They have to answer the legitimate fear underneath tariff politics: that the American economy can be globally successful and locally hollow at the same time. They have to prove that there is a route to well-paid industrial work that does not rely on permanent protection. They have to show that workers can gain from openness when the state actively shapes the conditions for investment, bargaining, and mobility. That means the anti-tariff argument must become a pro-capacity argument. Otherwise, tariff politics will keep winning by default. ([brookings.edu](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/tariffs-in-2025-short-run-impacts-on-the-us-economy/?utm_source=openai))

The 2026 tariff fight is therefore a referendum on competence. The administration’s supporters are right that the old trade consensus failed too many workers and too many communities. The critics are right that tariffs are taxing families, distorting prices, and creating uncertainty without solving the underlying industrial problem. The task for policymakers is to stop choosing between naive free trade and permanent protection. America needs a third path: strategic, selective, and boring in the best sense of the word. It should be harder to import strategic dependence, but easier to build domestic capacity. It should be easier to expand production in the United States, but harder to hide behind tariffs forever. And it should be plain by now that tariffs are a useful tool only when they are subordinate to a larger strategy, not when they are mistaken for the strategy itself. The Federal Reserve’s latest note and the IMF’s 2026 consultation both point in the same direction: tariffs have measurable price effects, but the broader economic question is how much damage policy makers are willing to accept in exchange for leverage that may prove fleeting. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/detecting-tariff-effects-on-consumer-prices-in-real-time-part-II-20260408.html?utm_source=openai))

That is the argument the country should have in 2026. Not whether tariffs feel tough. Whether they work. Not whether they can force headlines. Whether they can build anything lasting. On the evidence now available, the answer is: sometimes, in narrow doses, under strict rules. But as a governing philosophy, tariffs are a dead end. The future of American industrial strength will be built by investment, capacity, and discipline. Tariffs alone can only build resentment and a more expensive checkout line. The IMF, BLS, Fed, Brookings, NBER, CBPP, USTR, and OECD all point, in different ways, to the same basic conclusion: the tariff debate is real, but the answer cannot be tariff maximalism. ([imf.org](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/04/01/pr-26102-usa-imf-executive-board-concludes-2026-article-iv-consult?utm_source=openai))
