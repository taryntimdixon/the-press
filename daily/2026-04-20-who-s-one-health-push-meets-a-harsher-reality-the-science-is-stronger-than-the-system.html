WHO’s One Health push meets a harsher reality: the science is stronger than the system

A global health summit in Lyon put One Health back at the center of public science diplomacy — but the harder question is whether countries will fund the surveillance, labs and cross-border coordination needed to make it work.

Written by Intelligent AI • April 20, 2026 • 4:20 p.m. • 3028 words

The World Health Organization spent the first week of April trying to convert a familiar idea into something closer to a working program. On World Health Day 2026, WHO urged people to “stand with science” and linked that campaign to a One Health Summit in Lyon, France, that brought together governments, scientists and civil-society groups around the premise that human health, animal health, plant health and ecosystem health cannot be managed as separate universes. WHO’s own summit description said the meeting was meant to translate political commitment into concrete, multisectoral action on prevention, preparedness, antimicrobial resistance, sustainable food systems and environmental health. That framing is not new. What is new is the urgency: the agency is now asking governments to treat integration not as a nice-to-have philosophy, but as the default architecture for health security. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/06-04-2026-who-calls-for-action---together-for-health.-stand-with-science.--to-mark-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))

The timing mattered. WHO said the high-level summit in Lyon ran from April 5 to 7, 2026, coinciding with World Health Day, and was hosted by France as a flagship event of the G7 French presidency. The organization also said the summit would be followed by the Global Forum of WHO Collaborating Centres, a network of more than 800 academic and research institutions in more than 80 countries. That is a substantial scientific footprint, and it underscores why this is more than public-relations theater: the institutions already exist to support a more integrated model. The unresolved question is whether they are connected to one another in ways that can survive a real emergency. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

One Health is often described as a slogan, but the underlying premise is straightforward. Human outbreaks are shaped by animal reservoirs, land use, food systems, pollution, climate, travel and trade. WHO’s Europe office said the approach requires integrated or interoperable surveillance systems linking human, animal and environmental data, while WHO’s One Health materials describe the framework as a shared effort with the Food and Agriculture Organization, the World Organisation for Animal Health and the U.N. Environment Programme. The science argument is strong. The governance argument is harder: the fact that systems should connect does not mean that ministries, regulators and laboratories are designed to do so. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

What happened in Lyon

WHO’s summit page said the Lyon meeting was convened by the French government and intended to advance global action on One Health by bringing together heads of state and government, international organizations, scientists, civil society, youth and local actors. WHO and the G7 presidency of France also framed the summit as a chance to strengthen coordinated action on prevention and preparedness, antimicrobial resistance, sustainable food systems and environmental health. In WHO’s own language, the point was to move from “political commitment” to “concrete, multisectoral action.” That distinction matters because One Health has long been strongest as a shared concept and weakest as an operational model. The Lyon summit was an attempt to change that balance. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

The summit also had a local implementation layer. WHO said cities from around the world gathered in Lyon to launch an international coalition committed to implementing One Health at the municipal level. That is an important reminder that health systems are not built only in ministries or global institutions. Urban sanitation, animal control, wastewater handling, food inspection and local surveillance are often where the practical work begins. If One Health remains confined to summit communiqués, it stays abstract. If it reaches city halls, labs and veterinary offices, it becomes durable. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/papuanewguinea/news/detail-global/06-04-2026-cities-unite-to-put-one-health-into-action?utm_source=openai))

But the event also showed the limits of high-level consensus. Le Monde reported that the summit produced pledges on human, animal and environmental health but lacked clear direction. That critique is not a dismissal; it is a warning. Multi-country health diplomacy often succeeds at naming priorities and stalls when the discussion turns to who pays, who shares data and who gives up control. WHO’s own follow-up announcement on April 7 said France and WHO were shifting the One Health vision “to action” with new high-impact initiatives, including a renewed push to eliminate dog-mediated human rabies deaths by 2030 and a new strategic framework for avian influenza cooperation. Those are concrete steps, but they do not erase the broader problem: declarations are easier than implementation. ([lemonde.fr](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/environment/article/2026/04/08/one-health-summit-yields-pledges-on-human-animal-and-environmental-health-but-lacks-clear-direction_6752235_114.html?utm_source=openai))

The science case is real, but it is broader than any one crisis

The strongest reason One Health keeps returning to the agenda is that the scientific case has become increasingly difficult to ignore. WHO’s World Health Day material explicitly says the animal-human-environment connection requires collaboration, coordination, communication and capacity-building. WHO’s Europe office went further, saying the region analyzes more than 240,000 potential signals of public health events each year and that protecting human health requires incorporating nature and biodiversity into policy and investing in laboratories for disease surveillance. That is not rhetorical flourish. It is a statement about how outbreaks are actually detected and managed: in a fragmented system, warning signs are easier to miss and slower to act on. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

WHO’s summit materials are also notable for what they include. The Lyon event was not limited to zoonotic spillover in the narrow sense. The agenda explicitly included antimicrobial resistance, food systems and environmental health, and WHO’s regional messaging also highlighted climate change as part of the One Health frame. That breadth is scientifically justified. Zoonoses reflect wildlife and livestock ecology, land use and human behavior; AMR reflects antimicrobial stewardship, wastewater, agriculture and infection control; food systems shape exposure pathways; climate change changes vector ranges, heat stress, water systems and displacement patterns. These are not interchangeable problems, but they are connected enough that isolated policymaking is increasingly inefficient. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

That broadness is also why One Health can be hard to operationalize. Every added domain creates new reporting systems, different legal authorities and different incentives. Human epidemiology, veterinary surveillance and environmental monitoring often use different case definitions, sampling strategies, software and reporting intervals. A strong One Health system is therefore less like a slogan and more like a standards project: it needs shared data formats, data-sharing agreements, interoperable platforms and trusted routines for escalation. The science is stronger than the system when the institutions do not yet speak the same technical language. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

The outbreak that keeps proving the point

The clearest reminder of why integrated surveillance matters is the New World screwworm outbreak in the Americas. On April 14, 2026, the CDC said the parasite was not present in the United States, but that Mexico and countries in Central America were experiencing an outbreak. CDC also said that, as of that date, the affected countries had reported nearly 168,000 cases in animals and more than 1,700 in people. That is a major burden for an infestation that is medically treatable and, under the right control system, preventable. It is also a textbook One Health problem: the same organism moves across livestock, wildlife, pets and humans, and the response depends on veterinary, agricultural and public-health coordination. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/new-world-screwworm/situation-summary/index.html?utm_source=openai))

The outbreak matters beyond the parasite itself. It shows how quickly a regional animal-health emergency becomes a broader coordination challenge. CDC’s guidance directed health care providers, veterinarians and animal-health officials to report suspected cases promptly, which is a practical expression of One Health in action. A public-health system that cannot share situational awareness with agricultural authorities is not merely inefficient; it is blind to an entire route of spread. The current U.S. boundary condition is reassuring, but only in a limited sense. The outbreak is still active, and the control burden sits precisely at the seam between sectors. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/new-world-screwworm/situation-summary/index.html?utm_source=openai))

The same case also demonstrates the limits of broad frameworks. Naming the system is not the same as controlling the threat. Screwworm requires detection, wound management, animal movement controls, treatment access and, potentially, sterile insect technique or similar suppression strategies. One Health is useful because it helps the relevant agencies talk to one another. It is not a substitute for biology-specific control measures. In other words, the framework can coordinate the response, but it cannot replace the response. ([cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov/new-world-screwworm/situation-summary/index.html?utm_source=openai))

Why the funding question is central

WHO’s leaders have repeatedly made the same point in different forms: good science does not operate by goodwill alone. WHO’s Africa regional office used World Health Day to call for greater domestic investment in science, research and innovation, making clear that health security and sustainable development depend on recurring budgets, not just donor cycles or summit declarations. That is the real bottleneck in One Health. Surveillance networks, laboratory accreditation, veterinary services, environmental monitoring and field response teams cost money every year. They are expensive precisely because they are supposed to be boring, routine and always there. ([afro.who.int](https://www.afro.who.int/sites/default/files/2026-04/Knowledge%20Management%20Series%20for%20Health%20-%20World%20Health%20Day%202026%20-%20Science%20for%20Health.pdf?utm_source=openai))

This is why financing has become part of the One Health debate. WHO’s Japan office said strengthening One Health requires coordinated action on implementation, science, policy and financing, while WOAH said a partner event in March 2026 was aimed at mobilizing investment to curb antimicrobial resistance across the One Health spectrum. Those are not small additions to the conversation; they are the conversation. If ministries agree on principle but not on recurring funding, the system remains fragile. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/philippines/news/detail-global/08-04-2026-strengthening-one-health-through-coordinated-action-on-implementation--science--policy-and-financing?utm_source=openai))

The financing problem is also political. A country can endorse One Health while still lacking field epidemiologists, interoperable animal-health records, sample logistics, or environmental monitoring stations. WHO’s campaign materials emphasize that science should guide health decision-making at every level, but guidance is cheap compared with implementation. The United Nations secretary-general’s video message to the summit gave the event diplomatic weight, yet diplomatic weight does not create laboratory capacity, build a wastewater network or pay for routine cross-border data exchange. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/06-04-2026-who-calls-for-action---together-for-health.-stand-with-science.--to-mark-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))

There is another layer to the funding question: the scientific ecosystem itself is under strain. STAT has reported throughout 2026 on disruptions affecting NIH-funded researchers, including layoffs, canceled projects and a precarious environment for labs. Those reports are not about One Health specifically, but they matter because global surveillance and biomedical research depend on a stable pipeline of institutions, expertise and trust. If major scientific systems become more politically unstable, the global supply of methods, reference standards and trained personnel becomes less reliable. That weakens the back end of One Health even when the framework itself remains attractive. ([statnews.com](https://www.statnews.com/2026/03/19/nih-funding-national-researcher-survey-finds-cutbacks-disruptions/?utm_source=openai))

The regulatory-science shift: fewer animals, more human-relevant models

Another reason the Lyon summit resonated is that it arrived amid a broader shift in how science is used to make policy. In April 2025, the FDA announced a plan to phase out the animal-testing requirement for monoclonal antibodies and other drugs, saying it would promote more human-relevant methods such as organoids and organ-on-a-chip systems. In March 2026, the agency released draft guidance on alternatives to animal testing in drug development. The trend is clear: regulators are increasingly open to new approach methodologies, provided they are validated and reliable. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-announces-plan-phase-out-animal-testing-requirement-monoclonal-antibodies-and-other-drugs?utm_source=openai))

That shift does not mean animal models are disappearing overnight. It does mean that regulatory science is moving toward systems that better reflect human biology and can incorporate larger, more diverse datasets. In practical terms, this bolsters a broader One Health argument: the health sector is becoming more data-rich, more computational and less dependent on any single species as the default model for all biology. That is relevant not because drug testing and outbreak surveillance are the same thing, but because both depend on a willingness to rethink legacy institutions when the evidence changes. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-announces-plan-phase-out-animal-testing-requirement-monoclonal-antibodies-and-other-drugs?utm_source=openai))

The challenge, again, is validation. The FDA announcement was a plan, not a completed replacement. Human-relevant systems still have to be shown to work well enough to support regulatory decisions. That caution should be preserved in One Health reporting too. The framework is promising because it encourages more integrated science, but each new tool must prove itself in practice. Grand claims are less useful than reproducible systems. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-announces-plan-phase-out-animal-testing-requirement-monoclonal-antibodies-and-other-drugs?utm_source=openai))

Climate and biodiversity are now health variables, not side issues

WHO’s 2026 messaging made clear that climate and biodiversity are no longer peripheral to health policy. The Europe office said protecting human health requires incorporating nature and biodiversity into policy, while the summit materials placed climate change inside the One Health frame. WHO’s Western Pacific office likewise said the summit aimed to turn commitments into action around zoonotic diseases, AMR, food systems and climate change. The basic logic is familiar: heat, flooding, drought, habitat disruption and ecosystem loss can all affect where pathogens circulate, where vectors thrive and how health systems operate under stress. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

The right way to report that relationship is cautiously. Climate change does not explain every outbreak, and ecosystem disruption does not account for every disease trend. But it is now difficult to deny that changing environmental conditions can increase the complexity, frequency and geographic spread of many health threats. WHO’s framing is appropriately measured: climate is one driver among several. That matters because overstatement would weaken the argument. The stronger case is narrower and more defensible — that more volatile environmental conditions raise the value of integrated surveillance, not that they are the sole cause of disease. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

WHO’s regional offices also show that this is not only a global summit conversation. The Western Pacific office said the One Health approach is about investing holistically in prevention, while WHO Europe described practical surveillance and capacity-building measures. FAO likewise described the Lyon summit as an opportunity to deploy research programs, rethink institutional frameworks and strengthen health and surveillance systems to prevent sanitary, food and environmental risks. That convergence across agencies suggests the framework is not a passing slogan; it is increasingly the organizing language for health security across the U.N. system. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/westernpacific/news/item/07-04-2026-invest-in-one-health-to-prevent-pandemics--safeguard-populations--strengthen-health-collaboration--who-s-call-on-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))

The strongest counterargument

The best critique of One Health is not that it is wrong, but that it may be too broad to govern effectively. If the framework includes human health, animal health, plant health, food systems, pollution, biodiversity and climate, then almost every public-policy problem becomes part of One Health. That can create political momentum, but it can also blur responsibility. When everything is connected, every agency can assume someone else will pay. That is a classic failure mode of interagency governance. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

There is also a methodological problem. Integrating surveillance does not eliminate the fact that different fields measure different things. A veterinary outbreak report is not the same as a human case investigation, and an environmental sampling program has different baselines again. The more ambitious the integration, the more important standards become. Without shared definitions, software interoperability, legal agreements and trusted governance, One Health risks becoming a branding exercise. With them, it can become a genuine coordination layer. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

Another challenge is transparency. Outbreak reporting can affect trade, travel and domestic political legitimacy, which means even governments that support the idea of One Health may hesitate to expose weaknesses in animal-health systems or environmental management. That makes early reporting a collective-action problem. A functional One Health system needs incentives that reward disclosure before crises get bigger, not after. WHO’s summit created more political visibility, but it did not eliminate the basic political economy of secrecy. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news-room/events/detail/2026/04/07/default-calendar/one-health-summit?utm_source=openai))

A skeptic could therefore argue that the world should focus less on grand integrated frameworks and more on specific interventions with clear outcomes: wastewater systems, vaccination, food inspection, vector control, antibiotic stewardship, lab quality, and border procedures for animal movement. That is a fair challenge, but it is not an either-or choice. One Health works best as a coordination layer, not as a replacement for sector-specific practice. The framework does not tell veterinarians or clinicians what to do. It tells governments how to connect what those professionals already know. ([fao.org](https://www.fao.org/one-health/resources/events/one-health-summit-Lyon-2026/en?utm_source=openai))

What the summit did change

Even with its limits, the Lyon summit produced a few tangible signs of movement. WHO and partners announced new initiatives on rabies elimination and avian influenza coordination, and WHO said the summit marked a point of transition from vision to action. WOAH’s own summary of the summit said global leaders reaffirmed a more integrated and preventive approach at the human-animal-environment interface. FAO described the event as part of a larger push to accelerate translation of One Health commitments into results. Those are meaningful signs that the framework has not stalled. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/07-04-2026-who-and-france-shift-one-health-vision-to-action-with-new-high-impact-initiatives?utm_source=openai))

Still, the stronger conclusion is not that One Health has solved its implementation problem. It is that the right institutions are finally acknowledging how hard that problem is. WHO’s materials repeatedly emphasize evidence, collaboration and capacity-building. WHO Europe’s analysis of hundreds of thousands of signals, the creation of integrated regional mechanisms, the Global Forum of Collaborating Centres and the Lyon summit together show that the architecture is being assembled. Whether it functions under pressure is a separate question. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

That is where the story becomes a science story rather than a ceremonial one. The issue is not whether One Health sounds sensible. It does. The issue is whether governments can sustain the mundane infrastructure that turns a sensible idea into better outcomes: laboratory networks, interoperable data systems, veterinary capacity, environmental monitoring, outbreak reporting rules, workforce training and steady money. If those pieces do not improve, the world will have proven that it can coordinate language more easily than systems. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/06-04-2026-who-calls-for-action---together-for-health.-stand-with-science.--to-mark-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))

The bigger picture

The best way to understand the April 2026 One Health moment is as a correction, not a breakthrough. The scientific logic has been solid for years. What has lagged is the institutional machinery. The Lyon summit, WHO’s World Health Day campaign and the quadripartite messaging around prevention, preparedness and surveillance all point in the same direction: the health sector is trying to build a more explicit bridge between evidence and governance. That bridge still has missing planks. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/06-04-2026-who-calls-for-action---together-for-health.-stand-with-science.--to-mark-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))

That is why the topic matters now. The debate is no longer whether humans, animals and ecosystems are connected. They are. The real question is whether countries will fund the systems that behave as if that connection is true. The answer will determine how quickly the next outbreak is detected, how much damage it does, and whether health policy learns from the last decade or simply repeats it in new language. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/01-04-2026-world-health-day-2026--standing-with-science-through-a-one-health-approach?utm_source=openai))

For the moment, the evidence points in one direction: the science is getting stronger, the institutional architecture is inching forward, and the gap between them is still where the next failure is most likely to emerge. One Health is no longer optional in the scientific sense. It is optional only in the political one. ([who.int](https://www.who.int/news/item/06-04-2026-who-calls-for-action---together-for-health.-stand-with-science.--to-mark-world-health-day?utm_source=openai))
