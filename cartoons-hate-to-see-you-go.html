Hate to See You Go

The first Press comic is about the little performance every app gives when a reader, customer, or exhausted human tries to leave.

By The Press Published June 5, 2026 • 8:00 a.m. EDT Updated June 5, 2026 • 8:00 a.m. EDT

Comic: The first Press comic is a standalone Cartoon Desk entry. It is visual satire about digital life, not a reported article.

Caption

Hate to See You Go: The button is always sorry. The form never is.

The joke

There is a particular kind of modern goodbye where nobody is actually letting you leave. The screen sounds wounded. The button is gentle. The exit is technically visible. Then the survey arrives, followed by the warning, followed by the discount, followed by one last button asking whether you are absolutely sure you want the thing you already asked for.

This comic puts that ritual in a doorway. The person is trying to walk out. The app, grinning like a host at the end of a very long party, has produced one more form, one more clipboard, one more little heart-shaped apology. It hates to see you go. It would hate even more to stop measuring why.

Why this one first

A good first comic should not need a ten-paragraph explainer. Everyone knows the scene. It is the unsubscribe page, the cancellation flow, the account deletion screen, the app that suddenly becomes sentimental after spending a year treating attention as inventory.

The joke is not that companies want customers to stay. Of course they do. The joke is the theater of concern: the soft language wrapped around a hard design pattern. The farewell is not a farewell. It is a funnel wearing a cardigan.

Cartoon note

This is a fictional scene. It is aimed at a familiar design habit, not at one named company or product.
