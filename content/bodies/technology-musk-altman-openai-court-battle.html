The courtroom in Oakland is not where the future of artificial intelligence was supposed to be decided. The future was supposed to arrive in research papers, product demos, model releases, policy hearings, maybe some solemnly worded blog posts about “benefiting humanity,” because Silicon Valley has always enjoyed placing a halo over a cap table. But on April 28, 2026, the fight over OpenAI’s future began where many grand American promises eventually end up: in federal court, under fluorescent lights, before a judge, a jury, lawyers, reporters, and a cast of billionaires who had once imagined themselves building something too important to be governed by ordinary greed.

Elon Musk took the stand first. His argument was simple enough to fit on a protest sign and dramatic enough to dominate a news cycle: OpenAI, he said, began as a charity and had been turned into a profit machine. Musk testified that he conceived of OpenAI, helped name it, recruited key people, provided early funding, and deliberately chose a nonprofit structure because the point was not to enrich anyone. He is suing OpenAI, CEO Sam Altman, president Greg Brockman, and Microsoft, accusing them of breaching OpenAI’s charitable mission and unjustly enriching themselves through a for-profit transformation. He wants damages reported at $150 billion, with proceeds intended for OpenAI’s charitable arm, and he wants OpenAI returned to nonprofit status with Altman and Brockman removed from leadership.

OpenAI’s answer is just as direct: Musk is not a wronged donor protecting the public interest, but a competitor who failed to gain control of OpenAI, walked away, founded xAI, and then tried to use the courts to damage a rival. OpenAI’s attorney William Savitt told jurors that Musk wanted “the keys to the kingdom” and sued only after he did not get his way. OpenAI says there was no promise that it would remain nonprofit forever, that Musk himself supported a for-profit structure when the organization realized the cost of advanced AI research, and that the creation of a commercial arm was necessary to buy compute, hire talent, and compete with Google DeepMind.

This is the legal question. The larger question is more unsettling: what happens when a public-interest mission becomes attached to a private empire?

OpenAI was introduced to the world in 2015 as a nonprofit artificial intelligence research company. Its founding announcement said the goal was to advance digital intelligence in a way “most likely to benefit humanity as a whole,” specifically “unconstrained by a need to generate financial return.” That sentence now reads like either a noble founding charter or Exhibit A, depending on which side of the courtroom one sits.

The words mattered. OpenAI was not merely another startup trying to become the next platform. It presented itself as a counterweight to the idea that artificial intelligence, perhaps the most consequential technology of the century, should be developed behind the walls of a single dominant corporation. Musk, Altman, Brockman, Ilya Sutskever, and the other early figures were not just building software. They were building a narrative: if artificial general intelligence arrived, it should not belong exclusively to Google, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, or any one person brilliant or reckless enough to get there first.

That narrative was powerful because it fused two Silicon Valley instincts that rarely coexist comfortably: techno-optimism and fear. OpenAI’s founders believed powerful AI could transform medicine, education, productivity, and science. They also understood, or at least said they understood, that the same technology could produce dangerous concentration of power. Musk has long framed AI as a civilizational risk; during his testimony, he reportedly warned about catastrophic outcomes and argued that OpenAI was founded to avoid such danger.

But ideals, like server clusters, require funding. By 2019, OpenAI had changed course. It created OpenAI LP, a “capped-profit” structure designed to attract investment while preserving nonprofit oversight. OpenAI said investor returns for the first round would be capped at 100 times the original investment. Even with a cap, that kind of return can still produce enormous private upside, enough to make the word “capped” do heroic levels of public-relations labor.

OpenAI’s explanation was practical. Cutting-edge AI required billions of dollars for cloud computing, talent, and supercomputers. The AI Now Institute later summarized the shift as OpenAI arguing that no existing legal structure gave it the right balance between raising capital and serving its mission. In other words, the organization had discovered what many nonprofits discover after meeting the modern technology stack: virtue is admirable, but Nvidia chips do not accept payment in moral seriousness.

Musk’s side does not deny that advanced AI is expensive. That is not where the fight lives. The fight lives in whether the commercial structure remained subordinate to the charitable mission or swallowed it whole. Musk’s lawyers argue that OpenAI’s assets, reputation, and public trust were built under a nonprofit promise and later converted into private value. After Musk dropped his fraud and constructive fraud claims, the trial was narrowed to breach of charitable trust and unjust enrichment, plus an aiding-and-abetting theory against Microsoft. In plainer English: did the defendants misuse a charitable organization for private gain, and did Microsoft help?

That narrowing is crucial. Fraud would have centered more heavily on what Musk personally was told and whether he was misled. Breach of charitable trust shifts the focus toward OpenAI’s conduct and whether it remained faithful to the obligations embedded in its nonprofit origins. That matters because Musk is not the easiest plaintiff to cast as helpless. He is one of the richest and most legally sophisticated people on Earth. The image of Elon Musk as an innocent donor led astray by startup paperwork is, shall we say, a challenging sell to anyone who has ever seen him negotiate anything more complicated than a lunch order.

OpenAI knows this. Its defense leans hard into Musk’s power, ambition, and competitive motives. The company says Musk donated $38 million, received a tax deduction, and now wants the court to treat that donation like an investment that entitles him to ownership-like remedies. OpenAI also says Musk agreed in 2017 that a for-profit entity would be needed, demanded majority equity and control, and left when he did not get it. In a December 2024 post, OpenAI published a timeline claiming Musk wanted to merge OpenAI into Tesla and warned that the organization had “0% chance” of success without his preferred path.

The defense also has an obvious fact on its side: Musk later founded xAI, a direct competitor. Musk launched xAI in July 2023 to challenge companies including OpenAI, and OpenAI’s lawyers have pointed to that timing as evidence that Musk’s lawsuit is less about charity and more about competition. In 2025, OpenAI countersued Musk, accusing him of a harassment campaign, and a federal judge later denied Musk’s bid to dismiss OpenAI’s counterclaims.

The legal war did not remain confined to the original OpenAI suit. Musk’s xAI also sued Apple and OpenAI in Texas in 2025, accusing them of illegally conspiring to suppress competition in AI through Apple’s integration of ChatGPT. OpenAI called that filing consistent with Musk’s “ongoing pattern of harassment,” while Apple denied anticompetitive conduct and said choosing one partner first is not unlawful. These surrounding cases strengthen OpenAI’s preferred story: Musk is not defending the commons; he is fighting an AI market-share war with subpoenas.

Still, OpenAI’s own story has vulnerabilities. The company did not merely become a modest commercial lab with a gift-shop subsidiary. It became one of the most valuable private companies in the world. ChatGPT, launched on November 30, 2022, turned OpenAI from a relatively obscure research lab into the face of the generative AI boom. OpenAI’s own launch post described ChatGPT as a research preview trained on Azure AI supercomputing infrastructure, but the public saw something more dramatic: a product that made AI feel immediate, conversational, useful, and unavoidable.

After ChatGPT, the money arrived with the subtlety of a stampede. Microsoft had already partnered with OpenAI, but in January 2023 it announced a multiyear, multibillion-dollar investment. Reports have placed Microsoft’s 2023 investment at $10 billion. That partnership gave OpenAI the computing infrastructure it needed and gave Microsoft a head start in integrating generative AI across its products. It also gave Musk’s lawyers a centerpiece: a nonprofit-founded organization had become deeply intertwined with one of the most powerful corporations on Earth.

Microsoft is not a background extra here. It is a defendant because Musk’s side argues that Microsoft helped OpenAI’s leadership convert charitable assets into private value. Microsoft says it acted as a responsible partner and helped fund OpenAI’s mission. The stakes are high because OpenAI’s commercial value is no longer theoretical. OpenAI has raised enormous sums of outside funding, and reports have described potential valuations in the hundreds of billions, with speculation around a possible future public offering at a valuation that could approach $1 trillion.

A trillion-dollar company born from a nonprofit promise is the kind of fact that makes judges, regulators, donors, and ordinary citizens sit up straighter. The question is not whether nonprofits can have for-profit subsidiaries. They can. Museums have shops. Universities license patents. Hospitals own revenue-generating operations. The question is whether the subsidiary serves the mission or the mission becomes a decorative plaque in the lobby of the subsidiary.

OpenAI has tried to answer that question by restructuring again. In May 2025, OpenAI said its nonprofit would continue to oversee and control the company, while its for-profit LLC would transition into a public benefit corporation. OpenAI said the PBC structure would require consideration of both shareholders and the mission. That followed OpenAI reversing course from a plan that would have more fully converted its business operations, with the nonprofit remaining in control and becoming a major shareholder.

That move may help OpenAI in public messaging, but it does not erase the tension. A public benefit corporation is still a corporation. It may consider mission, but it also has investors, employees, market pressure, competitors, infrastructure bills, and the endless Silicon Valley urge to turn every noble idea into a subscription tier. OpenAI says its structure is still mission-driven. Musk says the mission has already been gutted. The trial asks the court to decide whether the law sees a faithful evolution or a betrayal dressed as corporate housekeeping.

The personal history between Musk and Altman sharpens the dispute, but it can also obscure it. Silicon Valley loves founder mythology because it turns corporate governance into character drama. One man is the visionary. Another is the operator. A third is the genius researcher. Then the emails come out, the board breaks apart, the lawyers arrive, and everyone discovers that “move fast” also applies to grudges. The trial is expected to include testimony from Musk, Altman, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, and other major tech figures.

The case also arrives with the memory of OpenAI’s 2023 board crisis still hanging over the company. On November 17, 2023, OpenAI’s board abruptly ousted Altman, saying he had not been “consistently candid” in communications with the board. Within days, after employee revolt and pressure from investors and partners, Altman returned. That episode matters because Musk’s lawsuit leans on the idea that OpenAI’s governance has been unstable and opaque at precisely the moment when the company is asking the public to trust it with increasingly powerful technology.

To OpenAI’s defenders, the 2023 crisis proves something else: the company survived a governance rupture, rebuilt, and kept moving. Altman himself later wrote that in 2022 OpenAI was still a quiet research lab working on what became ChatGPT, and that the launch triggered a growth curve unlike anything the company had seen. He described the past several years as rewarding, stressful, messy, and filled with conflicts and misunderstandings. That is the polished version. The courtroom version is less poetic: when growth becomes explosive, old promises become litigation risk.

The jury itself is unusual. The jury in the liability phase has been described as advisory, meaning Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers is not bound to follow it, although she has indicated she likely will. Many prospective jurors expressed strong feelings about Musk, often negative, while others had views about AI’s risks and usefulness. Outside the courthouse, protesters warned about artificial intelligence. Inside, lawyers argued about charitable trust, investor returns, and control. It was a fitting tableau: ordinary people asked to judge whether the people building extraordinary machines had honored their own rules.

Judge Gonzalez Rogers has already had to manage the human side of the spectacle. She admonished Musk after OpenAI’s lawyers complained about his social media posts attacking Altman and accusing him of stealing a charity. The judge urged Musk to control his use of social media, and Altman also agreed to minimize commentary. That exchange captured the absurdity of the moment: the future of artificial intelligence, allegedly, paused so adults in federal court could be reminded not to subtweet the trial.

But beneath the personal attacks is a serious governance problem. AI companies routinely speak in civilizational terms. They claim to be building systems that may transform work, medicine, education, warfare, scientific research, and democratic life. They ask for trust, secrecy, flexibility, capital, regulatory patience, and access to oceans of data. OpenAI’s founding mission gave it moral authority in that conversation. The company was not merely saying, “We can build this.” It was saying, “We can be trusted to build this for everyone.”

That is why Musk’s lawsuit lands, even for people skeptical of Musk’s motives. A plaintiff can be self-interested and still point toward a real problem. Musk can be both a competitor and an early donor with standing to challenge whether a charitable mission was hollowed out. OpenAI can be both commercially aggressive and genuinely convinced that scale is necessary to make safe AI possible. The uncomfortable possibility is that both stories contain truth. The legal system, tragically deprived of a “everyone is complicated and also kind of ridiculous” checkbox, must decide actual claims.

OpenAI’s strongest argument is necessity. The nonprofit model may have been inspirational, but inspiration does not train frontier models. Compute costs are huge. Talent is expensive. Competitors are not waiting around for OpenAI to solve governance philosophy before releasing products. Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Anthropic, Meta, xAI, and others are in the race. OpenAI says the commercial structure is not a betrayal of the mission but the only way to pursue it at the frontier.

The market is also changing around the trial. Microsoft’s relationship with OpenAI has loosened in some ways as OpenAI balances Microsoft against other cloud partners including Amazon, Google, and Oracle. Amazon has also announced expanded collaboration with OpenAI on AI agents while Altman was tied up across the Bay in federal court. The timing was almost too symbolic: while one room debated whether OpenAI had become too commercial, another announced the next commercial expansion.

Musk’s strongest argument is mission drift. OpenAI began with language about being free from financial obligations. It now sits at the center of vast investment flows, cloud partnerships, product launches, licensing deals, and IPO speculation. Even if every step was legally defensible, the arc is striking. A nonprofit created to avoid domination by giant tech platforms became dependent on giant tech infrastructure. A mission to broadly distribute AI benefits became attached to products, subscriptions, APIs, enterprise deals, and competitive secrecy. Maybe that is what survival required. Maybe it is what surrender looks like. The trial is, in part, an argument over which word fits.

The broader AI industry should be paying attention, though expecting Silicon Valley to learn restraint from litigation is like expecting a raccoon to use a salad fork. The case could influence how future AI labs use nonprofit language, how they structure public benefit corporations, how investors evaluate mission constraints, and how regulators view charitable assets inside hybrid corporate forms. If Musk wins, OpenAI could face governance changes that complicate its IPO ambitions and reshape its leadership. If OpenAI wins, the verdict may validate the idea that a nonprofit mission can coexist with vast private capital, provided the formal structure preserves enough mission control.

Either outcome will leave unresolved the hardest question: who should control technology that its builders say could change civilization? Courts can examine documents, testimony, fiduciary duties, statutes of limitation, charitable trusts, and unjust enrichment. They can order remedies. They can remove leaders or decline to. But they cannot easily answer whether humanity is safer when frontier AI is governed by nonprofit boards, public benefit corporations, trillion-dollar partnerships, billionaire rivals, government agencies, open-source communities, or some combination of all of the above, supervised by everyone and trusted by no one.

That is why the Musk-Altman trial matters beyond the spectacle. It is not simply a feud between two famous men who once shared a mission and now share a docket number. It is a test of whether mission language has legal force after money arrives. It asks whether “benefit humanity” is a binding commitment or branding copy. It asks whether a donor’s early trust can survive a company’s later scale. It asks whether public-interest AI can be built with private capital without becoming captive to private incentives.

The answer may not be clean. OpenAI may have needed money to compete. Musk may have wanted control. Microsoft may have supplied essential infrastructure and gained extraordinary leverage. Altman may have built the company into something the original nonprofit never could have become. The original mission may still exist, weakened but alive, or it may have become a ceremonial ghost haunting a balance sheet.

For now, all of that sits before Judge Gonzalez Rogers in Oakland. The testimony will continue. The lawyers will pull apart old emails, corporate documents, donations, restructuring plans, and private ambitions. Musk will present himself as the defender of a stolen charity. OpenAI will present him as a spurned founder trying to damage a rival. Microsoft will argue it funded the mission rather than captured it.

Outside the courthouse, the AI race will continue. Models will improve. Companies will sign deals. Investors will calculate valuations. Executives will talk about safety and scale in the same breath, as if the two naturally coexist because the slide deck says so. And somewhere beneath all the legal argument, the founding sentence remains: OpenAI was supposed to advance digital intelligence for the benefit of humanity, unconstrained by the need to generate financial return.

The court battle is about whether that sentence still means anything.
