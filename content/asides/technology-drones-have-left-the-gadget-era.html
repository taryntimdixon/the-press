Main idea

Drones have left the gadget era

The hard question is no longer whether small aircraft can fly useful missions. It is whether law, air traffic systems, police oversight, war planning and supply chains can see and govern the low-altitude sky now filling up.

Key points

  • The Press parsed 2,234 FAA UAS sighting reports from October 2024 through December 2025.
  • The median extracted reported altitude was 2,500 feet; 607 reports were at or below 1,200 feet.
  • FAA rulemaking is moving drones from special waivers toward routine BVLOS operations, UTM services and networked accountability.
  • Drone delivery, police DFR programs, Ukraine's battlefield lessons and U.S. supply-chain policy are all parts of the same system shift.
  • The article now weaves original analysis through the story, with clickable data graphics, 80 side rail source cards and 125 source notes.
  • The article labels original analysis separately from official facts and treats FAA sightings as reports, not validated collisions.

Image note

The lead visual and rail-card photos are new photorealistic editorial images created for The Press. They are not documentary photographs; in-story data graphics still link directly to the source notes behind their numbers.

Method note

FAA sighting reports are human reports from pilots, citizens and law enforcement. They can reveal where the airspace system is feeling stress, but they cannot prove the true number of drones, violations or near misses.
